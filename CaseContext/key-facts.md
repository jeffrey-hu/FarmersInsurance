## 1. Nic Brau's Inspection Contradicts Farmers' Denial

On 9/6/2024, Nic Brau (Farmers' Claims Spec Rep Prop) came to the property to perform an inspection. During the inspection, he found additional dents on windows and measured the sizes of a number of windows. His conduct — identifying damage, taking measurements — is consistent with someone who agrees that windows were damaged by hail.

When the family later requested his inspection report, Farmers stated they do not have such a report.

**Significance:** Farmers' own adjuster's actions during his personal inspection suggest he observed hail damage, yet Farmers claims no documentation exists from this visit. This raises both a spoliation concern and a question about whether Farmers had internal knowledge contradicting its denial before hiring Nathan Prieve.

## 2. Post-Denial Stonewalling: Prieve Report and Refusal to Discuss

After Nathan Prieve submitted his engineer report, Farmers denied the claim again on 11/4/2024. The family then:

- **Requested a meeting with Nic Brau** (Farmers' adjuster) to discuss the report — **Nic refused.**
- **Spoke with agent Pete Pietila**, who told the family to talk to Nathan Prieve directly.
- **Contacted Nathan Prieve** — **Prieve refused to discuss the report** and directed the family back to Nic Brau.

The family was caught in a loop: Farmers' adjuster refused to meet, the agent said to talk to the engineer, and the engineer said to talk to the adjuster. No one at Farmers would discuss the engineer report or its conclusions with the family.

**Significance:** This circular refusal to engage is relevant to unfair claims practices under Minn. Stat. 72A.201. An insurer that denies a claim based on an expert report and then refuses to discuss that report with the insured — while the expert also refuses — is failing to provide a reasonable explanation for its coverage decision.

**Significance:** The policy contains no requirement that evidence must be submitted by an engineer to be considered. This is an arbitrary gatekeeping standard imposed by a claims supervisor, designed to avoid reconsidering the denial. It is directly relevant to bad faith under Minn. Stat. 604.18 (showing the insurer knew or acted in reckless disregard of its obligations) and unfair claims practices under Minn. Stat. 72A.201 (failure to conduct a reasonable investigation and consider relevant evidence).

## 8. Appraisal Process — Foster Selection and Background

The appraisal process was set up by Pure Construction. They selected John Foster as the appraiser on behalf of the family. **The family did not interview Foster or have input into his selection.** After the appraisal was completed, Pure Construction informed the family that Foster used to work for insurance companies.

**Significance:** The family had no direct relationship with their appraiser and did not vet his background or qualifications. The fact that Foster previously worked for insurance companies raises a question about whether he was the most effective advocate for the insured's position. Combined with the fact that the family had no input into his selection, a court may give more weight to arguments that the appraisal process did not adequately protect the insured's interests — particularly on the question of scope.

## 9. Appraisal Award Deficiencies

The appraisal award is a single-page document. It has the following deficiencies:

- **No location details:** The award does not specify the location of the damages — it does not identify which windows were found to be hail-damaged, which were excluded, or why.
- **No explanation for exclusions:** There is no explanation of why other documented damages are not covered in the award.
- **Contradictory information from Farmers' appraiser:** When the family asked Farmers for details about the location of the damages, the appraiser for Farmers (Greg Tomes) gave contradictory information — first describing the windows as being in the "dining room," later acknowledging this was an error, but providing a corrected description that still conflicted with the executed award form.
- **Farmers refused to explain:** When the family asked for explanations of why other damages are not covered, Farmers did not provide an answer. Instead, they told the family to figure it out themselves.

**Significance:** An insurer that cannot or will not explain what its own appraisal award covers — and whose appraiser contradicts himself about the locations — is in a weak position to argue the award should be treated as a reliable, binding resolution. This supports challenges to the appraisal's scope and, if the appraisal bar can be overcome, the bad faith argument.

## 11. Window Manufacturer (Semco) Out of Business Since 2019 — Matching Implications

The manufacturer of the family's windows, **Semco Windows and Doors**, went out of business in 2019. Both Farmers and John Foster (the appraiser) are aware of this fact.

**Significance:** Because Semco no longer exists, replacement windows cannot be sourced from the original manufacturer. This has major implications across multiple dimensions of the case:

**A. Replacement Cost:** Farmers must pay for comparable replacement windows from another manufacturer, which will likely cost more than any estimate based on discontinued Semco pricing or parts availability. Any appraisal or estimate that relies on Semco pricing is fundamentally flawed because those prices cannot be obtained at any cost.

**B. Matching / Uniformity Argument:** Under Minnesota law, if replacement windows from another manufacturer do not match the existing undamaged Semco windows in appearance, style, or profile, the family may be entitled to replacement of *all* windows — not just the damaged ones. The leading Minnesota case is *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condominium Ass'n v. American Family Mutual Insurance Co.* (Minn. 2014), where the Minnesota Supreme Court held that when hail-damaged siding panels could not be replaced with a color match (same model, same specs, but color discontinued), the insurer was required to replace *all* siding on all 20 buildings — even though less than 2% was actually hail-damaged. The court held this constituted a "distinct, demonstrable, and physical alteration" requiring full replacement to achieve "comparable material and quality." The family's situation is even stronger: Semco is entirely out of business, so not just a color but the entire product line is unavailable.

**C. J6944 Endorsement — Limited Matching:** The family's policy includes Endorsement J6944 ("Limited Matching of Undamaged Property"), which may limit Farmers' obligation to replace undamaged windows for matching purposes. However, this endorsement should be scrutinized: (1) a Minnesota federal court voided a similar matching exclusion in American Family policies (see legal research); (2) the Eighth Circuit in *Noonan v. American Family Mutual Insurance* upheld a similar endorsement, creating a split in how these exclusions are treated; and (3) the enforceability of J6944 in the context of a manufacturer that no longer exists — where matching is literally impossible — may present a distinct legal question not yet addressed by Minnesota courts.

**D. Appraisal Award Deficiency:** The appraisal award of ~$12,800 RCV / ~$6,400 ACV for windows is suspect if it was calculated using Semco pricing or assumed Semco parts availability. Since both the appraiser (John Foster) and Farmers knew Semco was out of business, any estimate based on Semco costs is knowingly unrealistic. This strengthens the argument that the appraisal process did not adequately value the loss and supports a challenge to the award's scope and amount.

**E. Bad Faith:** The fact that both Farmers and the appraiser *knew* Semco was defunct — yet the appraisal award may reflect Semco-era pricing — supports the bad faith argument under Minn. Stat. § 604.18. An insurer that knowingly uses unobtainable pricing to calculate a loss payment is not acting in good faith.

**Key Case Law:**
- *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.* (Minn. 2014) — MN Supreme Court required wholesale replacement when matching unavailable
- *Noonan v. American Family Mutual Insurance* (8th Cir. 2019) — upheld limited matching endorsement, but distinguishable facts
- Minnesota federal court case voiding similar matching exclusion (additional research needed for full citation)
