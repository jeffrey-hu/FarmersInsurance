## The Core Problem

The appraisal award valued hail damage to the windows at approximately $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV. The deductible is $17,720. Because the award falls below the deductible, Farmers owes $0 under the award as written. If the award stands unchallenged, the lawsuit fails regardless of how strong the other arguments are. ## 1. Legal Standard for Challenging Appraisal Awards in Minnesota

### General Rule: Awards Are Binding

Under Minn. Stat. 65A.01, the written award of any two of the three panel members (appraiser, appraiser, umpire) determines the "amount of loss" and is final and conclusive upon the parties. Minnesota courts treat appraisal awards similarly to arbitration awards and are reluctant to disturb them.

### Narrow Exceptions

Minnesota courts will set aside an appraisal award only if:

1. **Made without authority** -- the appraisers acted outside the scope of what the policy's appraisal clause authorized them to decide
2. **Result of fraud, mistake, or misfeasance** -- material errors in the appraisal process that affected the outcome
3. **Not made in substantial compliance with the contract** -- the appraisers failed to follow the procedures the policy requires

These are the same grounds used to vacate arbitration awards. The burden is on the party challenging the award to establish one of these exceptions.

### Quade v. Secura Insurance (Minn. 2012) -- The Key Minnesota Precedent

The Minnesota Supreme Court held in Quade v. Secura Ins., 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), that a determination of "amount of loss" under an appraisal clause necessarily includes a determination of causation. This means the appraisal panel had authority to determine both what caused the damage and how much it costs to repair. Important implications for the family's case:

- The appraisal's finding that hail caused the window damage is binding. Farmers cannot relitigate causation -- their own appraiser (Greg Tomes) participated in a process that confirmed hail as the cause. This eliminates Farmers' earlier defenses of wear/tear, corrosion, inherent vice, neglect, and faulty construction.
- However, the appraisal's determination of the amount of loss also carries significant weight. Courts will not easily second-guess the dollar figure.
- The family's challenge must therefore focus on the scope of what was evaluated, not the dollar value assigned to the items that were evaluated.

## 2. Three Avenues for Challenging the Award

### Avenue A: The Award Was the Product of "Mistake" Because the Panel Failed to Evaluate All Damaged Windows

**This is the strongest avenue.**

The argument: The appraisal award valued only a small subset of windows. The family has photographic evidence of hail damage across all windows. If the panel never inspected or evaluated certain windows, then the award does not reflect the full "amount of loss" -- it reflects a partial assessment. A partial assessment that misses damaged property is a "mistake" within the meaning of the vacatur standard.

**Supporting facts specific to this case:**

- The award is a single page with no location details -- it does not identify which specific windows were found to be hail-damaged and which were excluded, or why
- The umpire (Wade Roos) produced no report, no estimate, and no documentation of his methodology (confirmed by Farmers on 9/24/2025)
- Farmers' own appraiser (Greg Tomes) gave contradictory descriptions of which windows the award covers -- initially describing the damaged windows as being in the "dining room," later acknowledging this was an error, but providing a corrected description that still conflicts with the executed award form
- Tomes did not sign the award -- the umpire sided with the insured's appraiser (John Foster), rejecting Farmers' position
- When the family asked Farmers why other damaged windows were not covered by the award, Farmers refused to answer and told the family to contact their own appraiser

**Legal framework:** Courts have recognized that an appraisal can be set aside when the panel failed to complete its contractually assigned task. If the panel was supposed to determine the amount of loss for all hail-damaged windows but only evaluated a fraction of them, the award was not made in substantial compliance with the contract. The lack of any documentation from the umpire makes it difficult for Farmers to prove the panel actually considered all windows and found only some were damaged -- there is simply no record of what was or was not assessed.

**Scientific evidence explains WHY the scope gap likely exists.** *(Added 4/11/2026 — from HailShapes research)* The family has assembled 24 peer-reviewed, government, and professional sources (see `HailShapes/DocumentIndex.md`) that demonstrate hail damage to windows naturally varies in appearance depending on impact angle, wind speed, hailstone shape, and material properties. An appraiser using a narrow damage definition — expecting only round, scratch-free dents — would systematically miss windows with elongated marks (from wind-driven angled impacts), spatter marks (documented by InterNACHI as legitimate hail damage), or frame cracking (documented for vinyl and fiberglass frames). The Calotescu et al. (2024) field study found that damage on non-windward facades presents as elongated rather than circular. If the appraisal panel used a visual standard that excluded these legitimate damage patterns, the scope gap is not just procedural but scientifically predictable. This evidence supports both Avenue A (the "mistake" was evaluating damage using an artificially narrow definition) and a future independent expert assessment of all windows using the proper scientific framework. See `HailShapes/KeyDecisionDocument.md` for the five scientific counter-arguments with formulas, and `HailShapes/RebuttalToFarmersDenial.md` for the point-by-point rebuttal of each Prieve Engineering opinion that established the original narrow damage definition.

**Semco pricing makes the valuation additionally suspect.** *(Added 4/12/2026)* The window manufacturer, Semco Windows and Doors, went out of business in 2019. Both Farmers and the appraiser (John Foster) are aware of this. If the ~$12,800 RCV was calculated using Semco pricing or assumed Semco parts availability, the valuation is doubly flawed — it covers only a subset of damaged windows AND uses prices that cannot be obtained at any cost. An award based on phantom pricing from a defunct manufacturer is arguably a "mistake" even if the scope were correct. The family should investigate the pricing basis of the award.

**What the family needs:** A written statement from John Foster (the insured's appraiser) explaining which windows the panel actually inspected and evaluated, whether any damaged windows were excluded from the panel's review, and why he agreed to the ~$12,800 figure. Foster is the family's best witness to what happened during the appraisal process. Because Pure Construction (not the family) selected Foster, the family should coordinate through Zach Sexe at Pure Construction to reach him.

### Avenue D: The Award Failed to Account for Matching — A Coverage Question for the Court

*(Added 4/12/2026)*

**This is a new avenue arising from Semco's closure.**

The argument: Because Semco Windows and Doors went out of business in 2019, replacement windows cannot match the existing Semco windows in the home. Under *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.* (Minn. 2014), when hail-damaged material cannot be replaced with a match, the insurer may be required to replace all of the material to achieve a uniform appearance. The appraisal award does not address matching at all — it simply values replacement of the damaged windows without considering whether that replacement creates a mismatch with the rest of the home.

**Legal basis:** In Cedar Bluff, the Minnesota Supreme Court held that replacement of "comparable material and quality" required a reasonable match in appearance. When only the damaged siding panels could not be replaced without creating a color mismatch, all siding on 20 buildings had to be replaced — even though less than 2% was actually damaged. The court held this was a "distinct, demonstrable, and physical alteration." The family's situation is stronger: Semco is entirely defunct, not just discontinued in a particular color.

**Why this is a coverage question, not an appraisal question:** Under *Quade*, the appraisal panel determines factual questions (amount of loss, causation) while courts determine coverage questions. Whether the policy requires matching — and what the cost of achieving a match is when the original manufacturer no longer exists — is a coverage question that the appraisal panel did not and could not resolve. The family can raise this issue directly with the court even if the appraisal award's factual findings are otherwise binding.

**J6944 complication:** The policy includes Endorsement J6944 ("Limited Matching of Undamaged Property"), which may limit matching coverage. However, the enforceability of J6944 when the manufacturer is entirely defunct — making matching literally impossible, not just aesthetically imperfect — is an open question. See `LegalReferences/Argument_Strength_Assessment.md` Section 7 for detailed analysis.

**Transformative impact on deductible math:** If matching requires full window replacement, the cost changes from ~$12,800 (below the $17,720 deductible) to the cost of replacing every window in the home — potentially six figures for a home with Coverage A of $886,000. This is the single most powerful avenue for overcoming the deductible barrier.

### Avenue B: The Appraisal Did Not "Resolve" the Full Claim -- Demand a Supplemental or New Appraisal

**This is a secondary avenue, particularly useful if Avenue A faces resistance.**

The argument: The appraisal addressed only a portion of the claimed damage. For the windows that were never evaluated by the panel, the appraisal process is incomplete -- those items were never submitted to or assessed by the appraisers. The family may be entitled to a supplemental appraisal covering the remaining damaged windows.

**Legal basis:** Under general insurance appraisal law, the scope of an appraisal should be confined to the specific items of claim that were disputed. If certain windows were never part of the dispute submitted to the panel -- either because the insured's appraiser did not raise them or because the panel simply did not get to them -- those items remain unresolved. A "supplemental claim" is recognized in some jurisdictions as a separate claim for additional loss from the same peril that was previously adjusted or appraised.

**Practical challenge:** Minnesota law on supplemental appraisals is not well-developed. The family would need to argue that the policy's appraisal clause permits a second appraisal for items not covered by the first, or that the first appraisal was so incomplete that it did not satisfy the policy's dispute resolution mechanism. This argument is stronger if Foster confirms that certain windows were never presented to or evaluated by the panel.

**Strategic note:** This avenue has an additional benefit -- if the appraisal did not "resolve" the full claim, then the appraisal bar under Minn. Stat. 604.18, subd. 4(b) (which blocks bad faith claims for matters "resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal") may not apply to the unresolved portions. This would preserve the family's ability to pursue bad faith damages.

### Avenue C: The Appraisal Process Was Fundamentally Unfair

**This is a supporting avenue that strengthens A and B.**

The argument: Even if a court is reluctant to second-guess the dollar amount, the process itself was so deficient that the award does not deserve the deference normally given to appraisal results.

**Specific process deficiencies:**

- **No umpire report or methodology.** The umpire issued a single-page award with no explanation. Courts and commentators have noted that the appraisal process lacks standardized rules, and that the absence of documentation creates a "black hole" that makes meaningful review impossible. When there is no record of how the umpire reached his conclusions, the award is vulnerable to challenge because neither party (nor the court) can verify that the umpire actually performed the task assigned to him.
- **Dissenting appraiser with contradictory statements.** Greg Tomes (Farmers' appraiser) did not sign the award and later provided inconsistent descriptions of what the award covers. An appraiser who dissents from the result and cannot describe its scope consistently undermines the award's reliability.
- **Insured's appraiser selection concern.** John Foster was selected by Pure Construction, not by the family directly. The family did not interview Foster or have input into his selection and was not aware until after the appraisal that Foster had previously worked for insurance companies. While this does not automatically invalidate the award, a court may be more sympathetic to a scope challenge when the insured had limited control over how aggressively their representative advocated for their interests.

## 3. The Appraisal vs. Coverage Distinction

An important legal principle works in the family's favor: **appraisers decide factual questions (amount of loss, causation); courts decide coverage questions.** Under Quade, the appraisal panel's factual findings are binding, but legal questions about what the policy covers remain for the court.

This distinction matters because several of Farmers' defenses are coverage questions that the appraisal does not and cannot resolve:

- **Whether Endorsement J6944 (matching exclusion) applies** is a coverage question. The appraisal panel cannot decide whether an exclusion in the policy applies -- that is a legal question for the court.
- **Whether matching requires full window replacement due to Semco's closure** is a coverage question. *(Added 4/12/2026)* Under *Cedar Bluff v. American Family* (Minn. 2014), whether the policy's "comparable material and quality" standard requires broader replacement when matching is impossible is a legal question for the court. The appraisal panel did not address matching, and this omission does not preclude the court from considering it.
- **Whether Farmers' original exclusion defenses (wear/tear, marring, corrosion, etc.) are valid** is a coverage question, though the appraisal's finding that hail caused the damage effectively answers the underlying factual question in the family's favor.
- **Whether the appraisal award's scope was consistent with the policy's coverage obligations** is a coverage question. If the policy requires Farmers to pay for all covered hail damage to the property, and the appraisal only valued a fraction of the damaged windows, the question of whether Farmers fulfilled its contractual obligation is for the court -- not the appraisal panel.

The family should frame the lawsuit not as a challenge to the appraisal's dollar figure (which courts resist) but as a coverage dispute: the policy covers hail damage to all windows, the appraisal confirmed hail causation, but the appraisal failed to account for the full scope of covered damage. The shortfall is a coverage issue, not a valuation issue.

## 4. Impact on Bad Faith Claim (Minn. Stat. 604.18)

### The Appraisal Bar

Minn. Stat. 604.18, subd. 3(c) (referenced as subd. 4(b) in some sources) states: "An award of taxable costs under this section is not available in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal."

This is a significant obstacle. The literal reading of the statute bars bad faith damages whenever an appraisal has occurred.

### The "Resolved" Argument

If the family succeeds in showing the appraisal was deficient in scope (Avenue A or B above), the claim was only partially addressed -- not "resolved" within the meaning of the statute. Key facts supporting this argument:

- The award is a single page that does not specify which windows are included
- Farmers' own appraiser cannot describe the scope consistently
- The umpire produced no supporting documentation
- The insured's appraiser may confirm that certain windows were never evaluated

No Minnesota court has directly ruled on whether a partially deficient appraisal "resolves" a claim for purposes of the 604.18 bar. This is an untested argument but the facts here are strong enough to preserve it.

### Strategic Approach

Do not allege bad faith in the initial complaint (the statute prohibits this anyway). Lead with breach of contract and the appraisal scope challenge. If the court finds the appraisal did not resolve the full claim, move to amend to add bad faith under 604.18's procedural requirements.

5. Why did he sign the award given the family's position that all windows are damaged?

### Build the Scope Gap Evidence

The family should prepare a detailed comparison showing:

- A list of all windows at the property, with photos showing hail damage on each
- The appraisal award's coverage (to the extent it can be determined from the single-page award and Tomes's contradictory descriptions)
- The gap: which damaged windows were not covered by the award
- An estimate of the cost to repair or replace the uncovered windows, ideally from a qualified contractor or public adjuster

### Document Functional Damage

The windows are reportedly leaking (documented 10/28/2025). This is critical because:

- Functional damage (water intrusion) defeats any cosmetic damage exclusion
- It demonstrates that the hail damage has real-world consequences beyond appearance
- It strengthens the argument that the appraisal undervalued the loss by failing to account for windows that are not just dented but functionally compromised

### Obtain Replacement Quotes and Semco Unavailability Confirmation

*(Added 4/12/2026)* This is now a high-priority step. The family should:

1. **Get written quotes from 2–3 reputable window companies** for replacing all windows with comparable (non-Semco) products. These quotes establish the actual replacement cost and quantify the matching argument.
2. **Obtain written confirmation from window suppliers/distributors** that Semco Windows and Doors is out of business and that Semco parts, sashes, and complete units are no longer available from any source.
3. **Document the mismatch:** If possible, get a window professional to confirm in writing that replacement windows from available manufacturers will differ from existing Semco windows in profile, dimensions, hardware, or appearance — supporting the *Cedar Bluff* matching argument.

This evidence serves multiple purposes: it challenges the appraisal's valuation (if based on Semco pricing), quantifies the matching argument (full replacement cost vs. the $17,720 deductible), and supports the bad faith claim (Farmers knew Semco was defunct yet may have used Semco pricing).

### Consider an Independent Expert

If the family can afford it, an independent window expert or forensic engineer (not affiliated with Farmers or Prieve) who can assess all windows and provide a comprehensive damage report would significantly strengthen the scope challenge. This expert's report would provide the court with an alternative valuation that accounts for all damaged windows.

## 6. How Courts Typically Handle These Disputes

### What Works

Courts are most receptive to appraisal challenges when:

- The challenging party can show specific items of damage that were never evaluated by the panel (not just a disagreement about the value of items that were evaluated)
- There is a lack of documentation from the appraisal process that prevents meaningful review
- The award on its face does not address all disputed items
- Independent evidence (photos, expert reports) contradicts the award's scope

### What Doesn't Work

Courts are least receptive when:

- The challenging party is simply unhappy with the dollar amount assigned to items the panel did evaluate -- this is "buyer's remorse" and courts will not second-guess the panel's valuation judgment
- The challenging party's own appraiser signed the award without objection -- this suggests the process was fair even if the result was disappointing
- The challenge is based on speculation about what the panel did or did not consider, without concrete evidence of a scope gap

### The Family's Position

The family's case falls closer to the "what works" category because: the award is a single page with no detail, there is no umpire methodology, Farmers' own appraiser contradicts himself about the scope, and the family has photographic evidence of damage beyond what the award appears to cover. The main vulnerability is that Foster signed the award -- but if Foster can explain that he signed based on what was evaluated (not as a concession that only those windows were damaged), this vulnerability is manageable.
