# Minnesota Case Law Research: Lu v. Fire Insurance Exchange

**Prepared:** April 4, 2026
**Purpose:** Identify legal arguments and precedent supporting the family's claims against Farmers Insurance (Fire Insurance Exchange) for denial of hail damage coverage to windows at 17756 George Moran Dr, Eden Prairie, MN. Loss date: July 13, 2024.

---

## Overview of Legal Arguments

Based on the facts of this case, the following arguments have strong support in Minnesota case law:

1. Breach of contract (failure to pay covered loss)
2. Declaratory judgment (determination of coverage rights)
3. Bad faith denial of insurance benefits under Minn. Stat. 604.18
4. Failure to conduct a reasonable investigation under Minn. Stat. 72A.201
5. Appraisal process irregularities (causation confirmed but valuation disputed)
6. Spoliation of evidence / failure to preserve adjuster notes
7. The matching endorsement (J6944) and its proper application

Each argument is detailed below with supporting case law.

## 1. Breach of Contract

### The Legal Framework

An insurance policy is a contract. Under Minnesota law, to establish a breach of contract claim, the insured must prove three elements: (a) the existence of a contract; (b) the insured's performance of any conditions precedent; and (c) a breach by the insurer. The Minnesota Supreme Court confirmed in 1979 that an insurer's failure to pay a covered claim, regardless of motive, constitutes a breach of the insurance contract.

### Elements Applied to Our Case

**Existence of a contract:** Policy 98055-49-65 was in force during the policy year 10/22/2023 through 10/22/2024, covering the property at 17756 George Moran Dr, Eden Prairie. The loss occurred on July 13, 2024, squarely within the policy period. The policy provides coverage for windstorm and hail damage, subject to a 2% of Coverage A deductible ($17,720).

**Performance by the insured:** The family timely reported the claim, cooperated with three separate inspections (Hancock on 8/29/2024, Nic Brau on 9/6/2024, and Prieve on 10/9/2024), submitted extensive documentation including photos, weather data, and neighbor surveys, filed a complaint with the MN Department of Commerce, and participated in the appraisal process by retaining their own appraiser (John Foster) and filing a sworn proof of loss.

**Breach by Farmers:** Farmers breached the contract by failing to pay for covered hail damage to the windows. The appraisal panel (including Farmers' own appraiser Greg Tomes) confirmed hail as the cause of loss. Despite this, the award valued only a small subset of damaged windows at approximately $12,800 RCV, which fell below the $17,720 deductible, resulting in a $0 payout. The family's position is that the appraisal failed to account for the full scope of hail damage across all windows, and thus Farmers' reliance on this undervalued award to pay nothing constitutes a breach.

### Damages

Compensatory damages in a breach of insurance contract claim aim to place the insured in the position they would have occupied had the contract been fulfilled. Here, the family's sworn proof of loss stated a total claimed loss of $310,000. Even if the actual covered damage is less than the full claim, the family has strong evidence that the damage exceeds the $17,720 deductible, meaning Farmers owes a net payment.

### Relevance of Sela

In Selective v. Sela, the insured filed a counterclaim for breach of the insurance policy after Selective sought a declaration of no coverage. The jury found no fraud, the appraisal panel assessed $493,789 in damages, and the court ordered Selective to pay. The breach of contract claim was the foundational cause of action upon which the bad faith claim was later built. The same structure applies here: breach of contract is the primary claim, and bad faith (Section 3 below) can be added by motion once the case is underway.

### Statute of Limitations

The policy's suit limitation provision requires any action to be commenced within 2 years of the loss date. The filing deadline is **July 13, 2026**. Note that for standard hail damage policies under Minn. Stat. 65A.01, the limitations period can be as short as 1 year. The family should verify which limitation applies to their specific policy form.

## 2. Declaratory Judgment: Minn. Stat. Ch. 555

### The Legal Framework

Under the Minnesota Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act (Minn. Stat. 555.01 et seq.), courts have the power to declare rights, status, and other legal relations, whether or not further relief is or could be claimed. A declaratory judgment has the force and effect of a final judgment. The Minnesota Supreme Court has consistently urged parties to resolve insurance coverage disputes through declaratory judgment actions.

### Why a Declaratory Judgment Claim Matters Here

Several unresolved coverage questions in this case make a declaratory judgment claim appropriate:

**Scope of the appraisal award:** The appraisal confirmed hail as the cause of loss for windows but valued only a small number of them. The family contends all windows were damaged. A court declaration is needed to resolve whether the appraisal award properly encompassed all damaged windows, or whether it was improperly limited in scope.

**Applicability of Endorsement J6944:** The "Limited Matching of Undamaged Property" endorsement restricts Farmers' obligation to replace undamaged property to match repaired property. A court declaration can resolve whether J6944 is even relevant when the family's position is that all windows (not just a few) sustained direct hail damage. If the court declares that the damaged windows are not an "undamaged property matching" situation but rather a "covered damage to all windows" situation, J6944 would not limit coverage.

**Validity of Farmers' exclusion defenses:** Farmers' November 4, 2024 denial cited wear/tear, marring, corrosion/deterioration, inherent vice, neglect, and faulty construction exclusions. The appraisal panel's subsequent finding that hail was the cause of loss directly contradicts these exclusion defenses. A declaratory judgment can definitively resolve whether any exclusions apply.

**The dissenting appraiser issue:** The insured's appraiser (John Foster) did not sign the award. While Minn. Stat. 65A.01 provides that a written award of any two of the three panel members determines the amount of loss, a court can still be asked to evaluate whether the appraisal process was conducted fairly and whether the award should be set aside or modified.

### Relevance of Sela

In Selective v. Sela, the insurer initiated the case by filing for a declaratory judgment of no coverage. The insured successfully turned this around by counterclaiming for breach and bad faith. The court's declaratory ruling on coverage — finding that the policy covered the hail damage — was the prerequisite to the breach of contract and bad faith awards that followed. In our case, the family can proactively seek a declaration of their coverage rights rather than waiting for Farmers to act.

### Practical Advantage

Filing for declaratory judgment alongside the breach of contract claim allows the court to resolve threshold coverage questions (does the policy cover this damage? do any exclusions apply? was the appraisal binding in full?) before or simultaneously with the damages determination. This is particularly useful here because Farmers has cycled through multiple rationales for denial — first the phone denial before any expert review, then the Prieve report's exclusion-based denial, and finally the "under deductible" position after the appraisal.

## 3. Bad Faith Denial: Minn. Stat. 604.18

### The Statute

Minnesota's first-party bad faith statute (Minn. Stat. 604.18, enacted 2008) allows a court to award taxable costs against an insurer if the insured proves two elements:

- **Prong 1 (Objective):** The absence of a reasonable basis for denying the benefits of the insurance policy. The question is whether a reasonable insurer under the circumstances would have denied the claim.
- **Prong 2 (Subjective):** The insurer knew of the lack of a reasonable basis, or acted in reckless disregard of the lack of a reasonable basis.

### Remedies Available

If bad faith is established, the court may award:

- Up to one-half of the proceeds awarded in excess of any amount offered by the insurer at least 10 days before trial, or $250,000, whichever is less
- Reasonable attorney fees actually incurred to establish the bad faith violation
- Prejudgment and postjudgment interest
- Costs and disbursements

### Key Precedent: Selective Insurance Co. of South Carolina v. Sela (D. Minn. 2020; affirmed 8th Cir. 2021, No. 20-2029)

**Facts:** Amit Sela's Minnesota home suffered severe hail damage in two storms (2010 and 2015). Selective Insurance filed a declaratory judgment action seeking no coverage, alleging fraud and misrepresentation. Sela counterclaimed for breach of policy and bad faith.

**Outcome:** A jury found Sela did not commit fraud. An appraisal panel assessed damages at $493,789. The district court then found that Selective denied coverage in bad faith and awarded $214,394.50 in bad faith damages under Minn. Stat. 604.18, plus taxable costs and prejudgment interest. The Eighth Circuit affirmed the bad faith finding and the award.

**Relevance to Our Case:** This is the first insurance bad faith award in Minnesota federal court and establishes that insurers face significant financial exposure when they deny hail damage claims without a reasonable basis. In our case, Farmers denied the claim outright on September 3, 2024 (before even hiring a forensic engineer), then denied again on November 4, 2024 based on the Prieve Engineering report, despite the family providing substantial evidence of hail damage including photos, weather data, and neighbor surveys showing 14 of 19 neighbors had insurance-covered hail repairs. The appraisal panel ultimately confirmed hail as the cause of loss, undermining Farmers' repeated denials.

**Citation:** Selective Ins. Co. of S.C. v. Sela, No. 0:2016cv04077 (D. Minn. 2020), aff'd, No. 20-2029 (8th Cir. Aug. 30, 2021).

## 4. Failure to Conduct a Reasonable Investigation: Minn. Stat. 72A.201

### The Standard

Minnesota law requires insurers to conduct a reasonable investigation and fairly evaluate all facts and circumstances before denying a claim. Under Minn. Stat. 72A.201, an insurer must:

- Complete its investigation and inform the insured of acceptance or denial within 30 business days after receipt of notification of claim, unless the investigation cannot reasonably be completed within that time
- Adopt reasonable procedures for determining whether to accept or reject a claim
- Weigh all evidence fairly, including evidence supporting coverage

The Minnesota Supreme Court has held that an insurer's evaluation must consider and weigh all facts and circumstances that a reasonable insurer would consider relevant. Insurers cannot ignore evidence that supports coverage; they must weigh all evidence in the balance.

### Application to Our Case

Farmers' investigation raises several red flags:

- **First denial before any expert review:** Farmers denied the claim by phone on September 3, 2024 — only 5 days after the first Hancock inspection and before hiring any forensic engineer. This suggests Farmers reached a conclusion before conducting a thorough investigation.
- **Refusal to consider new evidence:** After the family submitted neighbor survey results (14 of 19 neighbors confirmed hail damage), City of Eden Prairie permit data (25 permits for exterior work), and NWS weather data confirming severe hail on July 13, 2024, Nic Brau left a voicemail on February 10, 2025 stating Farmers would not review the new evidence.
- **Missing inspection documentation:** Farmers claims "the file does not contain a report" from Nic Brau's own September 6, 2024 inspection, during which he and the family observed dents on windows together and he measured window dimensions. A reasonable insurer would document such an inspection.

**Citation:** Minn. Stat. 72A.201, subd. 4-6.

## 5. Appraisal Process and Causation Determination

### Key Precedent: Quade v. Secura Insurance (Minn. 2012)

**Facts:** Secura Insurance denied a roof damage claim, arguing the damage was caused by deterioration rather than a covered storm event. The insured demanded appraisal, but the insurer argued the appraisers could only determine the dollar amount, not the cause of loss.

**Holding:** The Minnesota Supreme Court held that a determination of the "amount of loss" under an appraisal clause necessarily includes a determination of causation. In other words, appraisers must determine what caused the damage in order to calculate how much the insurer owes. This means factual causation questions (e.g., whether hail or wear-and-tear caused damage) fall within the appraisal panel's authority.

**Relevance to Our Case:** The appraisal award in our case (signed September 12, 2025 by Farmers' own appraiser Greg Tomes and umpire Wade Roos) confirmed hail as the cause of loss for the windows. Under Quade, this causation finding is binding as part of the appraisal award. This directly contradicts Farmers' November 4, 2024 denial, which attributed window damage to wear/tear, marring, corrosion, inherent vice, neglect, and faulty construction.

However, the appraisal valued only a small subset of windows at approximately $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV, which fell below the $17,720 deductible. The family's appraiser (John Foster) did not sign the award, indicating disagreement with the valuation scope.

**Citation:** Quade v. Secura Ins., 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012).

### Application: Challenging the Appraisal Scope

The key dispute is not whether hail caused the damage (the appraisal confirmed it did) but whether the appraisal properly valued all damaged windows. The family's evidence shows damage to all windows, yet the award appears to cover only a fraction of them. The family's appraiser's refusal to sign signals a potential challenge to whether the umpire and Farmers' appraiser properly assessed the full scope of damage. Under Quade, the appraisers had authority and obligation to determine which windows were damaged by hail and to value all such damage accordingly.

## 6. Spoliation of Evidence / Failure to Preserve Records

### Minnesota Law on Spoliation

Minnesota does not recognize an independent tort of spoliation. However, courts may impose remedies when a party fails to preserve evidence, including:

- **Adverse inference instruction:** The jury may infer that destroyed or missing evidence would have been unfavorable to the party who failed to produce it
- **Discovery sanctions:** Courts may sanction a party for failing to preserve relevant evidence
- **Barring duplicate evidence** where fraud or intentional destruction is indicated

Spoliation sanctions may be imposed where one party knew or should have known that the evidence should be preserved for pending or future litigation. The intent of the spoliator is irrelevant; even negligent destruction can trigger sanctions. The propriety of a sanction depends on the prejudice to the opposing party, considering the nature of the item lost in the context of the claims.

### Application to Our Case

Two critical documents are missing from Farmers' files:

- **Nic Brau's inspection notes from September 6, 2024:** Brau personally inspected the property, found dents on windows with the family, and measured window dimensions. On October 10, 2025, Farmers responded to the family's request for these notes by stating "the file does not contain a report on the requested date." An insurance adjuster who physically inspects a property, identifies damage, and takes measurements but produces no documentation raises serious questions.
- **Hancock Claims Consultants report (August 29, 2024):** Jayden Grimm of Hancock inspected the property, and the first denial followed 5 days later, but no Hancock report has been produced.

Both documents were created during the claim investigation and should have been preserved. Farmers' failure to produce them, particularly after the family made specific written requests, supports an adverse inference that the contents would have supported the family's claim.

## 7. The Matching Endorsement (J6944) and Its Limits

### Background: Cedar Bluff and the "Matching" Rule

The baseline rule in Minnesota was established by Cedar Bluff Townhome Condominium Association v. American Family Mutual Insurance (Minn. Ct. App. 2014), which held that when storm damage creates a mismatch between repaired and unrepaired portions of a structure, the insurer may be required to replace undamaged material to achieve a reasonable match.

### The Matching Exclusion Endorsement

Insurers responded by adding "matching exclusion" endorsements to policies. In our case, Endorsement J6944 ("Limited Matching of Undamaged Property") restricts Farmers' obligation to replace undamaged property to match repaired damaged property.

### Key Precedent on Matching Exclusions

**Noonan v. American Family Mutual Insurance (8th Cir. 2019):** The Eighth Circuit upheld the matching exclusion as unambiguous, finding American Family was not obligated to pay for undamaged property replacement due to mismatch with new repair materials.

**Pleasure Creek v. American Family (Minn. Ct. App.):** The Minnesota Court of Appeals followed Noonan and upheld the matching exclusion under Minnesota state law.

### Application to Our Case

The J6944 endorsement is likely central to the dispute, but it cuts both ways:

- **Farmers' expected argument:** Even if some windows are damaged, J6944 means Farmers does not have to replace undamaged windows to match.
- **Family's counter-argument:** The matching endorsement only applies to *undamaged* property. If the family can establish that all or most windows sustained hail damage (as their evidence suggests), then J6944 is irrelevant because there is no "undamaged property" issue. The real dispute is whether the appraisal properly identified and valued all damaged windows, not whether undamaged windows need to match. The family's photos show dents on all windows, and the appraisal confirmed hail causation. The narrow award scope (covering only a few windows) is the problem.

### Important Distinction

There is a critical legal distinction between (a) an insurer denying coverage for undamaged windows that don't match repaired windows (matching exclusion applies), and (b) an insurer failing to acknowledge and pay for all windows that were actually damaged by hail (coverage obligation, matching exclusion does not apply). The family's claim falls into category (b).

## 8. Additional Statutory Protections

### Minn. Stat. 65A.01 (Appraisal Demand)

This statute governs the appraisal process for fire and wind insurance policies. It provides that in case of failure to agree on the amount of loss, either party may demand appraisal, and a written award by any two of the three panel members determines the amount of loss.

### Minn. Stat. 65A.26 (Claims Practices)

This statute and related regulations impose fair claims handling obligations on insurers writing property insurance in Minnesota.

## Practical Considerations

**Statute of Limitations:** The policy imposes a 2-year contractual limitations period from the date of loss. The deadline to file suit is **July 13, 2026** (approximately 3 months away). This is the most critical deadline.

**Procedural Note on Bad Faith:** Under Minn. Stat. 604.18, bad faith cannot be alleged in the initial complaint. The insured must file the breach of contract claim first, then move to amend the complaint to add the bad faith claim, supported by affidavits.
