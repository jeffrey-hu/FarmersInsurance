# Case Law on Overturning, Vacating, or Remanding Insurance Appraisal Awards

**Prepared:** April 19, 2026
**Purpose:** Collect the Minnesota, Eighth Circuit, and persuasive out-of-state precedent on overturning or modifying insurance appraisal awards, and assess which avenues are realistically available to the Lu/Hu family given the 9/12/2025 award (~$12,800 RCV, below the $17,720 deductible). Companion to, and partial critique of, `LegalReferences/appraisal_research.MD`.

**Disclaimer:** This is research for internal strategy only, not legal advice.

## 1. The Controlling Minnesota Standard

### 1.1 The basic rule: awards are strongly presumed valid

Mork v. Eureka-Security Fire & Marine Ins. Co., 230 Minn. 382, 42 N.W.2d 33 (1950), is still the leading Minnesota decision. An appraisal award "is attended with every presumption of validity and will not be vacated unless it clearly appears that it was the result of fraud or because of some misfeasance or malfeasance or wrongdoing on the part of the appraisers." The burden is on the party challenging the award to establish a ground for vacatur by "clear allegations and proof." "Mere inadequacy" in the amount awarded is not a sufficient ground; the inadequacy must be "so gross as to" evidence fraud. Mork should be treated as the first sentence of any Minnesota vacatur argument.

McQuaid Market House Co. v. Home Ins. Co., 147 Minn. 254, 180 N.W. 97 (1920), holds that courts may not "lightly set aside" appraisal awards and that the award is treated as a common-law arbitration. McQuaid also recognized two challenge grounds the Lu/Hu family may try to use: (1) that the award is "invalid and void on its face because no facts were stated to fix the liability," and (2) that the appraiser or umpire was not "fair, impartial, and disinterested."

### 1.2 The actual Minnesota vacatur grounds — and a correction to the existing memo

The existing `appraisal_research.MD` states that Minnesota courts will set aside an appraisal award only if it was (a) made without authority, (b) the result of fraud, mistake, or misfeasance, or (c) not made in substantial compliance with the terms of the contract. That three-part formulation is **Texas** law, not Minnesota law. It comes from *Providence Lloyds Ins. Co. v. Crystal City Indep. Sch. Dist.*, 877 S.W.2d 872, 875 (Tex. App.–San Antonio 1994, no writ), and has been repeated in many insurance-coverage secondary sources. The family should not rely on it as though a Minnesota appellate court adopted it — no Minnesota opinion I could locate packages the rule that way.

The Minnesota rule is narrower and older. Under Mork and McQuaid, a Minnesota appraisal award is set aside only on a showing of fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing on the part of the appraisers, proved by clear evidence, with the burden on the challenger. "Mere inadequacy" of the award is not enough.

The substantive ideas behind the three Texas prongs do appear in Minnesota law, but not as a single labeled test:

- A "without authority" attack is possible through Quade v. Secura and its progeny — if the panel decides a coverage question the policy reserves to the court, the award is not binding on that question. This is a scope-of-authority limit, not a free-standing vacatur ground.
- A "not in substantial compliance" argument is pleadable by analogy to arbitration law and the language of Minn. Stat. § 65A.01's appraisal clause, but there is no Minnesota Supreme Court holding adopting "substantial compliance" as a named vacatur prong.
- The "fraud, accident, or mistake" prong tracks the actual Mork language ("fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing") reasonably closely, and is the safest to invoke.

Practical implication: when briefing this in a Minnesota court, lead with Mork and McQuaid and the fraud/misfeasance/malfeasance standard. Do not quote the Providence Lloyds three-prong test as Minnesota law; if you want the three prongs on the page, cite Providence Lloyds and argue by analogy.

### 1.3 Appraisal is not arbitration under the Uniform Arbitration Act

Oliver v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Ins. Co., 939 N.W.2d 749 (Minn. 2020), holds that insurance appraisal under the Standard Fire Policy is not an "agreement to arbitrate" and the Minnesota Uniform Arbitration Act (Minn. Stat. Ch. 572B) does not apply. This matters for two practical reasons. First, the 90-day statutory deadline to move to vacate under § 572B.23 does not automatically apply to appraisal awards; the governing rules are the common-law rules articulated in Mork, McQuaid, and their progeny. Second, the arbitration-vacatur grounds in § 572B.23 (including "evident partiality" and "refusal to hear evidence") are not directly binding on appraisals, though courts sometimes borrow from them by analogy.

Note: the existing memo cites David A. Brooks Enters. v. First Sys. Agencies, 370 N.W.2d 434 (Minn. App. 1985), for the proposition that appraisal awards are treated as arbitration awards. That framing has been substantially undermined by Oliver; rely on Mork and McQuaid for the substantive vacatur standard.

### 1.4 What the appraisal panel can and cannot decide — Quade

Quade v. Secura Ins., 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), holds that "amount of loss" under the standard appraisal clause "necessarily includes a determination of causation." Appraisers decide factual questions — what caused the loss and what it will cost to repair — but "may not construe the policy or decide whether the insurer should pay." Coverage questions stay with the court. Quade cuts both ways for the family. It forecloses any Farmers argument that causation is still open (hail has been established by the panel), but it also confines scope and valuation to the panel, which is what the family is trying to attack.

Itasca Paper Co. v. Niagara Fire Ins. Co., 175 Minn. 73 (1928), is the older authority for the same coverage/liability carve-out Quade codifies.

### 1.5 Matching — Cedar Bluff, Noonan, and a caution about the existing memo

Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co., 857 N.W.2d 290 (Minn. 2014), is the case the existing memo relies on for "Avenue D." Cedar Bluff holds that "comparable material and quality" can require a reasonable color match even for undamaged materials, and the appraisal panel there awarded full siding replacement on all 20 buildings. Important nuance the existing memo glosses over: in Cedar Bluff the appraisal panel itself found matching was not reasonably available and built it into the award. The Supreme Court was affirming the panel's factual judgment about comparability, not imposing matching as a separate coverage remedy the court could invent on its own.

Noonan v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co., 929 F.3d 633 (8th Cir. 2019), is a significant adverse authority the existing memo only mentions glancingly. The Noonans suffered roof hail damage; the panel awarded ~$141,000 for full roof replacement because shingles could not be matched. The Eighth Circuit reversed and held that American Family's "Limited Matching" endorsement — which stated the insurer "will not pay to repair or replace undamaged property due to mismatch" — unambiguously modified the Minnesota Standard Fire Policy and excluded the undamaged-material portion of the award. In other words, a Minnesota-approved matching-exclusion endorsement can defeat a Cedar Bluff-style matching claim, even when the panel already awarded matching. Endorsement J6944 in the Lu/Hu policy is a "Limited Matching of Undamaged Property" endorsement — the same family of endorsement as the one Noonan enforced. Any strategy built on matching needs to contend seriously with Noonan; the existing memo's one-sentence acknowledgment that "the enforceability of J6944 when the manufacturer is entirely defunct … is an open question" does not grapple with a direct 8th Circuit precedent.

### 1.6 Scope and causation in appraisal — QBE and Cochran

QBE Ins. Corp. v. Twin Homes of French Ridge HOA, 778 N.W.2d 393 (Minn. App. 2010), held that the appraisal panel was within its authority to apply the policy's replacement-cost valuation formula and include shingle match replacement in the award. Important point for the family: the panel has authority to decide valuation methodology, and a court will generally not second-guess that choice absent one of the Mork grounds.

Cochran v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co. (Minn. Dist. Ct. / D. Minn.) is cited in secondary sources for the proposition that when the insurer has admitted coverage for a peril (hail), it cannot carve out individual items from appraisal by re-labeling them as coverage disputes. This is useful support for the family's position that every hail-damaged window at 17756 George Moran Dr. is properly within the "amount of loss" the panel was supposed to determine.

### 1.7 Direct 8th Circuit precedent on how to attack an ambiguous or incomplete window appraisal

Two 8th Circuit cases — both applying Minnesota law — are the most useful authority for the Lu/Hu attack strategy, and neither is cited in the existing memo.

Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co., 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018), is remarkably close on facts. The Herlls suffered hailstorm damage to the windows of their Minnesota home, demanded appraisal, received an award, and then disputed what the award actually covered. Auto-Owners refused to pay for "front and right windows" on the theory the award excluded them; the insureds argued the award covered those windows on its face. The district court granted summary judgment confirming the full award; the Eighth Circuit reversed, holding that the award was ambiguous as to which windows were covered and the district court "should have returned the matter to the appraisal panel for clarification." Herll is the cleanest authority for the Lu/Hu family. It is a Minnesota window-hail case where the scope question — which specific windows are within the award — was not clear from the face of the award, and the remedy was remand to the panel for clarification rather than a court-imposed interpretation.

Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos., No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026), is the most recent 8th Circuit opinion and directly builds on Herll. The panel awarded $23,226 for "mall roof repair" with no further detail. The district court sought clarification from the panel; a majority of the panel clarified that only flashing replacement was included. The insured (Rymer) then tried to use later statements from the umpire to expand the scope of the award. The 8th Circuit held (i) the district court properly sought clarification from the panel for the ambiguous award, and (ii) appraiser and umpire testimony cannot be used to "reinterpret or expand" an award — such testimony is only admissible to show the award should be vacated for misconduct.

The practical teaching of Herll and Rymer for the Lu/Hu case is important and should be treated as the centerpiece of the litigation strategy. The appraisal award here is a single-page form that does not list specific windows; Tomes gave inconsistent descriptions of what is covered; Foster may or may not be able to explain the panel's methodology. That is exactly the kind of ambiguity the 8th Circuit says should be sent back to the panel for clarification. A remand-for-clarification request is a far easier lift than vacatur, and it is procedurally consistent with Minnesota's strong presumption of validity. However, Rymer also teaches that if clarification returns and confirms that only a narrow set of windows was evaluated, the family cannot then introduce post-hoc appraiser testimony to expand the award — they can only use that testimony to show misconduct warranting full vacatur.

### 1.8 Deadline to challenge — Garlyn and the 90-day issue

Garlyn Inc. v. Auto Owners Ins. Co. (Minn. App.) held that under the governing Minnesota statute, when no party moves to change, modify, correct, or vacate an appraisal award within 90 days of the award, the court is required to confirm the award. After Oliver, it is debatable whether that 90-day window is strictly statutory or is instead a common-law/policy-based period, but the family should treat 12/11/2025 (90 days after the 9/12/2025 award) as already past. That means any attack on the appraisal will have to be framed as a common-law vacatur or a request for remand for clarification in the context of a breach-of-contract suit, not a standalone motion to vacate under Ch. 572B. This is a real constraint the existing memo does not flag.

## 2. Federal and Out-of-State Cases Worth Citing

These are persuasive authority only, but Minnesota courts and federal courts applying Minnesota law cite them in appraisal disputes.

### 2.1 Vacatur for appraiser misconduct / dereliction of duty

The Portofino Towers litigation (S.D. Fla. / 11th Cir., Hurricane Sally litigation; ~$187M award) is a useful illustration that vacatur is available when an appraiser does not do the job the appraisal clause requires. The appraiser for the insured submitted a $233M "price list" rather than a true amount-of-loss statement and testified he intentionally left scope determinations to the umpire; the federal court vacated for "dereliction of duty" and "misconduct." The lesson for Lu/Hu is that if deposition discovery shows Wade Roos (the umpire) similarly offloaded scope decisions or didn't independently evaluate all the windows, that could supply the misconduct/misfeasance predicate that Mork requires.

### 2.2 Vacatur for umpire bias / undisclosed conflicts

Zurich American Ins. Co. v. Omni Health Solutions, LLC (Ga. App. 2015), vacated the business-interruption portion of a hail-loss appraisal award because the umpire, during the pendency of the appraisal, joined an independent adjusting firm that did work for the insurer. The Georgia court did not vacate the structure-damage portion because both party-appraisers had signed and agreed. The rule that emerges in many jurisdictions is that undisclosed conflicts of interest or a reasonable impression of bias are grounds to vacate. This avenue is relevant here only if discovery reveals an undisclosed relationship between Foster, Tomes, or Roos and one of the parties or their contractors. There is presently no evidence of that — Foster's prior insurance-side work is not, by itself, a conflict and was not undisclosed to anyone in a way that would satisfy a MN "evident partiality" showing. This avenue is speculative, not a primary attack.

### 2.3 Vacatur for failure to state facts / ambiguity / incomplete scope

Texas and Florida have a more developed body of case law on vacatur for awards that are facially inadequate because they do not state the scope or amount of loss with enough specificity to be enforceable. In Texas, an appraisal may be set aside for "mistake" when the award "fails to speak what the appraisers intended" or where the appraisers operated under a mistake of fact that resulted in an unintended award. These authorities are not binding in Minnesota but are routinely cited in Minnesota appraisal briefing.

## 3. Honest Re-assessment of the Existing `appraisal_research.MD`

The 4/11–4/12/2026 memo correctly identifies the rough shape of Minnesota law and is broadly right that the strongest avenue is a scope attack. It also overstates several points and understates several obstacles. The following should be corrected or hedged before any of this is put in front of a lawyer or the court.

Overstatements to walk back. The memo characterizes Quade as "binding" Farmers to the hail-causation finding in a way that "eliminates" their wear-and-tear defenses. Quade does make the hail-as-cause determination final for the items the panel actually evaluated. It does not eliminate coverage defenses for windows the panel never evaluated — for those, Farmers could still assert wear and tear, neglect, or anti-concurrent causation language, and the family would have to prove both hail causation and coverage. The "Farmers cannot relitigate causation" framing is true only within the scope of what the panel decided.

The memo's Avenue C (process unfairness) oversells the significance of Greg Tomes not signing the award. Under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 and the standard appraisal clause, agreement by any two of the three panel members is final and conclusive as to amount of loss. Foster plus Roos is a binding majority. Tomes's non-signature is a fact the family can use to argue that the award is "contested" or that Tomes's post-award statements are unreliable, but it does not itself create a vacatur ground. The Zurich/Omni case shows that, if anything, a majority signed award survives even when a panel member has a conflict.

The memo understates how the strong Minnesota presumption of validity affects burden. Under Mork the family has to prove one of the three vacatur grounds by clear evidence, and "mere inadequacy" in the award amount — even gross inadequacy — is insufficient absent proof of fraud or misfeasance. The memo argues that the absence of an umpire report "makes it difficult for Farmers to prove the panel actually considered all windows." That inverts the burden. The absence of an umpire report does not create a vacatur ground; the family still has to come forward with affirmative evidence that damaged windows were excluded from the panel's consideration.

The Avenue D (matching under Cedar Bluff) discussion does not confront Noonan. The J6944 endorsement in this policy is in the same family as the endorsement the Eighth Circuit enforced in Noonan. Before relying on matching, the family needs either (i) a factual distinction from Noonan (for example, that J6944's text materially differs from the Noonan endorsement, or that the matching issue here is "reasonable comparability of available material," not "undamaged-property mismatch"), or (ii) an argument that when the manufacturer is defunct the mismatch isn't a cosmetic mismatch at all but an inability to replace at all — putting the dispute outside the endorsement's "will not pay to repair or replace undamaged property" language. That argument is worth making but should be acknowledged as uphill.

Avenue B (supplemental appraisal). Minnesota has no clear statutory or case-law mechanism for a supplemental appraisal analogous to Florida's statutory scheme. The family can try this but should treat it as novel, not as established law. The more realistic procedural vehicle for "unevaluated items" is a breach-of-contract action seeking damages for those items (coverage not "resolved" by the appraisal), plus a request that the court remand the ambiguous award to the panel for clarification of scope under Herll/Rymer.

The Semco/pricing argument. The point that the award may rest on phantom pricing from a defunct manufacturer is factually interesting and worth developing through discovery. As a legal matter, though, "the panel used bad prices" is the kind of "mere inadequacy" or "wrong methodology" challenge that Minnesota courts are most reluctant to entertain. It becomes stronger only if coupled with evidence that no Foster or Tomes ever examined actual replacement-market quotes, which would support a "mistake of fact" or "failure to substantially comply" theory under Mork's second and third prongs.

The appraisal bar to bad faith (Minn. Stat. § 604.18). The memo suggests the "resolved or confirmed by … appraisal" bar may not apply if the award is incomplete. That is a plausible argument but untested, and a court could easily read "claim resolved by appraisal" to mean the claim as a whole was submitted to appraisal, even if the panel's findings are disputed. Do not rely on this as a sure thing; keep § 604.18 in reserve and lead with breach of contract.

## 4. Recommended Avenues of Attack, Re-ranked Under Actual Precedent

### Avenue 1 (strongest): File suit for breach of contract; request remand to the appraisal panel for clarification of scope under Herll and Rymer.

This is the most realistic way to overturn the existing award because it does not require satisfying the heavy Mork vacatur standard. The family argues the award is ambiguous on its face (no itemization of windows; Tomes's conflicting descriptions; no umpire report), and under Herll and Rymer the court should remand to Foster, Tomes, and Roos for clarification of which windows were included, which were excluded, and why. If the clarification confirms that only a subset of hail-damaged windows was evaluated, the family's breach-of-contract claim then seeks damages for the remaining windows as a coverage matter the appraisal did not "resolve" within the meaning of Quade. This keeps the appraisal award in place for what it did decide and attacks the scope gap directly.

Key evidence to develop: (a) a written statement from John Foster about what the panel actually inspected and evaluated; (b) a clean before/after photographic record of damage to every window; (c) if possible, deposition or affidavit testimony from Roos and Tomes about the inspection scope; (d) a contractor's itemized estimate of repair/replacement cost for windows the award does not cover.

### Avenue 2 (strong, fact-dependent): Vacatur for "mistake" or "failure to substantially comply" if discovery shows the panel did not inspect / evaluate all damaged windows.

This tracks the existing memo's Avenue A but should be pleaded as an alternative to Avenue 1, not in place of it. If Foster confirms certain damaged windows were never inspected or evaluated, that supplies a "mistake of fact" under Minnesota law analogous to the Texas mistake doctrine, and it supports a "not made in substantial compliance with the contract" argument because the policy clause requires the panel to determine the "amount of loss" for the disputed items — not a subset of them. This argument is stronger when coupled with HailShapes material showing the panel used an artificially narrow damage standard (the family already has this evidence). Be cautious about characterizing this as anything other than fact-dependent: the burden is the family's, the standard is high, and courts resist second-guessing panel judgment calls.

### Avenue 3 (coverage, not vacatur): Declaratory judgment that unevaluated hail-damaged windows are covered.

Pair with Avenue 1 or 2. Under Quade, coverage questions are for the court. If the court finds on remand or on summary judgment that certain windows were not within the scope of the appraisal, the family can ask the court to declare that Farmers owes coverage for those windows as a matter of policy interpretation, independent of the award amount. This is the path most aligned with existing MN precedent because it does not require setting the appraisal aside; it simply says the appraisal did not cover everything, so the uncovered items remain live.

### Avenue 4 (matching — viable but contingent): Cedar Bluff matching argument for full window replacement, with Noonan distinguished.

Preserve this as a secondary coverage argument, not a primary one. The family needs to brief why J6944 either does not apply on its text or does not extend to a situation where the manufacturer (Semco) is defunct and no matching part can be obtained at any price. A colorable distinction: Noonan involved shingles that could still be bought, just in a non-matching color/lot; here the "mismatch" is not aesthetic but physical impossibility. Whether that distinction holds is genuinely an open question in Minnesota, so frame it as a declaratory judgment request and not as a sure winner.

### Avenue 5 (misconduct — preserve, don't lead with): Vacatur for appraiser/umpire misconduct.

Pursue only if discovery actually turns up something. The theory would be: Roos produced no report and no methodology, so the family cannot tell whether he performed the task the appraisal clause required; Tomes did not sign and has given inconsistent descriptions of the award; Foster was retained by Pure Construction, not by the family, and may have had undisclosed insurance-side relationships that would have been disqualifying. Absent real facts, this is Portofino-style speculation and will not clear Mork's high bar. Keep discovery requests tailored to surface (a) any Foster-Farmers relationship, (b) Roos's scope of inspection and any communications with Tomes, (c) Tomes's refusal or failure to sign and the reasons.

### Avenue 6 (weakest): Supplemental appraisal demand.

Listed for completeness. Minnesota does not clearly recognize a supplemental appraisal right outside of the initial panel's authority. Ask for it only as a fallback in pleadings and do not rely on it.

## 5. Practical Next Steps

1. **Diary the timing.** The 9/12/2025 award is now past 90 days; assume any standalone motion to vacate under an arbitration framework is procedurally vulnerable. Any attack has to be embedded in a breach-of-contract complaint. The policy-based 2-year suit limit (expiring 7/13/2026) remains the controlling deadline.

2. **Get Foster's statement in writing now.** This is the same advice in the existing memo and it is correct. Foster is the only witness who can say whether certain windows were excluded from the panel's evaluation. Approach via Zach Sexe at Pure Construction.

3. **Build the scope-gap record.** For every window on the house: a photo showing damage, a cross-reference to any Hancock photo at the same location showing the "Damaged – No" tag from 8/29/2024, and a line-item note of whether Tomes's 9/18–10/14/2025 descriptions of the award put that window inside or outside the panel's coverage. This is the evidentiary backbone of Avenues 1 and 2.

4. **Obtain two to three non-Semco replacement quotes** for all hail-damaged windows, and written confirmation from Semco distributors that Semco parts cannot be sourced. This supports both the matching theory (Avenue 4) and the "phantom pricing" variant of Avenue 2.

5. **Do not lead with a vacatur motion.** File a breach-of-contract complaint that (a) seeks coverage for the full set of hail-damaged windows under Quade, (b) asks in the alternative for remand to the panel for clarification of scope under Herll/Rymer, and (c) pleads declaratory relief on matching. Preserve vacatur for Mork grounds only if discovery produces the evidence.

6. **Keep § 604.18 bad faith out of the initial complaint.** Minn. Stat. § 604.18 requires a separate motion and sequencing, and the existing appraisal-bar argument is not strong enough to anchor the case around. Make the contract claim first; revisit bad faith if the court finds the appraisal did not resolve the full claim.

## 6. Bottom Line

The most promising attack on the 9/12/2025 award is not "vacate it." It is "clarify it." Herll and Rymer — both Eighth Circuit, both applying Minnesota law — establish that an ambiguous appraisal award is remanded to the panel for clarification, and the family has genuine ambiguity on the face of this award (no window list, no umpire report, Tomes's contradictory descriptions, Tomes's non-signature). If clarification confirms that only a subset of the damaged windows was evaluated, the uncovered windows were never "resolved" by the appraisal in the Quade sense and remain open coverage items the court can decide. That strategy does not require clearing the Mork bar, does not require proving appraiser misconduct, and does not depend on untested supplemental-appraisal theory.

Vacatur under Mork — for misfeasance, mistake, or lack of substantial compliance — should be preserved as an alternative claim for a second round of discovery, not as the lead theory. Matching under Cedar Bluff is viable but has to be briefed against Noonan, which is adverse Eighth Circuit authority the existing memo underweights.
