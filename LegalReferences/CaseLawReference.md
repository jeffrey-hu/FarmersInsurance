## CL-1: Burgess v. Farmers Insurance Company, Inc.

### Case Details
- **Full Citation:** Burgess v. Farmers Insurance Company, Inc., Oklahoma Supreme Court (2006); jury verdict (2008)
- **Court:** Oklahoma County District Court; Oklahoma Supreme Court
- **Parties:** Bill Burgess et al. (Plaintiffs/Class) v. Farmers Insurance Group of Companies and Farmers Group, Inc. (Defendants)
- **Counsel:** Reggie Whitten and Michael Burrage (Whitten Burrage), Oklahoma City
- **Class Size:** Approximately 76,000 Oklahoma homeowner policyholders

### Facts
Tens of thousands of homeowner policyholders sued Farmers Insurance for knowingly failing to pay benefits for general contractor services. Farmers systematically withheld payment for general contractors' overhead and profit (O&P) from amounts paid on claims under homeowner's insurance policies. The class alleged that Farmers improperly reduced claim payments by excluding legitimate contractor costs that policyholders were entitled to receive under their policies.

### Verdict
- **Actual Damages:** $50 million
- **Punitive Damages:** $30 million
- **Fraud Damages:** $50 million
- **Total Verdict:** $130 million (largest class action jury verdict in Oklahoma history at the time)
- **With Statutory Interest:** Estimated total approaching $200 million

### Relevance to Our Claim
This case is directly relevant because our insurer is **Farmers Insurance (Fire Insurance Exchange)**, the same corporate family. The case establishes:

1. **Pattern of Systematic Underpayment:** Farmers has a documented, court-proven history of systematically shortchanging policyholders on legitimate claims. The $130M verdict and fraud finding demonstrate willful conduct, not mere error.
2. **Corporate Practice, Not Individual Error:** The class size (76,000 policyholders) proves this was company policy, not the mistake of a single adjuster. Our denial may reflect a similar institutional practice.
3. **Fraud Finding:** The $50M fraud component is significant — it means a jury found Farmers acted with intent to deceive, not just negligence.
4. **Punitive Damages Precedent:** Courts are willing to impose substantial punitive damages on Farmers for bad faith claims practices.

## CL-2: State Farm "Hail Focus Initiative" Litigation

### Case Details
- **Court:** Oklahoma County District Court; Oklahoma Supreme Court (intervention motion)
- **Parties:** 200+ Oklahoma homeowners (Plaintiffs) v. State Farm Fire and Casualty Company (Defendant); Attorney General Gentner Drummond (Intervenor)
- **Status:** Ongoing as of April 2026
- **Counsel:** Whitten Burrage (same firm that won Burgess v. Farmers)

### Allegations
Plaintiffs allege State Farm developed a secret program called the **"Hail Focus Initiative"** designed to systematically deny or underpay legitimate hail damage claims. The specific allegations include:

1. **Narrowed Damage Definitions:** State Farm secretly narrowed the definition of what constitutes "hail damage" to reduce the number of full roof replacements approved.
2. **Adjuster Training:** State Farm trained adjusters to deny total roof replacements and to reclassify legitimate hail damage as "preexisting conditions" or "normal wear and tear."
3. **Engineering Firm Collaboration:** State Farm collaborated with consultants including **Haag Engineering** and **Accenture** to manipulate damage definitions and policy interpretations.
4. **Massive Disparities:** One homeowner received a State Farm offer of $48,000 while independent adjusters estimated $290,000–$307,000 — a 6x to 7x discrepancy.

### Current Status
- Oklahoma Attorney General Drummond was granted intervention in December 2025, over State Farm's objection.
- State Farm asked the Oklahoma Supreme Court to block the AG's involvement — indicating the company views the AG's intervention as a serious threat.
- The case has not yet gone to trial. No court has ruled on whether State Farm's practices were improper.
- Over 125 individual cases accumulated by Whitten Burrage.

### Relevance to Our Claim
Although this case involves State Farm rather than Farmers, the pattern is remarkably similar to what occurred in our claim:

1. **Identical Playbook:** Prieve Engineering's report for Farmers uses the same tactics alleged against State Farm — reclassifying hail damage as "wear and tear," "installation damage," and "preexisting conditions."
2. **Engineering Firm as Denial Tool:** The allegation that insurers collaborate with engineering firms to produce reports supporting predetermined denial outcomes mirrors our experience with Prieve Engineering, whose report contains no quantitative analysis yet reaches sweeping conclusions.
3. **Industry-Wide Pattern:** This case, combined with Burgess v. Farmers, suggests a broader insurance industry practice of using engineering reports as instruments of denial rather than objective assessments.
4. **AG Involvement Precedent:** The Oklahoma AG's intervention establishes that hail claim denial schemes are matters of public interest, not just individual disputes.

## CL-3: Dagley v. Haag Engineering Co.

### Case Details
- **Full Citation:** Dagley v. Haag Engineering Co., No. 14-98-01053-CV (Tex. App.—Houston [14th Dist.] Mar. 16, 2000)
- **Court:** Texas Fourteenth Court of Appeals
- **Parties:** Dagley et al. (Appellants) v. Haag Engineering Co. (Appellee)
- **Insurer Involved:** State Farm Fire and Casualty Company / State Farm Lloyds

### Facts
Owners of 45 homes claimed significant hail storm damage. State Farm either denied claims outright or offered less than claimed damage amounts. State Farm hired **Haag Engineering** to perform engineering assessments on five of the homes. Haag had previously provided State Farm with materials stating that hailstones less than one inch in diameter would not cause damage to composition shingle roofs.

Haag estimated the hailstones from the storm at 1/2" to 3/4" in diameter. Plaintiffs alleged that based on this size estimate, State Farm's denial of their claims was **"preordained"** — the conclusion was determined before the inspection based on Haag's general guidance materials.

### Legal Claims
Appellants brought claims against Haag for:
- Negligence
- Conspiracy (with State Farm)
- Tortious interference
- Violations of the Texas Deceptive Trade Practices Act (DTPA)
- Violations of the Texas Insurance Code

### Outcome
The trial court granted summary judgment on all claims against Haag. The Fourteenth Court of Appeals **affirmed** the judgment.

### Relevance to Our Claim
While the plaintiffs lost this case, it establishes important legal theories and lessons:

1. **"Preordained" Denial Theory:** The argument that an engineering firm's general guidelines (e.g., "hail under X size can't cause damage") predetermine claim outcomes is a viable legal theory, even though it failed here. In our case, Prieve Engineering's blanket statement that hail was "not of sufficient size to dent window frames" — without ANY quantitative analysis — is even more vulnerable to this argument.
2. **Engineering Firm Liability Theory:** Plaintiffs can bring claims directly against the engineering firm, not just the insurer. If Prieve Engineering's report is scientifically baseless, they may face liability.
3. **Conspiracy Theory:** The theory that insurers and their retained engineers conspire to deny claims has been pled and litigated. While it failed here, stronger evidence of scientific inconsistency (which we have) could produce a different result.
4. **Lesson Learned:** The case underscores the need for strong expert testimony and scientific evidence to counter insurer-retained engineers — exactly what our 24-document research base provides.

## CL-4: North Shore Co-Owners' Association v. Nationwide / Nederveld

### Case Details
- **Court:** Indiana (Federal District Court)
- **Parties:** North Shore Co-Owners' Association, Inc. (Plaintiff) v. Nationwide Mutual Insurance Co. (Defendant); Nederveld, Inc. (Engineering firm)

### Facts
A condominium complex's roofs were damaged by hail. Nationwide retained **Nederveld, Inc.**, a forensic engineering firm described as a "preferred vendor," to inspect the roofs. Nederveld concluded there was **no hail damage** — but only because they defined "damage" as exclusively *functional* damage, deliberately excluding cosmetic damage that the policy actually covered.

Plaintiff alleged:
- Nationwide hired a preferred vendor (Nederveld) who applied an artificially narrow damage definition
- Nederveld reported "no damage" despite damage being "open and obvious"
- Nationwide misrepresented its own policy terms during the denial
- Nationwide conspired with Nederveld to deceive the insured

### Outcome
The court held that the policyholder did not meet the high burden required for a bad faith claim. The court characterized the dispute as a "good faith disagreement about the terms of Nationwide's insurance coverage" and found no evidence of delay, deception, or unfair pressure.

### Relevance to Our Claim
This case illustrates both risks and opportunities:

1. **Artificially Narrow Definitions:** Nederveld's tactic of defining "damage" narrowly to exclude covered damage is analogous to Prieve Engineering's approach. Prieve implicitly defines "hail damage" as only round, scratch-free marks — a definition contradicted by NOAA, peer-reviewed science, and InterNACHI standards.
2. **"Preferred Vendor" Bias:** The allegation that Nederveld was a "preferred vendor" raises the issue of financial incentive to produce denial-supporting reports. Similar questions can be raised about Prieve Engineering's relationship with Farmers.
3. **High Bar for Bad Faith:** This case warns that courts set a high bar for bad faith claims. To overcome this, we need strong evidence that Prieve's conclusions are not just wrong, but scientifically baseless — which our 24-document research base demonstrates.
4. **Differentiation:** Unlike North Shore, where the dispute was arguably about policy interpretation (functional vs. cosmetic damage), our case involves Prieve making factual scientific claims that are demonstrably false (e.g., hail is always round, east-side damage is impossible). False factual claims are harder to characterize as "good faith disagreement."

## CL-5: GuideOne Insurance — Church Property Hail Damage (Dallas)

### Case Details
- **Court:** Fifth Court of Appeals, Dallas, Texas
- **Date:** August 27, 2015
- **Parties:** Church property owner (Plaintiff) v. GuideOne National Insurance Company (Defendant)

### Facts
A church filed suit against GuideOne Insurance after its commercial property damage claims from a hailstorm were not properly paid. GuideOne's retained engineer produced a report that, while presenting facts to support the insurer's denial, contained "ambiguity and double-talk" that did not entirely discount hail damage as a source of water intrusion.

### Outcome
The Fifth Court of Appeals **denied GuideOne's petition for writ of mandamus**, allowing the bad faith litigation to proceed. The insurance company ultimately agreed to pay **$59,000** to cover the water damage — after initially denying the claim.

### Relevance to Our Claim
1. **Engineer's Own Report Undermined Denial:** The engineer's report, despite being written to support denial, contained enough ambiguity to undermine the insurer's position. Similarly, Prieve's report may contain internal inconsistencies that can be exploited.
2. **Water Damage Connection:** Like our claim (Opinion 3), the insurer tried to disconnect water damage from hail. The court allowed the case to proceed on this theory.
3. **Settlement After Litigation:** The insurer paid after being sued, suggesting that litigation pressure — backed by strong evidence — can reverse denials.

## CL-6: Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.

### Case Details
- **Full Citation:** Cedar Bluff Townhome Condominium Association, Inc. v. American Family Mutual Insurance Company, A13-0124 (Minn. 2014)
- **Court:** Minnesota Supreme Court
- **Parties:** Cedar Bluff Townhome Condominium Association (Plaintiff) v. American Family Mutual Insurance Company (Defendant)

### Facts
All twenty townhome buildings managed by Cedar Bluff sustained hail damage, with at least one siding panel on each building damaged. Cedar Bluff filed a claim under its businessowners' policy, which provided for replacement of "damaged property with other property…of comparable material and quality." Replacement panels were available from the same manufacturer in the same model, size, texture, and installation methodology — but NOT in the same color. The original color had been discontinued.

Cedar Bluff demanded an appraisal. The appraisal panel awarded $361,000 for total replacement of all siding on all 20 buildings. American Family disputed the award, arguing it should only pay for the damaged panels.

### Holding
The Minnesota Supreme Court affirmed the appraisal award, holding that under the policy's "comparable material and quality" language, Cedar Bluff was entitled to have ALL siding replaced on all 20 buildings — even though less than 2% was actually hail-damaged. The court reasoned that because replacement panels could not be obtained in a matching color, installing new panels alongside faded originals would create a visible mismatch constituting a "distinct, demonstrable, and physical alteration" to the undamaged property.

### Relevance to Our Claim
This is the most directly relevant case for the Semco matching argument:

1. **Stronger Facts:** In Cedar Bluff, the manufacturer still existed and replacement panels were available — just not in the same color. In our case, the manufacturer (Semco Windows and Doors) is entirely out of business. No replacement of any kind is available from Semco. This makes our matching argument even stronger than Cedar Bluff's.
2. **"Comparable Material and Quality" Standard:** The court interpreted this phrase to require a reasonable match in appearance. Windows from a different manufacturer will differ from existing Semco windows in profile, dimensions, hardware, and appearance — a much more dramatic mismatch than a color difference in identical siding panels.
3. **Appraisal Award Precedent:** The Cedar Bluff appraisal panel awarded full replacement and the Supreme Court upheld it. Our appraisal award ($12,800 RCV for only the damaged windows) did NOT address matching. This omission may support a challenge to the award's scope.
4. **J6944 Endorsement Distinction:** Cedar Bluff involved a businessowners' policy without a matching exclusion. Our policy includes Endorsement J6944 ("Limited Matching of Undamaged Property"), which may limit matching coverage. However, the enforceability of J6944 when matching is literally impossible (manufacturer defunct) is an open question.

## CL-7: Noonan v. American Family Mutual Insurance (8th Cir. 2019)

### Case Details
- **Full Citation:** Noonan v. American Family Mutual Insurance Company, No. 18-1393 (8th Cir. May 7, 2019)
- **Court:** United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit
- **Parties:** Noonan (Plaintiff/Appellant) v. American Family Mutual Insurance Company (Defendant/Appellee)

### Facts
Following the Cedar Bluff decision, American Family added a "Minnesota Amendatory Homeowners Endorsement" to its policies. This endorsement stated that American Family "will not pay to repair or replace undamaged property due to mismatch between damaged material and new material used to repair or replace damaged material." The policyholder challenged this endorsement after a hail claim.

### Holding
The Eighth Circuit upheld the matching exclusion endorsement, holding that it validly modified the policy to exclude matching coverage. The court found the endorsement language was clear and unambiguous.

### Relevance to Our Claim
This case presents both a risk and an opportunity:

1. **Risk — J6944 May Be Upheld:** If Farmers' J6944 endorsement contains similar language to American Family's matching exclusion, *Noonan* suggests it may be enforceable. This would limit the family's ability to claim full window replacement under the matching theory.
2. **Opportunity — Distinguishable Facts:** *Noonan* involved a situation where replacement materials were available but would not match in color. Our case involves a defunct manufacturer where no replacement is possible at all. The question of whether a matching exclusion applies when matching is physically impossible — not just aesthetically imperfect — may be factually distinguishable from *Noonan*.
3. **Split in Authority:** A Minnesota federal court separately voided a similar matching exclusion in American Family policies. This creates a split that may favor our position, particularly in state court (where we intend to file).
4. **Research Priority:** The exact text of J6944 must be obtained and analyzed. If its language differs from the endorsement in *Noonan*, or if it can be interpreted as applying only to aesthetic mismatch (not complete unavailability), the matching argument may still succeed.

## Emerging Legal Trends

### 1. Engineering Report Bias Is Under Increasing Scrutiny

Multiple sources document a growing pattern of insurer-retained engineering firms producing reports that systematically favor denial. Public adjusters and plaintiff attorneys report that these reports frequently:
- Apply definitions of "damage" narrower than the policy language
- Conclude "no hail damage" despite open and obvious damage
- Attribute hail damage to other causes (wear/tear, installation, aging) without quantitative support
- Are written by firms with ongoing financial relationships with insurers ("preferred vendors")

This pattern is described as creating "a path to the courthouse" because it exposes insurers to bad faith liability.

### 2. Scientific Expert Testimony Is Increasingly Required

Courts are requiring expert testimony (rather than lay witness testimony from contractors) to establish causation in hail damage cases. This trend actually favors our position because:
- Our research base includes 24 documents from NOAA, peer-reviewed journals, and professional organizations
- Prieve's report lacks quantitative scientific analysis
- An independent engineer using our formulas and research could provide the expert testimony courts require

### 3. Attorneys General Are Intervening

The Oklahoma AG's intervention in the State Farm case signals that systematic hail claim denials are being recognized as consumer protection issues, not just individual insurance disputes.
