## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The statute

Minn. Stat. § 604.18 (enacted 2008) is Minnesota's first-party bad-faith statute. It supplies a stand-alone statutory remedy — separate from common-law contract damages — when an insurer denies first-party benefits without a reasonable basis. The statute is structured around three elements: a substantive two-prong test for liability, a fixed remedial cap, and mandatory procedural mechanics that dictate when the claim can be pleaded.

### 1.2 The two-prong liability test

To recover under § 604.18, the insured must prove both prongs by a preponderance of the evidence (Minn. Stat. § 604.18, Subd. 2(a)):

- **Prong 1 (Objective) —** "the absence of a reasonable basis for denying the benefits of the insurance policy." The test is whether a reasonable insurer under the circumstances would have denied the claim. This is sometimes called the "fairly debatable" standard: if the insurer has a legitimate, non-pretextual reason to contest the claim, Prong 1 is not satisfied even if the insurer ultimately turns out to be wrong.
- **Prong 2 (Subjective) —** "the insurer knew of the lack of a reasonable basis for denying the benefits of the insurance policy or acted in reckless disregard of the lack of a reasonable basis for denying the benefits of the insurance policy."

Both prongs must be met. The Prong 1 objective inquiry asks about the denial's merits; the Prong 2 subjective inquiry asks whether the insurer's own internal knowledge (actual or reckless-disregard-equivalent) exposed the Prong 1 unreasonableness.

### 1.3 The remedy

Section 604.18, Subd. 3 provides that a successful claimant is awarded "taxable costs" in an amount equal to:

- "**[O]ne-half of the proceeds awarded that are in excess of an amount offered by the insurer at least ten days before the trial begins or $250,000, whichever is less**"; plus
- **"reasonable attorney fees actually incurred to establish the violation"**, capped at $100,000; plus
- "prejudgment and postjudgment interest, costs, and disbursements."

The remedy is capped, not open-ended, and it is tied to the difference between the insurer's pre-trial offer and the eventual judgment. It is best understood as a fee-shifting / contingency multiplier, not as a separate compensatory-damages award.

### 1.4 The appraisal bar — § 604.18, Subd. 4(b)

This is the single most important statutory provision for this case. Subdivision 4(b) states:

> "An award of taxable costs under this section is not available in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal."

The statute on its face denies the § 604.18 remedy in any claim "resolved or confirmed by … appraisal." Whether this bar applies here depends on whether the 9/12/2025 award is read as having "resolved or confirmed" the Lu/Hu family's claim in full, or only as having resolved a subset of it (the two windows the panel evaluated). This is the pivotal legal question for B1 and is addressed head-on in Section 4.

### 1.5 The pleading mechanic — § 604.18, Subd. 4(a)

A § 604.18 count cannot be included in the initial complaint. Subdivision 4(a) requires the insured to move the court for leave to amend, supported by affidavits:

> "A party may make a motion to amend the pleadings to claim recovery of taxable costs under this section. The motion must be supported by one or more affidavits showing the factual basis for the claim. . . . At the hearing, if the court finds **prima facie evidence in support of the motion**, the court may grant the moving party permission to amend the pleadings to claim taxable costs under this section."

The prima facie standard is the procedural gate that B1 must pass before it can be pleaded. It is meaningfully lower than a merits standard — the court decides only whether the movant has shown enough to put the claim before the jury, not whether the jury will ultimately agree. In practice this means B1 is always a second-stage move made after the breach-of-contract count has been filed and discovery is producing evidence of the insurer's conduct.

### 1.6 Key Minnesota precedent

The leading Minnesota authority on § 604.18 is *Selective Ins. Co. of S.C. v. Sela*, 353 F. Supp. 3d 847 (D. Minn. 2018), *subsequent proceedings*, No. 16-cv-4077 (D. Minn. 2020), *aff'd sub nom. Selective Ins. Co. of S.C. v. Sela*, 11 F.4th 844 (8th Cir. 2021). *Sela* upheld a $214,394.50 bad-faith award against Selective after the insurer filed a declaratory-judgment action alleging fraud, lost at the jury trial on fraud, and then lost the amount of loss through an appraisal that valued damages at $493,789. *Sela* is the first reported federal affirmance of a § 604.18 award in Minnesota and frames the statutory analysis the Eighth Circuit and district courts currently use.

Two additional points from *Sela* matter for this case:

1. *Sela* was decided on a record where the appraisal award was the vehicle that proved the claim should have been paid in full — i.e., the appraisal went against the insurer and above the deductible. On this record, the appraisal went below the deductible; the Lu/Hu family is in the opposite factual posture. The appraisal bar in Subd. 4(b) is therefore a much more live obstacle here than it was in *Sela*.
2. *Sela* also shows that bad faith can co-exist with appraisal as long as the appraisal result is itself being challenged by the insurer's misconduct during the claims handling. The core of the *Sela* bad-faith holding was the insurer's baseless fraud accusation and prolonged refusal to pay — misconduct that pre-dated and outlived the appraisal.

The Minnesota Supreme Court has not issued a definitive decision on the scope of the Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar, and the Eighth Circuit did not reach it in *Sela*. That means the precise question — whether a below-deductible appraisal award "resolves" a larger dispute for § 604.18 purposes — is an open legal question in Minnesota.

### 1.7 Relationship to § 72A.201 (unfair claims practices)

Section 604.18 does not incorporate § 72A.201 by reference, but § 72A.201 supplies the regulatory standard of conduct that Minnesota courts use to assess whether the Prong 1 objective basis exists. Documented § 72A.201 violations — premature denial, failure to weigh all evidence fairly, refusal to acknowledge supplemental evidence — feed directly into Prong 1 as evidence that a reasonable insurer would not have denied. They are proof inputs into § 604.18, not a separate cause of action.

### 1.8 What this count would look like in the amended pleading

Filed by motion after the initial complaint, the B1 count would allege, in substance:

> "Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange denied and continues to refuse to pay benefits owed to Plaintiffs under Policy 98055-49-65. On the record developed in this case, there is no reasonable basis for the denial and Defendant knew of, or acted in reckless disregard of, the absence of a reasonable basis. The 9/3/2024 denial was issued before any engineering review; the 11/4/2024 denial relied on an engineering report whose foundational factual premise ('no hail in Eden Prairie after 2020') was objectively disprovable by Defendant's own later-obtained appraisal award, by NWS storm records, by City of Eden Prairie permit data, and by a 14-of-19 neighbor canvass; Defendant's claims-handling supervisor imposed an 'engineer's report only' evidentiary gate found nowhere in the policy and used it to reject supplemental evidence without review; and Defendant's own adjuster personally observed hail damage during a 9/6/2024 inspection but no inspection notes have been produced. Plaintiffs seek taxable costs under Minn. Stat. § 604.18, including one-half of proceeds in excess of Defendant's pre-trial offer up to $250,000, attorney fees up to $100,000, and statutory interest."

The amendment would be supported by affidavits from the family members documenting the pattern of conduct and authenticating the correspondence and voicemail transcription.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **40 / 100** as filed today; **60 / 100** conditional on success in scope attack

B1 cannot be an initial-complaint count as a matter of procedure (Subd. 4(a)) and its remedy is directly threatened by the appraisal bar (Subd. 4(b)). That alone caps its strength as an "initial claim" well below what the raw factual record would otherwise support. The 40/100 rating reflects B1's realistic strength measured at the point of filing — not its strength after the breach-of-contract claim has created a factual record and either narrowed or eliminated the appraisal-bar obstacle.

- **Prong 1 (objective unreasonableness) factual record**: 70/100. Shifting denial rationales, a premature phone denial, a factually wrong engineer's report, and a documented refusal to review supplemental evidence all weigh toward Prong 1.
- **Prong 2 (knew or recklessly disregarded) factual record**: 55/100. Inference-based; no internal Farmers document yet shows knowledge of unreasonableness. Prong 2 is usually proved in discovery.
- **Procedural fit as initial claim**: 20/100. Subd. 4(a) forecloses B1 as an initial count. It is structurally a post-filing motion, not a lead cause of action.
- **Remedy availability against the appraisal bar**: 35/100. Subd. 4(b) is a significant obstacle. The claim's recovery is live only if the court reads the 9/12/2025 award as *not* "resolving" the claim.
- **Fit with adjacent case law**: 50/100. *Sela* is supportive but factually distinguishable. The precise question (below-deductible appraisal + bar applicability) is unresolved.

Weighted blend: ~**40/100** at filing; **~60/100** if the Track 1 scope-gap attack in A1 succeeds and the court finds the appraisal did not resolve the full claim.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

**The appraisal bar is the primary drag.** Minn. Stat. § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) denies the remedy "in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by … appraisal." Farmers will argue — with significant textual support — that the family demanded the appraisal, participated through appraiser John Foster, signed the Demand for Appraisal and Sworn Proof of Loss, and accepted the panel's authority to determine the amount of loss. The 9/12/2025 award fixed the amount at $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV for the windows; the siding items were resolved at $0; the only reason the family received nothing is that the award fell below the $17,720 deductible — not because Farmers refused to pay what the panel said. On that reading, the claim was "resolved by appraisal" and § 604.18 is unavailable as a matter of statutory command.

**The Prieve report creates a "fairly debatable" defense.** The existence of a licensed forensic engineer's report — Prieve Engineering LLC, File #241008, issued 10/9/2024 — attributing damage to wear/tear rather than hail is a classic Prong 1 defense. Even a scientifically weak engineering report typically suffices to make a claim "fairly debatable," because Prong 1 asks whether a reasonable insurer *could* have denied, not whether a court would agree with the denial. Rebutting that defense requires affirmatively showing Farmers knew or should have known the Prieve report was unreliable — which is a heavier lift than simply showing the report was wrong.

**Procedural sequencing limits leverage at filing.** Because § 604.18 cannot anchor the initial complaint, the bad-faith claim cannot be used to drive discovery scope, summary-judgment briefing, or settlement conversations at the start of the case. Its strategic weight grows only after discovery surfaces the kind of internal Farmers documents that would satisfy Prong 2 — if those documents exist.

**Insured's-appraiser problem.** John Foster signed the award. That makes it harder to argue the appraisal was a process in which Farmers alone had control or bad faith; the family's own representative endorsed the scope that produced the below-deductible outcome. A court evaluating Prong 1 can reasonably ask why the family is alleging bad faith over an award their own appraiser agreed to.

**Remedy arithmetic is uncertain.** The § 604.18 remedy is "one-half of proceeds in excess of the insurer's offer." The insurer's current "offer" is $0 (below-deductible). If the eventual judgment is also $0 or small, the § 604.18 remedy formula produces a small number even before the $250,000 cap. The fee-shifting ($100,000 cap) can still be meaningful, but it is not contingency-attorney money.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up

**The Prong 1 factual record is unusually thick for a first-party case.** The project's fact memos document, with primary-source citations, a pattern of conduct that pattern-matches to published § 604.18 and § 72A.201 bad-faith analyses: denial within five days of the first inspection and before any engineer was engaged; two denials on incompatible factual premises ("no other damages" vs. "wear/tear from 2020"); a forensic engineer's objectively-false weather premise; an "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule found nowhere in the policy; and a documented refusal by both the supervisor and the adjuster to review supplemental evidence. Each of these is a discrete, dated, documentable act of claim-handling conduct.

**Farmers' own later appraisal award is a self-disproving rebuttal of the denial theories.** The 9/12/2025 panel — including the umpire — found hail as the cause of loss on additional windows beyond the 2020 scope. Every factual premise of the 11/4/2024 denial that the windows were "wear/tear" or "already paid in 2020" is directly contradicted by Farmers' own subsequently-obtained appraisal. Prong 1 is rarely this clean: the insurer's own later-adopted factual finding disproves the denial rationale that preceded it.

**Prieve's "no hail after 2020" assertion is objectively disprovable and was the foundation of the 11/4/2024 denial.** Unlike a close causation dispute where the engineer and the homeowner's expert reasonably disagree, Prieve's denial rested on a categorical factual statement that storm data showed "no hail in Eden Prairie after 2020." That statement is disproved by (a) the NWS Twin Cities/Chanhassen official storm report with a 48 MPH wind gust recorded at Eden Prairie AP ASOS at 1:00 AM on 7/14/2024; (b) stormersite data submitted in the family's 1/31/2025 response; (c) City of Eden Prairie public permit records showing hail-driven window replacements at neighboring properties on the same street (EP187829 at 17490 George Moran Dr, EP190835 at 17708 George Moran Dr); (d) a 14-of-19 homeowner neighbor canvass; and (e) Farmers' own later appraisal award. The factual foundation of the denial is not merely contested — it is falsifiable and was falsified on the record before litigation.

**The "engineer's report only" rule is a documented, supervisor-level claims-handling position.** This is the kind of policy-untethered evidentiary gate that § 72A.201 is designed to regulate. It was stated by supervisor Bradlee Monson on 2/3/2025 and confirmed in writing by adjuster Nic Brau's 2/10/2025 voicemail. It feeds directly into Prong 2: if the rule was enforced at the supervisor level and documented in writing, Farmers knew (or recklessly disregarded) that rejecting the family's supplemental evidence on that basis was unreasonable.

**Missing 9/6/2024 Brau inspection notes raise spoliation-adjacent Prong 2 inferences.** Brau personally inspected the property on 9/6/2024, found dents, and measured dimensions. Farmers stated on 10/10/2025 that "the file does not contain a report on the requested date," and Brau personally refused to release notes on 10/29/2025. These facts support an adverse inference that internal Farmers documentation of the 9/6/2024 inspection was either not created (investigation failure) or not preserved (spoliation). Either inference feeds Prong 2.

**Conduct post-11/4/2024 is independently bad-faith-coded.** After the second denial, the family submitted, through Farmers' own channels: the 1/31/2025 appeal, the 2/10/2025 second response, the 5/28/2025 NWS / Interactive Hail Maps evidence (uploaded by Farmers' own agent). Each submission generated refusal, boilerplate, or silence. A pattern of post-denial conduct in which the insurer refuses to reconsider in light of obvious new evidence is the archetype of what the bad-faith statute was enacted to police.

**The below-deductible outcome is an artifact of scope, not of honest appraisal.** If the scope attack in A1 / A3 succeeds even partially — adding unevaluated hail-damaged windows, or clarifying that the panel did not evaluate the full set — the family's total covered loss crosses the $17,720 deductible, any pre-trial Farmers offer of $0 becomes the floor for the § 604.18 formula, and the appraisal-bar argument weakens significantly because the record shows the claim was *not* resolved by the appraisal.

### 2.4 What "40/100" means practically

B1 is a real argument and should not be abandoned. It is also not the anchor of the initial complaint and will not be what the family uses to break through to recovery in the first round of briefing. The realistic path is:

1. File A1 (breach of contract) and A2 (declaratory judgment) and A3 (modification/clarification of the award) in the initial complaint.
2. Use the first round of discovery to develop the evidentiary record on Farmers' claim handling — request the full claim file, the Hancock workproduct, Brau's notes (or proof of their non-existence), any internal communications about the "engineer's report only" rule, and any internal valuation worksheets used by Tomes.
3. If (and only if) the scope attack in A1 / A3 opens up or if discovery produces a concrete piece of Prong 2 evidence (an internal document acknowledging the denial was pretextual, for example), move under Subd. 4(a) to amend to add B1.
4. At the prima facie hearing, argue that the appraisal bar does not apply because the 9/12/2025 award did not "resolve" the claim — it resolved only two windows of a broader claim — and rely on the record built in A1 / A3 to support that framing.

### 2.5 Note on timing and the 7/13/2026 filing deadline

Because B1 is added by motion after filing, the 2-year contractual suit limitation (7/13/2026) does not independently cut off the B1 count. What matters for the deadline is that the underlying breach-of-contract complaint be filed on time; the B1 amendment can follow. There is no separate statute of limitations for § 604.18 that runs from the denial date — it follows the main case.

### 3.1 Prong 1 — Objective absence of a reasonable basis to deny

**A. The first denial was issued before any engineering analysis.**

- On 9/3/2024 — five days after the first inspection — Farmers denied the claim by phone and by formal written letter signed by Catastrophe Claims Rep Christian Dudley, relying entirely on Hancock's 8/29/2024 photo set. There was no opening created by the direct force of wind or hail that could have occurred and allowed water to enter and cause immediate damage." — was a baseline scope finding that Farmers' own later positions walked away from. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf pp. 1–2)*

**B. The 11/4/2024 second denial rested on a materially inconsistent factual premise.**

- The 9/3 theory ("no other damages observed / no opening") and the 11/4 theory ("damage exists but is wear/tear or from 2020") are incompatible as factual premises. One says nothing is there; the other acknowledges damage exists but reattributes its cause. *(Compare EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf pp. 1–2 with EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 1–3.)*

- Prieve's Weather Records section stated that stormersite and the NOAA Storm Events Database showed "most recent hail in Eden Prairie reported in 2020." *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 9)*

Prieve's central factual premise was wrong, and the wrongness was provable without expert testimony.

### 3.2 Prong 2 — Knowledge or reckless disregard of the lack of reasonable basis

Prong 2 is ordinarily proved through a combination of (a) the insurer's internal knowledge of contrary facts, (b) the insurer's failure to document or preserve investigation records, and (c) the pattern of conduct that survives a "fairly debatable" framing. The following facts populate each category on this record.

**A. Farmers' own 9/6/2024 adjuster personally observed hail damage but no investigation record exists.**

- On 9/6/2024 Nic Brau, Farmers' Claims Spec Rep Prop, inspected the property personally, found dents on windows alongside the family, and measured window dimensions. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md; CaseContext/key-facts.md §1)*
- Farmers stated on 10/10/2025: "The file does not contain a report on the requested date." *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/10/2025 entry))*
- Brau personally refused to release notes on 10/29/2025: "All the information we can release has already been sent to you." *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/28–10/29/2025 entries))*
- The 10/10 "no report exists" statement and the 10/29 "everything has been released" statement are in tension: if no notes exist, there is nothing to release; if notes exist and have been released, there is a report in the file.

The inference for Prong 2: Brau's own on-site observations on 9/6/2024 were consistent with hail damage (he found dents with the family and took measurements). If those observations had been documented and preserved, they would have been probative evidence of hail damage. Their absence — or the refusal to produce them — supports the inference that Farmers either (i) did not create contemporaneous written records because the records would have supported the family's position, or (ii) created and did not preserve them. Either way, Farmers' internal record-keeping does not support the "reasonable investigation" story that Prong 1 demands.

**B. The "engineer's report only" rule was a supervisor-level decision, not an adjuster's informal framing.**

- Field claim supervisor Bradlee Monson stated the rule first on 2/3/2025. *(CaseContext/key-facts.md §5)*
- Adjuster Brau confirmed the rule on 2/10/2025 by explicitly attributing it to Monson. *(EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG — Brau voicemail referring to "field claim supervisor ... did receive the information")*
- A rule enforced at the supervisory level is stronger Prong 2 evidence than an individual adjuster's ad hoc decision. It demonstrates an institutional policy of refusing to consider non-engineer evidence, which is inconsistent with § 72A.201's "weigh all evidence fairly" requirement.

**C. Farmers maintained the denial after disproving evidence was in hand.**

- The family's 1/31/2025 appeal and 2/10/2025 second response supplied disproving evidence — stormersite data, city permit data, neighbor canvass. *(EventDocuments/2025-01-31-ResponseToFarmer.pdf; EventDocuments/2025-01-31-ResponseToFarmer-01-31-25-StormsAfter2020.pdf; EventDocuments/2025-02-10-SecondResponseToFinalReport.pdf)*
- The 5/28/2025 NWS/Interactive Hail Maps evidence was uploaded to Farmers' system by Pete Pietila. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md)*
- Farmers did not retract the denial, did not reopen the claim, did not solicit an updated Prieve opinion, and did not engage with the new weather evidence on the merits. An insurer that receives disproving evidence for its denial's factual premise and does not respond is — on a Prong 2 inference — knowing or recklessly disregarding the absence of a reasonable basis.

**D. Farmers' shifting rationales are independent Prong 2 evidence.**

- 9/3/2024 denial: no opening / no other damages. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf)*
- 11/4/2024 denial: wear/tear / 2020 already paid / six additional exclusions. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf)*
- Post-appraisal position (10/21/2025): "The claim was settled per the binding appraisal award received. The loss is under your deductible." *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/21/2025 entries))*
- Each position rests on a factual premise the prior position denied. A jury or court can reasonably infer from the pattern that each rationale was ad hoc — selected to support the outcome Farmers wanted rather than derived from honest application of the policy to the facts.

**E. Post-appraisal inconsistency in describing what the award covers.**

- On 9/18/2025 Farmers described the awarded windows as "front right, and the rear elevation in the dining room." *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/18/2025 entry))*
- On 9/19/2025 Farmers said the dining-room reference was a mistake. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/19/2025 entry))*
- On 10/14/2025 Farmers repeated the original "dining room" description verbatim. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/14/2025 entry))*
- An insurer that cannot describe its own binding award consistently across three communications over four weeks is in a weak position to argue the award represents an honest resolution of the claim.

**F. The post-award stonewall.**

- On 10/21/2025 the family asked why the award covered only two windows despite photographic damage across most of the home; Farmers' same-day reply directed the family to their own appraiser for any explanation. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/21/2025 entries))*

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal Award?

### 4.1 Short answer

**The liability claim does not depend on vacating, modifying, or correcting the appraisal award. The remedy under § 604.18 may depend on it — specifically, whether the Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar applies.** The dependency is remedial, not substantive, and it affects a specific part of the remedy package rather than the claim's viability.

### 4.2 Liability vs. remedy: the two questions are different

**Liability side — independent of the appraisal.** The two statutory prongs focus on Farmers' conduct, not on the appraisal outcome. Prong 1 (objective absence of a reasonable basis) is evaluated against the record of what Farmers knew and how it handled the claim. Most of the liability-side conduct predates the appraisal: the 9/3/2024 premature denial, the 11/4/2024 denial on a false factual premise, the Monson / Brau "engineer's report only" rule articulated in February 2025, the missing Brau inspection record, the circular refusal to discuss the Prieve report, and the ignored NWS evidence submitted through Farmers' own agent. None of these facts requires the appraisal award to be disturbed. A court can find that Farmers acted in bad faith as to the handling of the claim regardless of what the panel later decided on amount of loss.

**Remedy side — potentially dependent on the appraisal.** The § 604.18 remedy formula is "one-half of proceeds awarded that are in excess of an amount offered by the insurer at least ten days before trial, or $250,000, whichever is less." Subd. 4(b) imposes an additional condition: the remedy is not available "in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal." This is where the appraisal award matters. If the 9/12/2025 award is read as having "resolved" the claim, Subd. 4(b) eliminates the taxable-costs remedy altogether, leaving only common-law remedies outside § 604.18. If the award is read as having resolved only part of the claim (the two windows it evaluated), the bar does not apply to the unresolved remainder, and the statutory remedy is available to that portion.

### 4.3 The appraisal-bar question is itself open in Minnesota

No Minnesota appellate court has definitively construed the "resolved or confirmed" language in Subd. 4(b). Three plausible readings survive on the present record:

1. **Strict reading (insurer's position).** Any claim that went through an appraisal is "resolved or confirmed by … appraisal," regardless of whether the award was for $0, $12,800, or $1,000,000. The family signed the Demand for Appraisal, participated, and their appraiser signed. The claim was submitted to the process and the process produced an award. Subd. 4(b) applies.
2. **Scope reading (insured's position).** A claim is "resolved or confirmed by appraisal" only to the extent the appraisal actually evaluated the disputed items. Where, as here, the award is ambiguous as to scope, covers only a subset of damaged windows, and is accompanied by Farmers' own later inability to describe it consistently, the claim as a whole was not "resolved." Subd. 4(b) applies only to the windows the panel actually evaluated (F1.7 and B1.2) and does not bar a § 604.18 claim for the unevaluated remainder.
3. **Process-quality reading (insured's alternative).** Subd. 4(b) presupposes an appraisal conducted in substantial compliance with § 65A.01 — including the "stating separately actual value and loss to each item" requirement. Where the award does not itemize and the umpire produces no report, there is not an "appraisal" within the meaning of Subd. 4(b), and the bar does not attach.

The scope reading (Option 2) is the strongest reading for the family and is the most likely to persuade a Minnesota court given the facts of this record. It aligns with *Quade*'s rule that coverage questions are reserved to the court and implicitly with the *Herll* / *Rymer* line's treatment of ambiguous awards as matters that remain open until clarified. It is untested at the appellate level, and a court could well disagree.

### 4.4 The A1–A3 scope attack is therefore load-bearing for B1's remedy, not for its liability

The practical dependency runs through the scope attack in A1 (breach of contract — unevaluated-windows theory) and A3 (action to clarify, modify, or correct the appraisal award):

- If A1 / A3 succeed in having the court recognize that the 9/12/2025 award covers only a subset of the family's claim — whether through *Herll* / *Rymer* remand for clarification, through modification to comply with § 65A.01 itemization requirements, or through a *Quade* coverage ruling on unevaluated windows — then the appraisal bar in Subd. 4(b) does not apply to the unresolved portion, and the § 604.18 remedy is available.
- If A1 / A3 fail and the court reads the 9/12/2025 award as fully "resolving" the claim at $0 net after deductible, then Subd. 4(b) most likely bars the § 604.18 remedy entirely.

In that sense, B1's statutory remedy is practically tied to the success of A1 / A3. But B1's underlying liability case — the factual record of unreasonable denial — does not depend on disturbing the award. Farmers' pre-appraisal and inter-appraisal conduct is what it is regardless of how the court handles the award itself.

### 4.5 Alternatives if the appraisal bar applies

Even if Subd. 4(b) bars the § 604.18 remedy, several adjacent theories preserve value from the bad-faith record:

- **Prejudgment interest under Minn. Stat. § 549.09** runs on any contract recovery and is not barred by the appraisal bar. This is an independent statutory entitlement.
- **Attorney fees and costs under § 549.211** (frivolous-litigation sanctions) may be available if Farmers' litigation positions are found to have been asserted without evidentiary support. Not directly tied to § 604.18.
- **Punitive damages under Minn. Stat. § 549.20** require clear-and-convincing evidence of "deliberate disregard for the rights or safety of others" and must be added by post-pleading motion under § 549.191. The § 604.18 factual record feeds directly into the § 549.20 framework but is not the same claim and is not governed by Subd. 4(b). Punitive damages are rarely granted in first-party insurance cases, but the factual record here is the kind that makes the motion non-frivolous.
- **Evidentiary use of the bad-faith record in the contract case.** The conduct evidence supporting B1 also supports the A1 narrative — it colors how a jury sees Farmers' performance of the contract. That narrative value is not barred by Subd. 4(b).

### 4.6 Summary on the key question

- The **liability** inquiry under § 604.18 does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting the appraisal award. The factual predicates — premature denial, false foundational engineering premise, shifting rationales, "engineer's report only" rule, missing Brau notes, refusal to consider disproving evidence — exist independent of the award.
- The **remedy** inquiry under § 604.18 is likely dependent. The Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar presents a serious obstacle to recovering taxable costs if the 9/12/2025 award is read as having "resolved" the claim. The most promising route to avoid that bar is a successful A1 / A3 scope attack that establishes the award did not resolve the claim as a whole.
- B1 therefore cannot be run as a standalone theory in the initial complaint — both because Subd. 4(a) requires a post-filing motion and because without A1 / A3's scope work, the remedy is exposed to the Subd. 4(b) bar. B1's practical value is realized only after the breach-of-contract framework has loosened the appraisal award's hold on the case.
