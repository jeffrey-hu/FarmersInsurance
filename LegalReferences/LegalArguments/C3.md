## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The substantive prohibition — § 325F.69

Minn. Stat. § 325F.69, Subd. 1 (the MCFA's core operative provision) prohibits:

> "The act, use, or employment by any person of any fraud, false pretense, false promise, misrepresentation, misleading statement or deceptive practice, with the intent that others rely thereon in connection with the sale of any merchandise, whether or not any person has in fact been misled, deceived, or damaged thereby[.]"

The statute is a consumer-protection prohibition, structured around a list of disjunctive categories (fraud, false pretense, false promise, misrepresentation, misleading statement, deceptive practice) and an "in connection with the sale of any merchandise" connective phrase. It does not require proof that any consumer was actually misled or damaged at the prohibition stage — those issues attach to the remedy.

Two further definitional moves are foundational:

- **"Merchandise" is defined broadly** in § 325F.68, Subd. 2 to include "any objects, wares, goods, commodities, intangibles, real estate, loans, **or services**." Minnesota courts and the Attorney General's Office have applied "merchandise" to **insurance** as a service or intangible. *Force v. ITT Hartford Life & Annuity Ins. Co.*, 4 F. Supp. 2d 843, 858 (D. Minn. 1998) (Minnesota's CFA "can be read to encompass the sale of insurance policies"). The State of Minnesota's published consumer-protection materials also identify insurance among the categories of "merchandise" for MCFA purposes. *(But see § 1.5 below for the open question of whether insurance-claim-handling conduct is "in connection with the sale" of insurance.)*
- **"Sale" is defined broadly** in § 325F.68, Subd. 4 to include any "sale, offer for sale, or attempt to sell" — and Minnesota courts have generally read the term to reach the entire transactional relationship between a seller and a consumer, not just the moment of contract formation.

### 1.2 The two private enforcement vehicles

There are **two distinct routes** for a private plaintiff to enforce § 325F.69. They have overlapping but not identical doctrinal hurdles, and the recent legislative landscape has changed the analysis materially since *Ly v. Nystrom* was decided in 2000.

**Route 1 — Private Attorney General Statute, Minn. Stat. § 8.31, Subd. 3a.**

> "[A]ny person injured by a violation of any of the laws referred to in subdivision 1 may bring a civil action and recover damages, together with costs and disbursements, including costs of investigation and reasonable attorney fees, and receive other equitable relief as determined by the court."

Subdivision 1 of § 8.31 lists the statutes the Attorney General is empowered to enforce. The MCFA is one of them. § 8.31 Subd. 3a accordingly opens a private-attorney-general route to enforce § 325F.69 with two attractive features that ordinary contract or tort claims do not provide: **statutory attorney fees** and **costs of investigation**. These are not capped, in contrast to § 604.18, Subd. 3's $100,000 attorney-fee ceiling. For a pro se plaintiff family that is contemplating litigation in part because no attorney would take the case on contingency (the 6/11/2025 MN Lawyer Referral fee refund), the fee-shifting potential of § 8.31 Subd. 3a is the most consequential remedy in this argument catalog.

**The "public benefit" gloss — *Ly v. Nystrom*.** The Minnesota Supreme Court in *Ly v. Nystrom*, 615 N.W.2d 302, 314 (Minn. 2000), held that § 8.31, Subd. 3a "applies only to those claimants who demonstrate that their cause of action benefits the public." A "single, one-on-one transaction" where the misrepresentation "was made only to appellant" did not satisfy the public-benefit test. Most subsequent appellate authority has applied *Ly* to filter out one-off consumer disputes that do not implicate broader regulatory concerns. *See also Collins v. Minn. Sch. of Bus., Inc.*, 655 N.W.2d 320, 330 (Minn. 2003) (public benefit shown where defendant's misrepresentations were made to numerous prospective students).

**Route 2 — Direct private action under § 325F.70 (post-2023 amendment).**

The 2023 Minnesota Legislature amended § 325F.70 to add a direct private-action subdivision specifically for natural-person consumers harmed by §§ 325F.68–325F.70 violations. The key features of the amendment:

> "A consumer injured by a violation of sections 325F.68 to 325F.70, **in connection with a sale of merchandise for personal, family, household, or agricultural purposes**, may bring a civil action and recover damages, together with costs and disbursements, including costs of investigation and reasonable attorney fees, and receive other equitable relief as determined by the court. **An action brought under this section benefits the public.**"

Two features matter for this case. First, the new subdivision provides the same fee-shifting benefits as § 8.31 Subd. 3a (damages plus costs, investigation costs, and attorney fees) without depending on the Attorney General's enforcement authority. Second, and more importantly, the statute itself **declares** that an action under it "benefits the public" — a legislative answer to the *Ly v. Nystrom* public-benefit hurdle for the personal/family/household-purpose subset of MCFA cases. The Lu/Hu family's homeowner's policy plainly fits the "personal, family, [or] household" purpose category, so the statutory public-benefit declaration is on its face available.

This 2023 amendment is the most consequential legal development for C3 since *Ly* was decided. It is also untested at the appellate level on any insurance-related fact pattern, which both creates uncertainty and gives the family (and any future similarly situated insured) the strategic upside of being early movers in shaping the doctrine.

### 1.3 Liability elements (private action under either route)

A private MCFA claim requires the plaintiff to plead and prove:

1. **A prohibited act under § 325F.69, Subd. 1** — fraud, false pretense, false promise, misrepresentation, misleading statement, or deceptive practice;
2. **Made "with the intent that others rely thereon"** — note this is intent that *others* rely, not proof that this plaintiff did rely (see § 1.4 on causation);
3. **In connection with the sale of any merchandise** — the textual nexus between the prohibited act and a merchandise transaction;
4. **Causal nexus to plaintiff's damages** — the prohibited act caused the plaintiff's injury (see § 1.4); and
5. **For Route 1 (§ 8.31 Subd. 3a)**: a public-benefit showing under *Ly v. Nystrom*. **For Route 2 (§ 325F.70)**: pleading that the merchandise was for "personal, family, household, or agricultural purposes" and invoking the statutory public-benefit declaration.

Compared to common-law fraud (B2), three of these elements are doctrinally easier to prove:

- **No "knowledge of falsity" requirement.** The MCFA does not require the plaintiff to prove the defendant's scienter. The statute prohibits "deceptive practice" "with the intent that others rely thereon" — a subjective element about the defendant's purpose to induce reliance, but not the heightened "knew or recklessly disregarded the falsity" requirement that fraud requires. *See* B2.md § 1.2. Negligent misrepresentation under Restatement § 552 has its own friction (arm's-length and "guidance of others in business transactions"); the MCFA does not impose those filters.
- **No "justifiable reliance" by the individual plaintiff.** This is the largest doctrinal difference from common-law fraud, and the central strategic upside of C3 over B2. Under *Wiegand v. Walser Auto. Groups, Inc.*, 683 N.W.2d 807, 813 (Minn. 2004) and *Group Health Plan, Inc. v. Philip Morris, Inc.*, 621 N.W.2d 2, 13–15 (Minn. 2001), an MCFA plaintiff need not prove individual justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation to establish causation. The plaintiff still must prove a "**causal nexus**" between the prohibited conduct and the claimed damages, but that causal nexus may be established by direct or circumstantial evidence other than the plaintiff's own reliance — including pattern-of-conduct evidence affecting a class of consumers. This loosens the central choke-point that limits B2's strength on this record (the family's prompt rejection of Farmers' false statements).
- **No requirement to prove out-of-pocket reliance damages.** The MCFA permits recovery of "damages" without distinguishing between contract-style expectation damages and tort-style reliance damages. The claim can therefore reach the same dollar exposure as A1 (covered hail-damage value) without the *Lewis v. Citizens Agency* out-of-pocket measurement that constrains B2.

### 1.4 Causal nexus standard

*Group Health Plan* is the leading Minnesota authority on causation under the MCFA. The Court held that "where, as in this case, the plaintiffs' damages are alleged to be caused by a lengthy course of prohibited conduct that affected a large number of consumers, the showing of reliance that must be made to prove a causal nexus need not include direct evidence of reliance by individual consumers of defendants' products. Rather, the causal nexus and its reliance component may be established by other direct or circumstantial evidence that the district court determines is relevant and probative as to the relationship between the claimed damages and the alleged prohibited conduct." 621 N.W.2d at 15.

*Wiegand* refined the rule for individual plaintiffs in single-transaction settings: "A private consumer fraud class action does not necessarily require the justifiable reliance standard of common law fraud." 683 N.W.2d at 813. The lower threshold is "causal nexus" (whether the prohibited conduct caused the injury), which can be shown by means other than individual subjective reliance — for example, by showing the conduct foreseeably caused the harm of which the plaintiff complains.

For an individual-plaintiff MCFA case (as the Lu/Hu family's case would be unless filed as a class action), the causal-nexus showing is meaningfully easier than common-law-fraud reliance but is not a giveaway. The family must still tie Farmers' specific prohibited conduct (e.g., the false weather statement, the "engineer's report only" rule) to specific damages in the case (e.g., the cost of the appraisal channel, the delay between denial and litigation, the unpaid policy proceeds, ongoing water intrusion).

### 1.5 The hardest legal hurdle — "in connection with the sale of any merchandise"

The single largest open question for C3 is whether **claims-handling conduct in 2024–2025**, several years after the original 2014 sale and after the 2023 renewal, is "in connection with the sale of any merchandise" within the meaning of § 325F.69.

There are at least three plausible readings, all of which have some support in the case law:

1. **Narrow reading (Farmers' position).** The "sale" is the formation of the insurance contract. The conduct alleged in the complaint occurred after the sale — denials, refusals to consider new evidence, deceptive engineering reports — and is therefore not "in connection with the sale." This reading is supported by the pure-text observation that § 325F.69 is structured around "sale" rather than "performance," and by Minnesota authority distinguishing insurance-claims-handling conduct from insurance-marketing conduct. *See, e.g., Morris v. Am. Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 386 N.W.2d 233 (Minn. 1986) (no private action under § 72A unfair-claims-practices statute; legislative scheme channels claims-handling regulation to the Commissioner). Although *Morris* construed § 72A rather than § 325F.69, its reasoning about the line between "sales" conduct and "claims-handling" conduct is the doctrinal parent of the narrow reading.
2. **Broad reading (insured's position).** The "sale" of insurance is a continuing relationship — premium is paid, the policy renews periodically (here, in 2023 within twelve months of the loss), and the insurer's representations about how it will handle claims are inducements to maintain the relationship. *Force v. ITT Hartford*, 4 F. Supp. 2d at 858, expressly read "sale of merchandise" to "encompass the sale of insurance policies" and rejected the insurer's narrow construction. Under *Force*, statements made about claims-handling that maintain the consumer's continued purchase of insurance are "in connection with the sale" of the policy. On this reading, Farmers' representations to the family during 2024–2025 were "in connection with" the existing 2023 renewal sale and the ongoing premium-paying relationship; they are not separate from the sale.
3. **Hybrid reading (specific-misrepresentation focus).** The "in connection with the sale" inquiry asks whether the prohibited conduct was a representation that played a role in inducing or maintaining a sale. The 2/3/2025 supervisor statement that "extruded aluminum windows are easily damaged" — used as a basis to refuse coverage — is a statement about the policy's coverage terms made to a paying consumer. The "engineer's report only" rule is a statement about the policy's effective evidentiary requirements. To the extent these statements describe (or misdescribe) the contract product the family purchased — as opposed to merely refusing to perform on a known-good contract — they are "in connection with the sale" of that product. The 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials, which incorporate Prieve's report, similarly describe the scope of the coverage product and what the policy will and will not pay for.

Reading 1 is the textually cleanest defense reading; Reading 2 has the strongest precedential support in *Force*; Reading 3 is the most factually specific to this record. The Minnesota Supreme Court has not definitively resolved which reading governs in a first-party hail-claim setting. The argument is therefore live and non-frivolous, but it is the principal doctrinal weight C3 must carry.

### 1.6 Pleading mechanics

Unlike § 604.18, the MCFA does not require a post-filing motion. C3 can be pleaded directly in the initial complaint as a freestanding count. It does not require a prima facie hearing under Subd. 4(a) of § 604.18, and it is not subject to § 604.18, Subd. 4(b)'s appraisal bar.

Two pleading-discipline notes:

- **Rule 9.02 particularity.** The MCFA "fraud" and "misrepresentation" categories are subject to Minn. R. Civ. P. 9.02 particularity. *Tuttle v. Lorillard Tobacco Co.*, 118 F. Supp. 2d 954, 962 (D. Minn. 2000). The "deceptive practice" and "misleading statement" categories are arguably more lenient. Plead each candidate misrepresentation with date, speaker, medium, and content, and frame the broader course of conduct under "deceptive practice" to preserve flexibility.
- **Public-benefit pleading.** For Route 1 (§ 8.31), allege facts supporting a *Ly* public-benefit showing. For Route 2 (§ 325F.70), invoke the personal/family/household purpose and quote the statutory "An action brought under this section benefits the public" declaration.

### 1.7 Statute of limitations

A claim under §§ 325F.69 / 8.31 Subd. 3a is generally subject to **6 years** as a "liability created by statute." Minn. Stat. § 541.05, Subd. 1(2). The 2023 amendment to § 325F.70 created a new statutory liability and the same 6-year period applies to a claim under that subdivision. Where the prohibited act sounds in fraud, § 541.05, Subd. 1(6) supplies a 6-year period that does not accrue until "the discovery by the aggrieved party of the facts constituting the fraud." Either way, the limitations period is not close to running on this record — the earliest candidate prohibited acts are dated 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024, and the most likely accrual dates are even later.

### 1.8 Remedies

A successful MCFA plaintiff under either route may recover:

- **Damages** — without an out-of-pocket-only measurement; the family's covered hail-damage loss can be the damages metric. There is no statutory cap (in contrast to § 604.18's $250,000 cap on the taxable-costs piece).
- **Costs and disbursements**, including **costs of investigation**.
- **Reasonable attorney fees**.
- **Other equitable relief** as the court determines.

The fee-shifting feature is the marquee remedy and is the single largest reason to carry C3. If the lawsuit succeeds at all on the MCFA theory, attorney fees are recoverable on the MCFA count without the § 604.18 $100,000 cap. For a family that has already exhausted the MN Lawyer Referral Service and has been operating under significant pro se constraints, fee-shifting under the MCFA could be the structural difference between affordable representation and not.

### 1.9 What the count would look like in a complaint

A pleaded C3 count would read approximately as follows:

> "Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange engaged in a course of misrepresentation, misleading statement, and deceptive practice in violation of Minn. Stat. § 325F.69, Subd. 1, in connection with the sale and renewal of homeowner's insurance Policy 98055-49-65 to Plaintiffs for personal, family, and household purposes. The prohibited conduct includes (a) the 9/3/2024 written denial that 'no other damages [were] observed' on the home, despite Defendant's own adjuster Nic Brau personally observing dents on multiple windows on 9/6/2024; (b) the 11/4/2024 written denial, on Defendant's letterhead, adopting and transmitting the Prieve Engineering report's representation that 'the most recent hail in Eden Prairie reported in 2020,' which was objectively disprovable by NWS Twin Cities/Chanhassen records, City of Eden Prairie permit records (EP187829, EP190835), a 14-of-19 neighbor canvass, and Defendant's own subsequent 9/12/2025 appraisal award; and (c) the supervisor- and adjuster-level imposition in February 2025 of an 'engineer's report only' evidentiary gate found nowhere in Policy 98055-49-65, used to reject Plaintiffs' supplemental evidence without review. Defendant made these representations and adopted these practices with the intent that Plaintiffs and similarly situated insureds rely on them and accept the denial. Plaintiffs were injured as a result, including by the costs of pursuing the contractual appraisal channel ($1,100 share of the $2,200 appraiser retainer), by ongoing consequential water damage to the property accruing during the period of induced delay, and by the unpaid policy proceeds owing under the policy. Plaintiffs seek damages, costs and disbursements, costs of investigation, and reasonable attorney fees under Minn. Stat. § 325F.70 (private-action subdivision) and, in the alternative, under Minn. Stat. § 8.31, Subd. 3a (Private Attorney General). The action benefits the public as a matter of statute (§ 325F.70, private-action subdivision) and, in the alternative, as a matter of fact, because Defendant's claim-handling rules — including the documented 'engineer's report only' evidentiary gate — affect numerous Minnesota homeowners insured by Defendant under similar policies."

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **20 / 100** as a standalone count; **30 / 100** as a fee-shifting overlay on A1**

The rating reflects C3's strength as a filed count in a Hennepin County state-court complaint. The factual underpinning — the same factual record that supports B2 — is meaningful. The legal hurdles are larger than B2's: the "in connection with the sale" prerequisite is the most active doctrinal fight, and even though *Wiegand* and *Group Health Plan* meaningfully ease the causation requirement, an individual-plaintiff MCFA case in a first-party insurance setting is not a well-trodden path in Minnesota appellate authority.

C3's principal value is **fee-shifting**, not merits-of-recovery. If C3 survives a Rule 12 motion and reaches summary judgment, the family (or any attorney they later retain) has a statutory hook for attorney fees that A1 alone does not provide. C3's secondary value is **appraisal-bar insulation**: the § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) bar does not apply to an MCFA count. C3 is therefore the third-best appraisal-independent misconduct count after B2 (common-law fraud) and B1 (§ 604.18 to the extent the bar can be overcome).

- **Prohibited-act element (§ 325F.69 Subd. 1):** 75/100. The Prieve "no hail after 2020" representation, adopted by Farmers, is a falsifiable factual claim disproven on the record — a paradigmatic "misrepresentation" or "misleading statement." The 9/3/2024 "no other damages observed" representation similarly qualifies. The "engineer's report only" rule is more arguable as a "deceptive practice" but not as headline strong.
- **"Intent that others rely":** 60/100. Statements made in formal denial letters and by supervisors and adjusters in claim-handling communications are made with the intent that the recipient rely — that is the function of the communications.
- **"In connection with the sale of any merchandise":** 30/100. The dominant legal hurdle. *Force* supports the broader reading; *Morris*'s claims-handling-vs-sales line cuts the other way; the answer in a first-party hail-claim setting is genuinely open.
- **Causal nexus:** 55/100. Easier than common-law-fraud reliance under *Wiegand* / *Group Health Plan*, but still requires connecting the specific conduct to specific damages. Strongest on the appraisal-channel costs and the consequential water damage; weaker on the policy-proceeds damages where A1 already reaches the same dollars.
- **Public benefit (§ 8.31 Subd. 3a route):** 35/100. *Ly v. Nystrom* is a meaningful obstacle. The family's case is largely a one-on-one transaction. The "engineer's report only" rule has plausible system-wide implications, but no record evidence yet shows Farmers applied it to other consumers; that would require third-party discovery.
- **Public benefit (§ 325F.70 private-action route):** 70/100. The statutory declaration that "An action brought under this section benefits the public" answers *Ly* on the personal/family/household-purpose path. The largest residual question is whether courts will read the 2023 amendment as cleanly displacing *Ly* in cases where the underlying conduct is a one-on-one consumer interaction, or whether they will graft a residual public-benefit analysis on top. Untested.
- **Procedural fit as initial claim:** 80/100. Pleadable in the initial complaint; not subject to § 604.18's procedural barriers; not subject to the appraisal bar.
- **Remedy upside (fee-shifting):** 75/100. The structural advantage of attorney-fee shifting under either route is the principal practical reason to carry C3.

Weighted blend, with the "in connection with the sale" hurdle weighted heavily because it is the gating doctrinal question: **~20/100 standalone**; **~30/100 if A1 is the lead theory and C3 functions as a fee-shifting overlay** that opens up attorney-fee exposure on facts the court has otherwise found compelling under A1.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

- **The "in connection with the sale" hurdle.** This is the single largest C3-specific weakness. *Force v. ITT Hartford* read insurance into "merchandise," but the conduct in *Force* was alleged sales misrepresentations made at the point of policy purchase ("vanishing premium," "churning," etc.). The Lu/Hu family's MCFA case rests on representations made in the claims-handling process years after the sale. A court applying a strict textual reading will distinguish *Force*. *Morris v. Am. Family*'s claims-handling/sales-conduct line is doctrinal company for that distinction. Most plaintiffs who succeed under the MCFA succeed on point-of-sale misrepresentations; this case must build out a step beyond that doctrine.
- **The MCFA is rarely deployed in first-party hail-claim litigation.** A targeted scan of post-*Sela* (2021) Minnesota decisions does not surface a published affirmance of an individual-plaintiff MCFA recovery against a homeowner's insurer for claim-handling conduct. The thin precedential footprint cuts both ways: Farmers cannot point to a wall of dispositive authority foreclosing C3, but the family equally cannot point to a roadmap of successful predecessors.
- **Damages duplication with A1.** The family's headline damages — the unpaid hail damage to the windows — are recoverable under A1 (breach of contract). Carrying the same dollars under C3 risks double-counting and may attract a motion to dismiss on that ground. The cleaner C3 damages are the appraisal-channel costs ($1,100 share of Foster's retainer), out-of-pocket investigation costs, costs of administrative complaint preparation, and the consequential water damage accruing during induced delay. These are not large in absolute dollars.
- **Public benefit under § 8.31 (Route 1) is doctrinally exposed.** *Ly v. Nystrom* is binding authority that single-transaction misrepresentations do not satisfy public benefit. The record does not yet contain evidence that Farmers applied the "engineer's report only" rule to multiple consumers. Without that record, Route 1's public-benefit showing depends on a court inferring system-level conduct from a single instance. That is doable but is a real summary-judgment risk.
- **The 2023 amendment to § 325F.70 (Route 2) is untested in insurance settings.** No published Minnesota appellate decision has applied the new private-action subdivision to a first-party insurance dispute. The interpretive questions — what "in connection with a sale of merchandise" means when the merchandise is insurance, how much weight to give the statutory public-benefit declaration when the conduct is one-on-one — are all live. The early-mover advantage is real but so is the early-mover risk of an unfavorable interpretive ruling.
- **Specific intent to induce reliance is not free.** While the MCFA's "intent that others rely thereon" element does not require knowledge of falsity (unlike fraud), it does require subjective intent. Farmers will argue (a) Prieve's report was prepared by a third party and Farmers passed it along without independent intent, and (b) the "engineer's report only" rule was a procedural processing decision rather than a representation intended to induce reliance. Both arguments are answerable but they have to be argued.
- **The B1, B2, and C3 misconduct counts have substantial factual overlap.** A court reading the complaint will see the same Prieve "no hail after 2020" misrepresentation feeding three different theories. That triple-coding is not improper but it can invite a "C3 is just B2 by another name" argument. The cleanest defense is the textual differences: § 325F.69 reaches "deceptive practice" beyond fraud, fee-shifting beyond A1, and individual-reliance-free causation beyond B2. The complaint should make those textual differences explicit.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up

- **The same factual record that supports B2 supports C3.** A genuinely disprovable, factually false statement exists in the record (Prieve's "no hail after 2020"). Farmers adopted and transmitted it in a formal denial letter on its own letterhead. The same record exists for the 9/3/2024 "no other damages observed" representation. The sufficient-misrepresentation-for-pleading-purposes question is well-anchored.
- **No requirement to prove individual justifiable reliance.** *Wiegand* and *Group Health Plan* eliminate the central B2 weakness (the family's prompt rejection of the false statements). Under the MCFA, the family's having immediately disbelieved Prieve's "no hail after 2020" claim is doctrinally irrelevant on causation — what matters is whether Farmers' prohibited conduct caused the family's damages, by whatever mechanism. The family's appraisal-channel costs, the litigation delay, and the ongoing water damage are all causally linked to Farmers' conduct without depending on the family's having believed the false weather claim.
- **No § 604.18 appraisal bar.** Subd. 4(b) is specific to § 604.18. The MCFA is unaffected. If B1 is barred (or if the bar's scope cannot be overcome through the A1/A3 scope-attack), C3 (with B2) becomes the principal vehicle for misconduct-based recovery.
- **Fee-shifting is the killer feature.** Either Route 1 or Route 2 supplies attorney fees and costs of investigation. The statute has no $100,000 cap (in contrast to § 604.18). For a family that may end up retaining counsel mid-litigation — or who proceeds pro se until counsel can be brought in on a fee-shifting basis — C3 is the most economically meaningful fee-shifting vehicle in this case.
- **The 2023 amendment provides a clean answer to the public-benefit question for the family's specific factual posture.** Homeowner's insurance for an owner-occupied primary residence is by any reasonable construction "merchandise for personal, family, [or] household … purposes." The amendment's express public-benefit declaration on its face removes the *Ly v. Nystrom* hurdle. Even if a court applies a residual gloss, the statutory text is the strongest single argument the family can put on the public-benefit issue.
- **The "deceptive practice" prong is broader than fraud.** § 325F.69's "deceptive practice" category does not require a specific false statement. It reaches courses of conduct that are misleading in their tendency. The "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule — applied without disclosure to insureds and used to reject contrary evidence — is a strong candidate for "deceptive practice" even where it is not strong as "misrepresentation."
- **The factual record is unusually complete.** The project's existing fact memos (`CaseContext/key-facts-excluding-legal-context.md`, `ClaimTimeline.md`) supply primary-source citations to every candidate prohibited act. The MCFA pleading does not require fact development beyond the existing record for purposes of surviving a motion to dismiss.
- **The post-amendment, untested-in-insurance status creates uncertainty Farmers also has to manage.** Farmers' general counsel will know that an adverse appellate ruling on the 2023 amendment's reach in an insurance context could open the door to broader MCFA exposure. That asymmetry is a settlement-leverage factor independent of C3's standalone merits.

### 2.4 What "20/100" means practically

C3 should be pleaded if the family is filing the case at all, because (a) the marginal cost of adding the count to the complaint is low, (b) the fee-shifting feature is meaningful and unavailable elsewhere, (c) the count is not subject to the § 604.18 procedural barriers, and (d) the count provides a path to misconduct-based recovery if the appraisal bar disposes of B1 and the reliance element kills B2.

C3 is **not the lead theory**. It is supportive of A1 (which is the lead theory) and runs alongside B2 (which is the lead misconduct theory not constrained by § 604.18's bar). The family should not expect to win the case on C3 alone. They should expect C3 to (i) survive Rule 12, (ii) be tested on summary judgment principally on the "in connection with the sale" hurdle, and (iii) carry meaningful settlement weight as a fee-shifting overlay if it survives summary judgment.

### 2.5 Note on timing and the 7/13/2026 deadline

C3's 6-year limitations period (whether under § 541.05, Subd. 1(2) or Subd. 1(6)) runs from a later date than the policy's 2-year contractual suit limitation. The 7/13/2026 contractual deadline is the binding filing constraint — but only because the underlying breach-of-contract count must be filed by that date. C3 is not foreclosed by the 7/13/2026 deadline if it is filed contemporaneously with the breach-of-contract count.

### 3.1 Element 1 — Prohibited act under § 325F.69, Subd. 1

The same three candidate misrepresentations identified for the B2 count carry into C3, with one structural advantage: the MCFA's "deceptive practice" and "misleading statement" categories reach conduct beyond a single false statement of past or existing fact, so the C3 count can frame the broader course of conduct as a "deceptive practice" rather than relying on any single statement.

**Candidate 1 — Prieve's "no hail after 2020" representation, adopted by Farmers in the 11/4/2024 denial.**

- Prieve Engineering's October 2024 report, transmitted as pages 4–10 of the 11/4/2024 denial letter, states in the "Weather Records" section that "the most recent hail in Eden Prairie reported in 2020." Prieve's Conclusion 1 rests on this premise. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 9–10)*
- The 11/4/2024 denial letter, on Farmers letterhead, expressly adopts and transmits Prieve's report as the basis for the denial. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 1–3)*
- The statement is disproven by:

**Course-of-conduct framing — "deceptive practice."**

The MCFA's "deceptive practice" category reaches a course of conduct that has the tendency to mislead, even where any single act is not actionable as a discrete misrepresentation. The pattern across the three candidates above — paired with (i) the unsigned umpire's award lacking item-by-item itemization required by § 65A.01 Subd. 3, (ii) Farmers' inability to consistently describe the awarded windows across 9/18, 9/19, and 10/14/2025 communications, and (iii) Farmers' refusal on 10/21/2025 to engage on why the awarded scope (2 windows) is so much narrower than the photographic record of damage (nearly every window) — supplies a "deceptive practice" theory that does not depend on any single false statement carrying the count. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png; EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/18, 9/19, 10/14, 10/21/2025 entries))*

### 3.2 Element 2 — Intent that others rely

- **Farmers issued the 11/4/2024 denial letter to the family** with the natural and intended consequence that the family would accept the denial and stop pursuing the claim. A formal denial letter is, by its nature, a communication intended to induce the recipient's reliance on the stated grounds. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf)*
- **Farmers issued the 9/3/2024 denial letter** with the same purpose. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf)*
- **Supervisor Monson and adjuster Brau communicated the "engineer's report only" rule** directly to the family in February 2025 with the intent that the family stop submitting non-engineer evidence and accept the denial as final. *(EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG)*
- **The 7/11/2025 generic boilerplate response** to the family's deadline question — which neither identified any specific deadline nor responded to the extension request — is consistent with an intent to induce reliance on Farmers' continued claim-handling-process posture rather than prompt litigation. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (7/10–7/11/2025 entries))*

The MCFA does not require proof of intent that *this* plaintiff rely; it requires intent that "others" rely. Farmers' general intent that consumers rely on its formal denials, coverage representations, and procedural rules is sufficient.

### 3.3 Element 3 — In connection with the sale of any merchandise

This is the doctrinal weight-bearing element. The factual record relevant to it is:

- **The original sale of insurance occurred on 10/22/2014.** Yibiao Lu signed the application and subscription agreement; Farmers issued Policy 98055-49-65 with Coverage A and a 2% Windstorm/Hail deductible. *(HomePolicy/2014_Application-PolicyDocument-980554965.pdf; HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf)*
- **The policy renewed annually**, including the renewal effective 10/22/2023–10/22/2024 — the policy in force on the loss date. *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf)*
- **The 2023 renewal identified the property as "Owner Occupied (Primary Res.)"** and applied a "New Roof" discount and Coverage A of $886,000. *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf)*
- **Farmers continued to collect premium throughout the claim-handling period**, including after the 9/3/2024 first denial and the 11/4/2024 second denial. The premium collected was for windstorm/hail coverage, which is the coverage Farmers was simultaneously refusing to honor. *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf; family premium records — confirm in discovery.)*
- **The denial letters and the engineer's report describe the scope and effective requirements of the policy product** — what is and is not covered, what evidentiary standards Farmers will apply, what the policy's exclusions reach. These representations are about the contract product the family bought and continues to pay for.
- **Each candidate misrepresentation was made to a consumer in the consumer's capacity as the holder of an active insurance policy**, not in any other context.

The "in connection with" inquiry is fact-dependent and the doctrine is open. The family's strongest framing is the *Force* line: a continuing insurance relationship and a recent renewal supply the "in connection with" connection, and the conduct alleged describes the very product the family purchased.

### 3.4 Element 4 — Causal nexus to plaintiff's damages

The causal-nexus standard under *Wiegand* and *Group Health Plan* allows the family to establish causation through circumstantial evidence other than individual reliance. The available causation theories on this record:

- **Causation Theory A — Appraisal-channel cost causation.** Farmers' "engineer's report only" rule and ongoing refusals to engage with the family's evidence caused the family to pursue the contractual appraisal channel as the only remaining avenue short of litigation. The family paid $1,100 (its share of the $2,200 retainer for John Foster) and incurred associated time and travel. Had Farmers fairly engaged with the 1/31/2025, 2/10/2025, and 5/28/2025 evidence as the policy and § 72A.201 require, the appraisal channel and its costs would not have been necessary. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf p. 2)*
- **Causation Theory B — Litigation-delay consequential damages.** Farmers' shifting denial rationales, the imposition of the "engineer's report only" rule, and the unanswered evidence submissions caused the family to delay the filing of suit while pursuing administrative and appraisal channels. During that delay, water intrusion through the damaged windows continued and worsened, with active leaks documented on 10/28/2025. The incremental water damage accruing during the induced delay is causally tied to Farmers' deceptive practices. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/28/2025 entry))*
- **Causation Theory C — Investigation costs and out-of-pocket expenses.** The family's 2/4–2/7/2025 neighbor canvass (40+ doors), 2/3/2025 City permit-records request, and 6/6–6/18/2025 outreach to MN DNR, FOX9, MN Bar Association, MN Lawyer Referral Service ($30 fee), the Hennepin Government Center Self Help Center, and the Attorney General's Office were all caused by Farmers' refusal to fairly engage with the claim. These are textbook "costs of investigation" recoverable under § 8.31 Subd. 3a / § 325F.70. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md)*
- **Causation Theory D — Unpaid policy proceeds.** To the extent the family has not been paid for covered hail damage to the windows, that loss is causally tied to Farmers' deceptive practices that produced the denials. This theory overlaps with A1 and should be pleaded in the alternative to avoid double-counting.

Under *Wiegand* and *Group Health Plan*, none of these theories requires the family to prove that they personally relied on the false statements. The family's prompt rejection of Prieve's "no hail after 2020" claim does not negate causation; what matters is whether Farmers' conduct caused the family's damages, and on each theory above, it did.

### 3.5 Element 5(a) — Public benefit (§ 8.31 Subd. 3a / Route 1)

Available public-benefit support on this record:

- **The "engineer's report only" rule was articulated at the supervisor level**, not by an individual adjuster. Bradlee Monson is a "field claim supervisor" — a supervisory role with claims-handling authority. The rule was confirmed in writing by adjuster Brau, who explicitly attributed it to Monson. A rule articulated and enforced at the supervisor level has institutional reach beyond a single insured. *(EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG; CaseContext/key-facts.md §5)*
- **The Prieve report's "weather records" methodology is the kind of methodology a forensic engineer applies across multiple claims**, not a methodology developed only for the Lu/Hu file. To the extent Farmers retains Prieve Engineering or similar firms across its Minnesota hail claims, the same factual-foundation flaw (incomplete consultation of NWS/stormersite data) is likely to repeat across other insured properties.
- **Farmers' shifting denial rationales** — the 9/3/2024 "no other damages" theory, the 11/4/2024 "wear/tear / 2020 already paid" theory, and the post-appraisal "below-deductible" theory — match the patterns documented in published litigation against insurers (the *Burgess v. Farmers* $130M Texas verdict, the State Farm "Hail Focus Initiative" pattern litigation). Pattern litigation against the same insurer or sister carriers supports the inference that the conduct alleged here is part of a broader pattern. *(LegalReferences/CaseLawReference.md.)*

The Route 1 public-benefit showing is non-trivial but is not airtight. The strongest path is Route 2 (§ 325F.70), which provides a statutory public-benefit declaration on the personal/family/household-purpose path.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No. C3 is structurally independent of the appraisal award.** Like B2, C3 rests on representations and deceptive practices made during the claims-handling process in 2024–2025, all of which predate or are independent of the 9/12/2025 award. Nothing about proving or disproving the MCFA elements requires the court to alter the appraisal award.

In fact, the 9/12/2025 award **affirmatively supports** the falsity element of C3's leading misrepresentation theory: the panel's hail-causation finding is direct evidence that Prieve's "no hail after 2020" representation, adopted by Farmers in the 11/4/2024 denial, was false.

### 4.2 Why C3 is structurally independent

Two doctrinal points anchor the independence:

- **Scope of appraisal under *Quade*.** *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), holds that appraisers determine "amount of loss," which includes causation. Appraisers do not adjudicate statutory consumer-protection claims, do not determine whether a deceptive practice occurred, and do not allocate statutory attorney fees. The award's binding effect runs only to the contractual amount-of-loss component.
- **Issue preclusion does not reach unaddressed elements.** *Hauschildt v. Beckingham*, 686 N.W.2d 829, 837 (Minn. 2004), requires the issue to have been actually and necessarily decided in the prior proceeding for collateral estoppel to apply. The appraisal panel did not address whether Farmers engaged in a "deceptive practice" or made a "misleading statement" — those questions are not "amount of loss" questions and the panel had no jurisdiction over them.

The MCFA claim therefore proceeds regardless of whether the award is upheld, modified, vacated, or left untouched.

### 4.3 The § 604.18 appraisal bar does not apply to C3

Minn. Stat. § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) provides that "[a]n award of taxable costs under this section is not available in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal." That bar is specific to § 604.18 statutory bad-faith remedies. It does not reach the MCFA. C3 is unaffected by Subd. 4(b) regardless of how the court ultimately reads "resolved or confirmed" for B1 purposes.

This is the principal structural advantage of C3 over B1. If the appraisal bar disposes of B1, C3 (along with B2) becomes a primary vehicle for misconduct-based recovery and for fee-shifting.

### 4.4 How the appraisal interacts with C3 without barring it

Three interactions shape pleading strategy:

**Interaction 1 — Appraisal award as affirmative evidence of the prohibited act.** The 9/12/2025 award's hail-causation finding is direct evidence that Prieve's "no hail after 2020" representation was false, supporting the § 325F.69 "misrepresentation" / "misleading statement" / "deceptive practice" element. C3 is stronger if the award stands than if it is vacated; vacatur would weaken C3 (along with B2) by removing one of the cleanest independent proofs of falsity. This interaction biases C3 toward award-preservation strategy where the appraisal challenge is uncertain.

**Interaction 2 — Award as a damages metric.** The award's $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV figures supply a baseline against which C3 damages can be measured. The C3 damages must be quantified without double-counting policy proceeds A1 reaches, but the award's existence does not preclude C3's path to investigation costs, appraisal-channel costs, consequential water damage during induced delay, and statutory attorney fees.

**Interaction 3 — Award scope and the "deceptive practice" course-of-conduct framing.** The award's deficiencies — no item-by-item itemization, no umpire report, mutually inconsistent post-award descriptions of which windows are covered (9/18, 9/19, 10/14/2025), and Farmers' refusal to engage on the scope gap (10/21/2025) — are themselves part of the broader "deceptive practice" course of conduct that supports C3. The award is not just evidence of the falsity of an earlier statement; the award's handling is itself part of the prohibited conduct.

### 4.5 Pleading framing implications

Because C3 does not depend on disturbing the award, the filing strategy should:

1. **Plead C3 without incorporating the appraisal vacatur theory** (A3 in the argument list). C3 stands on its own factual predicates.
2. **Use the appraisal award affirmatively as evidence of falsity** in the C3 count.
3. **Plead damages separate from contract damages** to maintain C3's independent economic footprint and avoid a duplicative-claim motion. The appraisal-channel costs ($1,100), investigation costs, consequential water damage from delay, and statutory attorney fees and costs of investigation under § 8.31 Subd. 3a / § 325F.70 are the cleanest C3-specific damages categories.
4. **Plead both Route 1 (§ 8.31 Subd. 3a) and Route 2 (§ 325F.70).** Route 2 provides the cleaner public-benefit path; Route 1 provides a backup if Route 2's 2023 amendment is given an unfavorable interpretation in the insurance setting.
5. **Anchor the "in connection with the sale" element in the *Force* continuing-relationship reading and the recent 10/22/2023 renewal.** That reading is the single most important interpretive choice in the C3 pleading.

### 4.6 Bottom line on the key question

- C3 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be filed, to survive Rule 12, or to reach the merits.
- C3 is **strengthened, not weakened**, when the award stands — because the award's hail-causation finding is direct evidence that Farmers' adopted-in-denial representations were false.
- C3 is **not barred by the § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar.** That insulation is one of the principal structural reasons to carry C3.
- C3 should therefore be pleaded as a freestanding count in the initial complaint, anchored in the same three candidate misrepresentations as B2 plus the "deceptive practice" course-of-conduct framing, with both Route 1 (§ 8.31) and Route 2 (§ 325F.70) invoked, and with damages framed to preserve C3's independent economic footprint.
