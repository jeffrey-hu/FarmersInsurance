## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 What the argument is, in plain terms

The argument is that Farmers, knowing or having reason to know it would be sued over the 7/13/2024 hail loss, failed to create or preserve internal claim-investigation documents the family is now entitled to see — and that the court should respond by (a) sanctioning Farmers under Minn. R. Civ. P. 37, (b) instructing the jury that it may infer the missing materials would have been adverse to Farmers' position, and/or (c) preclusively limiting the use Farmers may make of the missing evidence at trial.

The key documents at issue, on the existing record, are: (i) any written record of Nic Brau's 9/6/2024 personal inspection at which he found dents and measured windows; (ii) any narrative analytical report — as opposed to the photo set — from the 8/29/2024 Hancock inspection; (iii) the umpire's report or estimate, which Farmers admits does not exist; (iv) the 2020 claim's "which 2 sash" line-item descriptions and prior on-site documentation, which Farmers has refused to produce despite repeated requests; (v) the family's own damage documentation handed to Prieve on 10/9/2024 and never returned; and (vi) the internal Farmers claim-file notes between 8/29/2024 and 9/3/2024 that drove the first denial before any engineer was engaged.

### 1.2 D3 is not a freestanding cause of action in Minnesota

Minnesota law is clear that spoliation is **not** an independent tort. *See, e.g., Federated Mut. Ins. Co. v. Litchfield Precision Components, Inc.*, 456 N.W.2d 434 (Minn. 1990) (treating destruction of evidence as a basis for sanctions, not a separate claim); the doctrine is consistently described in Minnesota cases as a remedy or evidentiary device, not a count. The drafter of `Possible_Arguments_List.md` correctly treats D3 as a remedy/evidentiary doctrine and explicitly lists "independent tort of spoliation" under Section F as deliberately excluded.

This means D3 does not appear in the complaint as "Count VII — Spoliation." It appears in the complaint as: (a) a factual allegation tied to A1 (breach), (b) a basis for a discovery sanctions motion under Minn. R. Civ. P. 37 once Farmers refuses or fails to produce the items in formal discovery, (c) an adverse-inference jury instruction request developed at the pretrial conference, and (d) factual material supporting the eventual § 604.18 bad-faith motion (B1).

### 1.3 Minnesota's core doctrinal sources for spoliation

**Trigger of the duty to preserve.** A party has a duty to preserve evidence when it "knew or should have known that the evidence should be preserved for pending or future litigation." *Patton v. Newmar Corp.*, 538 N.W.2d 116, 118 (Minn. 1995). The standard is objective; "the intent of the spoliator is irrelevant" — even negligent failure to preserve can support sanctions. *Id.*; *see also Hoffman v. Ford Motor Co.*, 587 N.W.2d 66 (Minn. Ct. App. 1998).

**"Reasonably foreseeable" formulation.** The Minnesota Supreme Court has framed the trigger in terms of "reasonably foreseeable" litigation. *Miller v. Lankow*, 801 N.W.2d 120, 127–28 (Minn. 2011). This is "a flexible, fact-specific standard" allowing the trial court discretion based on the totality of circumstances at the time the evidence was created or destroyed.

**Available sanctions.** Minnesota courts have a range of sanctions available, with the trial court bearing "a duty to impose the least restrictive sanction available under the circumstances." *Patton*, 538 N.W.2d at 119. The catalog includes:

- **Adverse-inference jury instruction.** The jury may be told it may infer the missing evidence would have been unfavorable to the spoliator. *See Federated Mut. Ins. Co.*, 456 N.W.2d at 437.
- **Witness/exhibit preclusion.** A party can be barred from offering testimony or evidence whose foundational materials it failed to preserve. *Patton*, 538 N.W.2d at 118 (preclusion of plaintiffs' expert testimony where plaintiffs failed to preserve the motor home).
- **Issue preclusion or fact deemed established.** The trial court may treat a contested fact as established against the spoliator for purposes of the case.
- **Cost shifting and fee shifting** under Minn. R. Civ. P. 37.
- **In extreme cases, dismissal or default** — but only where the conduct is "egregious" and lesser sanctions cannot cure the prejudice. *Patton*, 538 N.W.2d at 119.

**Procedural lever — Minn. R. Civ. P. 37.** Independent of the common-law spoliation framework, Rule 37 provides discovery sanctions where a party fails to comply with a discovery order or fails to produce in response to a Rule 34 request. Rule 37 is the cleanest procedural vehicle for putting the missing-documents issue before the court because it does not require the family to first prove a duty-to-preserve trigger date — it kicks in as soon as Farmers fails to produce in litigation.

### 1.4 Two distinct theories within D3

D3 actually houses two adjacent but distinct doctrines, and the family should plead/argue both:

- **Spoliation proper** — Farmers had a duty to preserve evidence at the time the evidence existed; failed to do so (whether by destroying, failing to create in the first place where industry/regulatory practice required, or losing); and the absence of the evidence prejudices the family. Sanction: adverse-inference instruction + preclusion + cost shifting.
- **Discovery non-production under Rule 37** — Farmers does not produce in formal discovery what it possesses or controls. Sanction: a Rule 37 motion targeted at the specific non-production. The spoliation framework is not strictly necessary to win Rule 37 relief.

The two are different in their proof burdens but converge in what they get the family — the right to argue at trial that the missing materials would be bad for Farmers if produced.

### 1.5 The regulatory backdrop relevant to "should have preserved"

Minnesota's claims-handling regulations require insurers to maintain a complete claim file. *See* Minn. Stat. § 72A.201, Subds. 4–6 (claims-investigation, evaluation, and documentation obligations); Minn. R. ch. 2790 (unfair-claims-practices regulations and claims-file maintenance standards). Farmers, as a regulated property insurer in Minnesota, was on independent regulatory notice that its claim file must contain a record of inspections, evaluations, and decisions made. This has two implications for D3:

- **The duty-to-preserve trigger arrived early.** A regulated insurer has a built-in expectation that its claim file will be reviewed in litigation if the claim is contested. By the time of the 9/3/2024 first denial — which itself advised the insured of the right to file a regulatory complaint — Farmers should reasonably have anticipated dispute. By the 11/19/2024 Commerce complaint, certainly. By the 8/3/2025 appraisal demand and the family's 7/10/2025 inquiry about lawsuit deadlines, beyond doubt.
- **"No report exists" is itself an admission of regulatory non-compliance.** Farmers' 10/10/2025 statement — "the file does not contain a report on the requested date" — is, taken at face value, an admission that the adjuster of record went onsite, observed and measured damage, and produced no internal documentation. That is in tension with § 72A.201 Subd. 4's requirement that the insurer "complete its investigation" reasonably. Whether the document was destroyed or never created, it should have been there.

### 1.6 What D3 looks like in a complaint and in pretrial motions

D3 will surface in three places:

- **Inside the A1 (Breach) factual allegations**, as paragraphs describing each missing document, the family's repeated written requests, Farmers' responses, and the resulting prejudice. These paragraphs serve as the factual predicate for both spoliation and Rule 37 motions later in the case, and feed the bad-faith narrative for B1.
- **As a Rule 37 motion** (or motions, plural), filed after Farmers' responses to discovery requests show non-production. Rule 37 motions are filed in the litigation, not pleaded in the complaint.
- **As a request for an adverse-inference jury instruction** at the pretrial conference and during jury-instruction conferences. The instruction would tell the jury, in substance, that "if you find that Defendant had control over [the inspection notes / the umpire materials / the prior-claim records] and that Defendant knew or should have known that Plaintiffs would seek those materials in this dispute, you may — but are not required to — infer that the contents of those materials would have been unfavorable to Defendant."

### 1.7 Sketch of the factual paragraph in the complaint

> "On September 6, 2024, three days after Defendant's first written denial finding 'no other damages observed,' Defendant's own Claims Specialist Representative — Property, Nicholas Brau, conducted a personal onsite inspection of the property. During that inspection Mr. Brau identified dents on multiple windows in the presence of Plaintiffs and measured window dimensions. That paragraph would be reproduced, with appropriate factual substitution, for the Hancock inspection, the umpire's missing report and estimate, the 2020 claim line-item descriptions, the family's documentation handed to Prieve, and the internal pre-9/3/2024 claim notes.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **15 / 100** as a standalone count; **55 / 100** as a sanctions/adverse-inference tool inside A1 and B1; **45 / 100** as supporting material for A3 (vacate/modify award).

The split rating reflects that D3 is structurally a remedy and evidentiary device, not a freestanding claim, and so its strength depends on how it is deployed.

- **D3 as a standalone count: ~15/100.** Minnesota does not recognize an independent spoliation tort. Pleading "Count VII — Spoliation" invites a Rule 12 dismissal; the relief D3 actually provides is not damages but procedural and evidentiary remedies that arrive later in the case. The 15/100 floor is non-zero only because some plaintiffs do plead spoliation as a "claim" to lock in the factual record; nothing affirmative is gained.
- **D3 as a sanctions/adverse-inference tool inside A1 and B1: ~55/100.** This is where D3 earns its keep. The factual record contains an unusually clean set of missing-documents facts — the adjuster's own onsite observations are not memorialized; the umpire produced no report by the insurer's own admission; Farmers refused to produce the 2020 line items despite repeated requests. *Patton* and *Federated Mutual* support sanctions on materially less. The 55/100 reflects the genuine likelihood of getting at least one significant sanction (adverse-inference instruction is the most realistic) and the cumulative impact of multiple missing items on the trial narrative.
- **D3 as supporting material for A3: ~45/100.** The umpire's missing report and estimate is materially relevant to A3's argument that the 9/12/2025 award fails to "appraise the loss, stating separately actual value and loss to each item" under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01, Subd. 3. A court asked to remand under *Herll*/*Rymer* or modify under § 65A.01 is more receptive when the absence of an itemized basis is paired with the absence of any methodological record from the umpire.

Weighted blend for the question the user asked — "strength as an initial legal claim to be drafted in MN state court," with the most realistic procedural posture (D3 inside A1, B1, and A3 with Rule 37 motions to follow): **~50–55/100**, with the upper end achievable only if discovery development confirms (i) that Brau's notes existed and were destroyed (versus never created), and (ii) that Farmers controls but withholds 2020 inspection documentation rather than not having it.

### 2.2 What drives the rating up

- **The 10/10/2025 "no report exists" statement and the 10/29/2025 "already released everything" statement are mutually inconsistent.** They came from Farmers and from Brau personally, separated by 19 days. Either there is a file (then "released everything" is admissible against Farmers as an admission that Brau retained custody) or there is no file (then "released everything" is false, and Brau's onsite work generated nothing). Both readings hurt Farmers in front of a jury. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (10/10, 10/28, 10/29/2025 entries))*
- **Brau personally found dents and measured windows.** A reasonable adjuster generates a record. *(Sources: `CaseContext/key-facts.md` §1; `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md`)* The combination of "personal observation of damage" + "no produced record" is the textbook fact pattern in which an adverse-inference instruction is most likely to be granted.
- **The umpire's complete absence of any report or estimate is admitted by Farmers in writing.** "Unfortunately, the umpire did not provide a report or estimate. Our only recommendation is to refer you to your appraiser for any additional details he may have in his file." *(Source: `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (9/24/2025 entry))*. A § 65A.01 award is supposed to rest on an itemized determination of "loss to each item" — the absence of any umpire methodology is precisely the kind of missing materials a court will treat as either sanction-worthy or as evidence the award itself is invalid.
- **Farmers' 10/1/2025 production of a *previous owner's* 2014 claim document instead of the 2020 claim records the family requested** is a documented refusal to produce responsive materials. *(Source: `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (10/1/2025 entry))*. That is an unforced error by Farmers and lays the groundwork for a clean Rule 37 motion in litigation.
- **Farmers' "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule** is itself relevant to spoliation/sanctions analysis because it is an articulated reason the insurer would have suppressed lay evidence the family submitted (the neighbor canvass, City permit data, weather records). The rule was stated by supervisor Bradlee Monson on 2/3/2025 and corroborated in writing by Brau's 2/10/2025 voicemail. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG`; family's contemporaneous account of 2/3/2025 call)*.
- **The duty-to-preserve trigger date is easy to argue.** Farmers denied on 9/3/2024 in a letter that itself advised the insured of the right to file a regulatory complaint. From that date forward, dispute was reasonably foreseeable. By 11/19/2024 (Commerce complaint), 7/10/2025 (insured's deadline inquiry), 8/3/2025 (appraisal demand), and 9/12/2025 (appraisal proceeding), litigation was virtually certain. Any missing-documents fact arising during this window has a strong "should have known" pedigree.
- **Minnesota law explicitly does not require bad intent.** *Patton* holds that the spoliator's intent is irrelevant; even negligent failure to preserve can support sanctions. That makes D3 a "low-bar, low-ceiling" argument: the family does not have to prove Farmers maliciously destroyed anything; it has to prove Farmers should have preserved and didn't.
- **The "least restrictive sanction" framework gives the court easy off-ramps.** A trial judge who is uncomfortable granting case-dispositive sanctions can still issue an adverse-inference instruction, exclude Tomes's testimony from any post-award proceeding, or shift fees on the discovery motion. The family does not have to win big to win usefully.

### 2.3 What drives the rating down

- **D3 is not a damages claim.** Even fully successful, D3 generates evidentiary advantages and procedural remedies — not a dollar award. Its strength is therefore best understood as a multiplier on A1 and B1 rather than as an independent recovery.
- **"No report ever existed" is harder than "report destroyed."** If Farmers' truthful position is that Brau simply never wrote anything down, the family must argue spoliation by *omission* — i.e., that Farmers should have created and preserved a record because regulatory and industry practice required one. That is a colorable argument under § 72A.201 and Minn. R. ch. 2790, but it is harder than demonstrating a destroyed document. The leading Minnesota cases (*Patton*, *Federated Mutual*, *Hoffman*) all involve destruction or loss of pre-existing physical evidence, not failure to create.
- **The Hancock photo set was produced.** The family received the Hancock photos appended to the 9/3/2024 denial letter. That weakens the "no Hancock report" subargument — Hancock's view that "no damage exists" is documented by the photo set, even if no narrative analytical report is attached. The argument therefore narrows to "any narrative report from Hancock," which may not have existed.
- **The family's 9/12/2025 appraisal demand and signed retainer** introduce the family's own appraiser into the missing-documents picture. Foster, the insured's appraiser, also has no produced report or estimate; Farmers' 9/24/2025 reply directs the family back to Foster for "additional details." If Foster also has nothing in writing, that bilateralizes the missing-documents problem in a way that makes it harder to single out Farmers for spoliation sanctions. Better framed: the missing-umpire-report fact supports A3 (the award is statutorily defective), with D3 reaching only the materials Farmers itself controls.
- **The internal pre-9/3/2024 Farmers claim notes are pure hypothesis until discovery.** The family does not yet know whether Farmers' internal file contains adjuster handwritten notes, telephone-call summaries, supervisor approvals, or other pre-denial materials. The strongest spoliation arguments arise after discovery confirms what *should* be there is missing.
- **The family's own damage documentation handed to Prieve on 10/9/2024 (Missing Documentation entry #8 in `ClaimTimeline.md`) is family-side.** Its absence from the project folder — distinct from Farmers' file — is a project-organization issue more than a spoliation-by-Farmers issue. Worth raising in discovery (Farmers' Prieve materials should include what the family handed to Prieve), but not the strongest fact.
- **No reservation-of-rights letter is in the project folder.** A reservation-of-rights letter typically operates as a litigation-hold trigger inside the insurer; the absence of one is consistent with the more troubling "no internal preservation effort was made" reading, but the family does not currently have a document putting Farmers on formal notice of preservation duties beyond the regulatory baseline.
- **Adverse-inference instructions are discretionary.** The family must persuade the trial judge that the prejudice is sufficient and the inference is logically supported. *Patton*'s "least restrictive sanction" instruction means a judge may instead deny the instruction and grant only fee-shifting on the underlying discovery motion.

### 2.4 What "55/100" means practically

- **Do not plead D3 as a dedicated count.** It will be dismissed and the family loses nothing by leaving it out of the count list.
- **Do allege the missing-documents facts in detail in the A1 (Breach) and B1-supporting paragraphs.** Each missing item gets its own factual paragraph: when the document should have existed, who controlled it, what request was made, what response was given, why the absence is prejudicial.
- **Do plan a Rule 37 sequence as part of the litigation plan.** First request for production targeted at: (i) the entire 7007986837-1-1 claim file; (ii) the entire 3013539499-1 (2020) claim file including line-item itemizations and any prior on-site inspection notes; (iii) all materials provided to or generated by Hancock; (iv) all materials provided to or generated by Prieve, including the family's documentation handed to Prieve; (v) all internal communications between adjusters, supervisors, and the engineer engagements between 8/29/2024 and 11/4/2024; (vi) any reservation-of-rights letter; (vii) Farmers' litigation-hold notice (and the date it was issued); and (viii) all communications with Tomes and the umpire. Each non-production becomes the basis for a Rule 37 motion.
- **Do request adverse-inference instructions** at the pretrial conference for each material missing item that survives Rule 37 motions. Each instruction takes time at the jury-instruction conference but adds little procedural risk.
- **Do feed each missing-documents fact directly into the § 604.18 motion (B1).** Failure to document an inspection that found damage, refusal to produce prior-claim records, and operating an "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule are exactly the kinds of facts that establish the absence of a "reasonable basis" prong under § 604.18.
- **Do reserve the umpire-no-report fact for both D3 and A3.** That fact does double work and the complaint should preserve both arguments.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No.** D3 is structurally independent of the 9/12/2025 appraisal award. It does not need the award disturbed in order to function as a sanctions tool, an adverse-inference tool, or a fact pattern supporting B1 bad faith. In one specific direction — the missing umpire report — D3 actually reinforces a separate claim (A3) for vacating, modifying, or remanding the award, but the reinforcement is one-way: A3 leans on D3-style facts; D3 does not require A3 to succeed.

### 4.2 Why D3 is independent of the award

D3 operates at the level of evidentiary preservation and discovery production. Those obligations attach to Farmers as a regulated insurer and as a litigant; they do not depend on whether the appraisal award is binding, ambiguous, modified, vacated, or confirmed.

Three concrete observations:

**(a) Most of the missing items predate the appraisal.** Brau's 9/6/2024 inspection notes (or absence thereof), the Hancock narrative report (or absence thereof), the internal pre-9/3/2024 claim file, and the 2020 claim's "which 2 sash" descriptions all predate the appraisal demand by months or years. Whether the appraisal stands has nothing to do with whether Farmers should have preserved those materials and whether their absence today supports an adverse inference.

**(b) D3 supports A1 directly.** The breach-of-contract count exists whether or not the appraisal is challenged. An adverse-inference instruction at trial, or a Rule 37 sanction during discovery, narrows Farmers' ability to defend A1 regardless of the award's status. The award's hail-causation finding under *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), is binding on Farmers and forecloses Farmers' wear/tear and "no opening" theories for the awarded windows. The remaining contested windows — the bulk of the loss — are decided in the A1 case, where D3 lives.

**(c) D3 supports B1 directly.** Bad-faith analysis under § 604.18 turns on whether Farmers had a reasonable basis for the denial and knew or recklessly disregarded the absence of one. Missing claim-file documentation goes to both prongs. The appraisal award is not necessary to that analysis.

### 4.3 The one direction in which D3 and the appraisal interact

The umpire's missing report and estimate is the one fact that does both jobs: it supports D3 (Farmers controlled the appraisal process and produced no underlying methodology) and it supports A3 (the award is statutorily defective for failing to "state separately actual value and loss to each item"). These are complementary, not contradictory, deployments of the same fact:

- **For D3 purposes**, the missing umpire materials support a Rule 37 motion targeted at Farmers' production of the umpire's working file (Farmers selected the umpire jointly with Foster, communicated with him during the proceeding, and likely possesses correspondence the family does not).
- **For A3 purposes**, the missing umpire materials support remand under *Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018), and *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026), or modification under § 65A.01 Subd. 3.

The family does not have to choose between the two. The same factual paragraph in the complaint can support both.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- D3 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be raised, alleged in support of A1 and B1, supported by Rule 37 motions in litigation, or used as the basis for adverse-inference instructions at trial.
- D3 **is not weakened** by leaving the award in place. The award's hail-causation finding actually strengthens D3 by confirming the factual error baked into the 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials — which in turn reinforces the inference that any missing claim-file materials would be unfavorable to Farmers' denial position.
- The umpire-no-report fact does double duty for both D3 and A3. The family should not pick between deployments; both can be argued.

## 8. Sources and References

### 8.1 Minnesota case law (must be verified on Westlaw/Lexis before any filing)

- *Federated Mut. Ins. Co. v. Litchfield Precision Components, Inc.*, 456 N.W.2d 434 (Minn. 1990) — foundational Minnesota spoliation case; defines destruction of evidence as a basis for sanctions, not a separate claim, and recognizes the adverse-inference instruction as an available remedy.
- *Patton v. Newmar Corp.*, 538 N.W.2d 116 (Minn. 1995) — Minnesota Supreme Court decision establishing the "knew or should have known" trigger; intent of the spoliator irrelevant; trial courts have a duty to impose the least restrictive sanction available; dismissal reserved for the most egregious offenses. (Court of Appeals decision below at 520 N.W.2d 4 (Minn. Ct. App. 1994).)
- *Hoffman v. Ford Motor Co.*, 587 N.W.2d 66 (Minn. Ct. App. 1998) — application of the *Patton* framework; spoliation sanctions where party knew or should have known evidence should be preserved.
- *Miller v. Lankow*, 801 N.W.2d 120, 127–28 (Minn. 2011) — frames the duty-to-preserve trigger in terms of "reasonably foreseeable" litigation; flexible, fact-specific standard committed to trial-court discretion.
- *Wajda v. Kingsbury*, 652 N.W.2d 856 (Minn. Ct. App. 2002) — applies the spoliation-sanction framework in the insurance context; supports adverse-inference instructions where evidence under a party's control is not produced.
- *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012) — appraisal-causation doctrine; cited for the proposition that the 9/12/2025 award's hail-causation finding is binding on Farmers and therefore reinforces, rather than undermines, D3's "missing materials would have been adverse" inference.
- *Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018) — Minnesota-law hail-and-windows appraisal case; remand-for-clarification doctrine cross-referenced for the umpire-no-report fact's overlap with A3.
- *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026) — extends *Herll*; cross-referenced for the same A3 overlap.
