## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The case

*Atwater Creamery Co. v. Western Nat'l Mut. Ins. Co.*, 366 N.W.2d 271 (Minn. 1985), is the Minnesota Supreme Court's foundational adoption of the doctrine of reasonable expectations as a tool for construing insurance contracts. The insured, a creamery and farm-chemical supplier, was burglarized of approximately $15,586 in chemicals. The insurer denied coverage under a "Mercantile Open Stock Burglary Policy" because the policy's *definitions* section defined "burglary" to require "visible marks made by tools, explosives, electricity or chemicals upon, or physical damage to, the exterior of the premises at the place of such entry" — i.e., evidence of forcible entry. No such marks were visible at the burglarized building, so Western denied.

The Minnesota Supreme Court reversed. The court held that "the objectively reasonable expectations of applicants and intended beneficiaries regarding the terms of insurance contracts will be honored even though painstaking study of the policy provisions would have negated those expectations." The doctrine's stated purpose was to give Minnesota courts a "standard by which to construe insurance contracts without having to rely on arbitrary rules which do not reflect real-life situations and without having to bend and stretch those rules to do justice in individual cases." The court was particularly troubled by the fact that what looked like a definitional gloss on "burglary" was, functionally, a major coverage exclusion buried in the definitions section — exactly the kind of provision an ordinary insured would not be expected to read or understand.

The court applied the doctrine to honor the insured's reasonable expectation that a burglary policy covers a burglary, regardless of whether the technical definitional requirement (visible marks of forcible entry) was satisfied.

### 1.2 The doctrine's subsequent narrowing

Atwater is still good Minnesota law, but the Minnesota Supreme Court has limited its scope significantly in the four decades since. The most important narrowing decision is *Carlson v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 749 N.W.2d 41 (Minn. 2008). In *Carlson*, an injured pedestrian sought uninsured-motorist coverage under his father's Allstate policy; he was a listed driver but not a "named insured." The court refused to extend the reasonable-expectations doctrine to override the policy's plain definitional structure, holding that "the reasonable expectation test is not a license to ignore" exclusions in insurance policies "nor to rewrite the exclusion solely to conform to a result that the insured might prefer." *Carlson* limited the doctrine "to cases involving contracts with hidden exclusions," and confirmed that "where the subject exclusion is unambiguous and is plainly set forth in the exclusions section of the policy, it will be applied under Minnesota law."

The Eighth Circuit, applying Minnesota law, has likewise treated the doctrine as confined to "those few egregious occasions when an exclusion is disguised in a policy's definitions section" or where the policyholder's coverage is "significantly different from what the party reasonably believes it has paid for and where the only notice the party has of that difference is in an obscure and unexpected provision." *See* secondary commentary collected in the William Mitchell and Mitchell Hamline Law Review articles cited in §1.7 below.

The post-*Carlson* doctrine is sometimes described as the "weak" or "fine-print" version of reasonable expectations: it operates only when (i) the policy contains an ambiguity, or (ii) an exclusion is "unreasonably hidden" in a place an ordinary insured would not look — most commonly buried in the definitions section. Where an exclusion is unambiguous and squarely placed in the exclusions section, *Carlson* makes it almost impossible to invoke Atwater to override it.

### 1.3 What the doctrine actually does

In its narrowed form, the doctrine performs three discrete functions:

1. **Interpretation of ambiguous policy language.** Where two or more readings are reasonable, the court may select the reading that conforms to the objectively reasonable expectations of an ordinary insured. This is the "weak" version closest to *contra proferentem* (covered separately in E4 / `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/Possible_Arguments_List.md` E4).
2. **Defeating "hidden" exclusions.** Where what is functionally a major coverage exclusion is disguised in the definitions section or in an endorsement an ordinary insured would not encounter on a reasonable read, the court may refuse to enforce the exclusion to the extent it defeats the insured's objectively reasonable expectations. This is the *Atwater* core.
3. **Constraining gap between marketed coverage and actual coverage.** Where the gap between what the insured reasonably believed they were purchasing and what the policy delivers is "extreme," and the only notice of the gap is in an "obscure and unexpected provision," the court may fill the gap in favor of coverage.

The doctrine does **not** authorize the court to rewrite plainly worded exclusions, to manufacture coverage that no policyholder ever paid for, or to override clear policy language because the insured prefers a different result. That is what *Carlson* makes explicit.

### 1.4 What targets the doctrine could plausibly reach in this case

Three targets in the Lu policy and Farmers' actual claim conduct meet the "hidden exclusion" / "ambiguous reach" threshold for Atwater treatment. They are listed here in descending order of doctrinal fit; the supporting facts for each are developed in §3 below.

#### (a) The DEFINITIONS-section "Marring" definition that captures "denting"

Section "Definitions" of the Farmers Next Generation Homeowners Policy Minnesota, 2nd Ed. (`HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` Definitions § 16), defines "Marring" as:

> "any disfigurement, blemish, discoloration, weathering or stretching, or the like, of or to covered property, including by way of example but not limited to scratching, scorching, **denting**, creasing, gouging, fading, staining, tearing or thinning."

The word "denting" is included as an enumerated example of "marring." That has a powerful structural consequence: when the policy then excludes "Marring" in § I.A.8 (Uninsured Types of Loss or Damage) — citing § I.A.8 as one of the six exclusions in the 11/4/2024 denial letter (`EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf` p. 3) — the exclusion of "marring" is by definitional fiat an exclusion of "denting." Hail damage, as the family's record and Prieve's own narrative both repeatedly establish, manifests at this property as denting. So the policy's marring exclusion, read mechanically through the definitions section, captures and excludes the very phenomenon the policy elsewhere insures against (hail).

This is the *Atwater* fact pattern almost line-for-line. Just as Western used a definitions-section "burglary" definition to convert a burglary policy into something that did not cover an obvious burglary, Farmers cited the marring exclusion (after running it through the definitions-section gloss that includes denting) to convert a hail-coverage policy into one that does not pay for hail dents.

A critical wrinkle that strengthens the *Atwater* fit further: § I.A.8 itself contains an exception that *restores* coverage for marring "directly caused by, arises out of or results from … windstorm or hail" — see `HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` § I.A.8 (b) ("windstorm or hail"). Read together, the policy's actual structure is: marring is excluded; but if hail caused the marring, it is covered. Farmers' 11/4/2024 denial cited the marring exclusion to deny payment for hail-caused dents — i.e., used the exclusion in a way that ignores the policy's own restoration clause. That, on the family's reading, is exactly the kind of "use the definitions section to defeat a reasonable insured's coverage expectation" that Atwater addresses.

#### (b) The J6944 endorsement's "obsolescence or discontinuation" reason-gate

J6944 ("Limited Matching of Undamaged Property and Consequential Damage," 1st Edition, effective 11/25/2014; `HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` Policy Endorsements section; full text reproduced in `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/E2.md` §1.1) excludes from coverage "(1) cost to repair or replace property which is not damaged by the covered peril and does not match with the materials used to repair or replace damaged property because of … (iii) obsolescence or discontinuation."

The "obsolescence or discontinuation" carve-out is a reason-gate buried inside an endorsement that an ordinary homeowner would have to retrieve from a separate Endorsements section to read. The buy-back box that follows tells the insured the (1)(iii) limit can be removed — but only "for an added premium" and only as to "exterior siding or roof coverings, including soffit and facia." The buy-back is silent on windows. So a homeowner who bought a $886,000 dwelling policy with windstorm/hail coverage, paying premium each year for ten years, has no objectively reasonable way to know that if the manufacturer of their windows goes out of business between the policy's inception and the loss, the insurer's matching obligation — even for windows on the same building structure as the damaged ones — will be reduced or eliminated by an endorsement-level rule.

That is the *Atwater* "extreme gap" fact pattern: the coverage the family thought it had purchased (replacement cost for hail damage to an insured dwelling) is significantly different from what the policy delivers when an endorsement-level reason-gate is triggered, and the only notice the family has of that gap is in the obscure J6944 endorsement.

#### (c) The 11/4/2024 stack of causation-style exclusions used to defeat hail coverage

This third target is doctrinally the weakest of the three, but it is worth flagging. Farmers' 11/4/2024 denial cited six exclusions to deny a hail claim: wear/tear (§ I.A.7 / I.B.8), marring (§ I.A.8), corrosion/deterioration/decay/rust (§ I.A.9 / I.B.9), inherent vice/latent defect (§ I.A.4 / I.B.6), neglect/lack of maintenance (§ I.B.4), and faulty/defective construction (§ I.B.5). *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 3.)* Each of these exclusions is plainly placed in the EXCLUSIONS section of the policy. *Carlson* therefore makes it difficult to argue any individual exclusion is "hidden" in the *Atwater* sense.

But the cumulative use of six exclusions, each of which carries a hail/windstorm exception or otherwise restores hail coverage when the insured peril is the actual cause, to deny a hail claim where the appraisal panel later found hail causation, raises a related concern: the *practical operation* of the policy as Farmers applies it is significantly different from the coverage an ordinary insured would reasonably expect when paying for a hail rider with a 2% deductible. The argument is not that any single exclusion is "hidden" but that the combinatorial use of multiple exclusions to defeat the policy's own central coverage promise creates the kind of "extreme gap" *Atwater* and its progeny are concerned with. This is the weakest of the three Atwater frames; treat it as a narrative reinforcement for (a) and (b), not a standalone vehicle.

### 1.5 Companion doctrines

E3 lives close to two other interpretive doctrines and should be briefed alongside them:

- **E4 — *Contra Proferentem*.** Ambiguities in insurance policies are construed against the drafter (insurer). E4 supports E3 for any reading where the policy text is open to more than one interpretation. Where E3 reaches the "hidden exclusion in definitions" facts, E4 supplies the supporting interpretive principle that closes ambiguity in the family's favor.
- **E1 — *Quade* appraisal causation preclusion.** *Quade* binds Farmers to causation on F1.7/B1.2; E3 then operates on the policy *language* that Farmers relied on to deny in the first place. The two doctrines reinforce each other: *Quade* says Farmers cannot relitigate the cause; *Atwater* says Farmers cannot use definitionally-engineered exclusions to defeat the coverage promise the panel's causation finding triggers.

### 1.6 Procedural posture — how this argument shows up in a complaint

Like E1 and E2, E3 is best pleaded as an interpretive doctrine inside other counts rather than as a freestanding cause of action. Minnesota does not recognize a standalone "cause of action for violation of reasonable expectations." The doctrine is a tool of policy construction the court uses to resolve coverage disputes raised through:

- **A1 (breach of contract)** — supplying the interpretive premise that the marring/denting and J6944 (1)(iii) provisions cannot be read to defeat the coverage Farmers promised.
- **A2 (declaratory judgment under Minn. Stat. Ch. 555)** — asking the court to declare that the marring exclusion's coupling with the definitions-section "denting" example does not bar coverage for hail-caused dents on the family's windows, and that J6944's discontinuation reason-gate does not defeat the policy's "comparable material and quality" obligation when the original window manufacturer is no longer available.
- **Opposition to Farmers' affirmative defenses** — answering any argument that revives the 11/4/2024 exclusions for any hail-damaged window with the *Atwater* frame in addition to *Quade* preclusion.

A reasonable Atwater pleading frame inside A2 looks like:

> "Plaintiffs respectfully request that this Court declare, pursuant to Minn. Stat. Ch. 555 and the Minnesota doctrine of reasonable expectations under *Atwater Creamery Co. v. Western Nat'l Mut. Ins. Co.*, 366 N.W.2d 271 (Minn. 1985), that:
> (a) Defendant may not rely on the policy's 'Marring' exclusion in § I.A.8, as defined through the inclusion of 'denting' in Definition § 16, to deny coverage for hail-caused dents to insured windows, where the policy's own § I.A.8 (b) restores coverage for marring 'directly caused by, arises out of or results from … windstorm or hail';
> (b) The 'obsolescence or discontinuation' reason-gate in Endorsement J6944 (1)(iii) does not defeat Defendant's obligation to pay the replacement cost of damaged windows under the policy's 'comparable material and quality' standard where the original-equipment manufacturer of the insured's windows is no longer in operation; and
> (c) The objectively reasonable expectations of an ordinary policyholder purchasing the Farmers Next Generation Homeowners Policy Minnesota, 2nd Ed., for a primary residence with windstorm/hail coverage do not include the result Defendant has produced here — denial of coverage for hail-caused dents on a $886,000 Coverage A dwelling."

Pleaded this way, E3 is an interpretive request the court can grant without remand to the panel and without disturbing the appraisal award.

### 1.7 What to expect in opposition

Farmers will attack E3 on the following lines, and the family should pre-empt them:

- **"Each exclusion is in the exclusions section, not hidden."** *Carlson* defense to target (c) above. Counter: target (a) is not the exclusion itself but the *definitions-section* "denting" gloss that converts a marring exclusion into a hail-dent exclusion — Atwater's exact concern. Target (b) is an *endorsement* the average homeowner would not encounter without active retrieval — also within Atwater's reach.
- **"The 'denting' example in the marring definition is unambiguous."** Counter: the question is not just ambiguity but whether the structure of definitions + exclusion + windstorm/hail exception leaves the average insured with an objectively reasonable expectation of coverage that the policy then defeats.
- **"*Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* enforce J6944-style endorsements."** Counter: those cases enforce the matching exclusion *in general*, against arguments that it is unenforceable as a whole. They do not address Atwater as applied to the specific (1)(iii) discontinuation reason-gate where the manufacturer is defunct. (See E2 §1.3.) The family is not arguing J6944 is unenforceable; the family is arguing its (1)(iii) reason-gate, applied where matching is impossible because of manufacturer discontinuation, defeats the insured's reasonable expectation in a way Atwater is meant to police.
- **"The doctrine has been narrowed since *Atwater*."** Conceded; the doctrine is alive but narrow. The family's invocation lives squarely within the narrowed scope: target (a) is a textbook hidden-in-definitions exclusion; target (b) is a textbook obscure-endorsement-level limit on a major coverage promise.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **30 / 100** as a primary path to recovery; **45 / 100** as an interpretive doctrine inside A1 and A2

Like E2, E3 is two arguments with two different strength profiles, and the rating reflects what the doctrine actually does.

**As a primary path to recovery (~30/100).** The honest appraisal is that the post-*Carlson* doctrine is narrow, fact-dependent, and not a silver bullet. Minnesota courts are reluctant to invoke it. Farmers will have *Carlson* and the line of cases narrowing Atwater on its side as a matter of black-letter authority, and the family must make the case that the marring/denting and J6944(1)(iii) targets fit the narrow exception. Even where E3 succeeds, it does not directly produce a dollar award — it removes a Farmers defense or interpretive obstacle, after which the family still needs A1 and the underlying record to deliver recovery.

**As an interpretive doctrine inside A1 and A2 (~45/100).** Read as an interpretive overlay on declaratory and contract claims, E3 has more bite. It supplies a doctrinally clean reason for the court to refuse Farmers' attempt to convert a $886,000 hail-rider dwelling policy into a $0-payment outcome via definitions-section drafting. It is also useful narratively: the *Atwater* facts (definitions-section burglary definition defeating burglary coverage) are a striking analog to the *facts here* (definitions-section "marring includes denting" gloss defeating hail-dent coverage), and the symmetry is exactly the kind of comparison Minnesota state courts respond to. Coupled with E1 (Quade) and E4 (contra proferentem), E3 becomes part of a doctrinal package — not the lead doctrine, but a contributor.

**Weighted blend, reflecting how E3 is realistically used (as an interpretive overlay supporting A1 and A2, with target (a) being its strongest application): ~35/100.**

### 2.2 What drives the rating up

- **Target (a) is an unusually clean Atwater fit.** The "Marring" definition in § 16 includes "denting," and Farmers cited the marring exclusion to deny a hail-dent claim. The structural symmetry with *Atwater*'s "burglary defined to require evidence of forcible entry" facts is striking. Minnesota courts that cite *Atwater* explicitly look for definitions-section provisions that defeat coverage; the family has one.
- **The policy's own marring exception restores hail coverage**, demonstrating that the drafter knew "denting" could result from hail and chose to bracket the exclusion accordingly. Farmers' use of the marring exclusion to deny a hail-dent claim is therefore not just a hidden-definition problem — it is a misapplication of the policy's actual structure. Atwater operates against the misapplication.
- **The 11/4/2024 denial expressly stacks six exclusions, all of them causation-flavored, against a hail loss.** That use pattern reinforces the "extreme gap" framing — the practical effect of Farmers' position is that the policyholder paid premium for windstorm/hail coverage and, on the actual occurrence of windstorm/hail, was denied coverage by exclusions that reasonable insureds do not associate with hail.
- **The 9/12/2025 appraisal award binds Farmers to hail causation under *Quade*.** That foreclosure is a strong narrative anchor for the Atwater argument: a binding panel found hail damage; Farmers nonetheless used definitionally-engineered exclusions to refuse payment; the doctrine of reasonable expectations is the interpretive tool a Minnesota court has to neutralize that refusal.
- **The J6944 (1)(iii) discontinuation gate is buried at endorsement level**, several layers of paper away from the cover declaration. An ordinary homeowner reading the declaration page (Coverage A $886,000, windstorm/hail deductible $17,720) and the main policy form has no reason to expect that the matching obligation depends on whether the original-equipment window manufacturer remains in business years after policy inception.
- **The "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule** Farmers imposed during the claim — articulated by Bradlee Monson on 2/3/2025 and confirmed by Brau on 2/10/2025 — is itself a concrete, contemporaneous record of Farmers imposing an extra-policy requirement on the insured. Nothing in the policy says the insured must furnish an engineer's report to have evidence considered. This is not an exclusion in the policy at all, let alone a hidden one — it is a Farmers-created gate. *Atwater* is not the doctrinal home for that conduct (it is a § 72A.201 / B1 / C1 issue), but the conduct reinforces the narrative that Farmers' practical operation of the policy is significantly different from what an ordinary insured would expect.

### 2.3 What drives the rating down

- ***Carlson* substantially narrowed the doctrine in 2008.** Minnesota courts are reluctant to extend Atwater beyond the narrow circumstances of hidden-in-definitions exclusions or extreme gaps backed only by obscure provisions. The family must accept the post-*Carlson* limits going in.
- **The exclusions Farmers cited in the 11/4/2024 denial are all in the EXCLUSIONS section.** That is the weakest doctrinal posture for Atwater. The family's strongest target — the marring/denting interaction — is the one closest to *Atwater* itself, but even there Farmers will argue that "Marring" is itself "plainly designated" in the exclusions section (§ I.A.8) and that the definitions are merely doing definitional work. The family's counter is that the definitions-section inclusion of "denting" is what converts the exclusion into a hail-coverage defeat — but a court could read it the other way.
- **Atwater is rarely outcome-determinative.** The doctrine more often serves as one interpretive tool alongside others (ambiguity / *contra proferentem* / structural reading) than as the sole basis for a coverage ruling. Pleading it as a primary path overstates its likely role.
- **The dollar mechanism is indirect.** E3 does not by itself produce a dollar award. Its function is to remove or constrain Farmers' interpretive defenses; A1 still has to deliver the actual recovery once those defenses are out of the way. The family should not score E3 as a deductible-defeating doctrine on its own.
- **The doctrine does not displace clear endorsement language.** *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* enforce matching-exclusion endorsements; the family is not arguing J6944 is unenforceable. Atwater applied to the (1)(iii) discontinuation gate is a narrower, scope-limiting argument that depends on (i) the gate being characterized as obscure relative to the main coverage promise and (ii) the manufacturer-defunct facts holding (which currently turn on resolving the Senco/Semco identification issue noted in E2 §3.4 and §3.7).
- **The doctrine's "weak" version overlaps with E4.** Where E3's strongest move is to resolve ambiguity in favor of the insured, *contra proferentem* (E4) does the same work without the *Carlson* narrowing problem. The family should brief E3 and E4 together but expect a court to lean on E4 first.

### 2.4 What "30/100 (primary) / 45/100 (interpretive)" means practically

E3 is best filed as an interpretive doctrine inside A2's declaratory-judgment count and as an interpretive premise for A1's breach-of-contract count. Plead the three targets in §1.4 (in descending priority — start with the marring/denting hidden-exclusion argument, then the J6944 (1)(iii) endorsement-level limit, then the cumulative-effect framing for the six-exclusion stack). Couple E3 with E4 (*contra proferentem*) so the court sees the interpretive overlay from two complementary angles. Do not lead the case with E3 and do not score it as the lever that breaks the deductible. The doctrine's job here is to (a) neutralize Farmers' marring-as-denting-as-hail defense, (b) constrain J6944's (1)(iii) bite as applied to a defunct-manufacturer fact pattern, and (c) supply the doctrinal frame Minnesota courts use when an insurer's claim conduct produces a "significantly different" result from what the homeowner reasonably believed they were buying.

### 3.1 Facts supporting target (a) — the marring/denting hidden-in-definitions argument

- **The DEFINITIONS section's Marring definition (§ 16) expressly enumerates "denting."** Verbatim: "Marring - means any disfigurement, blemish, discoloration, weathering or stretching, or the like, of or to covered property, including by way of example but not limited to scratching, scorching, **denting**, creasing, gouging, fading, staining, tearing or thinning." *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf Definitions § 16.)*
- **§ I.A.8 excludes "Marring" generally.** Text of § I.A.8: "We do not insure loss or damage which is marring of covered property." *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf § I.A.8.)*
- **§ I.A.8 contains a windstorm/hail exception that restores coverage.** Text continues: "However, we do insure marring which is directly caused by, arises out of or results from the following causes of loss, subject to the terms and limitations set forth in subsection B. Excluded Causes of Loss or Damage and in Extensions of Coverage for any such cause of loss or extensions of coverage: a. fire or lightning; **b. windstorm or hail**; …" *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf § I.A.8 (b).)*
- **The 11/4/2024 denial letter cites the Marring exclusion (§ I.A.8) as one of the bases for denying the family's hail claim.** *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 3 ("Marring").)* The denial does not engage with the § I.A.8 (b) windstorm/hail exception; it cites the exclusion as a general bar.
- **Prieve's report — incorporated into the 11/4/2024 denial — repeatedly characterizes the observed window damage as "dents," "marks," and "indentations."** Examples appear at the front/west triple window, the upper-west 3-unit bedroom, the upper southwest bedroom, the east family-room bay projection, the upper-east window above the four-season porch, the four-season porch SE/E/NE/N windows, the kitchen east, the main-level NE 3-unit, the upper-north above the kitchen, and the rear/east garage window. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 4–9.)* Each of these observations is an instance of the very phenomenon the policy's definition of "marring" enumerates ("denting").
- **The 9/12/2025 appraisal panel found hail causation for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows.** *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png; EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Back.png.)* The panel's binding finding under *Quade* confirms the dents on those windows were caused by the very peril (hail) that § I.A.8 (b) restores marring coverage for.
- **Conclusion supporting Atwater target (a):** Farmers used a marring exclusion (defined-via-definitions to include denting) to deny a hail claim where (i) the policy's own restoration clause covers marring caused by hail and (ii) the appraisal panel found hail causation. An ordinary insured purchasing windstorm/hail coverage with a 2% deductible has no objectively reasonable expectation that the policy would defeat coverage by routing "denting" through the definitions section into the marring exclusion — the *Atwater* core fact pattern.

- **The buy-back box that follows is limited to "exterior siding or roof coverings, including soffit and facia."** *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf, J6944 endorsement.)* Windows are not within the buy-back's reach. Whether the box was X'd in this policy is an open factual question (`E2.md` §3.1), but even if X'd, it would not modify the windows analysis.
- **The 9/18/2025 Farmers email identifies the awarded windows as manufactured by Senco with manufacturer stamps in the sashes from 1993, 1997, and 2008.** *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/18/2025 entry); EventDocuments/2025-09-18-PostAppraisalEmailChain.md.)*

- **Conclusion supporting Atwater target (b):** A homeowner who paid premium for ten years on a $886,000 dwelling policy with windstorm/hail coverage has no objectively reasonable expectation that an endorsement-level reason-gate would limit the matching obligation if the original-equipment window manufacturer ceased operations between policy inception and loss. The Atwater "extreme gap with notice only in an obscure provision" frame fits.

### 3.3 Facts supporting target (c) — the cumulative-six-exclusion framing

- **The 11/4/2024 denial cites six exclusions to deny a hail claim:** Wear and Tear (§ I.A.7 / I.B.8), Marring (§ I.A.8), Corrosion/Deterioration/Decay/Rust (§ I.A.9 / I.B.9), Inherent Vice/Latent Defect (§ I.A.4 / I.B.6), Neglect/Lack of Maintenance (§ I.B.4), and Faulty/Defective Construction (§ I.B.5). *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 3.)*
- **The 9/3/2024 first denial cited a different exclusion theory entirely** ("no opening" plus "no other damages observed"), incompatible with the 11/4/2024 theory. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf pp. 1–2.)* The shifting between the two denials demonstrates that Farmers' ability to characterize the loss as falling within an exclusion is itself a moving target.
- **The 9/12/2025 appraisal award found hail causation, defeating each exclusion's causal premise as to the F1.7 and B1.2 windows under *Quade*.** *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png.)*
- **Conclusion supporting Atwater target (c):** The cumulative effect of a six-exclusion stack against a hail loss on a policy that the insured purchased specifically for windstorm/hail coverage produces a "significantly different" result from what an ordinary insured would reasonably expect. This argument is doctrinally weaker than (a) and (b) because each exclusion is plainly placed in the exclusions section, but it serves as narrative reinforcement for the *Atwater* frame.

### 3.4 Facts supporting the broader "extreme gap" / reasonable-expectation narrative

These do not map directly onto a hidden-exclusion theory, but they reinforce the family's position that Farmers' practical operation of the policy is significantly different from what an ordinary insured would reasonably expect.

- **Continuous, ten-year policy relationship.** The Lu/Hu family has been insured under Farmers policy 98055-49-65 (Fire Insurance Exchange) without interruption since 10/22/2014 — roughly ten years at the time of the 7/13/2024 loss. *(HomePolicy/2014_Application-PolicyDocument-980554965.pdf; HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf.)* Annual premium payment over a decade is the consideration the family paid for the coverage Farmers now refuses to honor.
- **Coverage A of $886,000 with a 2% windstorm/hail deductible** ($17,720) was renewed for the policy year 10/22/2023–10/22/2024, with "Owner Occupied (Primary Res.)" classification and a "New Roof" discount. *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf.)* The premium structure reflects coverage purchased specifically against catastrophic windstorm/hail loss to a primary residence — exactly the kind of coverage the homeowner reasonably believed was meaningful protection.
- **The family submitted three rounds of supplemental evidence** between 9/5/2024 and 5/28/2025 — photographs (9/5, 9/8, 9/10/2024); the 1/31/2025 written appeal with stormersite weather data, post-2021 gutter-replacement evidence, and a comparison to the 2020 Xactimate scope; the 2/10/2025 second response with City of Eden Prairie permit records and the 14-of-19 neighbor canvass; and the 5/28/2025 NWS Twin Cities/Chanhassen article and Interactive Hail Maps data, submitted through Farmers' own agent Peter Pietila. *(EventDocuments/2025-01-31-ResponseToFarmer.pdf; EventDocuments/2025-01-31-ResponseToFarmer-01-31-25-StormsAfter2020.pdf; EventDocuments/2025-02-10-SecondResponseToFinalReport.pdf; EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (5/28/2025 entry); Evidence/Significant Wind and Hail in Minnesota July 13-14.pdf.)* No reasonable insured paying premium for a decade would expect the insurer to refuse to substantively engage with this volume of corroborating evidence.
- **Farmers articulated an "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule and applied it to refuse to review the family's supplemental evidence.** On 2/3/2025 field claim supervisor Bradlee Monson told the family by phone that Farmers would not review evidence unless provided by an engineer; on 2/10/2025 at 11:06 AM Nic Brau confirmed the same rule by voicemail. *(EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG (Brau voicemail transcription); family's contemporaneous account of the 2/3/2025 phone call.)* **Nothing in the policy requires evidence to come from an engineer.** This is a Farmers-created gate, not a policy provision. Atwater is not the doctrinal home for that conduct (it is a § 72A.201, B1, and C1 issue), but the conduct directly supports the narrative that Farmers' practical operation of the policy is "significantly different" from what an ordinary insured would reasonably expect.
- **The 9/3/2024 first denial issued before any engineering analysis.** *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf pp. 1–2.)* A homeowner reasonably expects an insurer to investigate a claim before denying it — not to issue a formal denial within five days of the first inspection and before any forensic engineer has been engaged.
- **Farmers' adjuster's 9/6/2024 inspection produced no written report of record.** *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/10, 10/29/2025 entries).)* A homeowner reasonably expects that an insurance adjuster who personally inspects the property, finds dents alongside the homeowner, and measures window dimensions will document the inspection — and that the documentation will be available to the insured on request.

## 4. Does This Argument Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No. E3 is a standalone interpretive doctrine. It does not require the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be vacated, modified, or corrected, and it can operate whether the award stands or is disturbed in part.** Like E2's interpretive register, E3 is the kind of question Minnesota state courts decide all the time on a Minn. Stat. Ch. 555 declaratory record without touching an underlying appraisal. The only directional dependency is the same one E1 carries: full *Mork* vacatur of the award would erase the *Quade* causation finding that strengthens the E3 narrative for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows. That is a strategic constraint on A3's Tier-3 architecture, not an obstacle to E3 itself.

### 4.2 Compatibility with the rest of the case

| Argument | E3 compatibility |
|---|---|
| **A1 — Breach of contract** | Fully compatible. E3 supplies the interpretive premise that the marring/denting exclusion and J6944(1)(iii) reason-gate cannot be read to defeat the coverage A1 alleges Farmers breached. |
| **A2 — Declaratory judgment** | Fully compatible — and the most natural pleading vehicle. The three E3 targets (§1.4) translate directly into declaratory questions inside A2. |
| **A3 — Vacate / modify / correct appraisal** | Compatible with Tier 1 (*Herll*/*Rymer* clarification) and Tier 2 (§ 65A.01 itemization correction). In tension with Tier 3 (full *Mork* vacatur) only insofar as full vacatur erases the *Quade* causation finding that strengthens the E3 narrative; E3 itself does not require the appraisal to fall. |
| **B1 — Bad faith under § 604.18** | Synergistic in narrative. A reasonable-expectations frame sharpens the bad-faith showing: Farmers used definitionally-engineered exclusions to defeat the coverage promise the homeowner reasonably understood she was buying. |
| **B4 — Estoppel/waiver** | Synergistic. B4 says Farmers cannot revive denial theories inconsistent with prior positions; E3 says Farmers cannot use definitionally-engineered exclusions to defeat the policy's central coverage promise. They cover adjacent ground. |
| **C1 — Unreasonable investigation under § 72A.201** | Synergistic. C1 reaches the "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule directly; E3 reaches the same conduct as evidence of the "extreme gap" between policy promise and Farmers' practical operation. |
| **E1 — *Quade* preclusion** | Mutually reinforcing. *Quade* binds Farmers to causation; E3 then operates on the policy *language* that Farmers relied on to deny in the first place. The two doctrines together close the loop on the F1.7 and B1.2 windows. |
| **E2 — J6944 scope limits** | Mutually reinforcing on target (b). E2 supplies the textual scope-limit reading of J6944; E3 supplies the doctrinal reason a court should adopt the family's reading where the (1)(iii) reason-gate produces an outcome the homeowner could not reasonably expect. |
| **E4 — *Contra proferentem*** | Closely related. Brief together. E4 closes ambiguity in the family's favor where the policy text is open to more than one reading; E3 reaches the additional cases where the language is unambiguous on its face but the result is significantly different from the homeowner's reasonable expectation. |

### 4.3 Practical pleading recommendation

E3 should appear in two places in the complaint, both downstream of A1 and A2:

- **As declaratory questions in the A2 declaratory-judgment count.** Convert the three targets in §1.4 into the three sub-questions in §1.6 and put them in A2's prayer for relief. The marring/denting hidden-in-definitions question is the lead.
- **As an interpretive premise inside A1.** Use the *Atwater* doctrine, alongside *Quade* (E1) and *contra proferentem* (E4), to defeat any Farmers attempt to revive the 11/4/2024 marring exclusion as a defense to A1's breach claim, and to support the family's reading that J6944's (1)(iii) reason-gate does not reach the dollar mechanism the family needs to defeat the deductible.

E3 does **not** belong as a freestanding cause of action. Pleading it as one over-promises what the doctrine does, especially after *Carlson*. Plead it as relief inside A2 and as a doctrinal predicate inside A1, and the architecture stays clean.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- **E3 does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting the appraisal.** It is standalone in the sense that the appraisal does not need to be disturbed for E3 to operate.
- **E3 is *strengthened* — not required, just strengthened — by the appraisal staying in place.** The 9/12/2025 award's *Quade* causation finding for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows reinforces the *Atwater* frame for those two windows: Farmers' definitionally-engineered exclusions are pitted against a binding causation finding that the cause was the very peril the policy insures.
- **E3 is compatible with A3's Tier-1 *Herll*/*Rymer* clarification and Tier-2 § 65A.01 itemization-correction sub-claims.** Both leave the panel's findings intact and only sharpen what the panel decided. The E3 narrative tracks the same direction.
- **E3 is in mild tension with A3's Tier-3 full *Mork* vacatur** for the same reason E1 is — full vacatur erases the panel's causation finding, and the E3 narrative loses some force. The strategic ranking in `A3.md` §4 and `E1.md` §4 already reflects this: lead with Tiers 1 and 2; reserve Tier 3 for facts discovery actually delivers.
