## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 No Minnesota statute creates the cause of action

Unjust enrichment in Minnesota is a **common-law equitable claim** (often called quasi-contract or restitution), not a statutory cause of action. There is no statute to cite, and no statutory text to quote. The claim's outline is built entirely from Minnesota Supreme Court and Court of Appeals decisions.

This is a meaningful structural difference from the other counts under consideration: A1 (breach of contract) is grounded in Minn. Stat. § 60A.08 and ordinary contract law; A2 (declaratory judgment) is grounded in Minn. Stat. Ch. 555; A3 (vacate/modify the appraisal) is grounded in Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 and analogized to the Uniform Arbitration Act at Minn. Stat. § 572B.23. A4 has no statutory anchor, which makes pleading and surviving a Rule 12 motion correspondingly more dependent on case law fit.

### 1.2 Elements under Minnesota law

To state a claim for unjust enrichment, a plaintiff must plead and prove that:

1. The defendant **knowingly received** something of value;
2. To which the defendant **was not entitled**; and
3. **Under circumstances making it unjust** for the defendant to retain the benefit without paying for it.

*ServiceMaster of St. Cloud v. GAB Bus. Servs., Inc.*, 544 N.W.2d 302, 306 (Minn. 1996); *Schumacher v. Schumacher*, 627 N.W.2d 725, 729 (Minn. Ct. App. 2001).

### 1.3 What "unjust" means

The key Minnesota gloss is that "unjust" does not mean merely "the defendant got a benefit." The Minnesota Supreme Court, in *First National Bank of St. Paul v. Ramier*, 311 N.W.2d 502, 504 (Minn. 1981), narrowed the term: enrichment must be "illegal" or "unlawful" (e.g., fraudulent). Subsequent cases (e.g., *Cady v. Bush*, 283 Minn. 105, 166 N.W.2d 358 (1969); *Schumacher*, 627 N.W.2d at 729) extended the standard to enrichment that is "morally wrongful." The recognized factual triggers include **failure of consideration, fraud, or mistake**, plus situations where retention of a benefit would be morally wrong.

Translated to this case, an unjust enrichment count would have to characterize Farmers' retention of premium dollars (or its retention of money it should have paid as covered loss) as illegal, unlawful, fraudulent, or morally wrongful — not merely as a contract dispute.

### 1.4 The doctrinal hurdle: a valid express contract precludes recovery

Minnesota has a long-standing rule that **equitable relief, including unjust enrichment, is unavailable where the rights of the parties are governed by a valid express contract**.

> "Equitable relief cannot be granted where the rights of the parties are governed by a valid contract."

*United States Fire Ins. Co. v. Minn. State Zoological Bd.*, 307 N.W.2d 490, 497 (Minn. 1981); *accord Holman v. CPT Corp.*, 457 N.W.2d 740 (Minn. Ct. App. 1990) ("If the rights of the parties are governed by a valid employment contract, recovery in quasi-contract is inappropriate.").

The Minnesota Supreme Court reinforced the related "adequate remedy at law" branch of the doctrine in *ServiceMaster of St. Cloud v. GAB Bus. Servs., Inc.*, 544 N.W.2d 302 (Minn. 1996). There, the court reversed an appellate affirmance of unjust enrichment damages against an insurer because the plaintiff "had an adequate remedy at law" and the insurer "had no duty to" the plaintiff. *Id.* at 306–07.

For this case, the plain reading is bad for A4: the Lu/Hu policy is undisputedly in force, the parties' rights are governed by the policy, and there is an "adequate remedy at law" available — the breach-of-contract count (A1).

### 1.5 The narrow exceptions that keep A4 alive at all

Minnesota courts have not turned the contract-precludes-restitution rule into an absolute bar. Three narrow openings preserve A4 as a permissible alternative claim, not a winning standalone:

**(a) Pleading in the alternative under Minn. R. Civ. P. 8.05(2).** Rule 8.05(2) permits a party to "set forth two or more statements of a claim or defense alternatively or hypothetically" and to plead "as many separate claims or defenses as the party has regardless of consistency and whether based on legal or equitable grounds or both." Federal courts applying Minnesota law have routinely allowed unjust enrichment to be pled in the alternative to breach of contract at the Rule 12 stage, on the theory that until the contract's validity, scope, or coverage is conclusively established, the equitable claim may be necessary as a backstop. The claim is then dismissed (or dropped at summary judgment) once the contract's applicability is established.

**(b) Contract is valid but the parties' rights under it are ambiguous.** Some Minnesota authority recognizes that where the contract exists but does not fully determine the dispute (e.g., the agreement is silent on the specific issue, or the contract terms are genuinely ambiguous), unjust enrichment may remain available. This is a narrow doorway that depends on a court accepting that the policy genuinely fails to address the disputed scope — a tough sell where the policy expressly defines covered perils, exclusions, deductibles, and an appraisal procedure.

**(c) The contract is voided, rescinded, or otherwise unenforceable.** If, for any reason, Farmers were to take a position that the policy or some part of it is invalid or that the appraisal award has fully discharged the contract claim (a position Farmers has functionally taken in correspondence — "settled per the binding appraisal award"), an unjust enrichment claim could become the residual vehicle for recovery of (i) premiums paid in exchange for promised hail coverage that was never delivered, and/or (ii) money Farmers retained that "in equity and good conscience" belongs to the insureds.

### 1.6 What the count would actually look like in a complaint

In substance, a pleaded A4 count in this case would read approximately as follows (with the *Bell Atlantic*/*Twombly*-style minimum allegations):

> "In the alternative to Count I (Breach of Contract), Plaintiffs plead that, to the extent Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange contends the policy does not obligate it to pay for the full scope of hail damage to Plaintiffs' windows, Defendant has nonetheless retained (a) premium payments collected from Plaintiffs since 10/22/2014 in consideration of windstorm/hail coverage, and (b) the value of unpaid covered hail loss confirmed by the 9/12/2025 appraisal award, under circumstances making it unjust for Defendant to retain those benefits. Defendant knowingly received those benefits, was not entitled to retain them in light of its denials premised on factually incompatible theories and its failure to pay for hail damage its own appraisal panel confirmed, and equity and good conscience require restitution. Pleaded in the alternative under Minn. R. Civ. P. 8.05(2)."

This framing keeps the claim alive in case the contract theory falls into a procedural gap, without conceding the existence or validity of the policy.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **15 / 100**

That number reflects the count's standalone strength as a means of obtaining real-world relief beyond what the breach-of-contract count delivers. As an alternative pleading vehicle that costs nothing to include and may protect the family in marginal procedural scenarios, A4's instrumental value is higher (~40/100). But on the merits as a route to actual recovery on this record, the rating is low.

- **Standalone merits (does the family win on this count alone?):** ~10/100. The policy is indisputably in force. *U.S. Fire Ins.*, *Holman*, and *ServiceMaster* combine to make a standalone unjust enrichment count vulnerable to immediate Rule 12 dismissal.
- **Alternative-pleading viability (does the count survive Rule 12 if pled in the alternative?):** ~55/100. Rule 8.05(2) routinely permits unjust enrichment to be carried alongside breach until the contract's coverage of the dispute is established. Many Minnesota state and federal trial courts deny dismissal at the pleadings stage and revisit at summary judgment.
- **Rule 11 / sanctions risk:** low but non-zero. *See* the unpublished Minnesota appellate decision sanctioning a plaintiff who pursued an unjust enrichment claim after the defendant admitted liability on the contract claims. The risk goes up materially if Farmers concedes the policy is in force and that the dispute is purely about coverage scope.
- **Marginal contribution beyond A1 + A2 + A3:** very low. Anything the family could recover under unjust enrichment, it could also recover under (a) breach of contract for unevaluated windows, (b) declaratory judgment on coverage scope, or (c) modification/correction of the appraisal award. A4 does not unlock additional damages or remedies that A1–A3 cannot reach.

Weighted blend, with emphasis on the question the user asked — the strength of A4 as an initial claim in MN state court: **~15/100**.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

- **The policy is indisputably valid and in force on the loss date.** Coverage A $886,000, $17,720 windstorm/hail deductible, all premiums paid, "Owner Occupied (Primary Res.)" classification, "New Roof" discount active — the 2023 renewal is the controlling contract and there is no live argument that it is void, voidable, or rescinded. *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf)*
- **The dispute is unambiguously a coverage and amount-of-loss dispute under the contract.** Farmers' denials, the appraisal demand, the appraisal award, and the post-appraisal correspondence all turn on what the policy covers and how much. That is precisely the kind of dispute *U.S. Fire Ins.* and *Holman* hold falls inside the contract's domain and outside equity.
- **A1 is an adequate remedy at law.** The *ServiceMaster* "adequate remedy at law" doctrine is squarely on point: the breach-of-contract claim already supplies the family's vehicle for full recovery (or for a court to find no recovery is owed). Equity does not add a parallel pathway when the legal remedy is available.
- **The "morally wrongful" branch of *Ramier* and *Cady* requires more than a coverage dispute.** Even if the family characterizes Farmers' conduct as morally wrong (shifting denials, missing inspection notes, gatekeeping rules, ignored evidence), Minnesota courts use that language to capture transactions where there is no contract — not as a workaround to recharacterize a contract dispute as restitution.
- **Existing alternative claims do the same work better.** Breach of contract reaches every dollar A4 could reach. Bad faith under § 604.18, added by motion, captures the "morally wrong" conduct theme with statutory damages on top. A4 brings nothing additional to the table on this record.
- **Sanctions exposure is non-trivial if A4 is pursued past the pleading stage** after Farmers stipulates that the policy was in force and the dispute is purely about scope and amount.

### 2.3 What modestly drives the rating up

- **Rule 8.05(2) freely allows alternative pleading**, including legal and equitable theories side-by-side, "regardless of consistency."
- **Federal and Minnesota trial courts routinely deny Rule 12 dismissal of unjust enrichment counts pled in the alternative** to breach of contract, deferring the question to summary judgment after discovery clarifies whether the contract actually governs the dispute.
- **There is a low-probability, high-impact scenario** in which Farmers takes the position that the appraisal award fully discharged its obligations under the policy and the contract claim is barred. If a court were to credit that position, an alternative restitution theory becomes the only residual lane for recovery.
- **The rule against unjust enrichment in the face of a valid contract is doctrinally narrower than it sounds.** The exception for ambiguous contracts (*see* §1.5(b) above) and the long-standing equitable principle that one party should not "enrich himself at the expense of another" (*Cady*) leave room for creative argument, particularly if discovery reveals that Farmers retained sums (e.g., premium for hail coverage during the policy year) without delivering the bargained-for performance.

### 2.4 What "15/100" means practically

A4 should not be the lead count, the centerpiece, or even a featured count. It belongs in the complaint only as a **belt-and-suspenders alternative pleading** under Rule 8.05(2), placed after A1–A3, expressly identified as alternative, and pleaded with the minimum allegations needed to satisfy *Twombly*/Minnesota notice-pleading standards. It will likely be dismissed at summary judgment once the policy's applicability to the dispute is conclusively established. It is unlikely to drive any settlement leverage. It costs little to include and offers a small, marginal hedge against a procedural collapse of the contract count.

If filing room is at a premium or the family is concerned about Rule 11 / litigation-cost discipline, **A4 can be omitted entirely** without materially weakening the case. The decision to include it is closer to "cheap insurance" than "value-add."

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No — and this is one of the few structural advantages A4 has over A1.** The unjust enrichment count, by its nature, sits outside the appraisal framework entirely. It does not adopt the appraisal award, it does not seek to enforce it, and it does not seek to vacate, modify, or correct it. The appraisal is, at most, factual evidence supporting "knowing receipt" (Farmers' own panel admits hail damage and a $0 net payment) and "unjust retention" (the appraisal output does not reflect the broader photographed damage). The legal theory itself is independent of whether the award stands.

### 4.2 Why the appraisal is structurally orthogonal to A4

The appraisal procedure under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 is a contract-implementation mechanism — it resolves "amount of loss" disputes within the framework of an enforceable insurance contract. The award, once entered and signed by two of three panel members, is binding on the parties **as to the contract claim**.

Unjust enrichment is not a contract claim. It is an equitable claim that, by definition, presupposes either (a) the absence of an enforceable contract on the disputed subject, (b) the contract's silence or ambiguity on the disputed subject, or (c) the recharacterization of certain transfers as falling outside what the contract covers. Whichever doorway A4 walks through under §1.5 above, the appraisal's binding effect on the contract dispute does not directly reach it.

Three specific framings make this concrete:

**Framing 1 — Premium-for-coverage restitution.** If A4 is pled as "Farmers received premium dollars in consideration of windstorm/hail coverage and has refused to deliver the bargained-for performance, so equity requires restitution of the hail-coverage portion of premium," the appraisal award is irrelevant to that theory. The award did not consider, decide, or speak to the value of premium paid.

**Framing 2 — Unpaid-covered-loss restitution beyond the awarded scope.** If A4 is pled as "Farmers has retained value (in the form of unpaid covered loss) for windows the appraisal panel did not evaluate," the appraisal award is supportive (it confirms hail causation generally) but is not the operative legal grounding. The court can reach the unjust-retention question for unevaluated windows without ever touching the award's $12,800 / $0-net finding for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows.

**Framing 3 — Strict alternative-pleading hedge.** If A4 is pled solely as a Rule 8.05(2) alternative to A1 in case the contract claim is somehow procedurally derailed (e.g., by Farmers' "settled per the binding appraisal" position being credited as a complete bar), then the relationship to the appraisal is purely defensive. The appraisal is in fact the contingency that makes A4 useful.

### 4.3 The appraisal does, however, weaken A4 in at least two ways

- **The award is itself an exercise of the contract.** The mere fact that the parties used the policy's contractual appraisal procedure to resolve a portion of the dispute is some evidence that "the rights of the parties are governed by a valid contract" — the *U.S. Fire Ins. / Holman* hurdle. Farmers can point to the appraisal as proof the contract framework controls.
- **The award's $0 net payment after deductible cuts against "unjust retention."** Farmers can argue that even on the panel's own findings, no money was owed once the $17,720 deductible was applied, so there is no "value" being unjustly retained beyond what the contract entitles Farmers to keep. This is a real argument the family will need to address in any A4 briefing — the answer being that the alleged unjust retention reaches beyond the awarded windows, not just beyond the deductible math on the awarded scope.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- A4 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be filed, to survive Rule 12, or to reach the merits.
- A4 is in fact **most useful precisely when the appraisal award is left undisturbed** but is read narrowly enough that some portion of the loss remains uncompensated.
- The structural independence is real, but largely cosmetic: the same equitable concerns that motivate A4 are addressed more powerfully and more legitimately by A1 (Tracks 1 and 2 in `A1.md` §4.2) and A3. A4's appraisal-independence therefore does not by itself elevate the claim's merit; it simply means A4 does not add to the case's appraisal-related complexity.

## 6. Open Items and Next Steps Specific to A4

1. **Decision: include or omit A4.** This is a strategic choice for the lead attorney. The case for inclusion is "cheap belt-and-suspenders alternative under Rule 8.05(2)." The case for omission is "minimal added value, modest sanctions exposure, possible distraction from stronger counts." A neutral default is to include with a one- or two-paragraph alternative-pleading recital, and drop at the first summary-judgment opportunity.
2. **Premium ledger** for 2014–2024 to quantify the value Farmers received in consideration of windstorm/hail coverage. Useful both for damages quantification under any A4 framing and for the bad-faith narrative.
3. **Confirm Minnesota's most recent appellate guidance** on alternative pleading of unjust enrichment alongside breach in first-party insurance disputes specifically. The state's general Rule 8.05(2) tolerance for alternative theories is well-established; the application of *U.S. Fire Ins. / Holman / ServiceMaster* in the first-party property-insurance context post-*Quade* is worth a focused Westlaw search.
4. **If Farmers asserts in any answer or motion that the appraisal award fully discharged its contract obligations**, A4's relevance increases materially. Track Farmers' position carefully in pre-suit and pleading-stage correspondence.
5. **Consider whether the unpaid 2020 recoverable depreciation** for the never-completed 2 sashes and the exterior door (per `EventDocuments/FinalDocument_2020.pdf` "Work Not Completed") gives A4 a cleaner factual narrative for a small restitution recovery — Farmers issued recoverable-depreciation funds (2/18/2022) but the work was never completed because Farmers denied the COVID extension. This is a discrete, well-documented piece of "value retained" that does not require disturbing the 2024 hail claim or the 2025 appraisal.
