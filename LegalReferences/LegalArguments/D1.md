## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 Punitive damages in Minnesota are a *remedy*, not a freestanding cause of action

Minn. Stat. § 549.20 does not create an independent claim. It is a damages-augmentation remedy available where (a) the plaintiff has already established liability on some underlying cause of action that supports punitive damages as a matter of substantive law, and (b) the plaintiff carries the heightened punitive-damages standard by clear and convincing evidence. The complaint cannot be filed with a "Count for Punitive Damages." Section 549.20 must always be hooked onto another count — typically a tort. The procedural mechanics for adding the request are governed by Minn. Stat. § 549.191. This dual structure — substantive standard in § 549.20, procedural gate in § 549.191 — is the framework that controls D1.

### 1.2 The substantive standard — § 549.20, Subd. 1

Subdivision 1(a) of § 549.20 states the gating rule:

> "Punitive damages shall be allowed in civil actions only upon clear and convincing evidence that the acts of the defendant show deliberate disregard for the rights or safety of others."

Subdivision 1(b) defines "deliberate disregard" with two operative components:

> "A defendant has acted with deliberate disregard for the rights or safety of others if the defendant has knowledge of facts or intentionally disregards facts that create a high probability of injury to the rights or safety of others and: (1) deliberately proceeds to act in conscious or intentional disregard of the high degree of probability of injury to the rights or safety of others; or (2) deliberately proceeds to act with indifference to the high probability of injury to the rights or safety of others."

Two features of this language are central to the analysis. First, the burden is **clear and convincing evidence** — meaningfully higher than the preponderance-of-the-evidence standard that governs § 604.18 and the common-law tort claims. Second, the test demands a showing of **knowledge or intentional disregard of facts**, *plus* a deliberate decision to proceed. Mere negligence — even gross negligence — is insufficient. The defendant must have known or intentionally avoided knowing the facts that created the high probability of injury and must have proceeded anyway, either with conscious disregard or with indifference.

The phrase "rights *or* safety" is disjunctive. Minnesota courts have applied § 549.20 to property and economic rights as well as to physical safety, but the further the conduct strays from physical-safety harm, the more carefully courts scrutinize whether the "high probability of injury to the rights" requirement is met. First-party insurance disputes typically involve economic injury rather than physical-safety injury; D1 must therefore frame the harm as a deliberate disregard of the family's contractual and statutory **rights** under Minnesota's insurance scheme.

### 1.3 The factors — § 549.20, Subd. 3

When punitive damages are awarded, Subd. 3 governs the amount. The trier of fact must measure the award against:

- the seriousness of hazard to the public arising from the defendant's misconduct;
- the profitability of the misconduct to the defendant;
- the duration of the misconduct and any concealment of it;
- the degree of the defendant's awareness of the hazard and of its excessiveness;
- the attitude and conduct of the defendant upon discovery of the misconduct;
- the number and level of employees involved in causing or concealing the misconduct;
- the financial condition of the defendant; and
- the total effect of other punishment likely to be imposed upon the defendant as a result of the misconduct, including compensatory and punitive damage awards to the plaintiff and other similarly situated persons, and the severity of any criminal penalty to which the defendant may be subject.

These factors do double duty. They govern quantum at trial *and* they shape the factual story a complaint should tell. A § 549.191 motion that maps cleanly onto the factor list — duration, supervisor-level involvement, public-hazard scale, profitability, post-discovery attitude — is a stronger motion than one that asserts a generalized "bad faith" pattern.

### 1.4 The bifurcated trial — § 549.20, Subd. 4

If requested by any party, the trier of fact "shall" first determine compensatory damages and then, "in a separate proceeding, determine whether and in what amount punitive damages will be awarded." Subd. 4. This bifurcation matters for trial preparation. Evidence of the defendant's financial condition is admissible only in the punitive-damages phase, and the punitive-damages-specific evidence is presented after liability and compensatory damages have been resolved. The bifurcation does not, however, change the pleading or motion-stage analysis.

### 1.5 The procedural gate — § 549.191

Section 549.191 controls how a punitive-damages request enters the case. It states in relevant part:

> "Upon commencement of a civil action, the complaint must not seek punitive damages. After filing the suit a party may make a motion to amend the pleadings to claim punitive damages. The motion must allege the applicable legal basis under section 549.20 or other law for awarding punitive damages in the action and must be accompanied by one or more affidavits showing the factual basis for the claim. At the hearing on the motion, if the court finds prima facie evidence in support of the motion, the court shall grant the moving party permission to amend the pleadings to claim punitive damages. For purposes of tolling the statute of limitations, pleadings amended under this section relate back to the time the action was commenced."

Three operational consequences:

1. **The initial complaint cannot include punitive damages.** A draft complaint that pleads "Plaintiffs seek punitive damages under Minn. Stat. § 549.20" in the prayer for relief is procedurally improper and will be struck.
2. **The standard at the motion stage is *prima facie evidence*, not clear and convincing.** This is meaningfully lower than the trial standard. The court does not weigh competing evidence at the prima facie hearing; it asks only whether the movant has made a threshold showing that, if uncontradicted, would support the deliberate-disregard finding. *See Swanlund v. Shimano Indus. Corp.*, 459 N.W.2d 151, 154 (Minn. Ct. App. 1990) (prima facie standard under predecessor statute does not permit weighing of evidence; movant entitled to amend if affidavits, taken as true, support the claim).
3. **Relation-back is automatic.** A timely-filed underlying complaint preserves the punitive-damages request for purposes of the statute of limitations. The 7/13/2026 contractual suit limitation therefore does not independently cut off D1 so long as the underlying counts are timely filed.

### 1.6 The "independent tort" requirement — punitive damages in contract

Minnesota law generally bars punitive damages in actions sounding in contract unless the breach is accompanied by an **independent tort**. *Wild v. Rarig*, 302 Minn. 419, 442, 234 N.W.2d 775, 789–90 (1975) ("an action arising out of a breach of contract does not become a tort action because the breach was motivated by malicious or other bad-faith motive"). This principle applies to insurance-contract disputes: a plaintiff who pleads only breach of contract (A1) cannot piggyback a § 549.20 request onto that count.

The corollary is that **D1's viability depends on the survival of an underlying tort claim**. On the present pleading plan, the candidate carrying claims are:

- **B2 — Common-law fraud and/or negligent misrepresentation.** This is the cleanest carrier. Fraud is a long-standing intentional tort that supports punitive damages where the deliberate-disregard standard is met. Section 549.20 routinely runs alongside successful fraud counts.
- **B1 — Bad faith under Minn. Stat. § 604.18.** *Cannot* carry punitive damages. Section 604.18, Subd. 3(b), states expressly: "An insured may not recover punitive or exemplary damages or attorney fees under section 8.31 for a violation of this section." This is a categorical statutory bar — § 604.18 is a fee-shifting / taxable-costs remedy, not a punitive-damages predicate.
- **A1 — Breach of contract.** *Cannot* carry punitive damages standing alone. *Wild v. Rarig*.
- **A3 — Action to vacate, modify, or correct the appraisal award.** *Cannot* carry punitive damages. A3 is an equitable / statutory action seeking modification of an arbitral-style award; it does not produce tort damages.

This is the doctrinal bottleneck. D1 is functionally a remedy that travels with B2. If B2 survives a Rule 12 motion and the deliberate-disregard standard is met after discovery, D1 becomes available; if B2 fails or is omitted from the complaint, D1 cannot be reached.

### 1.7 The statutory exclusion in § 604.18 does *not* bar § 549.20 generally

Section 604.18, Subd. 3(b) says only that an insured cannot recover punitive damages **under § 604.18 itself**. It does not bar a § 549.20 request anchored to a different cause of action. The Bench & Bar commentary on § 604.18 has consistently noted this distinction: the statute substitutes a capped fee-shifting remedy *for the bad-faith claim*, but it leaves untouched any independent tort theory that might support punitive damages by its own terms. *See* Mike Bryant et al., *Is Minnesota's bad-faith insurance law working? Not so much*, Bench & Bar of Minnesota (discussing § 604.18's interaction with traditional remedies). The doctrinal map is therefore:

- B1 (§ 604.18) → no punitive damages, by statutory command.
- B2 (fraud / negligent misrepresentation) → punitive damages available, by post-pleading § 549.191 motion under § 549.20.
- A1 / A3 / A4 (contract / declaratory / vacatur / unjust enrichment) → no punitive damages, by *Wild v. Rarig* and the nature of the remedy.

### 1.8 The appraisal bar in § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) does *not* apply to § 549.20

Section 604.18, Subd. 4(b) states: "An award of taxable costs under this section is not available in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal." On its face, this bar attaches to the § 604.18 remedy. Nothing in § 549.20 or § 549.191 incorporates the appraisal bar. This is a meaningful structural advantage for D1 over B1: where the appraisal bar threatens to extinguish the § 604.18 taxable-costs remedy outright (see B1.md § 4), it leaves the § 549.20 punitive-damages request untouched on its own terms. D1's appraisal exposure runs through the carrying tort claim's exposure to the award, not through any § 549.20-specific bar.

### 1.9 Insurance against punitive damages

Minnesota law generally disfavors insurance against direct liability for punitive damages on public-policy grounds, while Minn. Stat. § 60A.06, Subd. 4 expressly authorizes coverage for *vicarious* liability for punitive damages. The combined effect is that an insurer's own direct punitive exposure is generally treated as uninsurable, though the doctrine has not been applied uniformly and the precise scope should be confirmed by counsel before filing. The practical relevance for D1 is that, to whatever extent the Minnesota uninsurability rule applies to a punitive judgment against Fire Insurance Exchange itself, a § 549.20 award becomes a real-dollar exposure FIE cannot offload — a settlement-leverage point distinct from the contract-damages claim, which can be reinsured or otherwise risk-transferred.

### 1.10 Constitutional ceiling — *State Farm v. Campbell*

Even if a Minnesota jury makes a § 549.20 award, the U.S. Supreme Court has established a substantive due-process ceiling on punitive-to-compensatory ratios. *State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Campbell*, 538 U.S. 408, 425 (2003) ("few awards exceeding a single-digit ratio between punitive and compensatory damages, to a significant degree, will satisfy due process"). On this case's facts, the appraisal-confirmed compensable hail loss is currently $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV, with the family's broader scope claim plausibly reaching the high five figures or low six figures if A1 / A3 succeed. A *Campbell*-compliant punitive award would therefore likely run between roughly 1× and 9× the eventual compensatory recovery — so the realistic D1 dollar exposure for Farmers is bounded below the scale that justifies large litigation budgets, and bounded above by the family's ability to win compensatory damages on B2.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **15 / 100** as part of an initial claim filed today; **30 / 100** conditional on B2 surviving summary judgment

D1 cannot be in the initial complaint as a matter of procedure (§ 549.191) and cannot be granted unless an underlying tort survives summary judgment. The 15/100 rating reflects D1's strength measured at the point of filing — when it is structurally invisible to the pleading, when no Prong 2-style internal Farmers documentation has been obtained, and when the case's litigation arc has not yet established whether B2 will hold up. The 30/100 rating reflects D1 if (a) B2 survives a Rule 12 motion and discovery, (b) the family identifies clear-and-convincing evidence of supervisor-level deliberate disregard, and (c) the § 604.18 motion under B1 is granted. None of those conditions is yet satisfied.

- **Substantive standard fit (clear and convincing of "deliberate disregard")**: 35/100. The factual record contains specific supervisor-level conduct that pattern-matches to deliberate-disregard analyses, but the clear-and-convincing burden is materially higher than the preponderance burden for B2's underlying scienter element, and most of the strongest material is internal Farmers documentation that has not yet been obtained.
- **Procedural fit as initial claim**: 0/100. § 549.191 forecloses inclusion in the initial complaint. D1 is structurally a post-discovery motion.
- **Carrying-claim viability**: 25/100. B2's overall strength is rated ~25/100 in `B2.md`, principally because the reliance element is fragile. D1 cannot exceed B2's strength because D1 travels with B2.
- **Independence from the appraisal bar**: 70/100. § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) does not apply. The award itself does not extinguish § 549.20.
- **Quantum exposure under *Campbell***: 30/100. With compensatory damages plausibly in the low five-figure to low six-figure range, the constitutional ceiling caps punitive exposure at a level that is meaningful but not transformative for the family's economic outcome.
- **Settlement leverage value**: 50/100. The mere existence of a non-frivolous § 549.191 motion in reserve is worth something at the negotiating table — punitive exposure is uninsurable under § 60A.07 — but it is not the kind of leverage that drives early settlement.

Weighted blend: ~**15/100** at filing; **~30/100** if B2 survives.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

**The clear-and-convincing burden is the dominant drag.** The same factual record that supports the § 604.18 "reckless disregard" prong by a preponderance of the evidence may not satisfy § 549.20's "deliberate disregard" by clear and convincing evidence. Deliberate disregard requires that Farmers had **knowledge of facts or intentionally disregarded facts** creating a **high probability of injury** to the family's rights *and* deliberately proceeded with conscious disregard or indifference. Each element must be shown with clear and convincing evidence. On the present record, the family can document the conduct (premature denial, false weather premise, "engineer's report only" rule, missing inspection notes, refusal to engage with disproving evidence), but the clear-and-convincing showing of subjective knowledge — what Farmers' adjusters and supervisors actually knew, and what they actually decided — is largely a discovery proposition.

**The carrying-claim problem.** D1 cannot survive without B2 (or some other independent tort). B2 is itself rated ~25/100 (see `B2.md`) because reliance is fragile and Minnesota courts are skeptical of fraud counts grafted onto coverage disputes. If B2 falls out at summary judgment, D1 falls with it. There is no realistic path to D1 if B2 fails — A1, A3, A4, B1, and the § 72A.201-related claims either cannot carry punitive damages (A1, A3, A4, B1) or have weaker independent-cause-of-action prospects (C-series).

**The "rights or safety" framing problem.** Section 549.20's protected interest is "rights or safety." First-party insurance disputes are economic-injury cases. Minnesota courts have applied § 549.20 to economic harm, but the "high probability of injury to the rights or safety of others" inquiry is most comfortable where the conduct exposes the public or specific persons to physical danger or to a systemic violation of legal rights. The family's harm is the wrongful denial of a contractual benefit and the secondary water damage from continued unrepaired windows (10/28/2025 leaks), not a physical-safety hazard. A motion to amend will need to frame the harm in "rights" terms — deliberate disregard of the policyholder's contractual and statutory rights as an insured under Minnesota's regulated insurance scheme — rather than in "safety" terms.

**Lack of a publicly-litigated punitive award against an insurer for first-party hail-claim handling in Minnesota.** *Selective Ins. Co. of S.C. v. Sela* — the leading § 604.18 authority — produced a $214,394.50 taxable-costs award; it did not adjudicate punitive damages under § 549.20. Minnesota appellate authority on § 549.20 in the first-party insurance context is sparse and skeptical. Out-of-state authority — including the *Burgess v. Farmers* $130M Oklahoma verdict — is suggestive but not binding. The doctrinal landscape does not favor first-party insurance punitive damages in Minnesota.

**Quantum is bounded by *Campbell*.** Even a successful § 549.191 motion that proceeds to a punitive verdict will be capped by the *State Farm v. Campbell* due-process ceiling at a single-digit ratio of punitive to compensatory. With compensatory exposure plausibly in the low five-figure to low six-figure range on the windows scope alone, the punitive ceiling is in the same order of magnitude — meaningful, but not transformative for the family's recovery.

**The insured's-appraiser problem complicates the "deliberate disregard" framing.** The 9/12/2025 appraisal award was signed by John Foster, the family's appraiser. A jury or court evaluating "deliberate disregard for the rights" of the insured can reasonably ask why the family's own representative endorsed the scope that produced the below-deductible outcome. This is the same drag that affects B1 and bleeds into D1 directly.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up

**The factual record contains discrete, dated, supervisor-level conduct that pattern-matches to § 549.20 factors.** Subdivision 3 directs the trier of fact to weigh the duration of the misconduct, the number and level of employees involved, the defendant's awareness, and the post-discovery attitude. On this record:

- Duration: roughly 14 months from the 9/3/2024 first denial through the 10/29/2025 refusal to release Brau's notes — sustained, not episodic.
- Employee involvement: at least four named Farmers personnel — Catastrophe Claims Rep Christian Dudley (9/3/2024 first denial), Claims Spec Rep Nic Brau (handling adjuster, 9/6/2024 inspection without report, 11/11/2024 refusal to meet, 2/10/2025 voicemail enforcing the "engineer's report only" rule, 10/29/2025 refusal to release notes), Field Claim Supervisor Bradlee Monson (2/3/2025 articulation of the "engineer's report only" rule), and Customer Relations rep Bradlee Monson (overlapping). The Monson involvement is supervisor-level — the higher-level employee involvement § 549.20 factor.
- Awareness: Farmers had on the record the family's stormersite data (1/31/2025), the City of Eden Prairie permit data and 14-of-19 neighbor canvass (2/10/2025), the NWS Twin Cities/Chanhassen storm summary uploaded by Farmers' own agent (5/28/2025), and ultimately Farmers' own appraisal panel's hail-causation finding (9/12/2025). Each of these is documented receipt; none generated a substantive Farmers response.
- Post-discovery attitude: the 10/21/2025 reply directing the family to their own appraiser, the 10/29/2025 personal refusal by Brau to release notes, and the inconsistent 9/18 / 9/19 / 10/14/2025 descriptions of which windows the award covers all post-date the appraisal — i.e., they are conduct after Farmers' own panel had confirmed hail causation.

Each of these factor-aligned facts is documented in the project's primary record. They are not Prong 2-style internal-document inferences; they are public-facing, externally-documented Farmers conduct.

**The "engineer's report only" rule is institutional-level evidence.** Field Claim Supervisor Bradlee Monson articulated the rule by phone on 2/3/2025; adjuster Brau enforced and confirmed the rule by voicemail on 2/10/2025. This is the exact pattern § 549.20, Subd. 3 asks about: a supervisor-level decision applied through subordinates to deny consideration of evidence the policy itself does not exclude. A factual showing that a supervisor instituted an off-policy evidentiary gate to deny review of materially relevant information is the strongest single deliberate-disregard hook on this record.

**Farmers' own later appraisal award disproves the denial's central factual premise.** Prieve's "no hail in Eden Prairie after 2020" assertion was foundational to the 11/4/2024 denial. The 9/12/2025 panel — including the umpire — found hail as the cause of loss on additional windows beyond the 2020 scope. This is a self-disproving rebuttal: Farmers' own subsequently-procured fact-finder rejected Farmers' own denial premise. For the deliberate-disregard inquiry, this matters because Farmers continued to maintain the denial throughout 2025 even after the family had supplied each piece of disproving evidence — the kind of persistence that supports an "indifference to the high probability of injury to the rights" finding.

**The missing 9/6/2024 Brau inspection record supports a deliberate-concealment inference.** § 549.20, Subd. 3 expressly weights "the duration of the misconduct and any concealment of it." Farmers' 10/10/2025 statement that "the file does not contain a report" from Brau's 9/6/2024 personal inspection — combined with Brau's 10/29/2025 personal refusal to release notes — supports either a non-creation inference (no contemporaneous documentation of an inspection that found dents) or a non-preservation inference (notes existed and were not preserved or not produced). Either inference fits the concealment factor.

**The appraisal-bar advantage.** Whatever the eventual outcome of A1 / A3, the § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar that drags B1's remedy down does not attach to D1. D1 is the cleanest non-§ 604.18-barred punitive vehicle on the record. If the case develops in a way that B1's taxable-costs remedy is foreclosed under Subd. 4(b) (see B1.md § 4), D1 inherits the role of the principal supplemental-damages route — though only so long as B2 survives.

**Settlement leverage and uninsurability.** Minnesota generally treats direct punitive-damages liability as uninsurable on public-policy grounds (see § 1.9 above; the vicarious carve-out under § 60A.06, Subd. 4 does not help an insurer facing a punitive award against itself). A non-frivolous punitive-damages motion under § 549.191 is therefore a settlement-leverage point that an insurer cannot easily offload to a reinsurer or excess carrier. Even a moderate-probability punitive request raises Farmers' real-dollar exposure in a way the contract claim does not.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal Award?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No, on the § 549.20 / § 549.191 mechanics directly. But yes, indirectly, through the carrying tort claim's exposure to the award.** D1 is structurally independent of the appraisal in a way B1 is not — § 604.18 Subd. 4(b)'s appraisal bar does not apply to § 549.20. The dependence runs through B2 (the carrying claim), which on its own analysis (`B2.md` § 4) is *strengthened*, not weakened, when the award stands. D1 is therefore the cleanest appraisal-independent supplemental-damages route on the record.

### 4.2 § 549.20 / § 549.191 do not incorporate the appraisal bar

Section 604.18, Subd. 4(b) bars the **§ 604.18 taxable-costs remedy** "in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by … appraisal." That bar is anchored to § 604.18's specific remedy and does not, by its terms, reach any other remedy. Sections 549.20 and 549.191 do not contain an analogous bar. There is no statutory mechanism by which an appraisal under § 65A.01 would extinguish a § 549.20 punitive-damages request hooked to a separate cause of action.

This is a meaningful structural advantage. Where the B1 analysis must contend with the live possibility that Subd. 4(b) eliminates the § 604.18 remedy outright (B1.md § 4.2), the D1 analysis does not face that question. The award's existence does not, by itself, foreclose D1.

### 4.3 The carrying claim's appraisal exposure flows through to D1

D1 cannot stand alone. It must travel with an underlying tort. The candidate carriers and their appraisal exposure:

- **B2 (fraud / negligent misrepresentation).** Per `B2.md` § 4, B2 is structurally independent of the appraisal award. The appraisal under § 65A.01 resolves the contractual "amount of loss" dispute; it does not adjudicate tort liability. *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012) — appraisers determine "amount of loss," which includes causation of loss, but they do not adjudicate fraud, scienter, reliance, or punitive-damages elements. The award's hail-causation finding affirmatively *supports* the falsity element of B2 (Prieve's "no hail after 2020" representation is disproven by Farmers' own panel). B2 therefore proceeds regardless of whether the award is upheld, modified, vacated, or left untouched.
- **B1 (§ 604.18).** Cannot carry punitive damages by statutory command. § 604.18, Subd. 3(b). Even if B1 itself survives the Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar, it cannot serve as D1's hook.
- **A1 (breach of contract).** Cannot carry punitive damages absent independent tort. *Wild v. Rarig*. The award's effect on A1 is irrelevant to D1.
- **A3 (vacatur / modification).** An equitable / statutory action to modify the award. Does not carry punitive damages.

The dependency is therefore exactly the dependency in `B2.md` § 4: D1 inherits whatever B2 inherits with respect to the award. Because B2 is structurally independent of the award, D1 is structurally independent of the award. D1's existence does not require the award to be vacated, modified, or corrected.

### 4.4 The award's *factual content* is affirmative evidence for D1

A subtle but important point: while D1 does not require disturbing the award, the award's hail-causation finding is *evidence* in D1's favor. The 9/12/2025 panel — including the umpire — confirmed hail as the cause of loss. That finding is affirmatively useful for the "knowledge of facts or intentionally disregards facts" prong of § 549.20 because:

- It demonstrates that hail causation *did* exist on the property.
- It came from Farmers' own subsequently-procured fact-finder.
- It directly contradicts the foundational factual premise of the 11/4/2024 denial.
- The contradiction is on the record before any litigation activity.

For D1's deliberate-disregard showing, this means: Farmers maintained a denial after the family supplied disproving evidence, and Farmers' own later panel agreed with the family on causation. The pattern fits the "intentionally disregards facts that create a high probability of injury to the rights" element by clear and convincing evidence.

The complaint's D1-related allegations should therefore expressly cite the 9/12/2025 award as evidence that Farmers' denial premise was wrong — without arguing that the award itself should be set aside. The award's *content* helps D1; disturbing the award is not required for D1.

### 4.5 The remedial arithmetic remains constrained by the carrying claim's compensatory recovery

Even with D1 surviving the appraisal-bar question, the *Campbell* due-process ceiling caps the realistic punitive recovery at a single-digit ratio of punitive to compensatory. The compensatory recovery in turn flows from B2's reliance-damages framework (`B2.md` § 3) — out-of-pocket appraisal costs, opportunity costs of evidence development, ongoing water damage from the 10/28/2025 leaks, and potentially the contract recovery if B2's reliance damages are calibrated to the policy proceeds the family did not obtain. The size of D1 is therefore tied to B2's damages exposition, not to the award's status.

If the A1 / A3 scope attack succeeds and the family ultimately recovers tens of thousands of dollars in compensatory damages (whether through contract or through B2 reliance), the *Campbell*-compliant punitive ceiling is in the same order of magnitude. If A1 / A3 fail and the family recovers little or nothing in compensatory damages, the punitive ceiling collapses with it.

### 4.6 Bottom line on the key question

- The **liability** inquiry under § 549.20 does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting the appraisal award. The deliberate-disregard predicates are claim-handling conduct, not award-content conduct.
- The **remedy** inquiry under § 549.20 is tied to the **carrying tort claim's** survival, not to the award. § 604.18 Subd. 4(b)'s appraisal bar does not attach to § 549.20.
- B2's analysis (`B2.md` § 4) confirms B2 is structurally independent of the award. D1 inherits that independence.
- The award's *factual content* — hail causation confirmed — is affirmative evidence in D1's favor. The award is more useful to D1 standing than fallen.
- D1 therefore cannot be in the initial complaint (procedural), travels with B2 (substantive), and is the cleanest appraisal-independent supplemental-damages vehicle on the catalog (structural).

## 6. Open Items and Next Steps Specific to D1

1. **Do not include D1 in the initial complaint.** Section 549.191 forecloses it. Preserve D1 for a post-filing motion under § 549.191.
2. **Plead B2 in the initial complaint as the carrying claim for D1.** D1 cannot reach the case without B2 (or some other independent tort) surviving Rule 12 and progressing toward summary judgment. The B2 analysis in `B2.md` already supports this framing.
3. **Draft the § 549.191 affidavits in parallel with the complaint.** The factual content is largely in `CaseContext/key-facts-excluding-legal-context.md` and `ClaimTimeline.md`. Affidavits from Yibiao Lu and Jie Hu authenticating the correspondence, the voicemail, the Brau-related communications, and the appraisal-related documents can be prepared now and finalized once discovery completes.
4. **Structure discovery to maximize the clear-and-convincing showing.** Priority items: (a) internal Farmers communications regarding the 9/3/2024 denial decision, the retention of Prieve, and the "engineer's report only" rule; (b) Brau's 9/6/2024 inspection notes (or a sworn confirmation of non-creation); (c) Prieve's underlying weather-research file and contemporaneous notes; (d) any Farmers training or policy material on claim-handling evidentiary standards; (e) Hancock workproduct beyond the photo set; (f) Tomes's working papers; (g) financial-condition discovery for the bifurcated punitive phase. The first round of requests should be tailored to produce documents that, taken together, would satisfy the prima facie standard at the § 549.191 hearing.
5. **File the § 549.191 motion only after discovery yields the corroborating internal documentation.** A premature § 549.191 motion that relies entirely on the family's external observations risks an early adverse ruling that hardens the doctrinal posture against the family. The motion should be timed to follow the production that converts the "intentionally disregards facts" inference from inferential proof into clear-and-convincing-grade documentary proof.
6. **Frame the deliberate-disregard inquiry around "rights," not "safety."** The harm here is economic deprivation and progressive water damage to the home — rights-based, not safety-based. The motion's drafting should anchor the "rights" prong of § 549.20 in (a) the family's contractual right to indemnification, (b) the family's statutory rights under § 72A.201 and § 65A.01, and (c) the deliberate disregard of those rights through the supervisor-level "engineer's report only" rule.
7. **Map the motion onto the Subd. 3 factor list.** Each factor should have at least one record-cite supporting it (see § 3.3 of this document). A motion that walks the court through the factor list with primary-source citations is more durable than one that asserts a generalized bad-faith pattern.
8. **Coordinate D1 with B1 and B2.** The same factual record supports all three — the differences are which standard applies and which remedy attaches. The discovery and affidavit work should be designed to support all three motions simultaneously rather than three separate factual builds.
9. **Preserve the *Campbell* analysis for opposition and trial.** Farmers will raise the constitutional ceiling at the punitive phase; the family's response should be ready and tied to whatever compensatory recovery is actually achieved.
10. **Treat D1's primary value as settlement leverage.** Even at 15/100 / 30/100, the prospect of an uninsurable punitive award against a regulated insurer is a settlement-leverage point that compensatory-only litigation does not provide. The motion's mere viability — independent of its eventual outcome — affects Farmers' settlement calculus.
