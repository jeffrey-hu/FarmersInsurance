## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 Minnesota does not recognize an independent tort of negligent claims handling against a first-party insurer

The threshold legal fact for B3 is unambiguous and unfavorable: Minnesota does not recognize a freestanding common-law tort that allows a first-party insured to sue its insurer for negligent (or even bad-faith) handling of the claim. The only viable path through the courts for the conduct B3 targets is the statutory remedy at Minn. Stat. § 604.18 (covered separately in B1).

The Minnesota Supreme Court drew the line in *Haagenson v. National Farmers Union Property & Casualty Co.*, 277 N.W.2d 648, 652 (Minn. 1979), holding that an insurer's "failure to pay an insurance claim, in itself, no matter how malicious, does not constitute a tort; it constitutes a breach of an insurance contract." The Court reaffirmed that holding in *Short v. Dairyland Insurance Co.*, 334 N.W.2d 384, 387–88 (Minn. 1983) — the case that recognized a narrow third-party duty in the excess-judgment context but explicitly preserved the contract-only treatment of first-party disputes. The Court of Appeals applied the same rule to bar tort recovery in *Pillsbury Co. v. National Union Fire Insurance Co.*, 425 N.W.2d 244, 249 (Minn. Ct. App. 1988): "A bad faith breach of contract does not convert the breach of contract into a tort, and consequently, a first party insured cannot recover punitive damages in a breach of contract action against her insurer in the absence of some independent tort." A long line of subsequent Minnesota and federal-applying-Minnesota decisions has applied the same rule.

The legislature responded to this gap in 2008 by enacting Minn. Stat. § 604.18, which creates a statutory cause of action and remedy specifically for first-party bad-faith claim denial. The statute is the legislature's chosen — and exclusive — vehicle for the conduct B3 targets. There has been no Minnesota appellate case in the post-§ 604.18 era recognizing a parallel common-law negligence tort for first-party claims handling, and the existence of § 604.18 itself is now used by Minnesota courts as a reason not to extend the common law to do the same work.

### 1.2 Why "negligence" framing does not fix the problem

A plaintiff sometimes attempts to evade the contract-only rule by relabeling the same conduct as "negligent claims handling" rather than "bad-faith refusal to pay." Minnesota courts have not accepted that maneuver in the first-party context. Three reasons:

First, the duty being asserted (to investigate fairly, to weigh evidence, to communicate accurately, to respect the insured's interests) is the duty of good faith and fair dealing implied in every contract. That duty is contractual; its breach sounds in contract, not tort. *See Wild v. Rarig*, 302 Minn. 419, 234 N.W.2d 775 (1975) (contract-based duties do not become tort duties merely because the conduct is "wrongful"). The "independent duty" rule that Minnesota uses to police the contract/tort line — best summarized in *Hanks v. Hubbard Broad., Inc.*, 493 N.W.2d 302 (Minn. Ct. App. 1992) — requires a duty arising independently of the contract. The duty to handle a claim arises only because of the insurance contract. There is nothing independent.

Second, recharacterizing a § 604.18-targeted theory as common-law negligence raises a serious preemption / supersession problem. Minnesota law disfavors common-law actions that duplicate or evade a statutory scheme the legislature enacted to occupy the same field. The legislature considered first-party insurance claim conduct, calibrated a remedy with caps ($250,000 on bad-faith taxable amounts, $100,000 on attorneys' fees), set procedural prerequisites (claim added by motion under § 604.18 Subd. 4 with affidavit support), and chose not to authorize a parallel tort. A common-law negligent claims handling theory would functionally read those legislative choices out of the statute.

Third, the "professional negligence" subspecies of negligence is also closed. Minnesota does not treat insurance adjusters as professionals owing a tort-grade duty of care to first-party insureds, and there is no licensing or fiduciary framework that would supply one. A "negligent investigation" theory styled like a malpractice claim against the adjuster fails for the same reasons the broader negligent claims handling theory fails.

### 1.3 The narrow remaining doorways (and why none of them are real on this record)

For completeness, the following are the only marginal openings under Minnesota law for any negligence-style first-party tort against an insurer. None is a viable B3 path on this record.

**(a) Independent-tort-arising-out-of-the-insurance-relationship doctrine.** *Pillsbury* and *Short* both leave open the possibility of "some independent tort" being available in a first-party insurance context — for example, fraud (B2), trespass, conversion of insured property, or intentional infliction of emotional distress under extreme facts. But these are not "negligent claims handling." They are separate, named torts with their own elements. If the family wants to plead common-law fraud or negligent misrepresentation based on Prieve's "no hail in Eden Prairie after 2020" assertion, that lives in B2, not here.

**(b) Special-relationship / fiduciary-duty extension.** In *Short*, the Minnesota Supreme Court recognized a quasi-fiduciary duty in the third-party-liability context where the insurer controls settlement and the insured is exposed to an excess judgment. That duty has not been extended to first-party property claims, and the conflict-of-interest predicate (insurer controls litigation; insured exposed to excess) is absent here. Nothing in the family's record supports a plausible argument that the policy or the parties' conduct created a fiduciary duty above the contract.

**(c) Statutory negligence per se via Minn. Stat. § 72A.20 / § 72A.201.** Some plaintiffs frame an unfair-claims-practices act violation as the predicate for common-law negligence per se. The Minnesota appellate guidance on private enforcement of § 72A.20 is unfavorable (those provisions historically have been enforced by the Commissioner, not by private action), and the negligence-per-se framing has not gained traction in Minnesota first-party cases. This is more responsibly pursued, if at all, as a § 604.18 predicate (treating § 72A.201's standards as the benchmark for "reasonable basis") rather than as a standalone negligence tort. *See* C1 in `Possible_Arguments_List.md`.

**(d) Future doctrinal change.** A creative plaintiff could ask the Minnesota Supreme Court to recognize a new common-law tort of negligent claims handling. The Court has been asked many times since 1979 and has consistently declined. There is no reason to believe a Hennepin County trial court would create a new tort that the Supreme Court has not.

### 1.4 What this means for the complaint

The honest answer is that B3 should not appear in the complaint as a named cause of action. The conduct B3 wants to address — Farmers' shifting denials, ignored evidence, gatekeeping rules, missing inspection notes, refusal to engage on the merits — is real and probative, but it is the factual substrate of B1 (statutory bad faith) and a key narrative element of A1 (breach of contract, including the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing). It is not its own count.

If a future research session wants to revisit this conclusion, the right place to look is for any post-2008 Minnesota Supreme Court or Court of Appeals decision (a) recognizing a common-law negligence tort against a first-party insurer that survives a Rule 12 motion, or (b) treating the negligent-claims-handling label as anything other than a relabeled bad-faith claim. As of this analysis date, no such decision exists.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **5 / 100**

That number reflects B3's standalone strength as a count in a Hennepin County initial complaint. It is not zero because Minnesota law is never perfectly settled, the doctrinal door is not literally closed (the *Pillsbury* "absent some independent tort" language is dicta-adjacent rather than a flat bar), and an unusually creative pleading might survive Rule 12 long enough to extract some discovery leverage. But on every realistic measure, this count loses.

- **Standalone merits (does the family win on this count alone?):** ~3/100. A motion to dismiss is essentially automatic. *Haagenson*, *Short*, and *Pillsbury* are squarely on point, the statutory remedy at § 604.18 is the legislature's chosen field-occupying response, and no Minnesota appellate decision has recognized this tort.
- **Survival past Rule 12.12:** ~5/100. A Hennepin County district court judge with the *Haagenson*/*Pillsbury* line in front of them has all the authority needed to dismiss with prejudice on the pleadings.
- **Discovery / settlement leverage if pled anyway:** ~10/100. The conduct facts that would support B3 are already in the case via B1 and A1, so adding B3 does not unlock additional discovery and does not move the settlement needle.
- **Rule 11 / cost-shifting risk:** non-trivial. A count flatly foreclosed by Minnesota Supreme Court precedent invites a Rule 11 motion or a fee-shifting argument. If the count is dismissed early and the rest of the case proceeds, the family does not want a "frivolous count" finding hanging over the docket.

Weighted blend, with emphasis on the question the user asked — the strength of B3 as an initial claim in MN state court: **~5/100**.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

- **Direct and unbroken Minnesota Supreme Court precedent.** *Haagenson* (1979) and *Short* (1983) are first-party-friendly authority for B3 only in the limited sense that they are clear; they are unambiguously bad for the existence of a freestanding negligent-claims-handling tort.
- **Court of Appeals confirmation.** *Pillsbury* (1988) is directly on the contract-only treatment of first-party bad-faith conduct.
- **Legislative occupation of the field.** Minn. Stat. § 604.18 (2008) is the legislature's calibrated answer to this exact problem. Recognizing a parallel common-law tort would (i) override the $250,000 / $100,000 statutory caps, (ii) sidestep the § 604.18 Subd. 4 motion-with-affidavit pleading mechanism, and (iii) enable jury submission of conduct that the legislature put in the judge's hands at the post-trial taxation stage. Trial judges are not eager to take that step.
- **No "independent duty" that arises outside the contract.** The duty B3 invokes is the duty of good faith and fair dealing the contract itself supplies. Under the Minnesota independent-duty rule, that duty cannot be parlayed into a separate tort.
- **No "professional negligence" framework.** Minnesota does not treat first-party insurance adjusters as professionals owing tort-grade duties of care.
- **B1 already captures the conduct.** Every fact that would support B3 supports B1 (statutory bad faith) and A1 (implied covenant). B3 adds no new evidentiary lane.
- **Small but real Rule 11 / sanction exposure.** Pleading a count squarely foreclosed by binding Minnesota Supreme Court precedent is the textbook scenario for a Rule 11 motion in state court.

### 2.3 What modestly drives the rating up (almost nothing)

- The *Pillsbury* phrasing — "in the absence of some independent tort" — is not a flat bar; it leaves theoretical room.
- Minnesota courts do allow plaintiffs to plead alternative theories under Minn. R. Civ. P. 8.05(2). A B3 count pled in the alternative (rather than as the lead theory) carries a slightly lower sanction risk because the plaintiff is not affirmatively claiming a settled theory but asking the court to extend the common law.
- A creative plaintiff could attempt to thread the needle by characterizing specific Farmers actions (e.g., the "engineer-only" gatekeeping rule, Brau's refusal to release inspection notes) as breaches of duties that exist independently of the policy — but this argument is far stronger as a § 604.18 / § 72A.201 predicate than as a freestanding tort.

### 2.4 What "5/100" means practically

B3 should be omitted from the complaint. The conduct is real and matters, but it should be channeled into B1 (statutory bad faith), A1 (breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing inside the contract count), and the spoliation / discovery-sanctions theory at D3 / D4. Pleading B3 as a separate count adds no recovery, no leverage, and a small but real downside (sanction exposure, dismissal-with-prejudice noise on the docket, distraction from stronger counts).

If the family insists on B3 as a placeholder, the lowest-risk way to do it is (a) plead it expressly in the alternative under Rule 8.05(2), (b) couple it with a request that the court extend the common law to recognize the tort, and (c) drop the count voluntarily before any motion practice. Even then, most practitioners would advise against it.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No.** B3, if it existed under Minnesota law, would be a conduct-based tort that targets how Farmers handled the claim during the investigation, denial, appeal, and post-appraisal phases. It does not require disturbing the 9/12/2025 appraisal award. The conduct it would punish is largely complete by the time the appraisal began, and the post-appraisal conduct (inconsistent scope statements, refusal to engage on coverage gaps, no umpire report) is itself part of the negligent-handling narrative — not an attack on the award.

This appraisal-independence is not, however, an advantage that rescues B3. The claim's standalone strength remains 5/100 because the doctrinal bar is in front of it regardless of what happens to the appraisal. The appraisal-independence question is meaningful only insofar as it tells the family that nothing they decide about A3 (vacate / modify / correct the appraisal) constrains B3 one way or the other.

### 4.2 Why the appraisal is structurally orthogonal to B3

The appraisal under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 resolves "amount of loss" disputes — including causation under *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012) — within the framework of the insurance contract. The award binds the parties as to the contract claim's quantum.

A negligent-claims-handling theory, by contrast, is about the duty of care the insurer owes during claim investigation and adjudication. It does not depend on what the right "amount of loss" is; it depends on whether the insurer's process for getting to its denial decision met an objective standard of care. The appraisal award:

- does not retroactively make the 9/3/2024 phone-and-letter denial reasonable;
- does not rehabilitate Prieve's "no hail in Eden Prairie after 2020" assertion;
- does not cure the "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule;
- does not produce the missing Brau notes;
- does not explain the inconsistent 9/18 / 9/19 / 10/14 scope statements; and
- does not change the fact that the umpire produced no report or estimate.

If anything, the post-appraisal conduct (Farmers' three different descriptions of which windows the award covers, the refusal to discuss scope and the redirection back to the family's own appraiser, the 10/29 personal refusal to release Brau notes) compounds the pre-appraisal record rather than displacing it. A B3 theory would treat all of that as one continuous course of conduct.

### 4.3 The appraisal does not weaken B3 the way it would weaken some adjacent theories

For A4 (unjust enrichment), the existence and use of the appraisal is itself evidence that "the rights of the parties are governed by a valid contract" and weakens the equitable claim. For A3 (vacate the appraisal), the analysis is by definition about disturbing the award. For B3, neither problem applies. The appraisal is irrelevant to B3 except as a chronological landmark in the conduct timeline.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- B3 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be filed, to survive Rule 12 (which it would not for unrelated reasons), or to reach the merits.
- B3 **does not depend** on the appraisal in any direction. The award is at most a date-stamp on the timeline.
- The appraisal-independence is real but does not improve B3's strength. The reason B3 is at 5/100 is the doctrinal bar from *Haagenson*, *Short*, *Pillsbury*, and § 604.18, not anything about the appraisal.
