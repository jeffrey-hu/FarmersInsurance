## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The statute

Minn. Stat. § 549.09 ("Interest on Verdicts, Awards, and Judgments") is the operative Minnesota statute on prejudgment interest. The provisions most directly relevant to this case live in Subdivision 1, paragraphs (a)–(c), with paragraph (b) doing the bulk of the work for first-party property cases.

- **Subd. 1(a) — Postverdict, postaward, and postjudgment interest.** Provides that "any judgment or award" carries interest from the time it is entered until paid, computed at the rate set by paragraph (c).
- **Subd. 1(b) — Preverdict, preaward, and prereport interest on pecuniary damages.** Interest "shall be computed … from the time of the commencement of the action or a demand for arbitration, or the time of a written notice of claim, whichever occurs first." If interest is to be claimed from the date of a written notice of claim, the action must be commenced within two years of that notice. Excludes (i) judgments or awards for future damages, (ii) punitive damages, (iii) fines, (iv) sanctions, (v) fees, costs, or disbursements, and (vi) judgments or awards upon which interest is otherwise contracted for or otherwise provided by law.
- **Subd. 1(b) — Offer-of-settlement mechanism.** If either party serves a written offer of settlement and the offer is rejected, interest accrual is adjusted at the time of the offer based on which side's offer is closer to the eventual judgment or award. Functionally analogous to Minn. R. Civ. P. 68 but operating under the statute (and on a 30-day acceptance window rather than Rule 68's 14-day window — the two are not interchangeable).
- **Subd. 1(c) — Rate.** For judgments or awards of $50,000 or less (and for any judgment or award against the state or a political subdivision), the interest rate is the secondary-market yield on one-year U.S. Treasury bills for the most recent reporting month, rounded to the nearest one percent, or four percent, whichever is greater. The state court administrator publishes the rate annually on or before December 20 for the succeeding year. **For calendar year 2026, the published rate is four percent.** For judgments or awards exceeding $50,000 entered on or after August 1, 2009 (excluding state-party and family-court matters), the rate is **ten percent per year**.

The statute supplies, in plain terms, a freestanding statutory right to prejudgment interest on pecuniary damages from the earliest of (a) the date the lawsuit is filed, (b) the date a written demand for arbitration is served, or (c) the date a written notice of claim is given — provided suit is filed within two years of any earlier notice. The right does not require a finding of wrongdoing, breach, or bad faith; it attaches by operation of law to the underlying recovery.

### 1.2 Application to first-party property insurance — *Poehler v. Cincinnati Insurance Co.* (Minn. 2017)

The controlling Minnesota authority for D2 on this case is *Poehler v. Cincinnati Insurance Co.*, 899 N.W.2d 135 (Minn. 2017). *Poehler* held three things that all matter here:

1. **Section 549.09 is not limited to wrongdoing-based recoveries.** The statute "unambiguously provides for preaward interest on all awards of pecuniary damages that are not specifically excluded by the statute, and does not restrict the recovery of preaward interest to cases or matters involving wrongdoing or a breach of contract." An appraisal award qualifies as an "award" within the meaning of the statute.
2. **Preaward interest runs on appraisal awards in first-party property cases.** "Absent contractual language explicitly precluding preaward interest, an insured may recover preaward interest on an appraisal award for a fire insurance loss, notwithstanding a contractual loss payment provision stating that the loss is payable after the filing of an appraisal award."
3. **The accrual trigger is the same as in any other case.** Interest accrues from the earliest of the three Subd. 1(b) triggers: commencement, demand for arbitration, or written notice of claim.

*Poehler* is the foundational decision; Minnesota courts have continued to apply its holding to property-loss appraisals and have not narrowed it on the facts presented here.

### 1.3 The contractual-preclusion exception — *Wesser v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Co.* (Minn. 2023)

The companion authority is *Wesser v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Co.*, A21-1587 (Minn. 2023). *Wesser* enforced the *Poehler* contractual-preclusion exception: where a homeowner policy contains an express provision that "no interest accrues on the loss until after the loss becomes payable," and the policy further provides that the loss "becomes payable" only after an appraisal award is filed, *Poehler*'s default rule is overridden and no preaward interest accrues. Per the secondary sources reviewed for this memo, *Wesser* is the principal post-*Poehler* limitation on the rule, and it turns on the explicit policy language.

The doctrinal cap is therefore: **(i) preaward interest under § 549.09 is the default for property appraisal awards in Minnesota; (ii) it is preempted only where the policy contains an express, specific contractual preclusion** (not merely a generic "loss payable after appraisal" clause without an explicit interest carve-out).

### 1.4 The Farmers policy on this case does not contain an express preaward-interest preclusion

Independent text review of `HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` — the operative Farmers Next Generation Homeowners Policy form — turns up no Section I property-coverage provision that bars preaward interest. Specifically:

- **Section I — Property Conditions, Item 14 ("Loss adjustment and payment")** states only that Farmers "will pay within 5 working days after: (a) we reach agreement with you; (b) a final judgment; or (c) an appraisal award." That clause sets a payment timeline; it does not state that no interest accrues until the loss becomes payable. *(Source: `HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf`, Section I — Property Conditions, ¶ 14.)*
- **Section I — Property Conditions, Item 10 ("Appraisal")** describes the § 65A.01-style appraisal process but contains no interest-preclusion language. *(Same source, ¶ 10.)*
- **The only "interest accruing" reference in the policy** is in Section II (Liability Coverage), Item 1.d, which addresses postjudgment interest on third-party liability damages and is not applicable to the first-party property dispute here.
- **The endorsements active on the policy at the loss date — J6898 (Amending General Conditions), J6944 (Limited Matching), and the MN079 / MN080 Minnesota amendatory endorsements** — do not add a preaward-interest preclusion. J6898 adds Additional Benefits and Services and Policy Notices conditions; J6944 limits the matching obligation; MN079 modifies Extended Replacement Cost and Building Ordinance/Law provisions. None of those endorsements contains a "no interest accrues" clause.

The policy is therefore **silent on preaward interest in the *Poehler* sense — i.e., the policy contains no explicit contractual preclusion**. Under *Poehler*, that places this case squarely inside the default rule: § 549.09 preaward interest is recoverable on the eventual recovery (whether the recovery comes through enforcing the appraisal award, expanding it via clarification or modification, or recovering on a separate breach theory).

### 1.5 What this remedy looks like on the face of a complaint

D2 is a remedy, not a freestanding cause of action. It rides inside a contract count (A1), an action to vacate/modify the appraisal (A3), or any other count that produces a pecuniary recovery. The complaint paragraph supporting D2 would read, in substance:

> "Plaintiffs are entitled, by operation of Minn. Stat. § 549.09, Subd. 1(b), to preverdict / preaward / prereport interest on the pecuniary damages awarded in this action, computed from the earliest of (a) the date of Plaintiffs' written notice of claim to Defendant on or about August 13, 2024; (b) the date of Plaintiffs' written Demand for Appraisal under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 served on August 3, 2025; or (c) the date of commencement of this action, whichever first occurred. By operation of Minn. Stat. § 549.09, Subd. 1(a), Plaintiffs are further entitled to postjudgment interest on any final judgment until paid. Pursuant to *Poehler v. Cincinnati Insurance Co.*, 899 N.W.2d 135 (Minn. 2017), this entitlement is unaffected by the contractual loss-payment provision in Section I, Property Conditions, ¶ 14 of the Policy, which contains no express preclusion of preaward interest."

The paragraph does not require pleading wrongdoing, breach, or bad faith. The entitlement is statutory and attaches to the recovery the family ultimately establishes through any of the substantive counts.

### 1.6 The amount that interest runs on

§ 549.09 runs on "pecuniary damages," not on the gross loss claimed. The figure that ultimately matters is the *eventual judgment or award* on the merits — not the $310,000 figure on the August 3, 2025 Sworn Statement on Proof of Loss, and not the $886,000 Coverage A limit. Practical possibilities, with very different interest profiles:

- If recovery is limited to confirming the existing 9/12/2025 award as written ($12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV / $0 net after the $17,720 deductible), preaward interest runs on the $0 net — i.e., zero recoverable interest. *(`EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png`; `EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Back.png`.)*
- If recovery includes confirmation of the existing award **plus** a court-ordered remand or expansion of scope (under A1 or A3) sufficient to exceed the $17,720 deductible, preaward interest runs on the net recovery above the deductible.
- If recovery includes a full re-scoping of damaged windows (the family's contention being that nearly all windows on the home were damaged, of which the panel awarded only two), the interest base is potentially substantial — e.g., on a hypothetical $200,000–$300,000 net recovery, interest at 10% from a notice-of-claim trigger date in 2024 produces substantial cumulative interest by the time of any 2026–2027 verdict.

The precise base is therefore a downstream function of the success of A1, A3, and the matching/scope arguments. D2 itself does not require the family to prove any particular base — § 549.09 attaches automatically to whatever pecuniary damages the court ultimately awards.

### 1.7 Rate and approximate dollar order of magnitude

Two rate possibilities apply, depending on the size of the eventual judgment:

- **Judgment or award of $50,000 or less.** Subd. 1(c) Treasury-yield rate, currently published at **4%** for calendar year 2026. (Minnesota state court administrator's annual publication.)
- **Judgment or award exceeding $50,000.** **10% per annum** under Subd. 1(c)'s post-2009 large-judgment provision.

For planning, assume a judgment between $50,000 and $300,000 carries 10% per annum simple interest from the accrual trigger. On a hypothetical $200,000 net recovery accruing from 8/13/2024 (the earliest plausible written-notice-of-claim trigger; see § 3 below) through, e.g., a 12/31/2027 judgment date, that yields approximately $66,000 in preaward interest before any offer-of-settlement adjustment. The actual figure obviously depends on (i) the eventual base, (ii) the trigger date the court adopts, and (iii) whether any offer-of-settlement event under Subd. 1(b) cuts off accrual at an earlier date.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **85 / 100**

D2 is rated as a remedy element rather than as a standalone cause of action. The 85/100 reflects (a) the strength of the doctrinal entitlement to preaward interest under *Poehler*, (b) the strength of the family's pre-suit notice-of-claim and demand-for-appraisal record establishing an early accrual trigger, and (c) the comparative weakness of any defense Farmers can mount against accrual, given the absence of an express preaward-interest preclusion in the policy.

The 15 points subtracted from a perfect score reflect:

- **Base-rate uncertainty.** The interest base depends entirely on the success of the A1 / A3 substantive counts. If those counts fail to expand the recovery beyond the existing $0 net under the 9/12/2025 award, interest runs on $0 and D2 produces no dollars. (Factual cap, not doctrinal cap.)
- **Offer-of-settlement exposure.** Subd. 1(b) lets Farmers cut off accrual prospectively by serving a written offer that ultimately turns out to be closer to the verdict than the family's offer. That is a real risk on this record because the appraisal award already exists and is presumptively valid; Farmers could use the award figure (or a marginal figure above it) as a Subd. 1(b) offer and force the family to litigate above its own offer to capture full preaward interest. Manageable with offer-tracking discipline, but not zero.
- **Verification gap on the operative policy text.** The conclusion that the policy contains no express preaward-interest preclusion is based on text extraction from the 2014 newBusiness policy form (Section I conditions and the J6898 / J6944 / MN079 endorsements visible in the project record) and not on a full re-reading of every endorsement page in MN080 or any 2023-renewal endorsement that may have been added later. The verification flag in § 1.4 must be cleared before filing.

### 2.2 What drives the rating up

- **The preaward-interest entitlement is a default rule, not a discretionary remedy.** *Poehler* is unambiguous: § 549.09 applies to appraisal awards as a matter of law absent express contractual preclusion. The court does not weigh equities, balance hardships, or assess insurer conduct. The right attaches to the recovery.
- **The policy is silent on preaward interest in the *Poehler* sense.** The only relevant Section I provision (¶ 14, "Loss adjustment and payment") sets a 5-working-day payment timeline; it does not preclude interest accruing before that point. *(`HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` Section I Conditions ¶ 14.)* That puts this case on the *Poehler* side of the *Poehler* / *Wesser* line, not the *Wesser* side.
- **The family has multiple, dated, fully-documented preaward-interest accrual triggers on the record.** The earliest plausible trigger — written notice of claim — is on or about 8/13/2024 (Legacy contractor opening of the claim and the family's prompt cooperation with Farmers' inspection process). *(`EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md`; `EventDocuments/11-19-complaint-to-dept-of-commerce.pdf` timeline.)* The 8/3/2025 Demand for Appraisal under § 65A.01 is itself a written demand for the contract's mandatory dispute-resolution mechanism. *(`EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf`.)* The 11/19/2024 MN Department of Commerce complaint also functions as written notice. *(`EventDocuments/11-19-complaint-to-dept-of-commerce.pdf`.)* The family has trigger candidates spanning 13 months, which preserves accrual even if a court were to reject the earliest candidate.
- **The 2-year filing rule under Subd. 1(b) is comfortably satisfied.** Subd. 1(b) requires the action to be commenced within two years of the written notice of claim used as the accrual trigger. The earliest candidate trigger is on or about 8/13/2024; the 2-year mark is 8/13/2026. The family's existing 7/13/2026 contractual suit-limitation deadline is earlier, so any timely-filed complaint on this record will easily satisfy § 549.09's 2-year notice-to-commencement requirement.
- **The rate is favorable.** At a 10% rate for any judgment over $50,000 — likely if any meaningful scope-expansion succeeds — preaward interest is a substantial component of the total recovery and is not capped by the size of the underlying award.
- **D2 is not affected by the *Morris* / 72A.201 private-right-of-action problem that haunts C1.** *(See `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/C1.md` § 1.2.)* § 549.09 is a freestanding statutory entitlement that runs against any pecuniary judgment regardless of whether the underlying claim is contract, statutory, or tort.
- **D2 is not subject to the § 604.18 appraisal bar that constrains B1's remedy.** *(See `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/B1.md` § 4.)* § 549.09 has no analog of § 604.18, Subd. 4(b)'s "resolved or confirmed by appraisal" disqualifier. Even if the family's B1 taxable-cost remedy is impaired by the appraisal posture, D2 interest still runs on whatever pecuniary recovery is ultimately achieved.

### 2.3 What drives the rating down

- **Base-rate dependence.** As above. Without success on A1 / A3, the interest base is too low to matter. D2 is a multiplier, not a generator.
- **Offer-of-settlement dynamics.** Farmers will very likely serve a Subd. 1(b) offer, anchored to the appraisal award, designed to put the family at risk on accrual. The family must respond with its own well-supported counteroffer to preserve accrual against the eventual judgment. This is procedural blocking and tackling, not a doctrinal weakness, but it requires active management.
- **Possibility of a contractual-preclusion finding the verification gap has not yet ruled out.** If a later text review surfaces a "no interest accrues until loss is payable" clause in MN080 or another endorsement, *Wesser* would override *Poehler* on this case. The current working conclusion is that no such clause exists; the verification step is to confirm.
- **Trigger-date contestability.** Farmers will likely argue for the latest possible trigger date — 8/3/2025 (Demand for Appraisal) or the date of suit commencement — rather than the earliest (8/13/2024). The family's strongest argument for the earliest trigger rests on the family's 8/13/2024 claim opening through Legacy and the family's contemporaneous communications with Farmers. The exact contour of "written notice of claim" under Subd. 1(b) is fact-specific and the case law tolerates a range of formats; *see* secondary-source notes that "a property loss notice submitted by a third-party insurance agent at the insured's direction and on the insured's behalf constitutes 'written notice of claim'" sufficient for accrual under Subd. 1(b). Independent verification on Westlaw is required.
- **Subd. 1(b) excludes future damages, fees, costs, and disbursements.** The interest base does not include any future-damage component, attorney fees obtained under § 604.18, sanctions, fines, or disbursements. The family should ensure pleading and judgment-form drafting separate the interest-bearing pecuniary recovery from the carve-outs.

### 2.4 What "85/100" means practically

- **Plead D2 as an explicit prayer for relief in the initial complaint.** Not as a separate count, but as a numbered prayer under each substantive count's relief section, citing § 549.09 by subdivision and *Poehler* by name.
- **Lock in the earliest defensible accrual trigger by reciting it expressly in the complaint.** The complaint should affirmatively allege the date of the earliest written notice of claim and request preaward interest from that date. The strongest candidate dates are catalogued in § 3 below.
- **Manage Subd. 1(b) offer-of-settlement procedure rigorously throughout the litigation.** Track every offer Farmers serves, verify whether it is properly framed under § 549.09 (not Rule 68; the two have different windows and different effects), and respond inside the 30-day acceptance window with a reasoned counteroffer.
- **Verify the operative policy text for any preaward-interest preclusion before filing.** The verification flag in § 1.4 must be cleared. If a preclusion is found, *Wesser* governs and D2 collapses to postaward interest only — a meaningful reduction in value but not an elimination of the entitlement.
- **Plan separately for postjudgment interest under Subd. 1(a).** Postjudgment interest is not affected by the *Poehler* / *Wesser* line and accrues by operation of law on the eventual judgment until paid.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal Award?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No. D2 stands on its own — independent of the 9/12/2025 appraisal award — both as a doctrinal matter and as a factual matter.** The § 549.09 entitlement is a freestanding statutory remedy that attaches to whatever pecuniary recovery the court ultimately enters. It does not require disturbing the existing award; it does not collapse if the existing award is left intact; and it is not narrowed by the existence of the award.

The only sense in which D2's *value* depends on the appraisal posture is downstream: the dollar yield of D2 turns on the size of the eventual pecuniary recovery, and the eventual pecuniary recovery turns in part on whether A1, A3, or related counts succeed in expanding scope beyond the existing award. That is a downstream-economics dependency, not a doctrinal-viability dependency. D2 itself stands and runs regardless.

### 4.2 Why D2 is doctrinally independent of the appraisal

**(a) § 549.09 attaches to "any award" — including an appraisal award — by operation of law.** *Poehler* squarely holds that § 549.09 reaches appraisal awards as a category. The family does not need to vacate, modify, or correct the existing award to invoke § 549.09; the existing award is itself a § 549.09-eligible "award" on which preaward interest can run, even if the net payment is $0. The family also does not need to obtain a *new* award to anchor § 549.09 — any pecuniary judgment in the litigation is enough.

**(b) § 549.09 has no analog of the § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar.** § 604.18's "resolved or confirmed by … appraisal" disqualifier — the doctrinal headache for the B1 remedy — has no parallel in § 549.09. The statutes are independent. D2 is therefore not vulnerable to the appraisal-bar construction that constrains B1's taxable-cost remedy.

**(c) The contractual-preclusion exception (*Wesser*) requires affirmative policy language; this policy does not contain it.** Where the *Poehler* default rule survives — as it does on this policy text — § 549.09 applies to the appraisal award and to any subsequent pecuniary recovery without further hooks.

**(d) Postjudgment interest under Subd. 1(a) is doubly independent.** Even if every preaward-interest argument were lost, postjudgment interest still runs on any final judgment until paid. The appraisal award has no effect on Subd. 1(a) post-entry interest, and no policy provision in the record purports to preclude it.

### 4.3 Why D2 is factually independent of the appraisal

The accrual triggers cataloged in § 3.1 above all predate or are independent of the 9/12/2025 award:

- 8/13/2024 — claim opening through Legacy. Predates the award by 13 months.
- 11/19/2024 — Department of Commerce complaint. Predates the award by 10 months.
- 1/31/2025 — formal written appeal package. Predates the award by ~7.5 months.
- 8/3/2025 — Demand for Appraisal. Predates the award by 40 days. *(`EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf`.)*
- Suit commencement. Will occur on or before 7/13/2026, ~10 months after the award.

The award itself is the *backdrop* against which interest is calculated, not a precondition to interest. Once an interest base exists (whatever its source — confirmation of the award, expansion of scope through litigation, or a separate breach recovery), § 549.09 attaches by operation of law.

### 4.4 The downstream-economics dependency, stated precisely

The dollar yield of D2 depends on the pecuniary recovery the court ultimately enters. That recovery depends on the success of the substantive counts:

- If A1 / A3 succeed in expanding scope (e.g., by establishing matching obligation under J6944 / *Cedar Bluff* / Semco-defunct, or by expanding the awarded windows to reflect the full damaged-window count), the interest base is meaningful and the D2 yield is meaningful. *(`LegalReferences/LegalArguments/A1.md`; `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/A3.md`.)*
- If those counts fail and the existing $0-net award stands, the interest base is $0 and the D2 yield is $0 — not because D2 was barred, but because there is no positive base on which to run.
- If A1 / A3 partially succeed (e.g., a remand for ambiguity-clarification under *Herll* and the subsequent expanded award yields a modest net above the deductible), the D2 yield is intermediate.

That dependency is properly analyzed under A1 / A3, not under D2. D2 itself is fully viable in every scenario; its dollar payoff is not.

### 4.5 The strategic implication

- **Plead D2 in the initial complaint regardless of which substantive counts the family ultimately prevails on.** The cost of pleading D2 is essentially zero (a paragraph in the prayer for relief); the upside is the full statutory accrual on whatever recovery is ultimately obtained.
- **Frame the earliest accrual trigger affirmatively in the complaint** — 8/13/2024 — so that a court has the family's strongest written-notice-of-claim date squarely on the record.
- **Do not let the existence of the appraisal award shape the D2 pleading.** D2 runs in parallel with whatever happens to the award.
- **Coordinate D2 explicitly with the A1, A3, and B1 analyses.** The appraisal-dependency profiles of those claims diverge: A1 (independent of the award; depends on it for facts but not for viability), A3 (directly attacks the award), B1 (independent in liability, exposed in remedy). D2 sits cleanly outside all of those dependency profiles and runs in parallel to all of them.

### 4.6 Bottom line on the key question

- D2 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be alleged, proved, or recovered.
- D2 **runs in parallel** to whatever happens to the award and to whatever substantive count generates the eventual pecuniary recovery.
- The **only downstream constraint** on D2's value is the size of the eventual recovery — which is properly analyzed under A1 / A3 / matching, not under D2.
