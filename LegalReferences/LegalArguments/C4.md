## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 Two distinct authority clusters share the C4 caption

The Possible-Arguments entry yokes two unrelated bodies of law under the "claims-practices regulations" heading. To do an honest job, the analysis treats each separately.

**Cluster A — Minn. Stat. § 65A.26 (the hail-policy appraisal statute).** A narrow, mandatory provision applied by its terms to hail policies. It prescribes a structural mechanism (appraisal), not a standard of conduct.

**Cluster B — Minn. Stat. §§ 72A.17–72A.32, especially § 72A.20 and § 72A.201, and Minn. R. Ch. 2790 (the actual claims-practices regulations).** A standard-of-conduct framework that defines unfair claims practices, assigns enforcement to the Department of Commerce, and is *not directly enforceable by private suit* under Minnesota Supreme Court precedent.

Each cluster has its own elements, its own remedy posture, and — crucially — its own answer to the appraisal-independence question. Sections 1.2 through 1.5 work through Cluster A; Sections 1.6 through 1.10 work through Cluster B.

### 1.2 Cluster A — Text and operation of Minn. Stat. § 65A.26

The statute reads, in substance:

> "Every policy of insurance against damage by hail issued by any company must provide that in case of loss and failure of the parties to agree as to the amount of the loss, on written demand of either party, the company and the insured each shall select a competent appraiser and notify the other of the appraiser selected within ten days of the demand. The appraisers shall first select a competent and disinterested umpire; and, failing for ten days to agree upon the umpire, then, on request of either appraiser, the umpire shall be selected by a judge of a court of record in the state in which the property covered is located. By mutual agreement the two appraisers may agree to have the umpire selected by a judge of a court of record and waive the ten-day provision.
>
> The appraisers and the umpire shall then appraise the loss. A written award of any two of these persons determines the amount of loss. The written award of a majority of these referees is final and conclusive upon the parties as to amount of loss, and **this selection, unless waived by the parties, is a condition precedent to any right of action to recover for a loss**.
>
> **No suit for the recovery of any claim by virtue of this policy may be sustained unless commenced within one year after the loss occurred.**"

Three structural features matter for the analysis here:

- **Mandatory hail appraisal.** Hail policies *must* contain an appraisal mechanism with the prescribed selection mechanics. The provision is policy-form regulatory; it operates by requiring the policy to contain certain terms, not by imposing a separately-enforceable duty of good claims handling.
- **Condition-precedent language.** Appraisal is a condition precedent to suit unless waived. This goes to whether suit is procedurally available, not to whether suit has a substantive right-creating cause of action.
- **One-year suit-limitation language.** The statute states that "no suit for the recovery of any claim by virtue of this policy may be sustained unless commenced within one year after the loss occurred." This is a textually shorter limitations period than the policy's two-year contractual limitation (and shorter than several other Minn. Stat. provisions on insurance suit limitations). The proper interaction of § 65A.26's one-year language with the policy's two-year language and with Minn. Stat. § 65A.01's two-year statutory homeowner suit-limitation provision is a real interpretive question; see Section 1.4.

### 1.3 Cluster A is policy-form regulation, not a private cause of action

Section 65A.26 does not create a stand-alone private right of action. It is a policy-form regulatory provision that prescribes contract terms for a class of policies. Once the appraisal mechanism is incorporated into the policy (whether by force of § 65A.26 directly or by independent contract drafting), the insured's enforcement vehicle is the contract itself — i.e., breach of contract (A1), declaratory relief (A2), and an action to vacate/modify/correct the appraisal award (A3) — not a § 65A.26 claim qua statute. The statute supplies the rules of the game, not a separate trophy.

The practical implication: a count titled "Violation of Minn. Stat. § 65A.26" cannot stand alone in the complaint. The arguments that flow from § 65A.26 are absorbed into A1 (breach of the policy's appraisal clause), A2 (declaratory judgment about the scope and validity of the appraisal), and A3 (vacatur on grounds the panel was constituted in violation of the statute or that the award did not satisfy the statute's "competent and disinterested" or "appraise the loss" requirements).

### 1.4 What § 65A.26 nonetheless contributes

Even though Cluster A is not a stand-alone claim, its text supplies several leverage points worth carrying into the complaint as part of A1, A2, and A3:

- **The "competent and disinterested umpire" requirement.** The statute's umpire-selection mechanic requires a "competent and disinterested" umpire and prescribes a fallback procedure in which a judge of a court of record selects the umpire if the appraisers cannot agree within ten days. On this record the umpire was Wade Roos, selected through the appraisal-prep mechanics. Pure Construction's Zach Sexe drove the process; the family did not vet the umpire and did not interview the appraisers. The "competent and disinterested" qualifier is enforceable through A3 vacatur litigation, and § 65A.26's text reinforces the same requirement that § 65A.01 contains for general homeowner appraisals.
- **The "appraise the loss" duty.** Section 65A.26 requires the panel to "appraise the loss" — language that, read in the same way as § 65A.01 Subd. 3's "stating separately actual value and loss to each item" requirement, supports the family's pleading that the 9/12/2025 award failed because it merely listed two cryptic codes ("F1.7" and "B1.2") against a lump-sum $12,800 RCV without identifying which windows were appraised. The award the family received does not "appraise the loss" item-by-item in any sense an outside reader can decode.
- **The condition-precedent language as a sword, not a shield.** Farmers cannot argue that appraisal foreclosed the family's contractual remedies; § 65A.26 makes appraisal a *gateway* to suit, not a *substitute* for suit. Once the gateway is satisfied — as it has been by the 9/12/2025 award — suit is available.
- **The one-year suit-limitation question is not a live threat here.** Section 65A.26's "one year after the loss occurred" language is older statutory text that predates the modern Minnesota homeowner regime. The policy in force on the loss date (`HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf`) contains a two-year contractual suit-limitation, which is consistent with the statutory homeowner-form practice and which Farmers cited in its 7/11/2025 generic deadline response. On the present record there is no indication Farmers has invoked the § 65A.26 one-year limitation, and any such invocation would face the doctrinal headwind that the policy expressly contracted for two years (the larger window controls under any reasonable reading of insured-friendly construction). The family should nonetheless pre-empt the issue in the complaint by pleading the contractual two-year period as the operative deadline and citing the policy text that replaces or supersedes any shorter statutory text. If the issue surfaces, it is best raised as a declaratory issue under A2.

### 1.5 What § 65A.26 does *not* support

Section 65A.26 cannot support, alone or in combination with the rest of Cluster A, a claim for damages, fee-shifting, injunctive relief, or any other affirmative recovery beyond what A1, A2, and A3 already reach. Treating it as a stand-alone count is a category error. It belongs in the pleading not as a separate count but as **citation language** within A1 (appraisal-clause performance), A2 (validity / scope of award), and A3 (statutory grounds for vacatur).

### 1.6 Cluster B — Text and operation of the claims-practices framework

Minnesota's actual fair-claims-handling regime sits in Chapter 72A of the Insurance Code and in the implementing rules at Minn. R. Ch. 2790. The most important provisions for this case are:

- **Minn. Stat. § 72A.20 (Methods, Acts, and Practices Defined as Unfair or Deceptive).** Enumerates a list of practices declared unfair, including misrepresenting policy provisions, knowingly making false statements about an insured's claim, failing to acknowledge claims, failing to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation of claims, and compelling insureds to litigate by offering substantially less than the amounts ultimately recovered. The list is broad and reaches a great deal of the conduct the Lu/Hu record documents.
- **Minn. Stat. § 72A.201 (Standards for Claim Filing and Handling).** Imposes specific timing and procedural requirements: 30-business-day investigation, prompt acknowledgment, fair weighing of evidence, written explanation of denials, etc. Section 72A.201 is the most claim-handling-specific statute in the Minnesota Insurance Code.
- **Minn. R. Ch. 2790 (Unfair and Deceptive Insurance Trade Practices).** Adopted by the Commissioner of Commerce under § 72A.20, Subd. 13 and § 72A.201. The rules elaborate on the statutes — covering matters such as misrepresentation in claim handling, failure to provide reasonable claim forms and instructions, denials without reasonable investigation, and unreasonable delay in claim processing.

These three authorities together are what most Minnesota practitioners think of when they refer to "claims-practices regulations." The Possible-Arguments entry's "fair-claims-handling obligations for property insurers" caption naturally points here, even though the cited statute number (§ 65A.26) does not.

### 1.7 No private right of action — *Morris v. American Family*

The most important Minnesota authority on Cluster B as a vehicle for relief is *Morris v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 386 N.W.2d 233 (Minn. 1986). In *Morris* the plaintiff sought to amend her no-fault complaint to add a count for violation of the Unfair Claims Practices Act (Minn. Stat. § 72A.20). The Minnesota Supreme Court rejected the amendment and held, in the holding most often cited for this point, that Minn. Stat. § 72A.20 does not create a private right of action. The Court read the legislature's repeal of an earlier private-action subdivision and the assignment of enforcement authority to the Commissioner (today, the Commissioner of Commerce) and to the Attorney General as evidence that private suit was deliberately not authorized.

Since *Morris*, the rule has been stable: violations of Chapter 72A and of Minn. R. Ch. 2790 cannot be the basis of a private cause of action. Enforcement runs through the Commerce Department's complaint and administrative-action procedures, not through civil suit. The Commerce regulator can fine, censure, suspend, or revoke licensure; the insured cannot collect.

The doctrinal effect of *Morris* on Cluster B as a stand-alone count is dispositive. **A count titled "Violation of Minn. Stat. § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790" cannot survive a motion to dismiss as a private cause of action.** The Lu/Hu family already exhausted the Commerce-complaint path; it produced internal escalation (Director-level review) but no enforceable result for the family.

### 1.8 Cluster B nonetheless has real utility as standard-of-conduct evidence

What Cluster B *can* do is supply the regulatory standard of conduct against which Farmers' claim handling is measured for purposes of other counts that *do* exist as private rights of action. Three concrete uses on this record:

- **§ 604.18 Prong 1.** As the B1 analysis already explains in some detail, § 72A.201 is the principal regulatory benchmark Minnesota courts use to evaluate whether a denial had a "reasonable basis." Documented departures from § 72A.201 — failing to acknowledge a claim within ten business days, failing to investigate within thirty business days, refusing to weigh non-engineer evidence on the merits, denying without adequate explanation — feed directly into Prong 1 of the bad-faith analysis. They are proof, not a separate claim.
- **§ 604.18 Prong 2.** Documented and repeated departures from the same rules, particularly when they occur after the insurer has been put on notice of contrary evidence, support the inference that the insurer "knew of or recklessly disregarded" the absence of a reasonable basis. The supervisor-articulated "engineer's report only" rule is a classic example: a written record of the insurer departing from a regulatory standard at the supervisor level.
- **MCFA / § 8.31 (C3) and Common-Law Fraud (C1 / B2) corroboration.** The repeated Cluster B departures on the record give the C3 institutional-practices framing factual support and corroborate the C1 / B2 misrepresentation theories by showing the misrepresentations occurred in a regulated environment where rules of conduct are well-understood.

This is the realistic role of Cluster B in any complaint the family files: **standard-of-conduct evidence within other counts**, never a free-standing count of its own.

### 1.9 What a hypothetical Cluster B count would look like — and why it should not be filed

For completeness, a hypothetical Cluster B count, framed as a private cause of action under § 72A.201 for violation of the Unfair Claims Practices Act, would read approximately:

> "Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange violated Minn. Stat. § 72A.201, Subds. [4 and 9] (and corresponding provisions of Minn. R. Ch. 2790) by [enumerating the documented conduct]. Plaintiffs are persons aggrieved by the violations and are entitled to damages, costs, and attorney fees."

That count would be dismissed under *Morris*. It should not be pleaded. The family is better served by reproducing the same factual allegations within (a) A1 (breach of contract — to show breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing), (b) the eventual § 604.18 motion (B1 — Prong 1 and Prong 2), and (c) C3 / C1 / B2 (consumer-protection and fraud counts).

### 1.10 The single useful private-right-of-action escape valve — § 72A.061 (twisting / churning)

For completeness: there is one narrow Chapter 72A provision that does provide a private right of action — Minn. Stat. § 72A.061 (twisting and churning). It applies only to misrepresentations made to induce policy lapse or replacement and does not reach claims-handling conduct. It is not available on this record and is flagged here only because future research should not assume *Morris* foreclosed every Chapter 72A private suit. *Morris* foreclosed § 72A.20 / § 72A.201 private suit. § 72A.061 is a different and unrelated provision.

## 2. Overall Strength as an Initial-Complaint Count

### 2.1 Headline number: **8 / 100** as a stand-alone count; **35 / 100** when used as the standard-of-conduct backbone of B1 / C1 / C3

C4 cannot survive a motion to dismiss as its own count. Both clusters foreclose stand-alone private suit — Cluster A by being a policy-form regulatory provision rather than a duty-creating one, Cluster B by *Morris*. The 8/100 stand-alone score reflects (a) the tiny but nonzero probability that a creative pleading might cobble together a § 65A.26 declaratory claim that survives Rule 12 by being framed as part of A2, plus (b) the absorption of all useful content into A1, A2, A3, B1, C1, and C3.

The 35/100 supporting-role score reflects what C4's content actually does for the case when treated correctly: it is the regulatory-standard evidence that thickens the B1 Prong 1 and Prong 2 records, the institutional-practices framing for C3's *Ly v. Nystrom* public-benefit theory, and the contextual corroboration that Farmers' adopted-in-denial misrepresentations occurred in a heavily regulated environment (C1 / B2). None of those is C4 standing on its own; all of them are downstream uses of C4-type material.

| Element | Cluster A (§ 65A.26) | Cluster B (§ 72A / 2790) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stand-alone private right of action | 5 | 5 | Neither cluster supplies one. *Morris* is dispositive on Cluster B; Cluster A is policy-form regulation. |
| Statutory-text violation showing | 50 | 75 | "Engineer's report only" rule, premature 9/3 denial, refusal to weigh new evidence, missing inspection notes are all clean § 72A.201 / Ch. 2790 violations. § 65A.26 requirements ("competent and disinterested," "appraise the loss") have viable factual hooks but their force is via A3, not stand-alone. |
| Surviving Rule 12 as filed | 5 | 5 | A stand-alone C4 count is dismissable. |
| Surviving as evidence in another count | 70 | 80 | Highly usable as standard-of-conduct evidence in B1, B2/C1, and C3. |
| Damages / remedies recoverable on this count alone | 5 | 5 | None directly. All damages flow through other counts. |
| Composite as filed count | **6** | **10** | Stand-alone failure dominates. |

The fairer description is: C4 is the *evidentiary backbone* that makes B1, B2/C1, and C3 stronger than they would be without it, but it does not earn its own line in the prayer for relief.

### 2.2 What pulls the score down

- ***Morris v. American Family* is dispositive on Cluster B.** No private right of action exists under § 72A.20 / § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790. The Department of Commerce path was already exhausted (`EventDocuments/11-19-complaint-to-dept-of-commerce.pdf`) and produced no compensable result. Re-presenting the same conduct as a private count fails as a matter of law.
- **Cluster A is policy-form regulation, not duty-creating.** Section 65A.26 mandates the *content of the policy*, not the *conduct of the insurer outside the policy*. It does not run alongside the contract claim as an independent duty; it lives inside the contract claim. A pleader who reads the statute as duty-creating is reading more out of the text than the text supports.
- **Damages are entirely duplicative of A1, B1, and C3.** Even if a creative court allowed a stand-alone § 72A.201 count to survive *Morris* (which would require an appellate-level departure from current law), the damages would not exceed what A1 contract recovery, B1 § 604.18 recovery, and C3 § 8.31, Subd. 3a recovery already capture. C4 produces no damages footprint of its own.
- **Filing a stand-alone C4 count risks losing credibility on the better counts.** A motion to dismiss that knocks out a clearly foreclosed C4 count first is an unforced concession to Farmers' counsel that the pleader is not careful about Minnesota case law. Better to fold the same factual content into B1 / C1 / C3 evidence sections from the start.
- **The § 65A.26 one-year suit-limitation language is a latent risk, not an asset.** A pleader who introduces § 65A.26 as a basis for affirmative relief invites Farmers to argue back that § 65A.26's *suit-limitation* clause should be read alongside the rest of the statute — and that the family is barred from suit unless it commenced within one year of the 7/13/2024 loss date. The defense fails on a careful reading (because the policy language controls, and because § 65A.26's text addresses suits "by virtue of this policy" — i.e., on the policy form prescribed by § 65A.26 — and not the modern Minnesota homeowner form), but it is a litigation cost the family has no reason to incur. C4 is best handled defensively on the limitations point, not affirmatively.
- **Department of Commerce's institutional response confirms the practical limit of Cluster B.** The 11/19/2024 complaint moved up to the Director level (Katherine Toomajian and her director, per `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` and `ClaimTimeline.md` Phase 5), but Commerce had no authority to compel reopening or payment. That is exactly the regulatory-only enforcement posture *Morris* described. The family already paid the cost of the Cluster B path and got the value Cluster B can deliver: a regulatory record useful to a court evaluating standard of conduct.

### 2.3 What pulls the score up

- **The factual record of regulatory departures is extensive and well-documented.** Section 3 below catalogs at least seven discrete documented practice violations spread across the 9/3/2024 first denial, the 11/4/2024 second denial, the 2/3 / 2/10/2025 "engineer's report only" rule, the 5/28/2025 NWS submission silence, and the post-appraisal stonewall. These are not contested at the substantive level; they are textually present in Farmers' own correspondence. As standard-of-conduct evidence, the record is unusually thick.
- **The conduct maps cleanly onto specific § 72A.201 subdivisions.** The premature 9/3/2024 denial maps onto § 72A.201 investigation-standards subdivisions; the "engineer's report only" rule maps onto the unfair-evidence-weighing provisions; the missing 9/6/2024 Brau notes map onto recordkeeping and investigation-completeness rules; and the failure to provide § 65A.01 / § 65A.26 itemization on the appraisal award maps onto general fair-claims-handling expectations. A well-drafted B1 motion can cite each subdivision in turn.
- **§ 65A.26's "competent and disinterested" and "appraise the loss" requirements give A3 additional textual purchase.** Even if § 65A.26's hail-policy specificity is imperfectly aligned with the modern homeowner-form regime, the parallel requirements in § 65A.01 Subd. 3 (which the case proceeded under) make the same arguments. The family's A3 vacatur theory is reinforced, not weakened, by the § 65A.26 echo.
- **The institutional-practices factual texture supports C3's *Ly v. Nystrom* public-benefit framing.** The supervisor-articulated "engineer's report only" rule and the shifting-rationale denial structure are exactly the kind of regulatorily-relevant institutional patterns that the public-benefit prong is designed to capture. C4 supplies the regulatory framework against which the institutional patterns are assessed.
- **The 9/12/2025 appraisal award is, on the record, a self-disproving rebuttal of the regulatory-standards departure.** The award confirmed hail as the cause of loss on additional windows beyond the 2020 scope. That confirmation is direct evidence that the 11/4/2024 denial — which adopted the Prieve "no hail after 2020" premise to reject the family's claim — failed § 72A.201's "fair weighing of evidence" requirement. The regulatory failure is documented by Farmers' own subsequently-obtained award.
- **Cluster A's A3 hooks survive even a narrow reading of the appraisal bar.** The § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar reaches § 604.18; it does not reach A3 vacatur litigation under § 572B.23 / § 65A.01 / § 65A.26 grounds. To the extent C4's most concrete contribution is the umpire-disinterestedness and item-by-item-itemization arguments, those arguments survive whatever the court ultimately decides about the bar's scope.

### 2.4 What "8/100 stand-alone, 35/100 supporting-role" actually means in practice

- **Do not file C4 as its own count.** The downside (a Rule 12 dismissal) is concrete; the upside (an unprecedented private-suit recovery under § 72A.201 / Ch. 2790) is essentially zero given *Morris*.
- **Cite § 65A.26 within A1, A2, and A3.** Use § 65A.26's "competent and disinterested" and "appraise the loss" requirements as additional textual support for the A3 vacatur grounds and for the A2 declaratory questions. The statute's policy-form-prescriptive nature is fine for those purposes; what it cannot do is anchor a separate count.
- **Cite § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790 within B1.** Use them as the regulatory benchmark for Prong 1 and Prong 2 of the bad-faith analysis. The Prong 1 case is meaningfully stronger when the denial is not just substantively wrong but also documentably outside the regulatory standards.
- **Cite § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790 within C3.** Use them as the institutional-practices framework for the *Ly v. Nystrom* public-benefit theory. The injunctive prayer that C3 needs to clear *Ly* is meaningfully stronger when it is anchored in named regulatory subdivisions and not just in a general "Farmers acted badly" framing.
- **Pre-empt the § 65A.26 one-year suit limitation.** In A2, plead that the policy's two-year limitation governs and that any shorter statutory text (including § 65A.26's "one year after the loss occurred") is superseded by the contract terms or, alternatively, by Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 Subd. 3's two-year suit window for the standard homeowner form.

### 2.5 Recommendation

**Do not file C4 as a stand-alone count.** Fold the C4 content into A1, A2, A3, B1, C1, and C3 as standard-of-conduct evidence and as additional textual support. The cost of doing so is essentially zero (the same factual record is already in the complaint for other reasons); the benefit is that it strengthens the surviving counts without exposing the pleader to a clean Rule 12 win for Farmers.

If, against this advice, the family wants the *appearance* of a separate count addressing claims practices in the prayer for relief, the closest available legitimate vehicle is **A2 (declaratory judgment)**, with declaratory questions framed around (i) the scope and validity of the 9/12/2025 award under § 65A.01 / § 65A.26's "competent and disinterested" and "appraise the loss" requirements, and (ii) the operative suit-limitation period (two years per the policy and § 65A.01 Subd. 3, not one year per § 65A.26's older text). That is not C4 dressed up; it is A2 doing C4's work.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**It depends on which cluster.** Cluster B (the actual claims-practices framework — § 72A.20 / § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790) is **structurally independent** of the appraisal award. The conduct it documents predates and outlives the appraisal, and proving regulatory-standards departure does not require disturbing the award. Cluster A (the § 65A.26 hail-appraisal statute) is **partially appraisal-dependent**: the umpire-disinterestedness and "appraise the loss" arguments live inside A3 vacatur, which by definition is an attack on the award. The condition-precedent and suit-limitation features of § 65A.26 are appraisal-independent and best handled defensively.

Because C4 is recommended above as a feeder into other counts rather than a stand-alone count, the more useful answer is to look at which counts the C4 content feeds and ask whether *those* counts depend on disturbing the award. The result is mixed:

| C4 content | Vehicle | Appraisal-dependence |
|---|---|---|
| § 72A.201 / Ch. 2790 departures (Facts 3.2.1–3.2.10) | B1 § 604.18 (Prongs 1 and 2) | Indirect: B1 itself is at risk under the § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar, but the regulatory-standards content does not require vacating the award. |
| § 72A.201 / Ch. 2790 departures (Facts 3.2.1–3.2.10) | C3 institutional-practices framing | Independent. C3 is appraisal-independent; the *Group Health* causation framework operates regardless of the award. |
| § 72A.201 / Ch. 2790 departures (Facts 3.2.1–3.2.10) | C1 / B2 corroboration | Independent. The misrepresentations existed and were transmitted before the award. The award is affirmative evidence of falsity. |
| § 65A.26 "competent and disinterested" umpire (Fact 3.1.1, 3.1.6) | A3 vacatur | Award-dependent in the literal sense — A3 *is* the attack on the award. |
| § 65A.26 "appraise the loss" itemization (Fact 3.1.2, 3.1.3, 3.1.4, 3.1.5) | A3 vacatur, A2 declaratory | Award-dependent for A3; partially appraisal-independent for A2 (declaratory questions about scope can survive even if vacatur fails). |
| § 65A.26 condition-precedent / one-year limitation features | A2 declaratory | Independent. These are textual interpretive questions, not attacks on the panel's substantive output. |

The cleanest one-line answer: **C4 content is not, on the whole, appraisal-dependent.** The regulatory-standards departures (Cluster B) live inside counts that are structurally independent of the award. Only the § 65A.26 appraisal-mechanic specifics that feed A3 are award-dependent — and those are already award-dependent because A3 itself is an attack on the award.

### 4.2 Why Cluster B is structurally independent

The § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790 departures occurred before the appraisal (9/3/2024 first denial, 11/4/2024 second denial, 2/3 / 2/10/2025 "engineer's report only" rule), through the appraisal (5/28/2025 silence on NWS submission), and after the appraisal (10/21/2025 refusal to engage on the scope gap, 10/29/2025 Brau refusal to release notes). The conduct exists as a factual matter regardless of the award. The award is in fact additional evidence that the conduct departed from regulatory standards — the panel reached a hail-causation conclusion the regulatory-standards-compliant Farmers would have reached itself.

Three doctrinal points reinforce the structural independence:

- **Scope of appraisal under § 65A.01 / § 65A.26 / *Quade*.** Appraisal panels resolve "amount of loss," which under *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), includes causation. Panels do not adjudicate regulatory-standards compliance, do not assess Prong 1 / Prong 2 of § 604.18, and do not allocate damages on tort or statutory consumer-protection theories. C4-fed counts therefore lie entirely outside the panel's adjudicative power.
- **No issue preclusion.** Collateral estoppel under *Hauschildt v. Beckingham*, 686 N.W.2d 829, 837 (Minn. 2004), requires that the issue have been "actually and necessarily decided" in the prior proceeding. The appraisal panel did not decide whether Farmers' 9/3/2024 denial complied with § 72A.201 investigation standards, whether the "engineer's report only" rule complied with Ch. 2790, whether the Brau inspection notes were preserved, or any other regulatory-standards question. None of those questions was addressed at all, let alone necessarily decided.
- **The § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar reaches § 604.18 only.** The bar text — "[a]n award of taxable costs under this section is not available in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by … appraisal" — limits the § 604.18 statutory remedy. It does not reach C3 (§ 8.31, Subd. 3a private-AG suit), it does not reach C1 / B2 (common-law fraud / negligent misrepresentation), and it does not reach the regulatory-standards content as standard-of-conduct evidence in those counts. Even if a court ultimately reads Subd. 4(b) broadly enough to bar B1, the C4 content remains usable in C3 and C1 / B2.

### 4.3 Why Cluster A's A3 hooks are award-dependent in form but not in availability

The § 65A.26 "competent and disinterested" and "appraise the loss" arguments live inside A3 because A3 is the count that attacks the award's validity. That is a definitional point about A3, not a constraint on C4. The same arguments are also available within A2 declaratory questions and A1 breach-of-the-appraisal-clause framing without literally vacating the award.

The one practical caveat: **if the court ultimately upholds the award against A3 attack, the Cluster A arguments do not produce additional remedy in their own right.** They functioned as A3 inputs; once A3 is decided, the inputs have done their work. They do not survive as a separate C4 recovery vehicle because, as Section 1 explained, they were never duty-creating to begin with.

### 4.4 Pleading-strategy implications

Because the C4 content is overwhelmingly appraisal-independent in its useful uses, the pleading strategy is:

1. **Do not plead C4 as its own count.** Confirmed; same recommendation as Section 2.5.
2. **Plead the Cluster B factual record in B1, C1 / B2, and C3** as standard-of-conduct evidence. The complaint's preliminary factual section should walk through the regulatory-standards departures at a level of detail that lets each count incorporate them by reference. The B1 motion-to-amend affidavits, when filed, should expressly cite § 72A.201 subdivisions and Minn. R. Ch. 2790 parts that map onto each documented departure.
3. **Plead the Cluster A appraisal-mechanic factual record in A3** as additional textual support for the vacatur grounds. The award's failure to identify items beyond "F1.7" / "B1.2," the umpire's lack of any report or estimate, and the family's selection-and-vetting record on Foster all anchor § 65A.26 / § 65A.01 vacatur grounds.
4. **Pre-empt § 65A.26's one-year suit-limitation language defensively.** In A2, plead that the policy's two-year limitation (and § 65A.01 Subd. 3's two-year window for the standard homeowner form) governs and that the older § 65A.26 one-year text does not apply. Anticipate Farmers' potential reliance on § 65A.26 as a limitations defense and brief it preemptively.
5. **Cite the Department of Commerce complaint as exhaustion** of the Cluster B regulatory enforcement path. The 11/19/2024 complaint demonstrates the family treated the conduct as a regulatory-standards problem and engaged the agency authorized to enforce; that the agency lacked the power to compel reversal supports inclusion of B1, C1 / B2, and C3 as the surviving private-suit vehicles.

### 4.5 Bottom line on the key question

- C4 content **does not, on the whole, depend on vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award**.
- The § 72A.201 / Minn. R. Ch. 2790 standard-of-conduct departures (Cluster B) are appraisal-independent and live inside B1, C1 / B2, and C3.
- The § 65A.26 appraisal-mechanic specifics (Cluster A) feed A3 vacatur and A2 declaratory questions; they are award-dependent only in the trivial sense that A3 is by definition an attack on the award.
- The cleanest framing is therefore: **fold the C4 content into A1, A2, A3, B1, C1 / B2, and C3 as standard-of-conduct evidence and as additional textual support for the appraisal-mechanic arguments. Do not plead C4 as its own count. The bulk of the resulting case is appraisal-independent; the part that is not (A3) is award-dependent because A3 is itself the attack on the award.**

4. **Confirm whether § 65A.26 applies of its own force to the Lu/Hu policy.** § 65A.26 by its text governs "every policy of insurance against damage by hail." 5. **Map each documented departure to a specific § 72A.201 subdivision and Minn. R. Ch. 2790 part** before any B1 motion-to-amend filing. The motion's affidavits gain force when each documented departure is matched to a numbered regulatory provision rather than asserted at a general level.
