## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 Two distinct torts, two distinct element sets

Minnesota treats common-law fraud (sometimes called fraudulent misrepresentation or intentional misrepresentation) and negligent misrepresentation as separate torts with distinct elements, distinct scienter requirements, and partially overlapping factual predicates. Both are common-law claims; neither is codified. A complaint that pleads B2 should ordinarily plead both in the alternative — the fraud count needs knowledge/reckless disregard, while the negligent-misrepresentation count needs only a failure to exercise reasonable care — but the two counts have different doctrinal hurdles.

### 1.2 Elements of common-law fraud in Minnesota

To prevail on a fraud (intentional misrepresentation) claim, a Minnesota plaintiff must plead and prove:

1. A **false representation of a past or existing material fact susceptible of knowledge** (or, in some cases, the concealment of such a fact where a duty to disclose exists);
2. Made **with knowledge of its falsity, or made as the party's own knowledge without knowing whether it was true or false** (reckless disregard satisfies this);
3. **Made with the intention to induce** the other party to act in reliance;
4. The representation **in fact induced reliance**, and that reliance was **justifiable**; and
5. The plaintiff **suffered pecuniary damages** as a result.

*Specialized Tours, Inc. v. Hagen*, 392 N.W.2d 520, 532 (Minn. 1986); *Hoyt Props., Inc. v. Prod. Res. Grp., L.L.C.*, 736 N.W.2d 313, 318 (Minn. 2007); *Valspar Refinish, Inc. v. Gaylord's, Inc.*, 764 N.W.2d 359, 368 (Minn. 2009); *M.H. v. Caritas Family Servs.*, 488 N.W.2d 282, 289 (Minn. 1992).

**Two ancillary Minnesota rules matter.** First, fraud must be pled with particularity. Minn. R. Civ. P. 9.02 mirrors Fed. R. Civ. P. 9(b): the complaint must state "the circumstances constituting fraud or mistake . . . with particularity." *Juster Steel v. Carlson Cos.*, 366 N.W.2d 616, 618 (Minn. Ct. App. 1985). Particularity means the who, what, when, where, and how of each alleged misrepresentation. Second, statements of opinion or prediction are ordinarily not actionable; only false statements of past or existing fact are. *Vandeputte v. Soderholm*, 298 Minn. 505, 216 N.W.2d 144, 147 (1974). Expert opinion can cross the line into actionable fact when the speaker misrepresents the factual basis or states as fact what the speaker knows to be false.

### 1.3 Elements of negligent misrepresentation in Minnesota

Minnesota follows Restatement (Second) of Torts § 552. The elements are:

1. The defendant, **in the course of its business, profession, or employment, or in any other transaction in which it has a pecuniary interest**, supplied false information;
2. The information was **supplied for the guidance of others in their business transactions**;
3. The plaintiff **justifiably relied** on the information;
4. The defendant **failed to exercise reasonable care or competence** in obtaining or communicating the information; and
5. The plaintiff **suffered pecuniary loss** as a result of the justifiable reliance.

*Bonhiver v. Graff*, 311 Minn. 111, 248 N.W.2d 291, 298–99 (1976) (adopting § 552); *Florenzano v. Olson*, 387 N.W.2d 168, 174 n.4 (Minn. 1986); *Valspar Refinish*, 764 N.W.2d at 368–69.

**Three Minnesota-specific limitations are important.** First, negligent misrepresentation generally does not lie in **arm's-length commercial transactions**: parties who are each looking out for their own interests and are not in a special relationship ordinarily do not owe each other a tort-based duty of care for the accuracy of their commercial statements. *Safeco Ins. Co. v. Dain Bosworth Inc.*, 531 N.W.2d 867, 870 (Minn. Ct. App. 1995); *Williams v. Smith*, 820 N.W.2d 807, 815–16 (Minn. 2012). A first-party insurance-coverage dispute is usually characterized as an arm's-length commercial transaction, which is the single largest obstacle to this prong. Second, the defendant must be supplying information **for the guidance of others in their business transactions** — the classic fact pattern is an accountant, auditor, appraiser, or engineer whose product is a representation the client relies on in dealing with a third party. An insurer communicating a denial decision to its own insured arguably is not supplying information in that § 552 sense. Third, the plaintiff still must prove **justifiable reliance and pecuniary damages** — the same reliance problem that afflicts the fraud count carries over.

### 1.4 Fraud is a recognized independent tort; *Short v. Dairyland* does not foreclose it

Minnesota refuses to recognize bad-faith claims handling as an independent tort separate from contract. *Short v. Dairyland Ins. Co.*, 334 N.W.2d 384, 388 (Minn. 1983). *Short* held that an insurer's failure to properly investigate or pay a first-party claim sounds in contract, not in tort — the Legislature's later enactment of Minn. Stat. § 604.18 was the statutory answer to that doctrinal gap. B2 is not foreclosed by *Short*, however, because fraud and negligent misrepresentation are **separate, long-standing, intentional-tort theories** that require a distinct misrepresentation of fact, not merely a breach of the insurer's claims-handling duty. The plaintiff cannot rebrand a breach-of-contract claim as a fraud claim just by calling the denial "dishonest"; the plaintiff must identify a specific false statement of past or existing fact, made with the requisite scienter, that caused reliance damages separate from the contract damages.

This is the doctrinal bright line B2 must navigate. Courts are skeptical of fraud counts grafted onto insurance-denial disputes because they suspect the plaintiff is trying to convert an unavailable bad-faith theory into something that looks like tort. *See, e.g., Wild v. Rarig*, 302 Minn. 419, 234 N.W.2d 775 (1975) (an action arising out of a breach of contract does not become a tort action simply because the breach was motivated by bad faith). The factual record must feature a real, independently identifiable false statement — ideally one that could have fooled a reasonable insured — before the claim has teeth.

### 1.5 Economic-loss doctrine (limited relevance here)

Minnesota's economic-loss doctrine, as narrowed by Minn. Stat. § 604.101, applies primarily to commercial transactions involving the sale of goods and services. It does not generally bar fraud or negligent-misrepresentation claims between parties to an insurance contract on these facts, because the statute has express carve-outs and because § 604.101 on its own terms regulates product-liability-style theories rather than tort claims arising from insurer–insured communications. The larger B2 hurdle is *Short*'s contract-not-tort framing of claims handling, not § 604.101 per se.

### 1.6 Statute of limitations

Fraud: **6 years** from discovery of the facts constituting the fraud. Minn. Stat. § 541.05, Subd. 1(6). The discovery rule applies. *Hydra-Mac, Inc. v. Onan Corp.*, 450 N.W.2d 913, 918 (Minn. 1990).

Negligent misrepresentation: **6 years** as either a tort involving pecuniary loss (§ 541.05, Subd. 1(5)) or an action on a liability created by statute or common law (§ 541.05, Subd. 1(2)). The accrual date generally runs from when damages become ascertainable. Neither tort is close to running on this record — the earliest candidate misrepresentation dates are 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024.

### 1.7 What the count would look like in a complaint

A pleaded B2 count would read approximately as follows, with Rule 9.02 particularity baked in:

> "On November 4, 2024, Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange, through Claims Specialist Representative Nicholas Brau, issued a written denial of the claim that expressly adopted and transmitted to Plaintiffs the Prieve Engineering report dated October 2024. The Prieve report, on page 9 thereof in the Weather Records section, represented as a fact that 'the most recent hail in Eden Prairie was reported in 2020.' That representation was false: the National Weather Service, Twin Cities/Chanhassen office, documents severe thunderstorms across Minnesota on July 13–14, 2024, including a 48 MPH wind gust recorded at the Eden Prairie Airport ASOS station at 1:00 AM on July 14, 2024. The City of Eden Prairie issued building permits EP187829 (11/15/2023, cause: hail) and EP190835 (5/9/2024, cause: hail) at neighboring properties, and 14 of 19 neighboring homeowners contacted by Plaintiffs confirmed hail-driven repairs after 2020. Defendant's subsequent appraisal award dated September 12, 2025, also confirmed hail as the cause of loss. Defendant knew, or in the alternative recklessly disregarded, the falsity of the 'no hail after 2020' representation when it adopted and transmitted the report to Plaintiffs. Defendant made the representation to induce Plaintiffs to accept the denial and to refrain from pursuing the claim. Plaintiffs' reliance damages include [enumerated damages]. In the alternative, Plaintiffs plead that Defendant failed to exercise reasonable care or competence in supplying the same information, supporting an alternative count for negligent misrepresentation under Restatement (Second) of Torts § 552."

Remedies sought: compensatory damages (reliance damages, not expectation damages — a meaningful difference from A1), prejudgment interest under Minn. Stat. § 549.09, and, by later motion under Minn. Stat. § 549.191, **punitive damages under Minn. Stat. § 549.20** for deliberate disregard. Punitive damages are the single real upside B2 offers that A1 and the other contract-based counts do not.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **25 / 100**

That number reflects B2's strength as a filed count in a Hennepin County state-court complaint. The factual underpinnings for a "false statement of fact" are strong on a few specific representations (most notably Prieve's "no hail after 2020" language, adopted by Farmers). The legal elements that turn a false statement into fraud — particularly **intent to induce**, **justifiable reliance**, and **reliance damages separate from contract damages** — are weak on this record. Negligent misrepresentation is weaker still because of the arm's-length / § 552 "supplying information for the guidance of others" issues.

- **Falsity element:** 80/100. The "most recent hail in Eden Prairie was reported in 2020" statement in Prieve's Weather Records section is a concrete, pled-with-particularity factual assertion that is objectively disproven by NWS data, City permit records, neighbor survey, and Farmers' own subsequent appraisal award.
- **Scienter (knowledge or reckless disregard):** 60/100 for fraud, 85/100 for negligent misrepresentation. Prieve's wholesale miss of the 7/13–14/2024 NWS event is strong evidence of either knowledge or reckless failure to investigate. Farmers' later receipt of NWS, stormersite, and permit evidence (1/31/2025, 2/10/2025, 5/28/2025) and refusal to engage with any of it pushes the scienter showing higher on a reckless-disregard theory.
- **Intent to induce reliance:** 35/100. Prieve's report itself says "This report is to be utilized only by the contracted party" — i.e., Farmers, not the insured. Farmers' forwarding of the report to the family as part of the denial arguably cures that problem, but the doctrine still looks for intent to induce specific action, and the closest candidate action is "accept the denial and drop the claim." The family did not drop the claim; they contested it immediately. The intent-to-induce element is filing-sufficient but not a strong front-end advantage.
- **Justifiable reliance:** 20/100. This is the central weakness. The family never relied on Prieve's "no hail after 2020" representation; within two weeks of receiving the 11/4/2024 denial they were filing a Commerce complaint rebutting exactly that representation with stormersite data (11/19/2024), and they followed with the 1/31/2025 appeal, the 2/10/2025 neighbor-canvass and permit-data submission, and the 5/28/2025 NWS submission. A reliance theory would have to be either (a) momentary-but-compensable reliance between 11/4 and 11/19 (too thin to produce meaningful damages), or (b) reframed reliance on something other than the false weather statement — e.g., reliance on Farmers' general representation that the claim would be handled fairly, reliance on the appraisal process being free of Farmers-influenced scope-narrowing, or reliance on Farmers' representation that an engineer's report was required to reconsider (which delayed the lawsuit). These reframes are weaker on "false statement of existing fact" but stronger on reliance.
- **Damages separate from contract damages:** 25/100. Any money the family recovers that overlaps with the policy proceeds is already reachable through A1. The tort-exclusive damages candidates — consequential damages from delay (ongoing water intrusion after 10/28/2025), the $1,100 share of Foster's $2,200 retainer, out-of-pocket costs of the Commerce complaint and AG complaint, and potential punitive damages under § 549.20 — are real but modest. Emotional-distress damages are generally not available in first-party property-insurance cases in Minnesota.
- **Rule 9.02 particularity:** filing-sufficient for fraud on the Prieve "no hail" misstatement; marginal on the 9/3/2024 "no other damages observed" statement and the "engineer's report only" rule because both are arguably statements of opinion or policy interpretation, not statements of past or existing fact. Particularity pleading can be satisfied for at least one candidate misrepresentation, which is enough to survive a 9.02 motion.

Weighted blend — with the reliance weakness dominating because courts will not let a fraud count past summary judgment without a concrete reliance showing: **~25/100 for fraud, ~15/100 for negligent misrepresentation.** Carry both counts if B2 is pled, with fraud as the primary and negligent misrepresentation as the alternative; the **composite B2 strength is ~25/100**.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

- **The family did not rely on the false statements.** The single most important fact for B2 is reliance, and the record affirmatively shows the family rejected Farmers' denials, rebutted them in writing, and pursued every available channel to prove them wrong. A plaintiff who publicly, promptly, and repeatedly contradicts the defendant's false statement has an uphill climb proving reliance. "Justifiable reliance" in Minnesota requires the plaintiff to have actually believed and acted on the misrepresentation; a plaintiff who never believed it generally has no claim. *See Specialized Tours*, 392 N.W.2d at 532; *Davis v. Re-Trac Mfg. Corp.*, 276 Minn. 116, 149 N.W.2d 37, 38–39 (1967) ("reliance, if any, must have been reasonable and justifiable").
- **Damages duplication with the contract claim.** The core damages the family seeks — the unpaid value of hail damage to their windows — are contract damages. Fraud damages are measured on a reliance theory ("out-of-pocket"), not an expectation theory. Minnesota follows the out-of-pocket-loss rule for fraud, *Lewis v. Citizens Agency of Madelia, Inc.*, 306 Minn. 194, 235 N.W.2d 831, 835 (1975), which limits recovery to the actual pecuniary loss caused by the reliance itself, not the value of what the plaintiff would have received if the representation had been true. This cuts against framing B2 as a path to policy-proceed damages; A1 already reaches those.
- **Short v. Dairyland framing risk.** A court reading the complaint will ask whether B2 is a genuine tort theory or a repackaged bad-faith claim. If the complaint's B2 allegations look like "Farmers handled the claim dishonestly, therefore they committed fraud," the court is likely to dismiss or convert those allegations into § 604.18 territory. B2 survives only to the extent it is anchored in a specific, factually false statement about a past or existing fact — not in a general claim of unfair handling.
- **Prieve's "intended user" disclaimer.** Prieve's report expressly limits its use to Farmers. That language cuts against the argument that Prieve intended to induce the family's reliance. Farmers' distribution of the report to the family as part of the denial letter is a plausible workaround, but the intent question is still a real point of friction.
- **Rule 9.02 particularity and pleading discipline.** Fraud counts that fail particularity are routinely dismissed at the pleading stage; the family's filing must pair each specific misrepresentation with date, speaker, medium, and content. That is doable here, but it limits how many candidate misrepresentations can be carried into the complaint.
- **Negligent-misrepresentation duty problems.** The arm's-length nature of the insurer–insured relationship on coverage disputes, and the question whether Farmers was "supplying information for the guidance of others in their business transactions" in the Restatement § 552 sense, together reduce the negligent-misrepresentation branch to a weak alternative rather than a real cause of action.
- **B1 (§ 604.18 bad faith) already captures the strongest misconduct narrative.** The statutory framework is the legislatively chosen vehicle for insurer misconduct in claims handling. A parallel fraud count will look duplicative and could invite a Rule 11 or procedural-abuse argument if the misconduct facts are identical to those supporting the § 604.18 motion.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up

- **A genuine, disprovable, factual misrepresentation exists on the record.** Prieve's "most recent hail in Eden Prairie reported in 2020" is not hedged, not caveated, and not framed as opinion. It is a concrete factual claim about weather history, a category in which objective truth exists and is knowable from public records. That is precisely the kind of assertion fraud law protects against. Most insurance-fraud-against-the-insured allegations are weaker because they attack expert opinion or claims judgment; this one attacks a statement of historical fact.
- **Farmers adopted and transmitted the false statement.** The 11/4/2024 denial letter, signed on Farmers letterhead, incorporates Prieve's report. Farmers cannot credibly argue later that Prieve alone made the statement; by adopting the report in the denial, Farmers restated the "no hail after 2020" claim in its own official communication to the insured. Adoption/ratification of a fraudulent expert report is a well-recognized route to issuer liability.
- **Scienter is supported by Farmers' subsequent refusal to engage with contrary evidence.** Once Farmers received the family's 1/31/2025 stormersite data, 2/10/2025 permit and neighbor-canvass package, and 5/28/2025 NWS submission through its own agent Pete, its refusal to revise its position — or even to acknowledge the NWS data — supports the reckless-disregard prong of fraud scienter. This is scienter developed post-representation, which Minnesota law permits; continued representation in the face of contrary evidence is evidence of original recklessness. *Cf. Florenzano*, 387 N.W.2d at 175.
- **A second candidate misrepresentation lives in the 9/3/2024 denial: "no other damages observed."** Three days later, on 9/6/2024, Farmers' own adjuster Nic Brau personally observed dents on windows and measured them. The 9/3 denial thus made a statement of past or existing fact that was either knowingly false at the time of issuance or was contradicted by Farmers' own subsequent inspection. This is a second, independent misrepresentation that survives the Rule 9.02 particularity test.
- **A third candidate misrepresentation lives in the "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule.** The 2/3/2025 phone statement by supervisor Bradlee Monson and the 2/10/2025 voicemail by Nic Brau both represented that the policy required evidence to be provided by an engineer. The policy contains no such requirement. This is a misrepresentation about the terms of an existing contract — historically a recognized fraud pattern — but it shades into legal interpretation, which weakens its status as a "fact susceptible of knowledge." Filing-sufficient but not headline-strong.
- **Punitive damages under Minn. Stat. § 549.20 are a real upside.** Fraud with the requisite deliberate-disregard showing can support punitive damages. This is a remedial category A1 cannot reach and § 604.18 does not fully substitute for (§ 604.18 caps at the lesser of 1/2 proceeds in excess of offer or $250,000). Even an adjacent, not-lead-count fraud theory that survives summary judgment gives the family leverage on punitive exposure.
- **The appraisal award indirectly corroborates the scienter prong.** The 9/12/2025 appraisal award — signed by the umpire and the insured's appraiser — expressly confirms hail as the cause of loss. Farmers' own post-award panel disagreed with the factual premise ("no hail after 2020") of its own prior denial. That is the sort of post-representation contradiction that juries find powerful on the "knew or recklessly disregarded" question.

### 2.4 What "25/100" means practically

B2 is **filable and will survive a Rule 12 motion on the strength of the Prieve "no hail after 2020" misstatement alone**, provided the complaint pleads it with Rule 9.02 particularity. It is **unlikely to survive summary judgment** in its current posture because the reliance element is thin and the damages element largely overlaps with the contract claim. It is therefore a **secondary, supporting count** in the complaint — useful for (a) preserving punitive-damages exposure for Farmers, (b) anchoring the factual narrative that Farmers' denial rested on objectively false premises, and (c) adding a second statutory basis for discovery that is not constrained by the appraisal bar in § 604.18, Subd. 4(b).

If filing economy is a priority, B2 can be omitted without devastating the case. Most of the factual power of B2 can be re-presented in A1 (as evidence of breach), in B1 (as evidence of § 604.18 bad-faith scienter), and in D3 (spoliation). B2's incremental contribution is punitive-damages exposure plus a modest discovery advantage, at the cost of a somewhat weaker-looking count on the face of the complaint.

The recommendation is: **include B2 as a secondary count, plead fraud as the lead theory with negligent misrepresentation in the alternative, anchor both in the Prieve "no hail after 2020" statement plus the two other candidate misrepresentations, and prepare for summary-judgment attrition of the reliance element** — accepting that the punitive-damages lane and the scienter-supported discovery are B2's real value.

### 3.2 Element 2 — Knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard

- **The public factual basis is Google-able.** NWS Twin Cities/Chanhassen data, the NOAA Storm Events Database, and stormersite are open-source tools a forensic engineer retained to evaluate hail causation would be expected to consult competently. Prieve either consulted them and misstated the results or failed to consult them — both support the "without knowing whether it was true or false" prong of fraud scienter and clearly support the "failure to exercise reasonable care" prong of negligent misrepresentation. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 9; Evidence/Significant Wind and Hail in Minnesota July 13-14.pdf)*
- **Farmers' own adjuster Brau had contradicting information in hand by 9/6/2024** (dents observed, dimensions measured). Farmers then commissioned Prieve to reach a no-causation conclusion, incorporated Prieve's factually false weather statement into the 11/4/2024 denial, and transmitted that denial to the family. Commissioning and distributing a report whose central factual premise is contradicted by the commissioning party's own field observations supports scienter on a reckless-disregard theory. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md; EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 4–10)*
- **Farmers persisted in its position after receiving conclusive contrary evidence.** Between 11/19/2024 (Commerce complaint) and 5/28/2025 (NWS submission through agent Pete), the family provided Farmers with NWS data, stormersite data, City of Eden Prairie permit records, and the 14-of-19 neighbor canvass. Farmers' response was (i) a 2/3/2025 blanket refusal to consider evidence unless it came from an engineer; (ii) the 2/10/2025 Brau voicemail confirming that rule; and (iii) no substantive response to the 5/28/2025 NWS submission that Pete personally uploaded. Persistence in a representation after receiving disproof is evidence of original recklessness and of ongoing fraud. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md; EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG)*
- **The 9/12/2025 appraisal award contradicts Farmers' adopted position.** When Farmers' own panel (including Farmers-selected appraiser Tomes) was presented with the full record, the resulting award confirmed hail as the cause of loss — demonstrating that Farmers' earlier "no hail after 2020" premise could not survive neutral factfinding. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png)*

### 3.3 Element 3 — Intent to induce reliance

- **Farmers sent the 11/4/2024 denial letter directly to the family** as the reason the claim was being closed with no payment. The natural, foreseeable, and intended consequence was that the family would accept the denial and not pursue coverage. A statement made in an insurer's formal denial letter is made to induce the insured to accept the denial; that is precisely its function. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf)*
- **The 9/3/2024 denial letter functioned the same way** and was delivered with the same purpose. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf)*
- **The "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule was communicated directly to the family** by supervisor Monson (2/3/2025) and by adjuster Brau (2/10/2025). The purpose was to induce the family to stop submitting non-engineer evidence and to accept the prior denials. *(EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG)*
- **Defensive fact to acknowledge:** Prieve's report itself states "This report is to be utilized only by the contracted party," which Farmers can use to argue Prieve did not intend to induce the family's reliance. The counter is that Farmers — not Prieve — is the defendant on the B2 count, and Farmers' adoption of Prieve's report for the denial letter sent to the family is the act that supplies intent. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 4–5)*

### 3.4 Element 4 — Reliance, and its weaknesses (the centrally vulnerable element)

This is the B2 count's single largest weak point, and it has to be confronted directly in the pleading rather than papered over.

**Points against reliance:**

- The family opened a Commerce complaint on 11/19/2024 — 15 days after the 11/4/2024 denial — expressly rebutting Prieve's weather claim. *(EventDocuments/11-19-complaint-to-dept-of-commerce.pdf)*
- The family filed a detailed 1/31/2025 written appeal rebutting Prieve's "no hail after 2020" claim with stormersite data. *(EventDocuments/2025-01-31-ResponseToFarmer-01-31-25-StormsAfter2020.pdf)*
- The family filed a 2/10/2025 second written response with the City permit data and the neighbor-canvass results. *(EventDocuments/2025-02-10-SecondResponseToFinalReport.pdf)*
- The family submitted the NWS Twin Cities/Chanhassen article through agent Pete on 5/28/2025. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md)*
- The family demanded appraisal on 8/3/2025 and ultimately obtained an award confirming hail causation on 9/12/2025. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf; EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png)*

On their face, these facts show the family never accepted Farmers' false representations.

**Points available to support reliance (recharacterized):**

- **Reliance on the appraisal channel as the route to resolution.** After the 2/10/2025 voicemail confirmed Farmers would not review any non-engineer evidence, the family pivoted away from continued administrative appeals and toward appraisal. That pivot — including the $2,200 retainer for Foster (split with Pure Construction, $1,100 of which the family paid) and the time/expense of the 9/12/2025 appraisal — is a concrete, documented action the family took at least in part in reliance on Farmers' representation that no further substantive review would occur absent an engineer's report. Had Farmers engaged with the 1/31 and 2/10 evidence in good faith, the costs of the appraisal channel would have been avoided. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf; EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG)*
- **Reliance on the premise that litigation was not immediately necessary.** The family's 7/10/2025 email to Brau, Monson, and Pete asking about claim deadlines and requesting an extension if any applied, and Brau's 7/11/2025 boilerplate non-answer, show the family was operating under the impression that Farmers' claims-handling process had to be exhausted before litigation. Farmers' refusal to clarify the deadline while simultaneously refusing to engage with the evidence is a form of induced reliance on continued administrative process that delayed filing suit. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (7/10, 7/11/2025 entries))*
- **Reliance on the policy's continuing in-force validity.** The family continued paying premium through the loss date and beyond on the implicit understanding that Farmers would process hail claims in good faith. Retention of premium on a contract whose claims-handling obligations Farmers had already, on this theory, fraudulently disavowed can support a modest reliance-damages theory. *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf)*

None of these recharacterizations is bulletproof. All of them share the problem that the damages they produce are either small (appraisal costs, filing fees) or hard to separate cleanly from contract damages (delayed consequential water damage). The pleading must pick the strongest reliance frame — likely the "reliance on appraisal as the route to resolution" frame — and build around it.

### 3.5 Element 5 — Pecuniary damages

Out-of-pocket reliance damages on the B2 count, distinct from contract damages under A1, include:

- **Appraisal process costs** — $1,100 share of Foster's $2,200 retainer. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf p. 2)*
- **Commerce complaint preparation costs** and family time invested in the 11/19/2024 filing.
- **$30 MN Lawyer Referral fee** (refunded, but still a documented out-of-pocket reliance expense pending refund). *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md)*
- **Ongoing consequential water damage.** The 10/28/2025 email documents active window leaks on a rainy day. To the extent these leaks progressed during the period in which the family was pursuing the routes Farmers induced (administrative appeals, appraisal) rather than promptly filing suit, the incremental water damage is reliance damages. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/28/2025 entry))*
- **Opportunity cost / out-of-pocket costs of the post-denial evidence development** — the family's 2/4–2/7/2025 neighbor canvass (40+ doors in cold weather), 2/3/2025 permit records request, 6/6–6/18/2025 outreach to DNR, FOX9, Bar Association, Law Library, Self Help Center, Lawyer Referral, and AG's office. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md)*
- **Punitive damages** under Minn. Stat. § 549.20 (added by later motion under § 549.191). The fraud theory, if proven with the deliberate-disregard standard by clear and convincing evidence, supports punitive exposure A1 cannot reach and § 604.18 caps.
- **Fees/costs per Minn. Stat. § 549.09** (prejudgment interest).

These damages are not large in absolute dollars. They are independently recoverable and do not overlap with the policy-proceeds recovery that A1 produces, which is the relevant test under the out-of-pocket rule in *Lewis v. Citizens Agency*. The punitive-damages component is the largest potential B2 upside.

### 3.6 Additional facts relevant only to the negligent-misrepresentation branch

- **Prieve is a retained expert whose professional output is presented as reliable information.** Prieve's report is styled as a forensic engineering report, includes credentials, a "File #241008," and opinions rendered in professional capacity. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 4–10)* These features support the Restatement § 552(1) predicate "in the course of his business, profession or employment" and "supplies false information for the guidance of others in their business transactions," at least as applied to Prieve personally. *Bonhiver v. Graff*, 248 N.W.2d at 298–99 (accountant's mistaken representations to third party actionable).
- **Farmers is Prieve's "contracted party."** The disclaimer language "This report is to be utilized only by the contracted party" is an obstacle to § 552 liability running from Prieve directly to the family, but does not foreclose Farmers' own liability when Farmers distributes the report as part of its denial communication. The § 552 analysis against Farmers depends on whether a first-party insurer communicating a denial is in a transaction in which it has a pecuniary interest (yes) and is supplying information for the guidance of the insured in its business transactions (contested). *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 5)*
- **Safeco / arm's-length barrier.** In a genuine arm's-length first-party claim dispute, Minnesota courts have hesitated to impose a § 552 duty. *Safeco*, 531 N.W.2d at 870. The insured's natural counter is that a first-party insurance-claims communication is not classic "arm's length" in the way a commercial negotiation between businesses is — there is an asymmetric information relationship, the insured is obligated to cooperate, and the insurer is the only party in a position to evaluate covered-loss facts. That counter is available but not free of doctrinal friction.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No. B2 is structurally independent of the appraisal award on both counts (fraud and negligent misrepresentation).** The tort claims rest on false statements of fact made during the claims-handling process in 2024–2025, well before the appraisal demand and award. Nothing about proving or disproving those torts requires the court to alter the appraisal award.

In fact, B2 is arguably the **cleanest standalone claim** in the entire argument catalog with respect to the appraisal, because the appraisal award actually **corroborates** the central B2 misrepresentation theory: the award confirms hail causation, which is exactly what Prieve's "no hail after 2020" statement denied.

### 4.2 Why B2 is structurally independent

The appraisal award under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 resolves the contractual "amount of loss" dispute. It does not — and could not — adjudicate tort liability. Two doctrinal points are relevant:

- **Scope of appraisal.** Under *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), appraisers determine "amount of loss," which includes causation of the loss. Appraisers do not adjudicate tort claims, do not determine whether false statements were made, and do not allocate punitive damages. The appraisal's binding effect runs only to the contract claim's amount-of-loss component.
- **Preclusion.** The appraisal award does not have issue-preclusive effect on tort elements the panel never addressed. Reliance, scienter, intent to induce, and reliance damages are not "amount of loss" questions. *Cf. Hauschildt v. Beckingham*, 686 N.W.2d 829, 837 (Minn. 2004) (collateral estoppel requires the issue to have been actually and necessarily decided in the prior proceeding).

The fraud and negligent-misrepresentation claims can therefore proceed regardless of whether the award is upheld, modified, vacated, or left untouched.

### 4.3 How the appraisal interacts with B2 without barring it

Three interactions are worth flagging because they shape pleading strategy.

**Interaction 1 — Appraisal award as affirmative evidence of falsity.** The 9/12/2025 award's hail-causation finding is affirmative evidence that Prieve's "no hail after 2020" representation and Farmers' adopted-in-its-denial reiteration of that premise were false. The award is better evidence for B2 if it stands than if it is vacated. Vacatur would not kill the B2 claim, but it would remove one of B2's cleanest independent proofs of falsity. This is one of the few arguments on the docket for which award-preservation is net-positive.

**Interaction 2 — Appraisal bar in § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) does not apply to B2.** Minn. Stat. § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) provides that "[a]n award of taxable costs under this section is not available in any claim that is resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal." That bar is specific to § 604.18 statutory bad-faith remedies. It does not reach common-law fraud or negligent-misrepresentation claims. If the appraisal bar defeats B1 on this record — an open question, depending on how broadly a court reads "resolved or confirmed" — B2 is the only remaining misconduct-adjacent theory. That elevates B2's strategic importance in the belt-and-suspenders calculation even where its standalone merits are modest.

**Interaction 3 — Damages measurement.** B2's out-of-pocket reliance damages must be quantified without double-counting the policy proceeds A1 seeks. The appraisal award's $12,800 RCV for the windows it evaluated is the metric against which any overlap is measured. If the family recovers $X under A1 for covered hail damage (whether by modifying the award, by adding unevaluated windows, or by matching relief), that $X is not also recoverable under B2. B2 damages are the reliance-specific items listed in § 3.5 — appraisal costs, filing fees, consequential water damage accrued during the induced delay, punitive exposure — and those do not overlap with the contract recovery. The award's existence therefore constrains B2's damages accounting but does not eliminate B2's path to meaningful recovery (principally through § 549.20 punitives, if the standard is met).

### 4.4 Pleading framing implications

Because B2 does not depend on disturbing the award, the filing strategy in the complaint should:

1. **Plead B2 without incorporating or adopting the appraisal attack** (A3 in the argument list). B2 should stand on its own factual predicates — Prieve's "no hail after 2020" statement, the 9/3 "no other damages observed" statement, and the "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule — regardless of what happens to the award.
2. **Use the appraisal award's hail-causation finding as affirmative evidence of falsity.** The complaint's B2 count should expressly cite the 9/12/2025 award's hail-causation confirmation as one of the independent proofs that Prieve's weather-history statement was false.
3. **Expressly plead reliance damages separate from contract damages** (appraisal costs, Commerce-complaint costs, consequential water damage, and punitive exposure under § 549.20). This preserves the B2 count's independent economic footprint and insulates it from any motion to dismiss on a theory that B2 is merely duplicative of A1.
4. **Preserve B2 as the non-§ 604.18-barred misconduct count.** If B1 is struck under § 604.18, Subd. 4(b), the B2 count becomes the only surviving vehicle for punitive damages and for framing Farmers' representations as actionable wrongdoing. That strategic value is independent of the award's status.

### 4.5 Bottom line on the key question

- B2 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be filed, to survive Rule 12, or to reach the merits.
- B2 is **strengthened, not weakened**, when the award stands — because the award's hail-causation finding is affirmative evidence that Farmers' adopted-in-denial representations about the weather history were false.
- B2 is the only misconduct-adjacent count whose availability is **not constrained by the § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar**. That independence is B2's clearest structural value in the overall pleading plan.
- The cleanest framing is therefore: **plead B2 as a standalone tort count, anchor it in the three specific misrepresentations identified in § 3.1, cite the appraisal award as evidence of falsity, seek reliance damages and (by later § 549.191 motion) punitive damages under § 549.20, and carry B2 alongside A1 through summary judgment even accepting that the reliance element will be the likely death point.**

## 6. Open Items and Next Steps Specific to B2

1. **Decision: include or omit B2.** The case for inclusion is (a) a genuinely disprovable factual misrepresentation in Prieve's weather statement, (b) the punitive-damages lane under § 549.20, and (c) insulation from the § 604.18, Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar. The case for omission is (a) thin reliance, (b) partial damages duplication with A1, and (c) some risk of a *Short v. Dairyland*-style recharacterization by the court. A neutral default is to include B2 as a secondary count and drop at the first summary-judgment opportunity if reliance cannot be developed through discovery.
2. **Confirm Minnesota's most recent case law on fraud claims against first-party insurers** — specifically whether appellate courts are permitting such counts past summary judgment on reliance theories built on post-denial conduct (appraisal-route reliance, litigation-delay reliance). A focused Westlaw search of the post-*Sela* (2021) authority.
3. **Discovery priorities driven by B2 specifically** (in addition to the A1/B1 lists): Prieve's underlying weather-research file and contemporaneous notes; Farmers' internal review of Prieve's draft report; internal communications regarding the "engineer's report only" rule; Brau's 9/6/2024 inspection notes (or the lack thereof); Hancock's workproduct beyond the photo set.
4. **Confirm Foster's written account of the appraisal panel's treatment of the weather evidence** — whether the panel considered the NWS data, stormersite, and permit records in reaching the hail-causation conclusion. A positive answer strengthens the affirmative-falsity prong of B2 (the same evidence Farmers refused to review was dispositive at the panel).
5. **Preserve the § 549.20 punitive-damages lane** by pleading B2 with the required scienter allegations and filing a § 549.191 motion to amend at an appropriate point post-discovery. § 549.20 requires "clear and convincing evidence" of "deliberate disregard for the rights or safety of others" — a higher bar than § 604.18's "reckless disregard" but reachable if discovery yields Prieve's underlying research file and Farmers' internal communications.
6. **Pleading order in the complaint.** Because B2's reliance element is the weakest, the complaint should pair B2 with a clear alternative-pleading recital that preserves A1 as the primary recovery vehicle and does not invite the court to read B2 as a recharacterization of the contract claim.
7. **Watch for a *Short v. Dairyland* invitation.** Farmers will likely move to dismiss B2 on the theory that the alleged conduct is really claims-handling misconduct dressed as fraud. The most durable response is the concrete-factual-misstatement framing of Prieve's "no hail after 2020" line — a statement about historical weather, not about coverage judgment — which is outside *Short*'s reasoning.
