## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The endorsement's actual text

The first task is to read what J6944 actually says. The endorsement was attached to the policy at inception (effective 11/25/2014) and remained in force on the 7/13/2024 loss date (`HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf`, "Policy Endorsements" subsection, pp. 1–2 (the J6944 endorsement appears immediately after the J6898 "Endorsement Amending General Conditions" page); `HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf` "Active endorsements" line). The full text, in context, modifies "Section I — Property Conditions, 5. How We Settle Covered Loss" by adding:

> "We do not cover as part of the settlement of covered loss any:
>
> (1) cost to repair or replace property which is **not damaged by the covered peril** and does not match with the materials used to repair or replace damaged property because of:
>     i. marring; or
>     ii. wear, tear, deterioration, corrosion or decay; or
>     iii. **obsolescence or discontinuation**; or
>
> (2) actual or perceived loss in value to any property because materials used to repair or replace the lost or damaged property does not match undamaged property; or
>
> (3) consequential loss or damage to property which is a result of a condition of the property which is uninsured or excluded under Section I — Uninsured Loss or Damage and Excluded Causes of Loss or Damage."

A buy-back box follows: "For an added premium, if this box is 'X'd,' then item (1) (iii) will not apply to loss involving exterior siding or roof coverings, including soffit and facia, on a damaged building structure," subject to (1) per-loss caps, (2) per-building application, (3) "not additional insurance," and (4) actual-incurrence requirement. By its express terms, this buy-back applies only to "exterior siding or roof coverings, including soffit and facia." It does not reach windows. Whether the box was X'd in the Lu policy is a discrete factual question for pre-filing review (the renewal declaration shows J6944 as an active endorsement but does not state whether the (1)(iii) buy-back was elected); even if X'd, it would not modify the windows analysis.

The text that controls the E2 analysis therefore has three operative limits embedded in its own structure:

- **Damage gating.** Subparagraph (1) reaches only "property which is **not damaged by the covered peril**." Damaged property is outside the endorsement's exclusion entirely. (`HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` p. 2542)
- **Reason gating.** Subparagraph (1) bars only mismatches arising from one of three enumerated reasons — marring; wear, tear, deterioration, corrosion or decay; or obsolescence or discontinuation. Mismatches arising from any other cause (e.g., the impossibility of source-matching specialty extruded aluminum frames generally available in 1993, 1997, and 2008 to anything currently in production) sit outside the enumerated reasons.
- **Diminution gating.** Subparagraph (2) bars only "actual or perceived loss in value to any property because" of the mismatch. It is a diminution-in-value bar; it is not a bar to coverage for the cost of replacement of damaged property.

These textual limits are the engine of the E2 argument. The argument is not that J6944 is unenforceable — *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* foreclose that — but that J6944 by its own terms does not reach what Farmers will try to use it for in this case.

### 1.2 The Minnesota matching baseline: *Cedar Bluff*

The starting point is the Minnesota Supreme Court's decision in *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condominium Association, Inc. v. American Family Mutual Insurance Co.*, 857 N.W.2d 290 (Minn. 2014). On a hail loss, the appraisal panel awarded full siding replacement on all 20 buildings of a townhome complex — even buildings whose siding was not physically dented — because the replacement panels available could not provide a reasonable color match to the existing panels. The Supreme Court affirmed the appraisal panel's interpretation, holding that the policy's "comparable material and quality" language permitted the panel to require a reasonable color match between damaged and undamaged siding, and that the inability to match constituted a "distinct, demonstrable, and physical alteration" requiring full replacement.

Two practical takeaways from *Cedar Bluff* matter for E2: (a) under a base policy without a matching exclusion, Minnesota recognizes a matching obligation that can require replacement of undamaged property when reasonable matching cannot be achieved; and (b) the appraisal panel has authority to factor matching into the "amount of loss" determination. *Cedar Bluff* is the cause of every matching-exclusion endorsement that came after, including J6944.

### 1.3 The matching-exclusion enforceability line: *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek*

After *Cedar Bluff*, insurers responded by adding endorsements that bar matching coverage. Two cases test those endorsements in Minnesota:

- ***Noonan v. American Family Mutual Insurance Co.***, 929 F.3d 633 (8th Cir. 2019). The 8th Circuit held that American Family's matching-exclusion endorsement — which stated American Family "will not pay to repair or replace undamaged property due to mismatch between undamaged material and new material used to repair or replace damaged material" — was unambiguous and enforceable. The court rejected the argument that the underlying form's coverage swallowed the endorsement, and applied the exclusion to deny matching coverage on a partial roof loss. Applied to our policy, *Noonan* means a Minnesota court (state or federal) will not strike J6944 as unenforceable on its face.
- ***Pleasure Creek Townhomes Homeowners Ass'n v. American Family Mutual Insurance Co.***, A19-0563 (Minn. Ct. App. 2019). The Minnesota Court of Appeals followed *Noonan* and upheld a similar matching exclusion under Minnesota state law. *Pleasure Creek* eliminates the open question of whether Minnesota state courts would diverge from the 8th Circuit; they have not.

The combined effect of *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* is that the family cannot win E2 by attacking J6944's enforceability. The family must instead win on scope: J6944, even fully enforceable, does not reach the result Farmers wants.

### 1.4 The discontinued-manufacturer angle: *Trout Brook* and the Sierra Pacific complication

A separate decision is relevant to the "obsolescence or discontinuation" language in J6944(1)(iii) — *Trout Brook South Condominium Ass'n v. Harleysville Worcester Insurance Co.*, 995 F. Supp. 2d 1035 (D. Minn. 2014). Hail damaged a portion of a condominium roof; the original shingle line was no longer manufactured. The court held there was a jury question whether the building "suffered a loss" because no matching shingles were available, and refused to grant summary judgment for the insurer.

*Trout Brook* helps the family in three ways. First, it confirms that matching-impossibility is a live factual question Minnesota federal courts have entertained on a discontinuation theory. Second, *Trout Brook* defined "covered property" as the building rather than as discrete units within the building, supporting an argument that hail damage to one elevation impairs the building's matched architectural integrity as a whole. Third, *Trout Brook* predates J6944's "obsolescence or discontinuation" subparagraph and is consistent with reading J6944(1)(iii) as the insurer's response to *Trout Brook*-type arguments.

But *Trout Brook* hurts the family in one important way: J6944(1)(iii) is precisely the kind of textual language that closes the door *Trout Brook* opened. Where the policy in *Trout Brook* had no specific discontinuation exclusion, J6944 has one. So *Trout Brook* maps onto the family's argument as historical context — the reason the endorsement was written — rather than as a holding the family can apply directly.

A second complication is factual rather than legal. Sierra Pacific Windows acquired Semco Window and Doors' operating assets in February 2020, including the 275,000-square-foot Merrill, Wisconsin, manufacturing facility and production equipment. Whether Sierra Pacific produces or can produce a matching profile for the family's specific 1993, 1997, and 2008 sash stamps is unknown on the current record but is a key pre-filing question. If Sierra Pacific can supply a matching window, the "matching impossible" framing weakens. If not, the framing holds.

### 1.5 *Quade* and the appraisal-causation backbone

*Quade v. Secura Insurance*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), holds that "amount of loss" under the appraisal clause includes causation. The 9/12/2025 panel found hail causation on the windows category. That binding causation finding is the foundation of the E2 argument: it removes the threshold defenses Farmers ran in the 9/3/2024 ("no other damages") and 11/4/2024 (wear/tear; marring; corrosion; inherent vice; neglect; faulty construction) denial letters and forces the dispute to be about scope and matching only.

### 1.6 What this argument looks like as a count

E2 is best pleaded as a sub-component of A2 (declaratory judgment) rather than as a freestanding cause of action. The natural pleading framing is:

> "Plaintiffs respectfully request that this Court declare, pursuant to Minn. Stat. Ch. 555, that:
> (a) Endorsement J6944 ('Limited Matching of Undamaged Property') applies, by its own terms, only to 'property which is not damaged by the covered peril,' and accordingly does not bar coverage for the replacement cost of windows that sustained hail damage on July 13, 2024;
> (b) Endorsement J6944, subparagraph (2), bars only the 'actual or perceived loss in value' to property due to mismatch and does not modify Defendant's obligation to pay the replacement cost of the damaged windows under the policy's 'comparable material and quality' standard;
> (c) The replacement cost obligation under the policy is the cost of replacing the damaged windows with windows of comparable material and quality currently available in the market — not the cost of replacing them with the original-equipment windows whose manufacturer is, as Defendant has acknowledged, no longer available;
> (d) To the extent Defendant relies on subparagraph (1)(iii) (obsolescence or discontinuation), that subparagraph applies only to property that is undamaged, and is further limited by its own terms because the buy-back box exception applies (if at all) only to siding and roof coverings, not windows; and
> (e) Plaintiffs' coverage for the damaged windows is not reduced by any matching limitation J6944 imposes."

Pleaded this way, E2 is a clean declaratory-relief target: it asks the court to interpret J6944, not to set anything aside. It does not require any finding about additional windows beyond the F1.7 / B1.2 windows in the appraisal award (although the family will still argue separately under A1 and A3 that other windows should have been included).

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **35 / 100** as an affirmative theory of liability; **55 / 100** as a defensive scope-limits / declaratory sub-issue

E2 is genuinely two arguments. They have different strengths.

**As an affirmative path to recovery (~35/100).** The realistic affirmative payoff of E2 is to push replacement cost meaningfully above the $17,720 deductible by either (a) requiring matching across all windows on a building or facade, or (b) re-pricing the F1.7/B1.2 award based on actual market replacement of the damaged windows (rather than phantom Semco pricing, if that is in fact what the panel used). Both paths are uphill:
- *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* uphold J6944-type exclusions on enforceability, leaving only scope/application arguments.
- The "all windows are damaged so J6944 isn't engaged" framing depends on either (i) showing the appraisal panel undercounted damaged windows (which is A3 work, not E2 work) or (ii) persuading a court to hold that scope claims for unaddressed windows survive the binding award. Neither is foreclosed; neither is easy.
- The "discontinuation creates an impossibility-of-matching obligation" framing runs straight into J6944(1)(iii), which textually addresses obsolescence or discontinuation as a basis for the matching exclusion. The family has to read J6944(1)(iii) as reaching only undamaged property — a textually defensible reading, but not one a court will adopt automatically.
- The factual predicate for the "manufacturer defunct" framing is unsettled. The family's account names "Semco Windows and Doors" and dates closure to 2019 (corroborated by independent reporting that Semco of Merrill, WI ceased operations on 12/31/2019, and Sierra Pacific Windows acquired the assets in February 2020). The 9/18/2025 Farmers email names "Senco" — which appears in publicly available information as a separate going-concern company (Senco Industries). The Senco/Semco identification has to be resolved before the family argues "manufacturer defunct" in court.

**As a defensive scope-limits / declaratory sub-issue (~55/100).** Read as a request that the court interpret J6944 and pin Farmers down to what it actually limits:
- The endorsement text itself is favorable. Its damage gating ("not damaged by the covered peril"), its reason gating (only marring, wear/tear, or obsolescence), and its diminution gating (subparagraph (2) is a diminution-in-value bar, not a coverage bar) all give the family textual hooks for the interpretive ruling it wants.
- *Cedar Bluff* establishes that matching is part of "amount of loss" in Minnesota when the policy permits; J6944 modifies but does not eliminate that — it modifies it only with respect to undamaged property's mismatch from three enumerated reasons.
- *Quade* binds Farmers to causation. *Cedar Bluff* binds Farmers to the matching dimension of "amount of loss." J6944 cannot logically bar what *Cedar Bluff*'s reasoning recognizes as part of "amount of loss" except for property that is both (i) undamaged and (ii) mismatched for one of the three enumerated reasons. The interpretive ruling the family seeks does not fight either case; it harmonizes them.
- A declaratory ruling is a low-friction request. The court does not have to vacate, modify, or remand the appraisal to issue it.

**Weighted blend.** Plaintiffs filing E2 should expect the affirmative version to do less work than the defensive/declaratory version. Practically, E2 is most useful as a tool to (a) preempt and narrow Farmers' anticipated affirmative defense and (b) preserve the matching question for further factual development (e.g., expert window-matching analysis, Sierra Pacific availability) without putting the entire case on the matching theory. **A blended rating of about 40/100 reflects that profile.**

### 2.2 What drives the rating up

- **The textual hooks are real, not strained.** "Not damaged by the covered peril" plainly excludes damaged property from the endorsement's reach. "Actual or perceived loss in value … because materials used to repair or replace the lost or damaged property does not match undamaged property" plainly addresses diminution, not coverage. "Obsolescence or discontinuation" is itself one of three reason-gates for the matching limit, indicating the drafters treated it as one specific scenario rather than a free-floating bar.
- **The family's photographic record and Prieve's own narrative document damage on most of the home's windows.** Prieve described dents, marks, and indentations across the front/west triple window above the main entry, the upper-west 3-unit bedroom, the upper southwest bedroom, the east family-room bay projection, the upper-east window above the four-season porch, the four-season porch SE/E/NE/N windows, the kitchen east, the main-level NE 3-unit, the upper-north above the kitchen, and the rear/east garage window. (`EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf` pp. 4–9). On Farmers' own expert's narrative, the universe of plausibly damaged windows is large enough that the J6944 "undamaged property" gate excludes most of the home from the exclusion's reach — even without an A3 expansion of the appraisal scope.
- **The 9/12/2025 award establishes hail causation as a binding finding under *Quade*.** Farmers cannot reopen causation. A defendant that has lost causation and is now litigating only scope and matching finds it harder to argue that J6944 "decides" the case.
- **The buy-back's express limit to siding and roofing helps the family on the windows analysis.** Even the policy itself treats the (1)(iii) discontinuation exclusion as inappropriate for some structural categories (siding/roofing) once additional premium is paid. A court reading J6944 in pari materia can fairly note that windows are not the kind of large-area uniform surface (like an entire roof or all four siding elevations) on which (1)(iii) was designed to operate; rather, windows are individual fenestration units that, when individually damaged, sit entirely outside the (1) gating.
- **No Minnesota appellate case forecloses the scope-limits reading.** *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* enforce matching exclusions; neither holds that a matching exclusion bars coverage for replacement of damaged property that the insurer concedes (or the appraisal binds it on) was hit by a covered peril. The family is not asking a court to undo *Noonan*; the family is asking a court to read J6944 the way *Noonan* would read it on these facts.

### 2.3 What drives the rating down

- **The realistic dollar mechanism for E2 to defeat the deductible is matching-driven full window replacement, and that mechanism runs straight into J6944(1)(iii).** A court asked to require Farmers to pay for replacing every window on the home because the existing windows cannot be matched is being asked to do exactly what J6944(1)(iii) was written to bar. The family's textual answer — that (1)(iii) only reaches undamaged property — is correct but not powerful, because the practical scenario where (1)(iii) bites is precisely the scenario where the family wants the matching obligation to attach.
- **The Senco/Semco identification problem.** The family cannot hold a court's attention on the manufacturer-defunct framing while the record itself disagrees on whether the windows were made by Semco (defunct, with assets at Sierra Pacific) or Senco (a going concern). Pre-filing factual cleanup is required.
- **The Sierra Pacific acquisition narrows the impossibility argument.** Sierra Pacific bought Semco's assets in February 2020. If Sierra Pacific can supply matching extruded aluminum profiles or close equivalents — even on special order — the "matching is literally impossible" framing softens to "matching is hard." That softening is enough to let *Noonan* / *Pleasure Creek* do work.
- **The appraisal award is binding on what it decided, and it decided only the F1.7/B1.2 scope.** *Quade* holds that the panel can decide causation; the panel decided causation for the windows it evaluated. Outside the appraised scope, the family's "all windows are damaged" framing is unproven on the litigation record and would have to be developed through expert testimony and an A3 remand or A1 trial of fact. E2 is not self-executing — it depends on a damage-scope finding, which is not E2's job.
- **Insured's appraiser signed.** The signature complicates not just A3 but E2 as well. A court considering whether to read J6944 as not reaching certain windows (because they are damaged) will want to know whether the panel — including the family's own appraiser — found those windows damaged. On the current record, the answer is no for everything outside F1.7/B1.2. That undercuts E2 unless coupled with A3 or with new expert evidence.
- **The "amount of loss" / coverage line in *Quade* and *Oliver* limits how far a coverage-side declaratory ruling can rewrite the panel's work.** *Quade* says causation belongs to the panel; coverage belongs to the court. *Oliver v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Ins. Co.*, 939 N.W.2d 749 (Minn. 2020), confirms the appraisal/UAA distinction. A court asked to declare that J6944 "does not bar matching coverage" will distinguish between (a) interpretive rulings on policy language (within the court's job) and (b) rewriting the panel's "amount of loss" findings (outside the court's job). E2 needs to stay on the (a) side of that line to stay clean.

### 2.4 What "40/100" means practically

E2 should be filed as a declaratory sub-issue under A2, with three explicit declaratory questions:
1. Does J6944 reach damaged windows? (Textual answer: no; rating: high.)
2. Does J6944 cap the policy's "comparable material and quality" obligation for replacement of damaged windows? (Textual answer: no; rating: medium-high.)
3. Does J6944(1)(iii) bar Farmers from owing matching coverage on undamaged windows where the original manufacturer is no longer available? (Textual answer: maybe — depends on whether the Senco/Semco fact pattern holds, whether Sierra Pacific can supply matches, and whether the buy-back box was X'd; rating: medium-low.)

Questions 1 and 2 are worth filing on their own, even if Question 3 is a longer shot. Questions 1 and 2 are interpretive rulings the court can make from the policy text and *Cedar Bluff* / *Quade* / *Noonan* / *Pleasure Creek* without any new factual development. They give the family a clean declaratory framework to overlay on A1's breach-of-contract count and any A3 remand.

Question 3 is the higher-risk, higher-reward sub-issue. It is the one that, if it lands, opens up wholesale window replacement under a *Cedar Bluff*-style theory. It should be pleaded but not relied upon as the path to break the deductible.

## 4. Standalone or Dependent on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 The short answer

It depends on which version of E2. Decomposed:

| Version of E2 | Standalone? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| (a) **Declaratory ruling that J6944 does not bar coverage for replacement of damaged windows.** | **Yes.** | Pure interpretive ruling on policy text. *Quade* binds Farmers to causation on F1.7/B1.2; the family asks the court only to confirm J6944's textual scope. No vacatur, modification, or correction of the appraisal is required. |
| (b) **Declaratory ruling that J6944(2)'s diminution-in-value bar does not modify the policy's "comparable material and quality" obligation for damaged-property replacement.** | **Yes.** | Same as (a). Pure textual interpretation. The court reads J6944(2) against the policy's replacement-cost provision; the appraisal is irrelevant to that interpretation. |
| (c) **Declaratory ruling that J6944(1)(iii) does not bar replacement of undamaged windows where the manufacturer is unavailable, applied to the Lu home's actual facts.** | **Mostly yes.** | The interpretation is standalone. The application requires a factual finding about manufacturer availability and matching impossibility, which is independent of the appraisal. |
| (d) **A claim for the cost of replacing all windows on the home (or on a specific facade) under a *Cedar Bluff*-style matching theory.** | **No — depends on either A3 work or independent damage findings outside the appraisal.** | The appraisal addressed only F1.7/B1.2. To convert E2 into recovery for additional windows, the family must either (i) expand the appraisal scope through A3 (remand for clarification under *Herll*), or (ii) persuade a court that windows the appraisal did not address are still in play under A1 / A2. |
| (e) **A re-pricing of the F1.7/B1.2 award to reflect actual market replacement cost rather than phantom Semco pricing.** | **Mixed — likely requires A3.** | If the panel based the $12,800 RCV on unobtainable Semco pricing, the corrective remedy is modification or remand of the award under A3 (*Herll* / *Mork*-style modification), not a freestanding E2 claim. E2 supplies the doctrinal reason the re-pricing is required; A3 supplies the procedural mechanism. |

### 4.2 The longer answer

E2 has two registers. The first is *interpretive* — what does J6944 mean? The second is *applied* — what dollar consequence does that interpretation have on the Lu home's facts?

In its *interpretive* register, E2 is fully standalone. It is the kind of count Minnesota state courts hear all the time under Minn. Stat. Ch. 555. The court does not need the appraisal to be vacated, modified, corrected, or remanded to declare that J6944, by its terms, only excludes from coverage the cost to repair or replace property that is "not damaged by the covered peril" and only when the mismatch arises from one of the three enumerated reasons. That ruling is good for the family even if the appraisal stands exactly as it is, because it forecloses Farmers' anticipated future defense that J6944 does additional work in the case beyond what its text allows.

In its *applied* register, E2 needs more. The deductible-defeating dollar argument runs through one of three channels:

1. **More damage is shown.** If the family proves additional windows beyond F1.7/B1.2 sustained hail damage — through expert testimony, the existing photographic record, the HailShapes scientific framework, and the Prieve narrative — then those windows are "damaged by the covered peril" within the meaning of J6944(1) and outside the endorsement's scope. The court can declare under E2 that the matching exclusion does not reach them, and A1's breach-of-contract count delivers the recovery. *This channel does not require vacatur or modification of the appraisal,* but it does require either (a) a court ruling that the appraisal addressed only F1.7/B1.2 and left other window damage unaddressed (so the family can litigate the rest under A1), or (b) an A3 remand for clarification under *Herll* to confirm what the panel did and did not evaluate.
2. **Matching obligation attaches under *Cedar Bluff*.** If the family shows that replacement of the F1.7/B1.2 windows cannot achieve a reasonable match with the rest of the home — because the original manufacturer is unavailable and no current production line provides a comparable profile — then the *Cedar Bluff* "comparable material and quality" obligation may require replacement of additional windows. *This channel requires the court to read J6944 narrowly enough to leave Cedar Bluff's matching obligation in place for damaged-property repair.* It does not require vacating the appraisal, but it does require the kind of expert window-matching opinion noted in §3.7. The textual hook is J6944's gating to "undamaged property" plus J6944(2)'s diminution-only language.
3. **The award itself was priced wrong.** If the $12,800 RCV reflects phantom Semco pricing or a non-current product line, the cure is modification or remand of the award under A3, with E2 supplying the underlying doctrinal explanation for why "comparable material and quality" requires a different number. This channel does require A3 work; E2 alone does not produce the corrective number.

### 4.3 Practical pleading recommendation

E2 should appear in two places in the complaint:

- **As declaratory questions in the A2 declaratory-judgment count.** Put the three sub-questions (§1.6) directly into A2's prayer for relief. This is where E2 lives as a pure interpretive matter and where its standalone strength is highest.
- **As a doctrinal predicate for A1 and A3.** Use the J6944 textual hooks to support A1's breach-of-contract theory (Farmers underpaid because, at minimum, J6944 does not reach damaged windows) and to support A3's modification/remand request (the panel's $12,800 RCV is uncertain because it does not state the "comparable material and quality" basis it used; E2 explains why that basis matters under the policy).

E2 does **not** need to be its own freestanding count, and trying to plead it as one over-promises what it can deliver. Plead E2 as relief inside A2, plus as a doctrinal predicate inside A1 and A3, and the architecture stays clean.
