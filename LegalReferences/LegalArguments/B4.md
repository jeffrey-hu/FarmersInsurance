## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 What the argument is, in plain terms

The argument is that once Farmers denied the claim on a specific factual and legal footing (9/3/2024: "no opening / no other damages observed"), it is not free to abandon that footing and assert incompatible grounds later (11/4/2024: "wear/tear / 2020 already paid"; 10/21/2025: "settled per the binding appraisal award / under your deductible"). The family seeks to hold Farmers to some earlier position it took on the claim — or, equivalently, to bar Farmers from now raising a position it previously disclaimed.

This is the conceptual territory of three related Minnesota doctrines:

- **Waiver** — voluntary and intentional relinquishment of a known right.
- **Equitable estoppel** — barring a party from asserting a position inconsistent with one it previously induced the other party to rely on.
- **Promissory estoppel** — enforcing a definite promise where reliance was reasonable and injustice would otherwise result.

It is also adjacent to the common-law "mend the hold" doctrine, which in some jurisdictions bars an insurer from changing denial grounds after the insured has relied on the original grounds. Minnesota has not clearly adopted "mend the hold" as a standalone substantive doctrine in insurance cases.

### 1.2 Minnesota's core doctrinal sources

**Waiver.** "Waiver is the voluntary and intentional relinquishment of a known right. Knowledge and intent are essential elements of waiver, although the requisite knowledge may be actual or constructive and the intent to waive may be inferred from conduct." *Illinois Farmers Ins. Co. v. Glass Serv. Co.*, 683 N.W.2d 792, 798 (Minn. 2004); *see also Engstrom v. Farmers & Bankers Life Ins. Co.*, 230 Minn. 308, 312–13, 41 N.W.2d 422, 424 (1950). Waiver does not require a showing of prejudice to the party asserting it.

**Equitable estoppel.** To invoke equitable estoppel, the party asserting it must prove: (1) promises or inducements were made; (2) reasonable reliance on those promises or inducements; and (3) harm if estoppel is not applied. *See, e.g., Drake v. Reile's Transfer & Delivery, Inc.*, 613 N.W.2d 428, 434 (Minn. Ct. App. 2000) (stating general elements).

**Promissory estoppel.** "A clear and definite promise" by the promisor, who "expected, or reasonably should have expected, the promise to induce definite and substantial action by the promisee," and enforcement is necessary to "avoid an injustice." *Martens v. Minnesota Mining & Mfg. Co.*, 616 N.W.2d 732, 746 (Minn. 2000).

**The *Shannon* limit — the structural bar for B4 as a standalone claim.** The dominant Minnesota rule in the insurance context is that **estoppel may not be used to enlarge an insurance contract**. *Shannon v. Great Am. Ins. Co.*, 276 N.W.2d 77, 78 (Minn. 1979). That rule is why estoppel-of-insurer arguments in Minnesota typically function defensively — to stop an insurer from denying a coverage fact it previously conceded or induced reliance on — rather than offensively, to create coverage that the policy itself does not provide.

### 1.3 How the doctrine operates procedurally in Minnesota

Waiver and estoppel are enumerated affirmative defenses under **Minn. R. Civ. P. 8.03**. They are, by the Rules' design, *defensive* tools: a defendant asserts them to cut off a claim the plaintiff is advancing.

Plaintiffs can and do invoke waiver/estoppel in Minnesota, but they typically do so in two ways:

- **Offensively, inside a live cause of action**, as an argument that a defendant has lost the right to raise an affirmative defense the defendant has pleaded. Example: the family, in opposition to summary judgment or at trial, argues Farmers waived its wear/tear or "2020-already-paid" defenses by failing to include them in the 9/3/2024 denial letter.
- **As a separate count for promissory estoppel**, which Minnesota recognizes as an independent cause of action where the elements are satisfied. *See Martens*, 616 N.W.2d at 746. In the insurance context, this is a narrow pathway — most commonly used where a policy term is ambiguous or not yet issued, or where an agent made a specific promise outside the policy that the insured relied on.

Minnesota does **not** have a well-established standalone "estoppel of denial rationales" cause of action the way some other jurisdictions apply a "mend the hold" rule as substantive insurance law. Based on available sources, Minnesota has not clearly adopted "mend the hold" as a basis for damages on its own.

### 1.4 The statutory overlay — § 72A.201 and § 604.18

Two Minnesota statutes inform the "insurer must explain denial grounds" idea but neither creates the B4 cause of action on its own.

**Minn. Stat. § 72A.201** requires that an insurer "shall not deny a claim on the grounds of a specific policy provision, condition, or exclusion" without referencing that provision in the denial. It is part of the Unfair Claims Practices chapter. The statute does **not** create a private right of action — violations are enforced via complaint to the Minnesota Department of Commerce. The family already exercised that route (11/19/2024 complaint, ID 93547). § 72A.201 functions here as a standard-of-conduct benchmark for § 604.18 and as a regulatory reference, not as a freestanding claim.

**Minn. Stat. § 604.18** is the first-party bad-faith statute. "Shifting denial rationales" are a classic factor supporting the § 604.18 showing that an insurer lacked a reasonable basis and knew it (or acted in reckless disregard). § 604.18 is a separate count (B1), added by motion under § 604.18 Subd. 4 — not pleaded in the initial complaint. The B4 narrative materially supports the B1 motion but does not merge into it procedurally.

### 1.5 What a B4 claim could look like in the complaint

There are three realistic pleading postures, each with different strength profiles:

**Posture 1 — Dedicated count for "Waiver / Equitable Estoppel."** A standalone count pleading that by issuing the 9/3/2024 denial on the "no opening / no other damages observed" theory, Farmers waived or is estopped from now asserting (a) wear/tear; (b) inherent vice, marring, corrosion, neglect, or faulty construction; (c) "already paid in 2020"; and (d) "under the deductible / settled by appraisal" as reasons not to pay. Relief requested would be a judicial declaration that those defenses are unavailable. This is closest to a declaratory-judgment framing and overlaps substantially with A2 (declaratory judgment) and with the evidentiary reply posture described below.

**Posture 2 — B4 as an affirmative reply / scope-limit within A1 (Breach of Contract).** Instead of a separate count, B4 operates inside the A1 pleading: the family pleads that Farmers' current grounds for non-payment are barred by waiver/estoppel because they are inconsistent with the 9/3/2024 denial, and therefore Farmers has breached by refusing to pay without any live, non-waived exclusion. No new relief; no new count. This is the cleanest procedural posture.

**Posture 3 — Promissory Estoppel as an alternative count.** If the family can identify a "clear and definite promise" beyond the adversarial denial language (for example, Pete Pietila's statements while advising the family, or an agent's assurance regarding coverage scope), a promissory-estoppel count could be pleaded. The factual record does not presently contain a definite Farmers promise of coverage; this posture is materially weaker than Postures 1 and 2 on this record.

### 1.6 What this claim looks like on the face of a complaint (Posture 1 version)

> "Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange's September 3, 2024 written denial affirmatively represented that 'no other damages' were observed and that 'no opening' existed — factual positions on which Plaintiffs relied in developing their administrative appeal, their 11/19/2024 complaint to the Minnesota Department of Commerce, and the factual record of this dispute. Defendant thereafter asserted materially inconsistent grounds for non-payment, including wear/tear, inherent vice, marring, neglect, faulty construction, and pre-existing 2020 damage. By its September 3, 2024 representations, Defendant waived, and is equitably estopped from asserting, the later grounds. Alternatively, a declaratory judgment should issue establishing that Defendant's post-September 3, 2024 grounds for non-payment are unavailable to it in this action."

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **25 / 100** as a standalone count; **55 / 100** as a scope-limiting reply within A1

The split rating reflects that B4 is structurally stronger as a tool *inside* other counts than it is as a separately pleaded count.

- **B4 as a standalone count (Posture 1): ~25/100.** Minnesota treats waiver and estoppel primarily as affirmative defenses, not as claims that themselves generate remedies. *Shannon v. Great Am.* caps the upside: estoppel cannot enlarge coverage; it can only bar an insurer from denying something it induced reliance on. A standalone B4 count asking the court to "bar Farmers from asserting later denial grounds" is vulnerable to a Rule 12 dismissal for failure to state a claim independent of the breach-of-contract count it supports. It is also redundant with the declaratory-judgment count (A2), which is a more conventional vehicle for the same relief.
- **B4 as an affirmative reply / scope-limit within A1 (Posture 2): ~55/100.** Inside A1, B4 does real work. It narrows Farmers' live defenses, forecloses the factual premise of both denials for the portion of the claim the appraisal panel evaluated, and supports the narrative that Farmers refused to pay a covered loss despite having abandoned its original reason for doing so. It also feeds directly into the § 604.18 bad-faith motion (B1).
- **B4 as promissory estoppel (Posture 3): ~10/100.** No clear, definite, non-adversarial promise of coverage from Farmers is in the record. Promissory estoppel was not designed for this fact pattern. Pleading it standalone invites a Rule 11 inquiry without comparable upside.

Weighted blend for the question the user asked — "strength as an initial legal claim to be drafted in MN state court" — with the most realistic procedural posture (B4 inside A1 + optional declaratory-judgment packaging under A2): **~30–40/100**, rounded down to **25/100** if the question is strictly "standalone claim B4" and up to **~55/100** if the question allows B4 to function as a scope-limiting tool integrated into A1.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

- **Minnesota's rule against using estoppel to enlarge coverage.** *Shannon v. Great Am. Ins. Co.*, 276 N.W.2d 77, 78 (Minn. 1979). The doctrine cannot be used to create coverage that the policy did not provide. On this record, the policy clearly covers hail damage, so the family does not need to enlarge coverage — but the defendant can still point to *Shannon* to box B4 into a narrow defensive role.
- **No clear Minnesota adoption of "mend the hold."** The doctrine — which bars a contracting party from switching legal positions after litigation is filed — is recognized in some jurisdictions (Illinois most notably) as a substantive rule against insurers that change denial grounds. Available sources do not show Minnesota appellate courts treating "mend the hold" as its own substantive bar in insurance disputes. Citing it here would be an invitation to adopt out-of-state law, not a settled argument.
- **Waiver and estoppel are listed as affirmative defenses in Minn. R. Civ. P. 8.03.** By default, the procedural architecture treats them as tools used against a plaintiff's claim, not as independent claims generating damages.
- **The 9/3/2024 denial contains catch-all exclusion language beyond "no opening / no other damages."** The denial letter cites Water Damage, Wear/Tear, Corrosion, Construction Defect, Inherent Vice, Neglect, Faulty Construction, Windstorm/Hail, and Weather Conditions exclusions as general-list grounds. Farmers will argue the letter expressly preserved these grounds and therefore did not waive them. The family's strongest factual reply is that the primary stated rationale was "no opening / no other damages observed" and that the catch-all list was boilerplate, but a waiver argument has to contend with the preserved catch-all text.
- **The insured signed — through Foster — the appraisal award that settled the amount-of-loss dispute.** "Under your deductible" is not a new denial theory; it is the mathematical consequence of an award the insured's appraiser joined. That position does not sit well as a "shifting rationale" in the *traditional* sense and is distinguishable from the 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials.
- **Waiver's knowledge-and-intent requirement is demanding.** *Illinois Farmers Ins. Co. v. Glass Serv. Co.*, 683 N.W.2d at 798. The family must show that Farmers knew of the wear/tear or 2020-already-paid defenses at the time of the 9/3/2024 denial and intentionally relinquished them. On 9/3/2024 no engineer had been engaged, which cuts both ways: Farmers didn't yet know the wear/tear theory was available (helping Farmers defeat waiver), but Farmers also didn't have a reasonable basis for the "no other damages" finding (supporting § 604.18).
- **Equitable estoppel requires reliance to the family's detriment.** The family can document reliance on the 9/3/2024 denial (developing the administrative record around that theory, filing the Commerce complaint around that theory), but tying the reliance to concrete, quantifiable harm for estoppel purposes is not a turnkey exercise.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up (especially in the Posture 2 reply role)

- **The denials' factual premises are genuinely incompatible.** "No other damages observed" cannot coexist with "damages exist but are pre-existing / wear/tear." One of those findings is factually wrong, and both came from Farmers. That internal contradiction is the strongest piece of B4's raw factual material.
- **Farmers' own subsequent conduct validates the family's critique.** Nic Brau's 9/6/2024 personal observation of dents contradicts the 9/3/2024 "no other damages observed" finding three days after it issued. The 9/12/2025 appraisal award confirms hail causation — expressly defeating both the 9/3 and 11/4 denial theories for the awarded windows. *See Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012) (causation findings are binding in appraisal).
- **Prieve's "most recent hail in Eden Prairie reported in 2020" assertion was objectively false.** NWS data, stormersite data, City of Eden Prairie permit records (EP187829, EP190835), the 14-of-19 neighbor canvass, and Farmers' own appraisal award contradict it. Farmers adopted Prieve's factually-erroneous premise in the 11/4/2024 denial. Minnesota equitable-estoppel doctrine squarely reaches representations of fact that are adopted and then later contradicted.
- **The Commerce-complaint, appeal, and litigation planning costs are identifiable reliance injuries.** The family spent months developing the administrative record against the "no opening / no other damages" theory, only to have Farmers pivot to "wear/tear / 2020 already paid" two months later, and then to "settled by appraisal / under deductible" after the award. Each pivot required the family to rebuild its factual posture from scratch. That is concrete, if modest, reliance-based harm.
- **The catch-all exclusion list in the 9/3/2024 denial is itself a *72A.201* red flag.** The statute's requirement that an insurer cite the "specific policy provision" on which the denial is based does not tolerate indiscriminate boilerplate. A denial that lists nine exclusions without tying each to the observed facts is weak ground from which to claim the catch-all "preserved" later theories.
- **Shifting rationales feed the § 604.18 motion (B1) directly.** Even if B4 does not by itself generate damages, its factual development narrows the space in which Farmers can argue it had a "reasonable basis" under § 604.18. That is a real and cumulative benefit.

### 2.4 What "25/100" and "55/100" mean practically

- **Do not plead B4 as a dedicated freestanding count labeled "Count IV — Estoppel."** The Rule 12 risk is real and the incremental relief is approximately zero beyond what A1 and A2 already provide.
- **Do weave the B4 theory into A1's breach-of-contract allegations** as the doctrinal reason Farmers cannot now raise incompatible defenses to its obligation to pay. Use the 9/3/2024 denial letter as an exhibit tied to paragraphs that allege the defenses Farmers has since advanced.
- **Do include the B4 theory in the A2 declaratory-judgment count** as one of the declarations sought (for example: "a declaration that Defendant is estopped from asserting [wear/tear / 2020-already-paid / marring / inherent vice / neglect / faulty construction] as grounds for non-payment in this action"). That is a procedurally appropriate vehicle for the "bar Farmers from raising these grounds" remedy without inventing a new cause of action.
- **Do build out the shifting-rationale factual narrative** in parallel so it is available as core material for the § 604.18 bad-faith motion (B1) post-discovery.
- **If a dedicated B4 count is included anyway**, plead it in the alternative, keep it short, and cite it explicitly as a backstop to A2. Expect it to be dismissed or merged at the Rule 12 stage; its marginal cost is low but its marginal benefit is also low.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No.** B4 is structurally independent of the 9/12/2025 appraisal award. It does not ask the court to disturb the award; in fact, its strongest form actually presupposes the award stands. The award is evidence *supporting* B4, not an obstacle to it.

### 4.2 Why B4 is independent of the award

The award is, under *Quade*, a binding determination of "amount of loss" (and causation) for the windows the panel evaluated. B4 is a doctrine about the grounds an insurer can assert for refusing to pay. Those are two different legal questions.

Three concrete observations:

**(a) The award does not address the 9/3/2024 or 11/4/2024 denial theories.** The appraisers did not decide whether "no opening" was a correct factual finding, whether "wear/tear" was a valid exclusion, whether "already paid in 2020" was a supportable defense, or whether Prieve's "no hail after 2020" assertion was accurate. The panel decided: cause of loss is hail, RCV is $12,800, ACV is $6,400. B4's territory — whether Farmers waived or is estopped from raising the denial theories on the merits — was not touched by the panel.

**(b) The award *confirms* the factual premise that supports B4.** The panel's hail-causation finding directly defeats the 9/3/2024 "no opening / no other damages observed" theory and the 11/4/2024 "wear/tear / already paid in 2020" theory for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows. Under *Quade*, that finding is binding. B4 leverages the award; it does not attack it.

**(c) B4 remains useful whether the award stands, is clarified via remand under *Herll*/*Rymer*, or is modified under *Mork*.** If the award stands: B4 shows Farmers cannot raise the old denial theories to justify paying $0 on windows the family identifies as unevaluated. If the award is remanded for scope clarification: B4 constrains Farmers' ability to re-argue the original denial theories on remand. If the award is modified: B4 narrows what Farmers can say on the modification-remand hearing. None of these paths requires B4 to attack the award itself.

### 4.3 The one area where the appraisal does weaken B4

**The "under the deductible" position is arithmetic, not a shifting denial theory.** Farmers' post-appraisal statement that the loss is "under your deductible" and "settled per the binding appraisal award" is, mechanically, a consequence of the $17,720 deductible exceeding the $6,400 ACV awarded. In the purest form of a "mend the hold" or waiver argument, the family wants to characterize all three positions — 9/3 no opening, 11/4 wear/tear, 10/21 under deductible — as incompatible grounds Farmers cannot all hold simultaneously. The third position is qualitatively different from the first two. A careful defense lawyer will point out that Farmers did not "shift" when it said "under deductible" after the award — it applied the contract's math. B4 is stronger on the 9/3 → 11/4 shift than on the 11/4 → 10/21 pivot.

That distinction narrows B4's sweep but does not make it dependent on vacating the award. It means the B4 arguments should target the incompatible factual theories (9/3 vs 11/4) directly rather than trying to rope the post-award deductible statement into the shifting-rationale frame.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- B4 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be filed, pleaded as a reply within A1, incorporated into A2's declaratory relief, or argued in support of the eventual § 604.18 motion (B1).
- B4 **is not weakened** by leaving the award in place. If anything, the award's hail-causation finding strengthens B4 by confirming the factual error baked into the 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials.
- The *only* piece of the "shifting rationales" factual narrative that is appraisal-dependent is the 10/21/2025 "under the deductible" position — and that position is probably best described as contract arithmetic rather than a new denial theory. That sub-issue does not drive the rest of the B4 analysis.
