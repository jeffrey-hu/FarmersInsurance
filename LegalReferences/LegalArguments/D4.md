## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 What D4 actually is — a procedural sanctions rule, not a cause of action

Before anything else, the single most important point about D4 is that **Rule 37 is not a cause of action**. It is a *procedural* rule of civil procedure that authorizes a court to impose sanctions on a party that fails to comply with discovery obligations during litigation. It is not a basis on which the family can sue Farmers; it is a remedy the court can grant inside a lawsuit the family has already filed under a different cause of action.

That distinction governs the entire strength analysis below. Rule 37 is not the engine of a complaint. It is a litigation-phase tool that becomes available only after (a) the family has filed a complaint pleading substantive claims (e.g., A1 breach of contract, A2 declaratory judgment, A3 vacate/modify the appraisal award, eventually B1 bad faith by Subd. 4(a) motion), (b) discovery has commenced under Minn. R. Civ. P. 26–37, and (c) Farmers has failed to comply with a discovery obligation in a manner the court is willing to police.

What the Possible Arguments List captures correctly is that Rule 37 is *distinct from spoliation* (D3). D3 is the pre-litigation preservation theory — the duty to preserve evidence once litigation is anticipated, and the adverse-inference / sanctions consequence of failing to do so. D4 is the in-litigation compliance theory — the duty to actually produce evidence in response to a discovery request or court order, and the sanctions consequence of failing to do so. The same factual record (e.g., the missing Brau 9/6/2024 inspection report) can give rise to both D3 and D4 remedies, but the doctrinal mechanics, timing triggers, and remedial menus differ.

### 1.2 The text of the rule (Minnesota state court)

Minn. R. Civ. P. 37 is the Minnesota analog to Fed. R. Civ. P. 37. The two are materially similar but not identical, and either may govern this case depending on whether Farmers removes to federal district court (a strategic possibility flagged in `KeyDecisions.md` and discussed in `chatGPTLog.txt` regarding diversity-jurisdiction analysis of Fire Insurance Exchange as a reciprocal exchange).

The sub-rules most directly relevant to this case are:

- **Rule 37.01 — Motion for Order Compelling Disclosure or Discovery.** Authorizes a party to move to compel a recalcitrant party to answer interrogatories, produce documents, designate a Rule 30(b)(6) representative, attend a deposition, etc. A successful motion to compel ordinarily produces a court order that is itself the predicate for harsher sanctions under Rule 37.02 if the recalcitrant party still fails to comply. Rule 37.01 also authorizes fee-shifting against the non-compliant party (or its attorney) absent substantial justification.
- **Rule 37.02 — Failure to Comply with Order.** If a party fails to obey a Rule 37.01 order, the court may impose progressively harsher sanctions, including: (i) treating designated facts as established for purposes of the action; (ii) prohibiting the disobedient party from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses, or from introducing designated matters in evidence; (iii) striking pleadings in whole or in part; (iv) staying further proceedings until the order is obeyed; (v) dismissing the action or proceeding in whole or in part; (vi) rendering a default judgment against the disobedient party; and (vii) treating as contempt of court the failure to obey. Reasonable expenses, including attorney fees, "must" be awarded unless the failure was substantially justified or other circumstances make an award unjust.
- **Rule 37.03 — Failure to Disclose; False or Misleading Disclosure; Refusal to Admit.** Sanctions for failure to make required disclosures under Rule 26.01(a) or to supplement under Rule 26.05, including the powerful preclusion remedy: a party that fails to disclose information or to identify a witness "is not allowed to use that information or witness to supply evidence on a motion, at a hearing, or at a trial, unless the failure was substantially justified or is harmless." Also covers the "expense of proof" remedy where a party refuses to admit the truth of a matter that is later proved at trial.
- **Rule 37.04 — Failure of Party to Attend at Own Deposition or Serve Answers to Interrogatories or Respond to Request for Inspection.** Authorizes Rule 37.02-class sanctions without first requiring a Rule 37.01 motion to compel where the non-compliance is total — failing to appear at a noticed deposition, failing to serve any answers to interrogatories, or failing to respond at all to a request for production.
- **Rule 37.05 — Failure to Preserve Electronically Stored Information.** Minnesota's analog to Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(e). Provides remedies for the loss of ESI that should have been preserved in the anticipation or conduct of litigation, including curative measures and (on a finding of intent to deprive) the harshest sanctions of adverse-inference instructions, presumption of unfavorability, dismissal, or default judgment. Rule 37.05 is the doctrinal hinge between D3 (pre-litigation preservation) and D4 (in-litigation production) for ESI specifically.
- **Rule 37.06 — Failure to Provide Information About Insurance Agreement.** Sanctions for non-disclosure of insurance-coverage information.

The federal counterpart (Fed. R. Civ. P. 37) tracks the same architecture — motion to compel under (a), order-violation sanctions under (b), failure-to-disclose sanctions under (c), failure-to-attend sanctions under (d), ESI preservation sanctions under (e), and a meet-and-confer requirement that is closely paralleled in Minnesota practice.

### 1.3 The remedial menu in plain terms

Rule 37 is one of the most powerful procedural tools in civil litigation because of the breadth of its remedial menu. In ascending order of severity, the typical sanctions are:

1. **Order compelling production / answer.** Rule 37.01.
2. **Fee-shifting on the motion.** Rule 37.01(d) (Minn.); Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(a)(5).
3. **Award of expenses caused by the non-compliance.** Rule 37.02(b)(3); Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(b)(2)(C).
4. **Adverse-inference instruction.** Allowing the trier of fact to infer that the missing evidence would have been unfavorable to the non-compliant party. Available expressly under Rule 37.05 for ESI on a finding of intent to deprive; available more generally under Rule 37.02(b)(2)(i) ("designated facts shall be taken as established").
5. **Issue preclusion.** Rule 37.02(b)(2)(ii) — prohibiting the non-compliant party from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses, or from introducing designated matters in evidence.
6. **Striking pleadings.** Rule 37.02(b)(2)(iii).
7. **Stay.** Rule 37.02(b)(2)(iv).
8. **Dismissal.** Rule 37.02(b)(2)(v) — for plaintiff non-compliance.
9. **Default judgment.** Rule 37.02(b)(2)(vi) — for defendant non-compliance.
10. **Contempt.** Rule 37.02(b)(2)(vii).

The middle of that menu — fee-shifting, adverse-inference, issue preclusion — is where most insurance-discovery battles actually resolve. Default and dismissal are rare and require persistent and willful non-compliance. The threat of climbing the ladder is itself a litigation lever.

### 1.4 Procedural mechanics — when Rule 37 actually becomes available

D4 has a strict procedural sequence that defines when it is and is not on the table:

**Step 1: Complaint filed.** The family files an initial complaint pleading substantive causes of action. Rule 37 is not pleaded; it cannot be a count. (See § 1.6 below for the closest cousin to a "Rule 37 count," which is none.)

**Step 2: Answer and discovery begin.** Once Farmers answers, the parties exchange initial disclosures (Rule 26.01(a)), Rule 26 conferences occur, and discovery (interrogatories, requests for production, depositions, requests for admission) opens.

**Step 3: A discovery dispute arises.** Farmers either fails to respond, responds incompletely, asserts unsupported privilege/work-product objections, fails to disclose witnesses or information under Rule 26.01, or refuses to admit matters that are later proved.

**Step 4: Meet and confer.** Minn. R. Gen. Prac. 115.10 (and Rule 37.01(b)(1) itself) require a good-faith meet-and-confer effort before a motion to compel.

**Step 5: Motion to compel under Rule 37.01.** The family moves the court to compel production. If granted, the court may award fees on the motion under Rule 37.01(d).

**Step 6: Continued non-compliance.** If Farmers continues to disobey the resulting order, Rule 37.02 sanctions are available — fee-shifting, adverse-inference, issue preclusion, striking pleadings, default judgment, contempt.

**Step 7 (alternative track): Total non-compliance.** Rule 37.04 permits Rule 37.02-class sanctions without a prior order where the non-compliance is total (no answers to interrogatories at all, complete failure to appear at deposition, etc.).

**Step 8 (parallel track): Rule 37.03 disclosure sanctions.** Independent of any motion practice, if Farmers fails to make required Rule 26.01(a) initial disclosures or fails to supplement under Rule 26.05, Rule 37.03 directly authorizes preclusion of the undisclosed material at trial.

The practical implication: D4 does not become a real arrow in the quiver until well after the complaint is filed. It cannot be alleged in the complaint, cannot be litigated at the Rule 12 stage, and does not generate damages of its own. Its role is to police Farmers' discovery conduct in support of A1 (breach of contract), A2 (declaratory judgment), A3 (vacate the appraisal), and the eventual B1 (§ 604.18) motion.

### 1.5 D4 versus D3 — the doctrinal demarcation

The Possible Arguments List flags this expressly: D4 is *distinct* from D3 (spoliation / adverse inference). Confirming the demarcation is important because the underlying facts overlap and conflating them produces sloppy briefing.

- **D3 — spoliation / pre-litigation preservation.** Triggers when a party knew or should have known evidence was relevant to anticipated or pending litigation and either destroyed, lost, or failed to preserve it. The legal source is the common-law preservation duty (Minnesota recognizes spoliation remedies but not as an independent tort — *see* `Possible_Arguments_List.md` § F). Remedies include adverse-inference instructions and Rule 37-style sanctions on a separate showing of preservation duty, breach, and prejudice. Triggering moment: as early as the date the duty to preserve attached (commonly when litigation became reasonably foreseeable — here, arguably as early as the 1/31/2025 appeal letter, the 11/19/2024 Department of Commerce complaint, or the family's first attorney-referral inquiry on 6/10/2025).
- **D4 — Rule 37 in-litigation discovery sanctions.** Triggers when, after litigation is filed, a party fails to comply with the discovery rules or a court order. The legal source is Rule 37 itself. Remedies are itemized in the rule. Triggering moment: only after the complaint is filed and a discovery request, court order, or required disclosure is in place.

The same fact (no produced Brau 9/6/2024 inspection record) can be the basis for both:

- **D3 angle.** If Farmers had a pre-litigation duty to preserve Brau's notes (because litigation was reasonably foreseeable by, e.g., 11/19/2024), and Brau's notes were destroyed or lost, that is spoliation.
- **D4 angle.** If Farmers fails to produce the notes (or the underlying claim file portion containing them) in response to a litigation-phase document request, that is a Rule 37 violation.

D3 and D4 are complementary, not duplicative. D4 picks up where D3 ends — D3 polices preservation; D4 polices production.

### 1.6 What this argument would (and would not) look like in a complaint

Three drafting postures, each with very different strength profiles:

**Posture 1 — Dedicated count for "Discovery Sanctions / Violation of Minn. R. Civ. P. 37."** Non-viable. Rule 37 does not create a substantive cause of action; courts uniformly reject attempts to plead "Rule 37 violation" as a count. Any such count would be dismissed at Rule 12 with no possibility of repleading.

**Posture 2 — A factual-predicate paragraph block in the complaint flagging Farmers' anticipated discovery non-cooperation.** A short narrative section under "Facts" laying out the pre-litigation pattern (refusal to produce Brau's notes; refusal to identify the 2 sashes paid in the 2020 claim; production of the wrong document; the supervisor-level "engineer's report only" rule; Brau's personal refusal to release notes; the contradictory "no file" / "everything has been released" statements) framed as conduct evidence supporting A1, B1, and (downstream) Rule 37 motion practice. No standalone count, no Rule 37 cite in the complaint itself; the conduct is simply part of the factual record. This is the appropriate treatment of D4 inside an initial pleading.

**Posture 3 — A pre-litigation preservation letter and detailed initial discovery requests prepared in parallel with the complaint.** Functionally, the most important "D4 deliverable" is not a complaint paragraph but a litigation-readiness package: (a) a Rule 26-anticipating preservation letter to Farmers issued on or about the day the complaint is filed; (b) a full set of initial document requests, interrogatories, and Rule 30(b)(6) deposition notices targeted at the specific evidentiary gaps the pre-litigation record has already revealed; and (c) a Rule 26.01 disclosure list designed to box in Farmers' supplementation obligations. None of this lives in the complaint, but all of it is what makes Rule 37 available later when Farmers fails to comply.

The realistic recommendation is **Posture 2 inside the complaint** plus **Posture 3 as the parallel litigation-readiness work** that turns Rule 37 from a paper threat into a real lever.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **5 / 100 as a standalone count in the initial complaint; 75 / 100 as a downstream litigation lever once discovery is underway**

The split rating is necessary because, as with C1, "strength as an initial claim" has very different answers depending on whether the question means "Can D4 itself be a count in the complaint?" (it cannot) or "How valuable is the D4 framework to the litigation strategy as a whole?" (very).

- **Standalone count in the initial complaint (Posture 1): ~5/100.** Rule 37 is procedural. It is not a cause of action. A count titled "Discovery Sanctions" or "Violation of Rule 37" would be dismissed at Rule 12 immediately. The 5/100 (rather than 0) reflects only the marginal possibility that a court might construe such a count as a poorly-styled spoliation count (D3) or a poorly-styled § 72A.201 count (C1) and let it go forward in some other form. As a Rule 37 count, it is foreclosed.
- **Factual-predicate paragraph block in the complaint (Posture 2): ~50/100.** Including a discrete narrative section laying out Farmers' pre-litigation pattern of non-production and stonewalling adds rhetorical force to the complaint and signals to the court (and to Farmers) that the family is prepared to litigate discovery aggressively. It is not the "D4 claim" — it is conduct evidence that will eventually feed a Rule 37 motion. The 50/100 reflects its value as part of the overall complaint narrative; it is not the centerpiece of any substantive claim.
- **Downstream litigation lever (Posture 3 + later motion practice): ~75/100.** Once discovery is underway, the Rule 37 remedial menu is genuinely powerful, and the pre-litigation record on this case unusually well sets up Rule 37 motion practice. The 75/100 reflects (a) the unusually thick documentary record of pre-litigation non-production to which Farmers will be measured against in litigation; (b) the multiple specific evidentiary gaps that point to where Rule 37 motions will likely concentrate; (c) the specific Rule 37.05 ESI-preservation issues lurking behind the missing Brau record; and (d) the practical fact that even partial Rule 37 success (fee-shifting, an adverse-inference instruction) substantially shifts the litigation arithmetic.

For the question the user asked — "strength of this argument for an initial legal claim" — the structural cap is real: D4 cannot be in the initial complaint as a count. The honest answer is **5/100 as a count in the complaint; 75/100 as a discovery-phase litigation lever the family is exceptionally well-positioned to use.** The recommended posture and the recommended rating are therefore **75/100 as a downstream litigation lever, with the structural ceiling on standalone pleading expressly noted.**

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

- **The dispositive structural problem: Rule 37 is a procedural rule, not a cause of action.** No Minnesota or federal court will treat "Rule 37 violation" as a complaint count. This is not a debatable doctrinal question.
- **It cannot be raised before discovery begins.** Rule 37 cannot be activated until at least the Rule 26 conference and the service of discovery requests. In a typical Minnesota state-court schedule, that pushes the earliest realistic Rule 37.01 motion to roughly 60–120 days after the answer — months into the litigation, not at the front end.
- **It does not generate damages.** Rule 37 produces fee-shifting, adverse-inference instructions, evidentiary preclusion, and (rarely) default judgment. None of these are direct damages flowing to the family. The contract recovery, the bad-faith taxable costs, and the punitive damages all flow from other claims (A1, B1, D1). D4 is a process accelerator, not a separate pot of money.
- **Default and dismissal sanctions are rare.** Courts impose them only on a clear pattern of willful non-compliance and after lesser sanctions have failed. Counting on a default-judgment outcome is unrealistic at the planning stage.
- **The meet-and-confer requirement adds friction.** Minn. R. Gen. Prac. 115.10 requires a good-faith effort to resolve the dispute before motion practice. Each Rule 37 motion has cost and delay overhead. Smaller skirmishes will routinely be resolved without motion practice; the family's ability to actually realize Rule 37's remedies depends on Farmers continuing to be non-cooperative even under court supervision.
- **Some of the most attractive Rule 37 targets are also vulnerable to "no record exists" defenses.** If Brau truly never created notes, Rule 37 cannot order Farmers to produce something that does not exist; the failure is a preservation/spoliation issue (D3) rather than a production issue. Whether to frame the same fact as D3 or D4 depends on what discovery actually reveals.
- **Rule 37 is most effective when the moving party can show the missing material exists and is being withheld.** Where the dispute is whether the material *should have been created* (Brau's notes), Rule 37 is harder to deploy than it is where the dispute is whether existing material is being produced (e.g., internal emails, claims-handling policies, the Hancock workproduct).

### 2.3 What drives the rating up (for the downstream-lever posture)

- **Farmers has already, in the pre-litigation record, refused to produce specific responsive material.** Rule 37 strength is largely a function of whether the moving party can credibly project that the responding party will continue to be non-cooperative. The pre-litigation record on this case is unusually predictive: Farmers has refused to produce Brau's notes when asked directly (10/29/2025); has provided the wrong claim file (a 2014 previous owner claim instead of the 2020 claim records the family requested, 10/1/2025); has stated "the file does not contain a report" for an inspection at which its own adjuster personally observed dents (10/10/2025); and has told the family, when asked about scope, to redirect their questions to their own appraiser (10/21/2025). That record makes Rule 37 motion practice substantially easier to execute and substantially more likely to succeed.
- **The "no Brau report" gap is a textbook Rule 37 / Rule 37.05 issue.** Brau personally inspected the property, observed dents alongside the family, and measured window dimensions on 9/6/2024. *(`EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md`; `CaseContext/key-facts.md` § 1.)* If discovery reveals that Brau created notes (handwritten, electronic, or otherwise) and they have been preserved but not produced, Rule 37.01 / 37.02 sanctions are direct. If Brau created ESI (claim-file entries, photographs, internal messages) that has been lost, Rule 37.05 supplies the ESI-specific framework, which on a finding of intent to deprive includes adverse-inference instructions, presumption of unfavorability, and even default judgment. *(`EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (10/10/2025 entry: "the file does not contain a report on the requested date"); same file (10/28–10/29/2025 entries: Brau's "all the information we can release has already been sent to you").)* The 10/10 and 10/29 statements are mutually inconsistent — if no record exists there is nothing to release; if everything has been released the file contains a record — and that inconsistency is itself a Rule 37 / Rule 37.05 hook.
- **The 9/3/2024 first denial creates a highly specific document-trail target.** The first denial issued five business days after the first inspection, before any forensic engineer was engaged. *(`EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf` pp. 1–2.)* That timing necessarily implies a chain of internal communications between the Hancock vendor, Farmers' Christian Dudley (Catastrophe Claims Rep), and any supervisor approving the denial — communications that are squarely responsive to ordinary document-production requests in a bad-faith case. If those communications are withheld, Rule 37 gives the court direct authority to compel them and to sanction non-compliance.
- **The "engineer's report only" institutional rule has a clear documentary trail.** Articulated by supervisor Bradlee Monson (2/3/2025 phone call) and corroborated by adjuster Brau (2/10/2025 voicemail). *(`EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG`; `CaseContext/key-facts.md` § 5.)* If that rule is reflected in Farmers' written claims-handling policies, training materials, or supervisor playbooks, those documents are squarely responsive to standard Rule 34 requests. If they exist and are withheld, Rule 37 is the enforcement mechanism. If they do not exist in writing, Rule 37 still supports compelling Rule 30(b)(6) testimony on the topic.
- **The Hancock workproduct beyond the photo set is a discrete identified gap.** The Hancock 8/29/2024 photo set is in `EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf` (pp. 5–74), but the Hancock narrative report (if any), Hancock's communications with Farmers, scope memos, and internal Hancock decisions on how to characterize each window are not in the project folder and not produced to the family. These are standard responsive material in any insurance-claim discovery; if not produced, Rule 37 provides the lever.
- **The 2020 claim's specific approved-window positions are an identified production target.** The 9/12/2025 Appraisal Award identifies the awarded windows only by the codes "F1.7" and "B1.2." *(`EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png`.)* On 9/28/2025 the family asked Farmers to identify the positions of the 2 sashes paid in the 2020 claim — the basic information needed to evaluate Farmers' "already paid in 2020" theory. *(`EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (9/28/2025 entry); `EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/2025-09-18-PostAppraisalEmailChain.md`.)* Farmers has not provided that information — instead, on 10/1/2025, Farmers provided a 2014 previous owner claim document. *(`EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (10/1/2025 entry).)* In litigation, that information has to come out — the 2020 claim file (claim #3013539499-1) is plainly responsive — and Rule 37 is the enforcement mechanism if it does not.
- **Rule 37.03 preclusion is unusually well-suited to the case theory.** If Farmers fails to identify any expert witness, claim-handling witness, or supporting document at the Rule 26.01 disclosure stage, Rule 37.03 directly precludes that material at trial unless the failure was substantially justified or harmless. Given the small universe of Farmers personnel (Dudley, Brau, Monson, Tomes, Pietila, Zorphion Miller, Twonda Miller, Jed Jones) and outside actors (Hancock / Jayden Grimm, Prieve / Nathan Prieve, John Foster, Wade Roos), any Farmers failure to identify a deponent or witness is easy to surface and police.
- **Insurance bad-faith litigation is a discovery-intensive practice area.** Most of the operative evidence in B1 / § 604.18 cases is in the insurer's claim file, training materials, and internal communications. Insurers routinely resist producing this material on privilege, work-product, and proprietary grounds. The case law in Minnesota and federally on insurance-claim-file discovery is well-developed and generally favorable to insureds — particularly post-denial materials, which are typically not protected by attorney-client privilege or work-product where the claim was being handled in the ordinary course. Rule 37 is the regular mechanism by which courts police those battles.
- **Even partial Rule 37 success is high-leverage.** A single granted motion to compel that produces fee-shifting (Rule 37.01(d) / Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(a)(5)) recovers attorney fees attributable to the dispute and signals to Farmers that further non-cooperation will be expensive. An adverse-inference instruction at trial on the missing Brau record would be devastating to Farmers' "no other damages observed" / "no hail after 2020" position. A Rule 37.03 preclusion order excluding a witness or document from trial can be outcome-determinative on an issue.
- **Rule 37 sanctions feed the B1 motion.** Conduct that produces Rule 37 sanctions is the same conduct that supports the eventual § 604.18 Subd. 4(a) prima facie showing on Prong 1 (absence of reasonable basis) and Prong 2 (knowledge / reckless disregard). A court order finding that Farmers wrongfully withheld responsive material has direct evidentiary weight in the bad-faith motion. *(See `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/B1.md` § 3 for parallel conduct evidence.)*

### 2.4 What "75/100 downstream lever" means practically

- **Do not include a "Rule 37 violation" or "discovery sanctions" count in the initial complaint.** The Rule 12 risk is real and the count adds nothing.
- **Do include a "Pre-Litigation Pattern of Non-Cooperation in Production of Records" paragraph block in the Facts section of the complaint.** Lay out (a) the missing Brau report, (b) the wrong document produced for the 2020 claim records request, (c) the contradictory "no file" / "everything has been released" statements, (d) Brau's personal refusal to release notes, and (e) the supervisor-level "engineer's report only" rule. Frame these as conduct evidence supporting A1 and B1, and as the predicate for the discovery-phase motion practice that will follow.
- **Issue a litigation-hold / preservation letter to Farmers contemporaneous with filing the complaint.** The letter should specifically itemize the records the family is putting Farmers on notice to preserve: the Brau 9/6/2024 inspection notes and any associated ESI; the Hancock workproduct beyond the photo set; internal communications among Dudley, Brau, Monson, Pietila, and any supervisor regarding the 9/3/2024 denial, the retention of Prieve, the 2/3–2/10/2025 articulation of the "engineer's report only" rule, and the 5/28/2025 supplemental evidence submission; Farmers' written claims-handling policies and supervisor training materials; the Prieve retention agreement and statement of work; the 2020 claim file (claim #3013539499-1) including any document identifying which 2 sashes were approved; the post-appraisal correspondence with the family; and any surveillance, recorded calls, or other ESI generated in connection with the claim.
- **Prepare and serve initial discovery requests at the earliest available moment.** Include (a) Rule 34 requests for the items in the preservation letter; (b) interrogatories targeting the identity of every claims-handling employee who touched the file, the dates of every internal review of the file, and the existence and form of any training materials supporting the "engineer's report only" rule; (c) requests for admission asking Farmers to admit the basic timeline facts (e.g., that no Brau inspection report was created, that the 2020 claim approved exactly 2 casement windows, that the 9/12/2025 appraisal award found hail as the cause of loss); and (d) Rule 30(b)(6) deposition notices on the topics of claims handling, supplementary-evidence review policies, and the "engineer's report only" rule.
- **Track discovery non-compliance in real time and document the meet-and-confer history.** Every email, every call, every objection. Rule 37 is most effective when the moving party can show the court a clean record of patient escalation.
- **Coordinate D4 with D3 so the same fact pattern can ride either rail.** If discovery shows that responsive material existed and was withheld, lead with Rule 37 (D4). If discovery shows that responsive material was destroyed or never preserved, lead with spoliation / Rule 37.05 (D3 plus the ESI-specific track inside D4). Both rails should be preserved.

### 2.5 The numerical question, plainly answered

If the user is asking, **"How strong is D4 as a separate count in an initial Minnesota state court complaint?"** — the answer is **5/100**. Rule 37 is procedural; it cannot be a count.

If the user is asking, **"How strong is D4 as a downstream litigation lever the family will be exceptionally well-positioned to deploy once discovery is underway?"** — the answer is **75/100**. The pre-litigation record on this case sets up Rule 37 motion practice unusually well, the targets are specific and identifiable, and Rule 37's remedial menu is tailored to the kinds of insurer non-cooperation already on the record.

The recommended posture and the recommended rating are therefore **75/100 as a downstream litigation lever, with the structural ceiling on standalone pleading expressly noted**.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal Award?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No. D4 is fully independent of the 9/12/2025 appraisal award — both as a doctrinal matter and as a factual matter — to a greater degree than any other argument in `Possible_Arguments_List.md`.** Whether the appraisal is upheld, vacated, modified, or corrected has no effect on Farmers' obligations under Rule 37 once litigation is filed, no effect on the family's ability to seek Rule 37 sanctions for non-compliance with discovery, and no effect on the remedial menu Rule 37 supplies. D4 is procedural; the appraisal is substantive; the two operate on entirely separate planes.

### 4.2 Why D4 is doctrinally independent of the appraisal

**(a) Rule 37 is a procedural rule of civil procedure governing in-litigation conduct.** It applies to any party in any civil action that has filed a complaint and is engaged in discovery. It does not depend on the existence of any prior appraisal, arbitration, or other alternative-dispute-resolution proceeding, and it does not change its content depending on whether such a proceeding occurred.

**(b) The appraisal addresses "amount of loss" and causation; Rule 37 addresses discovery compliance.** Under *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), the panel's authority is limited to the amount of loss, including causation. The panel did not — and could not — police Farmers' production of records during a future lawsuit. Whether the 9/12/2025 award stands, is vacated, is modified, or is corrected has no bearing on whether Farmers must produce its claim file in litigation.

**(c) The Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar in Minn. Stat. § 604.18 has no analog in Rule 37.** § 604.18 expressly disqualifies its taxable-cost remedy where the claim is "resolved or confirmed by … appraisal." Rule 37 contains no such bar. The appraisal does not have any direct effect on whether Rule 37 sanctions can be imposed for discovery non-compliance.

**(d) Rule 37 sanctions can be sought regardless of whether the appraisal award is being challenged.** Rule 37 supports A1 (breach of contract — does not require disturbing the appraisal) just as well as it supports A3 (vacate / modify the appraisal). A family that takes the position "we accept the binding causation finding of the appraisal but seek additional relief on scope, conduct, and bad faith" gets the same Rule 37 toolkit as a family that takes the position "we seek to vacate the entire award." The toolkit does not change.

### 4.3 Why D4 is factually independent of the appraisal

The conduct facts that support D4 are spread across the entire claim history; they are not tied to the appraisal:

- 8/29/2024 Hancock photo set with no produced narrative report.
- 9/3/2024 first denial with no produced internal-review record.
- 9/6/2024 Brau inspection with no produced report.
- 10/9/2024 Prieve inspection with no produced retention agreement, statement of work, or transmittal record.
- 11/4/2024 second denial with no produced internal-review record.
- 1/31/2025, 2/10/2025, and 5/28/2025 family supplemental-evidence submissions with no produced claim-team disposition record.
- 2/3/2025 and 2/10/2025 articulations of the "engineer's report only" rule with no produced written policy.
- 9/28/2025–10/1/2025 wrong document produced in response to the 2020 claim records request.
- 10/10/2025 "no report on file" statement on the 9/6/2024 Brau inspection.
- 10/29/2025 Brau personal refusal to release notes.

Several of these conduct facts predate the appraisal entirely (everything from 8/29/2024 through the August 2025 appraisal demand). Several occurred during the appraisal preparation but not as part of it. A few occurred in the post-appraisal correspondence period. None of them was created, altered, or "resolved" by the appraisal. The family's litigation-phase Rule 37 motions will target the same evidentiary gaps regardless of whether they ultimately seek to disturb the award.

### 4.4 Why D4 actually *helps* the appraisal-related claims

Although D4 is independent of the appraisal, it materially supports the appraisal-related claims (A2, A3) once litigation is filed:

- **A3 (vacate / modify / correct the appraisal).** The A3 record depends in part on documents that have not yet been produced — e.g., correspondence between Farmers and its appraiser Greg Tomes, communications between Tomes and the umpire Wade Roos, any internal Farmers analysis of the items that ended up scored "F1.7" and "B1.2," and any explanation of why the cryptic codes were used. Rule 37 supplies the mechanism for compelling that material in discovery.
- **A2 (declaratory judgment).** Many of the declarations the family might seek — that specific exclusions cannot be relied upon, that specific denial rationales were waived, that specific subdivisions of § 72A.201 were violated — depend on the underlying claim-handling record. Rule 37 supplies the mechanism for getting that record.

In other words, D4 is the lever that makes A2 and A3 (and A1, B1, etc.) actually function in litigation. It is not appraisal-dependent; it is what makes the appraisal-related claims work.

### 4.5 The one place the appraisal matters for D4's strategic value

The strategic value of D4 derives from where its conduct evidence ultimately ends up. Once Rule 37 sanctions are imposed (or threatened with credibility), the resulting fee-shifting, adverse-inference instructions, and preclusion orders feed:

1. **A1 (breach of contract).** A Rule 37 fee award compounds the contract recovery and increases the cost to Farmers of continued non-cooperation. Independent of the appraisal.
2. **A2 (declaratory judgment).** Rule 37-compelled material may produce the declarations the family seeks. Independent of the appraisal.
3. **A3 (vacate / modify / correct the appraisal).** Rule 37-compelled material may strengthen the showing on the partiality / disinterested-appraiser / itemization issues. The downstream consequence here turns on the appraisal's status, but D4 itself does not require the appraisal to be vacated.
4. **B1 (§ 604.18 bad faith — by post-pleading motion).** Rule 37 sanctions and the underlying conduct evidence directly support the prima facie showing on Prong 1 (absence of reasonable basis) and Prong 2 (knowledge / reckless disregard). The B1 *remedy* is exposed to § 604.18 Subd. 4(b)'s appraisal bar; D4's *availability* is not. D4 simply produces the conduct evidence; how that evidence is then used inside B1 is a separate analysis. *(See `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/B1.md` § 4.)*

In none of these scenarios does D4 itself require the family to vacate, modify, or correct the appraisal award. Some of the further claims D4 supports have their own appraisal-dependency profiles, and those should be evaluated under the relevant claim's analysis.

### 4.6 Bottom line on the key question

- D4 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be available, alleged, proved, or used in the litigation.
- The appraisal award's status (upheld, vacated, modified, or corrected) has **no effect** on Farmers' Rule 37 obligations, on the family's ability to file Rule 37 motions, or on the remedies the court can grant under Rule 37.
- D4 actually **helps** the appraisal-dependent claims (A2, A3) by supplying the procedural mechanism through which the discovery record needed to support those claims is compelled.
- The only appraisal-related dependency that touches D4's downstream value is the same one that touches B1's remedy (§ 604.18 Subd. 4(b)). That dependency belongs to B1, not to D4. *(See `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/B1.md` § 4.)*

9. **Track meet-and-confer history meticulously.** Every email, every call, every objection. Document patient escalation; document Farmers' responses (or non-responses).
10. **Coordinate D4 with D3.** Same fact pattern; different rails. If discovery shows responsive material existed and was withheld, lead with D4 (Rule 37). If discovery shows responsive material was destroyed or never preserved, lead with D3 (spoliation) plus the Rule 37.05 ESI track. Both should be preserved in the early discovery strategy.
11. **Coordinate D4 with C1 and B1.** C1 supplies the regulatory standard-of-conduct narrative; D4 supplies the procedural mechanism for getting the records that prove it. B1 (eventually) supplies the bad-faith remedy. The conduct evidence is the same; the procedural posture is different. Drafting drift between them should be actively managed.
12. **Evaluate forum-selection implications.** If the case is removed to federal court, Fed. R. Civ. P. 37 applies. The federal rule's text is materially similar but not identical, and federal practice on insurance-claim-file discovery (privilege, work-product, dual-purpose-document doctrine) is well-developed and may be more or less favorable than Minnesota state-court practice on specific issues. Coordinate with the forum-selection analysis in `KeyDecisions.md` and `chatGPTLog.txt`.
