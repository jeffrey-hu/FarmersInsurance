## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The Minnesota Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act

Declaratory judgment in Minnesota is governed by Minn. Stat. Ch. 555, the Minnesota Uniform Declaratory Judgments Act ("UDJA"). It has no federal-law component; for a case filed in Hennepin County District Court, Ch. 555 supplies the full procedural and substantive framework. (The federal Declaratory Judgment Act, 28 U.S.C. § 2201, applies only if the case is removed or filed in federal court.)

The four sections that matter for this claim are:

**Minn. Stat. § 555.01 — Courts to Construe Rights.**

> "Courts of record within their respective jurisdictions shall have power to declare rights, status, and other legal relations whether or not further relief is or could be claimed. No action or proceeding shall be open to objection on the ground that a declaratory judgment or decree is prayed for. The declaration may be either affirmative or negative in form and effect; and such declarations shall have the force and effect of a final judgment or decree."

**Minn. Stat. § 555.02 — May Have Instruments Construed.**

> "Any person interested under a deed, will, written contract, or other writings constituting a contract, or whose rights, status, or other legal relations are affected by a statute, municipal ordinance, contract, or franchise, may have determined any question of construction or validity arising under the instrument, statute, ordinance, contract, or franchise and obtain a declaration of rights, status, or other legal relations thereunder."

**Minn. Stat. § 555.03 — Contracts.**

> "A contract may be construed either before or after there has been a breach thereof."

**Minn. Stat. § 555.08 — Supplemental Relief.**

> "Further relief based on a declaratory judgment or decree may be granted whenever necessary or proper. The application therefor shall be by petition to a court having jurisdiction to grant the relief. If the application be deemed sufficient, the court shall, on reasonable notice, require any adverse party whose rights have been adjudicated by the declaratory judgment or decree, to show cause why further relief should not be granted forthwith."

Section 555.06 is the counterweight — it permits (but does not require) the court to refuse declaratory relief if the declaration "would not terminate the uncertainty or controversy giving rise to the proceeding." That provision does not defeat A2 here, because a declaration on the questions described below would substantially narrow the issues in dispute.

⚠️ The verbatim text above reflects the long-standing Minnesota UDJA language and tracks the Minnesota Revisor's Office version of the statute; it should be confirmed against the most recent session-law version (the Revisor's site could not be fetched from this environment) before being quoted in any filing.

### 1.2 What the UDJA requires

To plead a viable count under Ch. 555 in Minnesota state court, the plaintiff must establish:

1. **Justiciable controversy.** There must be a genuine, present, adverse legal dispute between the parties affecting a protected right, not a hypothetical or abstract question. *Onvoy, Inc. v. ALLETE, Inc.*, 736 N.W.2d 611, 617–18 (Minn. 2007); *Seaway Port Auth. of Duluth v. Midland Ins. Co.*, 430 N.W.2d 242, 246 (Minn. App. 1988).
2. **Interested party.** Plaintiff must be "interested under" a contract or "whose rights, status, or other legal relations are affected" by the contract. § 555.02.
3. **A question of construction or validity** arising under the instrument — i.e., a real interpretive dispute for the court to resolve.
4. **A useful declaration.** The requested declaration must meaningfully terminate at least part of the controversy. § 555.06.

All four are satisfied on this record. The parties have taken directly opposing written positions on coverage, the policy is a written contract, the exclusions and endorsement J6944 present construction questions, and a judgment declaring the scope of coverage would substantially resolve the underlying dispute.

### 1.3 Minnesota courts routinely resolve insurance coverage disputes by declaratory judgment

Minnesota's appellate courts have consistently characterized the declaratory-judgment vehicle as well-suited to insurance coverage disputes. *Seaway Port Auth.*, 430 N.W.2d at 246 (coverage disputes "particularly amenable to declaratory relief"); *Am. Standard Ins. Co. of Wisconsin v. Le*, 551 N.W.2d 923, 926 (Minn. 1996) (using declaratory judgment to resolve coverage); *St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. v. Nat'l Chiropractic Mut. Ins. Co.*, 496 N.W.2d 411, 417 (Minn. App. 1993). In the 8th Circuit, *Selective Ins. Co. of S.C. v. Sela*, No. 20-2029 (8th Cir. 2021), is instructive: the insurer filed a declaratory-judgment action seeking a declaration of no coverage on a Minnesota hail claim, the insured counterclaimed for breach and bad faith, and the court's declaration on coverage became the predicate for the subsequent breach-and-bad-faith findings. A Minnesota insured may proactively mirror that structure by invoking § 555.02 first, rather than waiting for the insurer to do so.

### 1.4 What the Declarations Would Ask the Court to Decide

On this record, the candidate declarations fall into five categories. They can be pleaded together in a single A2 count (with sub-parts), or split across multiple counts.

**DJ-1. Coverage for the 7/13/2024 hail loss under Section I (Loss or Damage Insured).** A declaration that the policy covers direct physical loss by hail to the insured dwelling, subject only to the $17,720 Windstorm/Hail deductible, based on the facts of the 7/13/2024 storm and the 9/12/2025 panel causation finding.

**DJ-2. Preclusion of the 11/4/2024 denial exclusions.** A declaration that the exclusions cited in the 11/4/2024 denial letter — Wear and Tear (§ I.A.7 / I.B.8), Marring (§ I.A.8), Corrosion/Deterioration/Decay/Rust (§ I.A.9 / I.B.9), Inherent Vice or Latent Defect (§ I.A.4 / I.B.6), Neglect/Lack of Maintenance (§ I.B.4), and Faulty/Defective Construction (§ I.B.5) — do not apply to the windows the 9/12/2025 award addresses, and, as to any other hail-damaged window on the property, do not apply where the damage is caused by hail within the meaning of the policy. This turns on *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), and on the coverage/amount-of-loss allocation in the appraisal clause.

**DJ-3. Scope — appraisal did not resolve coverage for unevaluated windows.** A declaration that the 9/12/2025 appraisal award — signed only for "Windows (F1.7/B1.2)" with a lump $12,800 RCV and no itemization — did not "resolve" under Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 and *Quade* the coverage dispute for windows the panel did not evaluate. Those windows remain live coverage items the court may adjudicate without disturbing the award. Paired with an alternative request under *Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018), and *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026), for remand to the panel for clarification of scope.

**DJ-4. Applicability of endorsement J6944 (Limited Matching of Undamaged Property).** A declaration construing the text of J6944 and its application to the facts of this loss — specifically, that J6944 does not reach windows that themselves sustained direct hail damage (because it limits payment for "undamaged property"), and, alternatively, that where the manufacturer (Semco) is defunct and the original window cannot be sourced at any price, J6944's text does not bar replacement of otherwise-undamaged but unmatchable adjacent windows. This is the family's *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 857 N.W.2d 290 (Minn. 2014), hook, briefed against *Noonan v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 929 F.3d 633 (8th Cir. 2019), as adverse authority.

**DJ-5. Construction of "amount of loss" and the appraisal clause.** A declaration — as a pure construction question under § 555.02/§ 555.03 — of the scope of authority the § 65A.01 appraisal clause vests in the panel. Affirmatively, under *Quade*, the panel resolved hail causation; that finding is binding on Farmers for the items evaluated. Reciprocally, the panel did not resolve coverage as to items it did not evaluate, and did not resolve the legal effect of J6944 (a coverage question reserved to the court).

### 1.5 What the A2 count looks like on the face of a complaint

The A2 count would plead, in substance:

> "Plaintiffs bring this Count under Minn. Stat. §§ 555.01–.16 for a judicial declaration of the parties' rights under Policy 98055-49-65 with respect to the 7/13/2024 hail loss at 17756 George Moran Dr., Eden Prairie, MN. A justiciable controversy exists in that Defendant has denied coverage on 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024, has paid $0 on an appraisal award confirming hail as the cause of loss, and now takes the position that the claim is 'settled per the binding appraisal award received' and 'under your deductible.' Plaintiffs dispute each of those positions and seek a declaration that: (a) the 7/13/2024 loss is a covered loss under Section I of the policy; (b) the exclusions cited in the 11/4/2024 denial do not apply; (c) the 9/12/2025 appraisal award did not 'resolve' coverage as to windows the panel did not evaluate; (d) endorsement J6944 does not bar replacement of windows that themselves sustained direct hail damage or where matching windows cannot be obtained; and (e) Defendant owes Plaintiffs the replacement cost of all hail-damaged windows net of the deductible. Plaintiffs further request such supplemental relief under § 555.08 as is necessary to enforce this declaration."

Relief sought: declaratory relief plus supplemental relief under § 555.08; damages are pursued through the companion A1 breach-of-contract count.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **70 / 100**

That number reflects A2's strength **as a pleaded count in the initial complaint** — a framing vehicle with multiple, partially-independent declarations — rather than a prediction of dollar recovery.

- **Strength as a procedural vehicle** (survival of Rule 12, availability of the cause of action, fit to the facts): **90/100.** Declaratory relief is well-settled in Minnesota; insurance-coverage disputes are specifically described by Minnesota appellate courts as "amenable" to it.
- **Strength of the underlying declarations on the merits**: weighted average ~**60/100**, broken down by declaration (see 2.3 below).
- **Strength as a case-organizing device** (narrowing disputes, forcing coverage questions into the court rather than the panel): **85/100.** A2 is the vehicle that keeps coverage questions out of Farmers' "already appraised" box.

Blended with emphasis on merits of the declarations: **~70/100.**

### 2.2 Why A2 is not really an independent "argument" — and why that is a feature, not a bug

Declaratory judgment in a first-party property dispute is typically a framing vehicle rather than a stand-alone theory. It is the mechanism by which coverage questions — which *Quade* reserves to the court — are pushed in front of the judge rather than buried inside a damages dispute. Minnesota's prior strength-assessment memo treats it the same way (see `Argument_Strength_Assessment.md` §2: "Strong as a procedural vehicle" / "the proper legal mechanism for presenting the breach of contract and coverage disputes to the court"). That framing is correct here. The 70/100 score should be read as: A2 is a procedurally excellent count whose merit on any given sub-declaration depends on the underlying law and facts supporting that sub-declaration.

### 2.3 Declaration-by-declaration strength

| # | Declaration | Strength | Why |
|---|---|---|---|

Weighted against a realistic filing that emphasizes DJ-1, DJ-2, and DJ-3 and preserves DJ-4 and DJ-5 as secondary: **~70/100**.

### 2.4 What drives the rating up

- **Procedural fit is excellent.** *Seaway Port Auth.* and a long line of Minnesota coverage cases describe declaratory judgment as the correct framing for insurance disputes. A2 will not be dismissed at the pleading stage for any UDJA-specific reason.
- **The appraisal award supports, rather than cuts against, most of the declarations.** The panel's hail-causation finding under *Quade* is affirmatively useful for DJ-1 and DJ-2. This is the opposite of the usual posture in a post-appraisal dispute.
- **Facial ambiguity of the award supports DJ-3.** The award's "F1.7" / "B1.2" lump-sum format, absence of an umpire report, Tomes's inconsistent descriptions, and the family's photographic record of damage across most of the home all supply genuine, concrete scope-ambiguity — exactly the facts *Herll* and *Rymer* treat as warranting a court-ordered clarification or a coverage declaration on unevaluated items.
- **No "mere inadequacy" problem.** Because A2 avoids asking the court to change the $12,800 RCV the panel set, it avoids the *Mork*-style gross-inadequacy bar. The declarations target coverage questions and scope, not valuation judgment calls by the panel.
- **Policy-driven exclusions cannot be revived.** Once the panel found hail causation, the Prieve-based exclusions in the 11/4/2024 letter (wear/tear, marring, corrosion, inherent vice, neglect, faulty construction) lose their factual predicate for the items the panel evaluated. A declaration that formalizes that result costs Farmers a large portion of its merits defense.

### 2.5 What drives the rating down

- **Ch. 555 does not generate damages.** A2 produces only a legal declaration; the companion A1 count is required to monetize it. A2 is therefore derivative in value — which is partly why its peak is 70 rather than 85.
- **DJ-4 (matching) has adverse 8th Circuit authority.** *Noonan* enforced a Minnesota-approved Limited Matching endorsement against a matching-based appraisal award. The "defunct manufacturer" distinction helps but has not been adopted by a Minnesota appellate court or the 8th Circuit. Treat DJ-4 as meaningful but not high-confidence.
- **DJ-5 has one dead sub-argument.** The family's historical argument that the award is non-binding because Tomes did not sign is foreclosed by § 65A.01 Subd. 3. Plead DJ-5 around the *Quade* coverage/amount-of-loss allocation, not around Tomes's non-signature.
- **Dependency on fact development for DJ-3.** The strongest version of DJ-3 requires evidence — the Foster written statement, a window-by-window damage record, deposition or affidavit from Roos or Tomes — that has not yet been assembled. On the current record, DJ-3 is a good pleading but needs discovery to win.
- **§ 555.06 discretion.** The court has discretion to refuse declaratory relief if the declaration would not "terminate the uncertainty or controversy." Any A2 framing that asks the court to address abstract or academic questions (e.g., what J6944 might mean in hypothetical facts) risks being denied under § 555.06. The declarations must be tightly anchored to concrete factual disputes in the record.

### 2.6 What "70/100" means practically

A2 is the right second count after A1 and belongs in the complaint. It will not carry the case alone because it produces declarations, not dollars — but it locks in coverage-question wins that make the companion breach count far more likely to deliver recovery. The 70 score reflects a procedurally excellent, thematically correct count whose substantive payoff tracks the strength of the underlying sub-declarations and the subsequent discovery record.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No — A2 can be pleaded, briefed, and won without vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award on any of the five candidate declarations, with one narrow caveat.** Every declaration described in Section 1.4 targets either (i) the policy's coverage language, which *Quade* reserves to the court, or (ii) the *scope* of what the appraisal actually resolved, which is a court-level interpretive question rather than an attack on the award's validity. The single caveat is that DJ-3 is strongest when coupled with a *Herll*/*Rymer* remand for clarification of scope, which is not a vacatur or modification — it is a request that the panel clarify its own work. That is a distinct remedy from the *Mork* vacatur framework.

### 4.2 Declaration-by-declaration dependency analysis

| # | Declaration | Depends on vacating the award? | Depends on modifying/correcting the award? | Dependent on anything else touching the award? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| DJ-1 | Coverage for the 7/13/2024 hail loss | No | No | No. Declaration uses the award affirmatively (*Quade* binds causation). |
| DJ-2 | 11/4/2024 exclusions do not apply | No | No | No. The award's causation finding defeats the exclusions; no change to the award is required. |
| DJ-3 | Appraisal did not resolve coverage for unevaluated windows | No | No | **Yes (soft):** strongest paired with a *Herll*/*Rymer* remand for clarification — which is a clarification request, not a modification. The award's $12,800 figure is untouched. |
| DJ-4 | J6944 does not bar full replacement | No | No | No. Pure coverage/construction question. |
| DJ-5 | Construction of the § 65A.01 appraisal clause | No | No | No. Interpretive declaration only; expressly preserves the award's binding effect on what it resolved. |

None of the five declarations asks the court to set aside or rewrite the $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV figure the panel assigned to the F1.7/B1.2 windows. Each declaration either uses the award affirmatively or identifies what the award did not resolve.

### 4.3 Why DJ-5 does not extend to "the award is non-binding because Tomes did not sign"

One sub-argument the family's earlier research floated — that the appraisal is not binding because Farmers' appraiser refused to sign — is foreclosed by the statute and should not be pleaded as part of DJ-5. Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 Subd. 3 provides that "a written award of any two shall determine the amount of such loss." Foster + Roos is a binding majority. Pleading otherwise weakens A2 without advancing it. Tomes's non-signature is still useful as context (it shows the award's scope was contested, which helps DJ-3), but it is not a standalone basis for a declaration that the award is non-binding.

### 4.4 The relationship between A2 (declaratory judgment), A1 (breach of contract), and A3 (vacatur/modification of the award)

A2 operates in the space *between* A1 (damages for breach of the covered loss) and A3 (the limited set of sub-claims that ask the court to change the award amount). A2's reason for existing is to extract coverage determinations from the appraisal framework and put them in front of the judge.

- **A1 needs A2 to eliminate Farmers' exclusion defenses and to establish that unevaluated windows are live coverage items.** Without A2, A1 can still survive Rule 12, but the coverage landscape is murkier.
- **A2 does not need A3 to succeed** on DJ-1, DJ-2, DJ-3 (with *Herll*/*Rymer* clarification), DJ-4, or DJ-5. Vacatur and modification of the $12,800 RCV figure belong in the separate A3 count and attack only the portion of the award the family wants changed.
- **If A3 eventually succeeds in modifying or vacating the award in part**, A2's declarations are unaffected — they were addressed to coverage, not to valuation.
- **If A3 fails entirely**, A2 still has independent force: declarations in favor of the family on coverage, exclusions, and scope would still be entered, and supplemental relief under § 555.08 could still follow.

### 4.5 Dependence on *Herll*/*Rymer* remand is not a dependence on vacatur

Under *Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018), and *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026), an ambiguous appraisal award is remanded to the panel for clarification of its own scope. A remand for clarification is a different remedy from a vacatur or modification, both procedurally and doctrinally. It does not require the family to satisfy the *Mork v. Eureka-Security Fire & Marine Ins. Co.*, 230 Minn. 382, 42 N.W.2d 33 (1950), "clear evidence" of "fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing" standard. A remand preserves the award for what it resolves and asks the panel to say, on the record, what that is. Pairing DJ-3 with a *Herll*/*Rymer* alternative request therefore does not convert A2 into a dependent-on-vacatur claim.

### 4.6 The one place where dependence does appear

If the family ever pushes a declaration to the effect that the $12,800 RCV itself is wrong — for example, because the panel valued the F1.7/B1.2 windows using phantom Semco pricing — that declaration **would** require an adjacent modification or correction under *Mork*. But that is an A3 claim, not A2. Keep that sub-theory out of A2. A2's discipline is: coverage and scope yes; re-pricing the awarded items no.

### 4.7 Bottom line on the key question

- A2's viability and most of its expected value do **not** depend on vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award. A2 is largely standalone.
- DJ-3 is most potent paired with an alternative *Herll*/*Rymer* remand for clarification — which is not vacatur or modification.
- A2 deliberately does not attempt to change the $12,800 RCV; that theory belongs in A3 and should not be pleaded inside A2.
