## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The Minnesota rule

Minnesota recognizes *contra proferentem* — the rule that ambiguous contract language is construed against the party that drafted it — as a settled, generally applicable principle of insurance contract construction. The Minnesota Supreme Court has stated the rule in materially similar form for decades. The most frequently quoted formulation comes from *Nathe Brothers, Inc. v. American National Fire Insurance Co.*, 615 N.W.2d 341, 344 (Minn. 2000): insurance policies are contracts that are "interpreted in accordance with general principles of contract law" and "construed as a whole." Where the language of an insurance policy is reasonably subject to more than one interpretation, "the policy is ambiguous and is construed against the drafter." *Hammer v. Investors Life Ins. Co. of N. Am.*, 511 N.W.2d 6, 8 (Minn. 1994); see also *Columbia Heights Motors, Inc. v. Allstate Ins. Co.*, 275 N.W.2d 32, 34 (Minn. 1979) ("the language of an insurance policy must be given its plain and ordinary meaning… any ambiguity… is resolved in favor of the insured").

The rationale is structural. Insurance contracts are contracts of adhesion: the insurer drafts the form, the insured pays premium and accepts; there is no meaningful negotiation over policy text. Because the insurer wields the "power of the pen," any drafting choice that produces an ambiguity must be resolved against the insurer and in favor of coverage. *Henning Nelson Construction Co. v. Fireman's Fund American Life Ins. Co.*, 383 N.W.2d 645, 652 (Minn. 1986), is the principal Minnesota Supreme Court statement of this adhesion-contract rationale in the exclusion context — exclusions are construed strictly against the insurer because the insurer chose the words and the exclusion narrows the coverage the insured paid premium to obtain.

### 1.2 The two-step ambiguity test

Minnesota courts apply *contra proferentem* through a two-step analysis:

1. **Step one — is the language ambiguous?** The court examines the policy text and asks whether it is "reasonably subject to more than one interpretation." *Hammer*, 511 N.W.2d at 8. The court is not free to manufacture ambiguity. *Columbia Heights Motors* sets the limiting principle: "appellate courts must not create an ambiguity where none exists in order to afford coverage." 275 N.W.2d at 34. Where one reading is reasonable and another is not, the reasonable reading controls and ambiguity does not arise. The Minnesota Court of Appeals routinely cites this guardrail to police aggressive ambiguity arguments.
2. **Step two — if ambiguous, who drafted the language?** If the ambiguous language is the insurer's drafting (true for the entire base form, all standard endorsements, and any insurer-supplied schedule entries), the ambiguity is resolved in favor of the insured. The court does not need to find bad faith, fraud, or sloppy drafting; the rule is mechanical once ambiguity is established.

A third predicate — that the dispute is between the insurer and its insured — is implicit. The Minnesota Court of Appeals has held that *contra proferentem* does not apply in coverage disputes between two insurers where one was not a party to the policy at issue, because the rationale (disparity of bargaining power) is absent. That carve-out is irrelevant here: the family is the insured under the policy Farmers drafted.

### 1.3 What *contra proferentem* actually does

The doctrine performs three discrete functions, each useful in this case:

1. **Resolves ambiguous coverage grants in favor of coverage.** Where the policy's "Loss or Damage Insured" section, the Section I.A windstorm/hail exception, or the J6944 endorsement's reach is open to two reasonable readings — one that covers the family's hail-caused window damage and one that does not — the court selects the reading that delivers coverage.
2. **Resolves ambiguous exclusions narrowly.** Where Farmers' six 11/4/2024 exclusions (wear/tear, marring, corrosion/deterioration/decay/rust, inherent vice/latent defect, neglect/lack of maintenance, faulty/defective construction) interact with each other or with the policy's windstorm/hail restoration clauses in ways that produce more than one reasonable reading, the court narrows the exclusion. *Henning Nelson* says it directly: exclusions are construed against the insurer.
3. **Resolves ambiguous procedural / definitional language in favor of the insured.** This includes the policy's "Lawsuits Against Us" / suit-limitation clause (which Nic Brau characterized in his 7/11/2025 email as boilerplate that "may have been extended by statute or case law") and the "comparable material and quality" replacement-cost language read against J6944's matching limits.

### 1.4 Relationship to E3 (reasonable expectations)

E4 and E3 are doctrinally adjacent. They are different rules with different scopes, often briefed together but not interchangeable.

- **E4 (*contra proferentem*) operates only where the language is ambiguous.** If the text is reasonably susceptible to more than one reading, ambiguity exists, and the rule resolves the ambiguity for the insured. Where the text is unambiguous, *contra proferentem* has no work to do.
- **E3 (reasonable expectations under *Atwater*) can operate even where the language is unambiguous on its face.** If a major coverage defeat is buried in the definitions section or in an obscure endorsement-level provision and the result is significantly different from what an ordinary insured would reasonably expect, E3 reaches the case even when E4 cannot.

Practically, E4 is the broader and more frequently used doctrine. Minnesota courts deploy *contra proferentem* routinely; *Atwater* is invoked sparingly and is constrained by *Carlson*. The honest assessment is that any time the family wants an interpretive ruling in its favor, E4 is the first doctrine the court will look at — E3 is the backstop for cases where the text is unambiguous but the result is intolerable.

That ordering matters for the complaint. Brief E4 first, then E3. Where E4 produces the result the family needs, the court does not have to reach the harder *Atwater* question. Where E4 does not (because the text is unambiguous), E3 supplies the secondary doctrine.

### 1.5 What targets the doctrine could plausibly reach in this case

Five pieces of policy or appraisal language in this record are reasonably susceptible to more than one interpretation, satisfying the threshold step-one inquiry. They are listed here in descending order of doctrinal fit; supporting facts are developed in §3 below.

#### (a) The interaction between § I.A.8 marring exclusion, the Definitions § 16 inclusion of "denting," and the § I.A.8(b) windstorm/hail restoration

The same drafting cluster that drives the E3 hidden-in-definitions argument is also fertile ground for E4. Three pieces of policy text interact:

- *Definitions § 16* — defines "Marring" to include "denting" as an enumerated example.
- *§ I.A.8* — excludes "loss or damage which is marring of covered property."
- *§ I.A.8(b)* — restores coverage for "marring which is directly caused by, arises out of or results from … windstorm or hail."

Read one way (Farmers' 11/4/2024 reading): the marring exclusion excludes denting because the definitions enumerate denting under marring; the (b) exception applies only to "marring," and Farmers can deny by characterizing observed damage as "wear/tear" or "blunt object" rather than as marring "caused by … hail" — so the restoration clause never engages. Read another way (the family's reading): the (b) exception expressly restores coverage for marring that "results from" hail, and dents on a home where the appraisal panel has bound Farmers to a hail-causation finding under *Quade* are exactly that. Both readings are reasonable on the face of the text. Step one of *Hammer* is satisfied; step two resolves the ambiguity in the family's favor.

#### (b) The J6944 endorsement's interaction with the policy's "comparable material and quality" replacement-cost obligation

J6944 says it does not cover "cost to repair or replace property which is **not damaged by the covered peril** and does not match" because of marring, wear/tear/deterioration/corrosion/decay, or "obsolescence or discontinuation." It also bars "actual or perceived loss in value to any property because materials used to repair or replace the lost or damaged property does not match undamaged property."

The endorsement is reasonably susceptible to two readings on at least three discrete points:

- **The damage gate.** Read one way, "not damaged by the covered peril" means the endorsement only reaches windows that are physically undented. Read the other way (Farmers' anticipated reading), "not damaged by the covered peril" means anything the panel did not affirmatively award — even windows the family says were dented but the panel did not evaluate. The first reading is closer to the plain-meaning text; the second collapses the gate. Both are arguably reasonable. Ambiguity is satisfied.
- **The reason gate.** Subparagraph (1)(iii) bars matching coverage where the mismatch is due to "obsolescence or discontinuation." Read one way, that provision only operates when the original-equipment material is unavailable due to the manufacturer's discontinuation of that material specifically. Read another way, it operates whenever the manufacturer is no longer in business for any reason. The first reading would not exclude coverage where Sierra Pacific (Semco's successor) can produce a comparable extruded aluminum profile; the second would. The text leaves both reasonable.
- **The "comparable material and quality" replacement standard.** Section I — Property Conditions, How We Settle Covered Loss obligates Farmers to replace damaged property with property of "comparable material and quality." J6944 does not eliminate that obligation; it limits matching of *undamaged* property. Whether replacement of the damaged windows with current Pella Reserve Traditional or Marvin Ultimate equivalents (the family- and contractor-identified extruded aluminum equivalents per `EventDocuments/ComunicationLogsWithPureConstruction.md` 6/12/2025) satisfies "comparable material and quality" or whether the obligation reaches further (e.g., requires replacement of adjacent windows to maintain comparable profile/finish) is reasonably susceptible to two readings. *Cedar Bluff* leans toward the broader reading; J6944 seeks to constrain it; the boundary between them is contested.

Each of these interpretive seams is a clean E4 target.

#### (c) The 9/3/2024 first denial's interaction with the 11/4/2024 second denial

The 9/3/2024 letter denied because "there were no other damages observed" and "no opening" — a Section I.A.B.2 interior-water-damage-without-opening reading. The 11/4/2024 letter denied because the dents were attributable to "wear/tear" and the 2020 storm — a Section I.A.7 / I.B.8 wear/tear reading and a "previously paid" reading not actually rooted in any policy provision. The two letters cannot both describe the policy's correct application — under one, the home has no exterior damage; under the other, the home has exterior damage that happens to be excluded.

This is not, strictly, a *contra proferentem* target in the textbook sense. It is a use-pattern target: Farmers' own reading of the same policy text shifted between two incompatible theories. That shift is direct evidence that the policy text Farmers cited is reasonably susceptible to more than one reading — Farmers itself read it two different ways within sixty-two days. The argument is that an insurer who twice attempts to fit the same loss into the policy's exclusions and produces two incompatible characterizations has, by its own conduct, demonstrated step-one ambiguity. Step two then operates against the drafter.

This frame is doctrinally weaker than (a) and (b) — most courts apply *contra proferentem* to text on the page rather than to the insurer's interpretive history — but it is a useful narrative reinforcement and a clean estoppel-adjacent argument that pairs with B4 (waiver / estoppel of denial rationales).

#### (d) The 9/12/2025 Appraisal Award's "F1.7" and "B1.2" codes

The award identifies the awarded windows only by the codes "F1.7" and "B1.2" against a lump-sum $12,800 RCV. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png.)* Farmers' own descriptions of which windows the codes refer to have been mutually inconsistent: "front right, and rear elevation in the dining room" (9/18/2025); "dining room reference was a mistake" (9/19/2025); "front right, and rear elevation in the dining room" (10/14/2025, repeating the original error). *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/18, 9/19, 10/14/2025 entries).)*

The codes are not policy text — the appraisal award form is generated by the panel, not by Farmers — but they sit inside a contract Farmers drafted (the policy's appraisal clause and its statutory backstop), and where the resulting award produces obligations Farmers is now enforcing (the deductible-exceeds-RCV result), Farmers carries the same drafting risk for any ambiguity in how its own personnel translate the codes into payment positions. Where Farmers offers the award as the ground for its $0 payment outcome, it cannot then resist construction of the codes against itself when the codes are not self-defining.

This target is the weakest of the four because the doctrine is principally a policy-text doctrine. But it has narrative force and supports the *Herll*/*Rymer* clarification request in `LegalReferences/LegalArguments/A3.md` Tier 1: if Farmers is going to enforce a $12,800 number against a $17,720 deductible, the family is entitled to know exactly what the panel decided, and any ambiguity in the way Farmers describes the panel's decision should be resolved in the family's favor.

#### (e) The "Lawsuits Against Us" suit-limitation clause

When the family asked Farmers directly about claim deadlines on 7/10/2025, Nic Brau responded with a paragraph of policy boilerplate: "Please note there are time limits set forth in the Conditions ('Lawsuits Against Us' or 'Legal Action Against Us' or 'Suit Against Us') section of the policy which, depending on your state, **may affect the time within which you may pursue your claim. This period may have been extended by statute or case law.**" *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (7/11/2025 entry).)*

The "may have been extended by statute or case law" clause acknowledges in Farmers' own words that the 2-year contractual suit limitation is not unambiguously the deadline that controls. *Atwater* and Minn. Stat. § 65A.01's Standard Fire Policy interaction with the contractual suit limitation is itself an open question on the family record (see `A1.md` §6 and `A3.md` §6). To the extent Farmers later asserts the 2-year deadline as a hard bar, *contra proferentem* should resolve any ambiguity in the family's favor — and Farmers' own 7/11/2025 written acknowledgment that the period "may have been extended" is the strongest possible record of the policy's drafted ambiguity on this point.

This is not a coverage-question target. It is a procedural/limitations target. It is included here because the same doctrine governs and because the family will need this argument if Farmers ever challenges timeliness.

### 1.6 Companion doctrines

E4 lives alongside three other doctrines and should be briefed in coordination with them:

- **E1 (*Quade* preclusion).** *Quade* binds Farmers to the panel's hail-causation finding for F1.7 and B1.2. E4 then operates on the policy *text* — particularly the marring exclusion and J6944 — that Farmers cited or might re-cite to defeat coverage. The two doctrines reinforce each other: *Quade* says Farmers cannot relitigate the cause; *contra proferentem* says any ambiguity in the language Farmers relies on is resolved in the family's favor.
- **E3 (*Atwater* reasonable expectations).** Brief together. E4 handles the cases where the policy text is ambiguous on its face. E3 reaches the additional cases where the language is unambiguous but produces a result significantly different from what an ordinary insured would reasonably expect.
- **B4 (estoppel / waiver of denial rationales).** Where E4 says the policy text is reasonably susceptible to more than one reading, B4 says Farmers' own use of the text in the 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials shifted between incompatible readings — and Farmers is estopped from re-asserting any reading inconsistent with positions it has already taken. The two arguments cover adjacent ground and should be briefed together as a doctrinal package against Farmers' moving rationale.

### 1.7 Procedural posture — how this argument shows up in a complaint

Like E1, E2, and E3, E4 is best pleaded as an interpretive doctrine inside other counts rather than as a freestanding cause of action. Minnesota does not recognize a standalone "cause of action for *contra proferentem*"; the doctrine is a tool of contract construction the court uses to resolve coverage disputes raised through:

- **A1 (breach of contract)** — supplying the interpretive premise that any ambiguity in the marring/denting interaction, the J6944 endorsement, the windstorm/hail restoration clause, and the "comparable material and quality" replacement standard is resolved in favor of coverage.
- **A2 (declaratory judgment under Minn. Stat. Ch. 555)** — asking the court to declare that, where ambiguous, each contested provision is to be construed against Farmers and in favor of the insured.
- **Opposition to Farmers' affirmative defenses** — answering any Farmers attempt to revive the 9/3/2024 or 11/4/2024 exclusions for any window with the *contra proferentem* frame in addition to *Quade* preclusion (E1) and the *Atwater* hidden-exclusion frame (E3).
- **Opposition to any timeliness defense** — answering any Farmers argument that the 2-year contractual suit limitation hard-bars the lawsuit with the doctrine of *contra proferentem* applied to the suit-limitation clause and to Farmers' own 7/11/2025 written acknowledgment that the period "may have been extended by statute or case law."

A reasonable E4 pleading frame inside A2 looks like:

> "Plaintiffs respectfully request that this Court declare, pursuant to Minn. Stat. Ch. 555 and Minnesota's settled rule that ambiguities in insurance contracts are construed against the drafter (*Nathe Brothers, Inc. v. American Nat'l Fire Ins. Co.*, 615 N.W.2d 341, 344 (Minn. 2000); *Hammer v. Investors Life Ins. Co. of N. Am.*, 511 N.W.2d 6, 8 (Minn. 1994); *Henning Nelson Constr. Co. v. Fireman's Fund Am. Life Ins. Co.*, 383 N.W.2d 645, 652 (Minn. 1986)), that:
> (a) The interaction of the policy's Definitions § 16 ('Marring … denting'), § I.A.8 ('marring of covered property'), and § I.A.8(b) (windstorm/hail restoration) is, at minimum, reasonably susceptible to more than one interpretation, and is to be construed in favor of coverage for the family's hail-caused window dents;
> (b) Endorsement J6944's gate ('not damaged by the covered peril'), its (1)(iii) reason-gate ('obsolescence or discontinuation'), and its interaction with the policy's 'comparable material and quality' replacement-cost obligation are, at minimum, reasonably susceptible to more than one interpretation, and are to be construed in favor of coverage for the cost of replacing the damaged windows with current-production extruded aluminum equivalents and any matching obligation that attaches under *Cedar Bluff*; and
> (c) Any ambiguity in the policy's 'Lawsuits Against Us' / 'Legal Action Against Us' / 'Suit Against Us' provision — including the ambiguity Defendant itself acknowledged in its 7/11/2025 written communication that the period 'may have been extended by statute or case law' — is to be construed in favor of the timeliness of this action."

Pleaded this way, E4 is a standalone interpretive request the court can grant without disturbing the appraisal.

### 1.8 What to expect in opposition

Farmers will attack E4 on the following lines, and the family should pre-empt them:

- **"The text is unambiguous on its face."** *Columbia Heights Motors* defense. Farmers will argue the marring exclusion is plainly worded, the J6944 endorsement is plainly worded, and the family is "creating ambiguity where none exists." Counter: the family does not need to make every contested provision ambiguous; it needs only to identify the specific interpretive seams where two readings are reasonable. The marring/denting/windstorm-restoration interaction is one such seam (the policy's own (b) exception confirms the drafter knew hail produces denting); J6944's "not damaged by the covered peril" gate is another (where windows are dented, they are not "undamaged"); the "comparable material and quality" obligation read against J6944's matching limits is a third. Each is a discrete textual seam, not a manufactured ambiguity.
- **"Carlson and Columbia Heights Motors require us to apply unambiguous text."** Conceded — and not the family's fight. *Contra proferentem* operates only after step-one ambiguity is established. The family is asking the court to find ambiguity at specific identified seams, not to override unambiguous text generally.
- **"The doctrine has been narrowed since Atwater."** That is true of *Atwater* (reasonable expectations / E3); it is not equally true of *contra proferentem*. *Hammer* and *Nathe Brothers* remain unmodified statements of the rule, and the Minnesota Supreme Court has not narrowed *contra proferentem* itself in the way *Carlson* narrowed *Atwater*. Brief the distinction explicitly.
- **"The family's reading is unreasonable."** Standard step-one fight. The family's burden is to show its reading is "reasonable," not "the only reasonable reading." Where two reasonable readings exist, the rule operates. The family's strongest move is to point to the policy's own text — particularly the § I.A.8(b) hail/windstorm restoration clause and the J6944 "not damaged by the covered peril" language — and show that the family's reading is one the drafter contemplated and provided for.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **35 / 100** as a primary path to recovery; **55 / 100** as an interpretive overlay inside A1 and A2; weighted blend ~**45 / 100**

E4 is two arguments in one, and the rating reflects the difference.

**As a primary path to recovery (~35/100).** *Contra proferentem* by itself does not produce a dollar award. It removes Farmers' interpretive defenses where ambiguity exists and resolves the ambiguity in favor of coverage. The downstream dollar recovery still has to come from A1 (breach of contract), and A1 still has to navigate the deductible math. The doctrine is also constrained by *Columbia Heights Motors*: a court will not invent ambiguity to reach coverage. The family must identify specific reasonable interpretive seams, not gesture broadly at "the policy is ambiguous."

**As an interpretive overlay inside A1 and A2 (~55/100).** Read as the operative interpretive principle for the contract and declaratory counts, E4 is one of the cleanest doctrines the family has. *Hammer* and *Nathe Brothers* are unbroken Minnesota Supreme Court authority. The doctrine is not narrowed by any subsequent decision. Farmers cannot argue it away — only argue around it on a seam-by-seam basis. Each seam the family wins under E4 is one less defense Farmers has under A1 and A2.

**Weighted blend, reflecting how E4 is realistically used (as an interpretive overlay supporting A1 and A2): ~45/100.** That is slightly higher than E3's blended rating (~35/100) because *contra proferentem* is the broader and more frequently used Minnesota doctrine, and it does not carry the *Carlson*-narrowing problem. It is below E1's blended rating (~80/100 in its preclusion role) because preclusion is a stronger procedural lever than interpretation.

### 2.2 What drives the rating up

- **The doctrine is bedrock Minnesota Supreme Court law.** *Hammer*, *Nathe Brothers*, *Henning Nelson*, *Atwater*, *Columbia Heights Motors* — all squarely state the rule. No subsequent case narrows *contra proferentem* itself. Farmers cannot challenge the doctrine; it can only fight on whether step-one ambiguity exists at any given seam.
- **The marring/denting/windstorm-restoration interaction is a textbook E4 target.** The policy's own (b) exception restores hail-caused marring. Farmers cited the marring exclusion to deny a hail claim. The interaction between the exclusion and the restoration clause, mediated through the definitions section's inclusion of "denting," is reasonably susceptible to two readings. The 9/12/2025 appraisal panel found hail causation under *Quade*. Farmers' reading of the marring exclusion as defeating hail-caused denting is, at minimum, not the only reasonable reading.
- **J6944's textual gates are reasonably susceptible to multiple readings.** The damage gate ("not damaged by the covered peril"), the reason gate ("obsolescence or discontinuation"), and the diminution gate (J6944(2)) each carry interpretive seams the family can target. *Noonan* and *Pleasure Creek* uphold matching exclusions in general; neither holds that J6944 is unambiguous on its face as applied to a defunct-manufacturer / mostly-damaged-home / extruded-aluminum-windows fact pattern.
- **Farmers' own use of the policy text supports step-one ambiguity.** The 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials read the same policy two incompatible ways within sixty-two days. The 7/11/2025 Brau email acknowledged the suit-limitation period "may have been extended by statute or case law." Farmers' interpretive history is a contemporaneous record that the policy's exclusions and conditions are not unambiguous in the way Farmers will later need them to be.
- **The doctrine pairs cleanly with E1 and E3.** *Quade* preclusion (E1) closes the causation question; *Atwater* (E3) closes the hidden-exclusion question; *contra proferentem* (E4) closes the ambiguity question. Together, they form a layered interpretive defense: causation is locked in, hidden definitions cannot defeat coverage, and any remaining textual ambiguity is resolved in the family's favor.
- **The doctrine does not depend on disturbing the appraisal.** It is fully compatible with the 9/12/2025 award standing — and is in fact strengthened by the award standing, because *Quade* causation pairs with *contra proferentem* construction to leave Farmers with no remaining defense on the F1.7/B1.2 windows.

### 2.3 What drives the rating down

- **Step-one ambiguity is contested at every seam.** *Columbia Heights Motors* explicitly forbids manufactured ambiguity. Farmers will fight the ambiguity-existence question on each of the family's targets, and a court can plausibly side with Farmers on any one of them. The marring/denting target is the family's strongest; J6944's gates are mid-tier; the 9/3-vs-11/4 conduct frame is the weakest.
- **The doctrine is a tool, not a weapon.** It produces interpretive rulings, not damages. Even where E4 succeeds at every seam the family pleads, the family still needs A1 to deliver the dollar recovery and the broader factual record (more damaged windows; matching obligation; pricing methodology) to break the deductible.
- **Some of the language is plainly worded.** The wear/tear, corrosion/deterioration, neglect, and faulty-construction exclusions cited in the 11/4/2024 denial are squarely placed in the exclusions section and are not facially ambiguous. *Quade* preclusion (E1) handles those for the F1.7/B1.2 windows; for windows the panel did not evaluate, Farmers retains those exclusions as live defenses, and *contra proferentem* will not defeat them on their face. The family must beat those exclusions on the record (causation, hail evidence, expert opinion) — *contra proferentem* does not.
- **The dollar mechanism is indirect.** Even where E4 wins on every seam, the dollar recovery still depends on the broader case mechanics: more windows proved damaged (under A1, possibly with A3 *Herll* clarification); matching obligation attaching (*Cedar Bluff* via E2/E4); accurate replacement-cost pricing. E4 is one piece of the lever, not the lever itself.
- **Minnesota courts are sometimes skeptical of ambiguity arguments.** *Columbia Heights Motors*'s "fastidiously guard against the invitation to create ambiguities" framing has produced a body of court-of-appeals decisions that resist ambiguity claims. The family should expect a court to apply *Hammer* carefully and to scrutinize each interpretive seam separately.
- **The doctrine's weak version overlaps with E3.** Where E4 resolves ambiguity, E3 in its narrow post-*Carlson* form sometimes does the same work. The family should brief them together but expect a court to lean on E4 first because it carries no *Carlson*-narrowing problem.

### 2.4 What "35/100 (primary) / 55/100 (interpretive)" means practically

E4 is filed as an interpretive doctrine inside A1 and A2, with five identified seams: (a) the marring/denting/windstorm-restoration interaction; (b) the J6944 damage gate, reason gate, and "comparable material and quality" interaction; (c) the 9/3-vs-11/4 use-pattern frame (briefed alongside B4); (d) the F1.7/B1.2 award-code construction issue (briefed alongside A3 Tier 1); and (e) the suit-limitation clause. Plead E4 as the operative interpretive principle for A1 and A2; do not plead it as a freestanding count. Use it to lock in favorable readings of contested seams. Couple it with E1 (preclusion), E3 (reasonable expectations), and B4 (estoppel) so the court sees a layered interpretive package rather than a single doctrine pulling all the weight.

### 3.1 Facts supporting target (a) — the marring/denting/windstorm-restoration interaction

- **Definitions § 16 enumerates "denting" as a form of "Marring."** Verbatim: "Marring - means any disfigurement, blemish, discoloration, weathering or stretching, or the like, of or to covered property, including by way of example but not limited to scratching, scorching, **denting**, creasing, gouging, fading, staining, tearing or thinning." *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf Definitions § 16.)*
- **§ I.A.8 excludes "Marring."** "We do not insure loss or damage which is marring of covered property." *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf § I.A.8.)*
- **§ I.A.8(b) restores marring coverage when caused by hail.** "However, we do insure marring which is directly caused by, arises out of or results from the following causes of loss …: a. fire or lightning; **b. windstorm or hail**; …" *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf § I.A.8(b).)*
- **The 11/4/2024 denial cited the Marring exclusion as a basis for denying the family's hail claim and did not engage with the windstorm/hail restoration in (b).** *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf p. 3 ("Marring").)*
- **Prieve's report — incorporated into the 11/4/2024 denial — repeatedly described the observed damage as "dents," "marks," and "indentations"** at the front/west triple window, the upper-west 3-unit bedroom, the upper southwest bedroom, the east family-room bay projection, the upper-east window above the four-season porch, the four-season porch SE/E/NE/N windows, the kitchen east, the main-level NE 3-unit, the upper-north above the kitchen, and the rear/east garage window. *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 4–9.)* Each instance is the very phenomenon Definitions § 16 enumerates as "denting" under "Marring."
- **The 9/12/2025 Appraisal Award found hail causation for F1.7 and B1.2.** *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png.)* Under *Quade*, that finding is binding. The denting on those windows is therefore "marring … directly caused by … hail" within § I.A.8(b)'s restoration clause — the family's reading. Farmers' contrary reading (denting falls within the marring exclusion regardless of cause) is at minimum not the only reasonable reading; the (b) exception's existence shows the drafter knew hail produces denting and chose to restore coverage for it.
- **Step-one ambiguity is established by the policy text itself.** Whether Farmers can deny a hail claim by characterizing the loss as "marring" without engaging the windstorm/hail restoration is reasonably susceptible to two readings. Step two of *Hammer*/*Nathe Brothers* resolves the ambiguity against Farmers (the drafter) and in favor of coverage.

### 3.2 Facts supporting target (b) — the J6944 endorsement gates

- **J6944 is an active endorsement on the policy in force at the loss date.** *(HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf "Active endorsements" line; HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf, "Policy Endorsements" subsection.)*
- **The endorsement excludes "cost to repair or replace property which is not damaged by the covered peril and does not match" because of (i) marring; (ii) wear/tear/deterioration/corrosion/decay; or (iii) obsolescence or discontinuation.** *(HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf, J6944.)* Each of these textual gates carries an interpretive seam — see §§3.2.1, 3.2.2, 3.2.3 below.

#### 3.2.1 The damage gate

- **Prieve's report documents dents across most of the home.** *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter-11-4.pdf pp. 4–9.)* The family's record corroborates with photographs submitted on 9/5, 9/8, 9/10/2024, the 1/31/2025 appeal package, the 2/10/2025 second response, and the 5/28/2025 NWS submission through agent Pete. *(EventDocuments/2025-01-31-ResponseToFarmer-01-31-25-DamagesAfter2020.pdf; EventDocuments/2025-02-10-SecondResponseToFinalReport.pdf; EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (5/28/2025 entry).)*
- **The family wrote to Farmers on 10/21/2025: "pretty much all the windows were damaged, as evidenced by the photos submitted through the claim process."** *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (10/21/2025 entry).)* Farmers did not dispute the photographic record on the merits; it directed the family to its own appraiser for any explanation of the scope gap.
- **Step-one ambiguity:** Whether "not damaged by the covered peril" reaches windows the family has documented as dented (but the panel did not award) is reasonably susceptible to two readings. Read literally, dented windows are not "undamaged" and fall outside the gate; read as Farmers will press, anything not on the award is "undamaged" for endorsement purposes. The first reading honors the plain words; the second collapses the gate. Both are arguably reasonable. *Contra proferentem* resolves the ambiguity in favor of the literal reading and against Farmers.

#### 3.2.2 The reason gate ("obsolescence or discontinuation")

- **Farmers identified the awarded windows as manufactured by Senco with stamps from 1993, 1997, and 2008.** *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/18/2025 entry).)* The family's account names the manufacturer as Semco Windows and Doors. *(CaseContext/key-facts-excluding-legal-context.md Phase 7 fact #11.)* The Senco/Semco identification has not yet been resolved on the project record; pre-filing factual cleanup is required before relying on the manufacturer-defunct framing in court (`E2.md` §3.7).

- **Whether (1)(iii) "obsolescence or discontinuation" reaches a fact pattern where the manufacturer is closed but a successor exists with the assets — and may or may not be able to produce a comparable profile — is reasonably susceptible to two readings.** Farmers will press the broader reading; the family will press the narrower reading. Step one is satisfied; step two resolves in favor of coverage.

#### 3.2.3 The "comparable material and quality" interaction

- **Step-one ambiguity:** Whether "comparable material and quality" requires pricing replacement against current-production equivalents (the Pella Reserve / Marvin Ultimate identification the family and contractor reached) or whether J6944's matching limits permit pricing against a closed-manufacturer reference is reasonably susceptible to two readings. *Cedar Bluff* leans toward the broader reading; the boundary with J6944 is contested. *Contra proferentem* resolves the contested boundary in the family's favor.

### 3.3 Facts supporting target (c) — the 9/3/2024 vs 11/4/2024 use-pattern frame

- **The 9/3/2024 first denial said "interior water damage around your windows was observed. There were no other damages observed. There was no opening created by the direct force of wind or hail that could have occurred and allowed water to enter and cause immediate damage."** *(EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf pp. 1–2.)* This is a Section I "Limited Water Coverage" / I.B.2 reading: no exterior damage, no opening, so no coverage for interior water damage.

- **The two readings are mutually incompatible.** Under the first, the home has no exterior damage. Under the second, the home has exterior damage that is excluded under wear/tear. Both cannot be correct. Farmers' own interpretive history demonstrates that the same policy text supports more than one reading — and Farmers' first written reading is incompatible with its second.
- **Within sixty-two days, Farmers used the policy text two incompatible ways.** That use pattern is direct evidence that the policy is reasonably susceptible to more than one reading. Step-one ambiguity is established by Farmers' conduct, not just by the words on the page. *Contra proferentem* then operates against the drafter.
- **The frame pairs with B4 (estoppel/waiver of denial rationales).** Where E4 says the policy is reasonably susceptible to more than one reading, B4 says Farmers cannot revive any reading inconsistent with positions it has already taken. The two arguments cover adjacent ground. *(See `B4.md`.)*

## 4. Does This Argument Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No. E4 is a standalone interpretive doctrine. It does not require the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be vacated, modified, or corrected, and it can operate whether the award stands or is disturbed in part.** Like E3's interpretive register, E4 is the kind of question Minnesota state courts decide all the time on a Minn. Stat. Ch. 555 declaratory record without touching an underlying appraisal. The only directional dependency is the same one E1 and E3 carry: full *Mork* vacatur of the award would erase the *Quade* causation finding that strengthens the E4 narrative for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows. That is a strategic constraint on A3's Tier-3 architecture, not an obstacle to E4 itself.

### 4.2 Compatibility with the rest of the case

| Argument | E4 compatibility |
|---|---|
| **A1 — Breach of contract** | Fully compatible. E4 supplies the operative interpretive principle for any contested seam in the policy text and resolves each in favor of coverage. |
| **A2 — Declaratory judgment** | Fully compatible — and the most natural pleading vehicle. The five E4 targets (§1.5) translate directly into declaratory questions inside A2. |
| **A3 — Vacate / modify / correct appraisal** | Compatible with Tier 1 (*Herll*/*Rymer* clarification, including the F1.7/B1.2 code-construction question — target (d)) and Tier 2 (§ 65A.01 itemization correction). In tension with Tier 3 (full *Mork* vacatur) only insofar as full vacatur erases the *Quade* causation finding that strengthens the E4 narrative for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows; E4 itself does not require the appraisal to fall. |
| **B1 — Bad faith under § 604.18** | Synergistic in narrative. Where E4 establishes that ambiguous policy text is to be construed against the drafter, B1's objective-unreasonableness prong is sharpened: Farmers denied coverage based on a reading the doctrine resolves against it. |
| **B4 — Estoppel/waiver** | Synergistic. B4 covers Farmers' shifting denials directly; E4 reaches the same conduct as evidence of step-one ambiguity in the policy text. The two arguments are best briefed together as a doctrinal package against Farmers' moving rationale. |
| **C1 — Unreasonable investigation under § 72A.201** | Synergistic. Farmers' "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule has no policy basis at all (it is not a policy provision); E4 reaches the policy provisions Farmers did cite (the marring exclusion, the J6944 gates, the suit-limitation clause) and resolves their ambiguities against Farmers. C1 reaches the gatekeeping rule itself. |
| **E1 — *Quade* preclusion** | Mutually reinforcing. *Quade* binds Farmers to causation; E4 then operates on the policy *text* Farmers would otherwise rely on to deny. The two doctrines together close the loop on the F1.7 and B1.2 windows. |
| **E2 — J6944 scope limits** | Mutually reinforcing on the J6944 targets. E2 supplies the textual scope-limit reading; E4 supplies the doctrinal reason a court should adopt the family's reading at any contested seam. |
| **E3 — *Atwater* reasonable expectations** | Closely related. Brief together. E4 closes ambiguity in the family's favor where the policy text is open to more than one reading; E3 reaches the additional cases where the language is unambiguous on its face but the result is significantly different from the homeowner's reasonable expectation. |

### 4.3 Practical pleading recommendation

E4 should appear in two places in the complaint, both downstream of A1 and A2:

- **As declaratory questions in the A2 declaratory-judgment count.** Convert the five targets in §1.5 into declaratory sub-questions inside A2's prayer for relief. The marring/denting/windstorm-restoration interaction (target (a)) is the lead. The J6944 gates (target (b)) follow. The 9/3-vs-11/4 use-pattern frame (target (c)) and the F1.7/B1.2 award-code construction issue (target (d)) are narrative reinforcements briefed alongside B4 and A3 Tier 1 respectively. The suit-limitation clause (target (e)) is a standalone procedural sub-question that should be pleaded preemptively to forestall any timeliness defense.
- **As the operative interpretive principle inside A1.** Use *contra proferentem*, alongside *Quade* (E1) and *Atwater* (E3), to defeat any Farmers attempt to revive the 11/4/2024 marring exclusion as a defense to A1's breach claim, and to support the family's reading that J6944's gates do not reach the dollar mechanism the family needs to defeat the deductible.

E4 does **not** belong as a freestanding cause of action. Pleading it as one over-promises what the doctrine does. Plead it as relief inside A2 and as a doctrinal predicate inside A1, and the architecture stays clean.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- **E4 does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting the appraisal.** It is standalone in the sense that the appraisal does not need to be disturbed for E4 to operate.
- **E4 is *strengthened* — not required, just strengthened — by the appraisal staying in place.** The 9/12/2025 award's *Quade* causation finding for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows reinforces the *contra proferentem* frame for those two windows: Farmers' interpretive defenses are pitted against a binding causation finding plus an interpretive doctrine that resolves any remaining ambiguity in the family's favor.
- **E4 is compatible with A3's Tier-1 *Herll*/*Rymer* clarification and Tier-2 § 65A.01 itemization-correction sub-claims.** Both leave the panel's findings intact and sharpen what the panel decided. The E4 narrative on target (d) (the F1.7/B1.2 award codes) tracks the same direction as A3 Tier 1.
- **E4 is in mild tension with A3's Tier-3 full *Mork* vacatur** for the same reason E1 and E3 are — full vacatur erases the panel's causation finding, and the E4 narrative for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows loses some force. The strategic ranking in `A3.md` §4, `E1.md` §4, and `E3.md` §4 already reflects this: lead with Tiers 1 and 2; reserve Tier 3 for facts discovery actually delivers.

- [ALFA International — Minnesota Insurance Law Compendium](https://www.alfainternational.com/compendium/insurance-law/minnesota/) — Minnesota chapter on policy interpretation, ambiguity rule, and insured-vs-insurer adhesion-contract treatment.
- [Aaron Hall — "Fundamentals of Insurance Policy Construction in Minnesota"](https://aaronhall.com/fundamentals-of-insurance-policy-construction-minnesota-legal-overview/) — practitioner-oriented overview of the Minnesota ambiguity rule.
- [Park, "Reasonable Expectations" (Maslon)](https://www.mfllaw.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/Park-Reasonable-Expectations-article.pdf) — practitioner article on *Atwater* and *contra proferentem* interplay.
- [Cornell LII — *contra proferentem*](https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/contra_proferentem) — general background on the doctrine.
- [Mitchell Hamline Law Review — "Ensuring Insurance Insures the Insured"](https://open.mitchellhamline.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=1684&context=wmlr) — Minnesota policy-construction overview.
- [North Dakota Law Review — "The Ambiguity in the Doctrine of Reasonable Expectations"](https://commons.und.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2042&context=ndlr) — comparative treatment of ambiguity / reasonable-expectations interaction.
