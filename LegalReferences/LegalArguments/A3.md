## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The underlying appraisal statute: Minn. Stat. § 65A.01, Subd. 3

The Minnesota Standard Fire Insurance Policy's appraisal clause — embedded verbatim in every policy governed by § 65A.01, including this one — is the primary statutory source for what the appraisal panel was required to do, and therefore the source of the substantive yardstick for whether the 9/12/2025 award was properly made. The exact statutory text (from `EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf` p. 4, reproducing Minn. Stat. § 65A.01, Subd. 3) is:

> "In case the insured and this company, except in case of total loss on buildings, shall fail to agree as to the actual cash value or the amount of loss, then, on the written demand of either, each shall select a **competent and disinterested appraiser** and notify the other of the appraiser selected within **20 days** of such demand. . . . The appraisers shall first select a **competent and disinterested umpire**; and failing for **15 days** to agree upon such umpire, then a presiding judge of the above mentioned court may appoint such an umpire. . . . The appraisers shall then appraise the loss, **stating separately actual value and loss to each item**; and, failing to agree, shall submit their differences, only, to the umpire. **An award in writing, so itemized, of any two when filed with this company shall determine the amount of actual value and loss.** Each appraiser shall be paid by the selecting party, or the party for whom selected, and the expense of the appraisal and umpire shall be paid by the parties equally."

(Emphasis supplied.) Three statutory requirements matter most for A3:

1. Each party-selected appraiser must be "competent and **disinterested**."
2. The award must "appraise the loss, **stating separately actual value and loss to each item**."
3. The binding two-of-three award must be "so itemized."

A panel that does not comply with these requirements has not produced an award that § 65A.01 describes. A court asked to enforce such an award — or invited to modify, correct, remand, or set it aside — has ample textual footing to act, because the statute itself supplies the standard of what a valid § 65A.01 award looks like.

### 1.2 The governing common-law vacatur standard: *Mork* and *McQuaid*

The Minnesota Supreme Court's leading case remains *Mork v. Eureka-Security Fire & Marine Ins. Co.*, 230 Minn. 382, 42 N.W.2d 33 (1950). *Mork* holds that an appraisal award "is attended with every presumption of validity and will not be vacated unless it clearly appears that it was the result of fraud or because of some misfeasance or malfeasance or wrongdoing on the part of the appraisers." The burden is on the challenger to establish a ground for vacatur by "clear allegations and proof." "Mere inadequacy" of the award is not enough; the inadequacy must be "so gross as to" evidence fraud.

*McQuaid Market House Co. v. Home Ins. Co.*, 147 Minn. 254, 180 N.W. 97 (1920), treats the appraisal award as a common-law arbitration award that "may not be lightly set aside." *McQuaid* recognizes two challenge grounds that remain live today: (i) an award "invalid and void on its face because no facts were stated to fix the liability"; and (ii) an appraiser or umpire who was not "fair, impartial, and disinterested."

These two cases, not any three-prong "without authority / fraud or mistake / substantial compliance" formulation, supply the Minnesota vacatur standard. The three-prong test that appears in some secondary sources (and in this project's earlier `appraisal_research.MD`) is Texas law; see `appraisal_vacatur_caselaw.md` §1.2. Any filing in Minnesota court should lead with *Mork* and *McQuaid* and treat the substantive ideas behind the three Texas prongs as compatible with — but not a substitute for — the *Mork* standard.

### 1.3 Appraisal is not UAA arbitration — *Oliver v. State Farm*

*Oliver v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Ins. Co.*, 939 N.W.2d 749 (Minn. 2020), holds that insurance appraisal under the Standard Fire Policy is not an "agreement to arbitrate" and that the Minnesota Uniform Arbitration Act (Minn. Stat. Ch. 572B) does not directly apply. Two consequences matter:

- **Section 572B.23's grounds — "corruption, fraud, or other undue means"; "evident partiality"; "misconduct prejudicing the rights"; "exceeded the arbitrator's powers"; "refusal to postpone the hearing" — are not directly binding on appraisals.** They are persuasive by analogy and are routinely borrowed in appraisal briefing, but a Minnesota court will not hold Farmers to § 572B.23 as a matter of statutory obligation.
- **Section 572B.23's 90-day filing window does not directly bind appraisal challenges.** The parallel 90-day window that Minnesota courts have recognized for appraisals — see *Garlyn Inc. v. Auto Owners Ins. Co.* (Minn. App.), as summarized in `appraisal_vacatur_caselaw.md` §1.8 — nonetheless ran from the 9/12/2025 award and expired on or about 12/11/2025. Any attack on the award must therefore be embedded in a breach-of-contract complaint rather than filed as a standalone motion to vacate. That is the operative constraint for A3.

### 1.4 The clarification-and-remand avenue: *Herll* and *Rymer*

*Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018) (applying Minnesota law), is the most useful authority for this family. On facts remarkably close to ours — hail damage to the windows of a Minnesota home, demand for appraisal, dispute over which windows the award actually covered — the Eighth Circuit reversed a district-court judgment confirming the award and held that where the award is "ambiguous" as to scope the matter should be "return[ed] . . . to the appraisal panel for clarification." *Herll* treats remand-for-clarification as the ordinary remedy for ambiguity, distinct from vacatur.

*Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026), builds directly on *Herll* and adds two rules important here: (i) the district court properly sought clarification from the panel when the award was ambiguous; and (ii) post-award appraiser or umpire testimony cannot be used to "reinterpret or expand" the award — such testimony is admissible only to show the award should be vacated for misconduct.

These cases matter for A3 because the realistic remedy on this record is not wholesale vacatur under *Mork* but (a) modification or correction of the award to conform to § 65A.01's itemization requirement, and (b) remand to the panel under *Herll*/*Rymer* for clarification of scope and methodology.

### 1.5 The related arbitration-vacatur grounds available by analogy under § 572B.23

Although *Oliver* forecloses direct application, § 572B.23(a) is useful persuasive structure. The statute authorizes vacatur where:

1. The award was "procured by corruption, fraud, or other undue means";
2. There was "evident partiality" by an arbitrator, "corruption" by an arbitrator, or "misconduct by an arbitrator prejudicing the rights of a party";
3. An arbitrator refused to postpone the hearing on sufficient cause, "refused to consider evidence material to the controversy," or otherwise so conducted the hearing as to prejudice the rights of a party;
4. An arbitrator "exceeded the arbitrator's powers"; or
5. There was no agreement to arbitrate, or the arbitration was conducted without proper notice.

A Minnesota court may draw on these categories when applying the *Mork* standard of "fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing." The misconduct categories (No. 2, 3) and exceeded-powers category (No. 4) have textual analogues in Minnesota appraisal case law and are the categories most relevant here.

### 1.6 The modification / correction remedy specifically

Separate from vacatur, Minnesota courts retain common-law authority to *modify* or *correct* an award where the error is computational, descriptive, or procedural rather than substantive — and where leaving it uncorrected would produce an award that does not conform to the policy's appraisal clause. The idea tracks § 572B.24's grounds for modification ("evident mathematical miscalculation," "evident mistake in the description of any person, thing, or property," award "upon a matter not submitted," imperfect form), which apply by analogy. The family's strongest modification/correction argument on this record targets the award's failure to "state separately actual value and loss to each item" as required by § 65A.01, Subd. 3 — i.e., the award needs to be reformed to identify which windows it covers and at what item-level value.

### 1.7 What this count looks like on the face of a complaint

Pleaded as a subsidiary count to A1 (breach of contract), A3 would allege, in substance:

> "The September 12, 2025 appraisal award fails to 'appraise the loss, stating separately actual value and loss to each item' as Minn. Stat. § 65A.01, Subd. 3 requires. The award identifies the covered windows only by the undefined designations 'F1.7' and 'B1.2' against a lump-sum replacement cost of $12,800. No umpire report, estimate, or methodology accompanies the award. Defendant's own appraiser did not sign, and Defendant has given inconsistent post-award descriptions of which windows the award purports to cover. Plaintiffs respectfully request that this Court (a) remand the award to the appraisal panel for clarification under *Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018), and *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. 2026); or in the alternative (b) modify or correct the award to comply with § 65A.01, Subd. 3 and to conform to the evidence of hail damage to the full scope of damaged windows; or in the further alternative (c) vacate the award as the product of misfeasance by the appraisal panel under *Mork v. Eureka-Security Fire & Marine Ins. Co.*, 230 Minn. 382, 42 N.W.2d 33 (1950)."

Relief sought would be an order clarifying, modifying, correcting, or vacating the award, with the case then proceeding on the A1 breach-of-contract count for the full scope of covered hail damage.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **45 / 100** overall — but variance is wide and depends on which sub-relief is pursued

The global rating reflects a weighted blend of three distinct sub-claims that have very different strengths:

- **Sub-claim (a) — Remand to the panel for clarification under *Herll*/*Rymer*: ~65/100.** The award is genuinely ambiguous on its face (no itemization; undefined codes; Farmers' conflicting descriptions; no umpire report). *Herll* is a Minnesota-law, hail-damage, window-scope case on all fours with ours. The remedy is lighter than vacatur and does not require meeting *Mork*. This is the strongest A3 sub-claim and should be pleaded first in the alternative.
- **Sub-claim (b) — Modification or correction to comply with § 65A.01 Subd. 3's "stating separately ... to each item" requirement: ~45/100.** The statutory text is clear. The award is plainly not itemized; the codes "F1.7" and "B1.2" are not "items" as § 65A.01 uses the word. But the remedy — court-directed modification of an award — is unusual, and most courts prefer remand over re-drafting. This sub-claim is better framed as the substantive reason for remand rather than as a stand-alone request for the court to re-write the award.
- **Sub-claim (c) — Vacatur under *Mork*: ~25/100 on the current record, ~50/100 if discovery produces misfeasance evidence.** *Mork*'s bar is high: clear proof of fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing. The facts we have now — umpire producing no report, Foster's contractor-driven selection, Tomes's non-signature, conflicting Farmers descriptions of scope — are suggestive but not yet "clear" proof of wrongdoing. The rating moves materially only if discovery shows (i) Foster had an undisclosed insurance-side relationship, (ii) Roos did not independently evaluate every item, or (iii) the panel valued the award using Semco prices it knew were phantom. Without those facts, *Mork* vacatur is an alternative-pleaded long-shot, not a primary claim.

Weighted blend, with emphasis on (a) (the realistic remedy) and (b) (the statutory hook): **~45/100**.

### 2.2 What drives the rating up

- **The award on its face does not satisfy § 65A.01, Subd. 3.** The statute requires the award to "appraise the loss, stating separately actual value and loss to each item." The 9/12/2025 award lists only "F1.7" and "B1.2" against a lump $12,800 RCV / $6,400 ACV. No line-item description of each window, no separate valuation, no umpire report. The insured could not decode the award and had to email Farmers on 9/18/2025 just to learn that "F1.7" and "B1.2" allegedly referred to "front right" and "rear elevation" windows — a description Farmers then contradicted itself on 9/19/2025 and 10/14/2025. A direct statutory-noncompliance theory is rare in appraisal litigation and, on these facts, squarely supported by the statutory text.
- ***Herll* is a hail-damage, window-scope, Minnesota-law case.** The Eighth Circuit remanded an ambiguous window appraisal for clarification — exactly the relief the family needs. *Herll* is recent (2018) and applies Minnesota substantive law through diversity jurisdiction; a Minnesota state court would treat it as highly persuasive. *Rymer* (2026) reinforces *Herll* and provides a contemporary framework for how the remand mechanics should work.
- **Multiple independent facial defects converge.** The award is a single-page form. There is no umpire report (confirmed by Farmers 9/24/2025). Farmers' own appraiser Tomes did not sign. Farmers' post-award descriptions of scope are mutually contradictory. The insured's own appraiser Foster was retained by the contractor Pure Construction (not by the family) and later disclosed a prior insurance-industry background. On their own, each defect is modest; together, they meaningfully support the "this award is not what § 65A.01 requires" framing.
- **No Minnesota appellate case forecloses the argument.** *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012), fixes causation for items the panel decided but does not shield the panel's scope decisions from judicial review where the award fails § 65A.01's itemization requirement. *Oliver v. State Farm* rules out direct UAA application but preserves the common-law *Mork* standard and does not displace *Herll*'s clarification doctrine.

### 2.3 What drives the rating down

- ***Mork*'s presumption of validity is heavy.** Every version of A3 runs into the baseline rule that appraisal awards are "attended with every presumption of validity" and will not be disturbed absent clear proof of fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing. Courts have repeatedly said "mere inadequacy" is not enough. The family's most intuitive complaint — that the award should cover more windows — is exactly what *Mork* tells courts to ignore without a separate misconduct showing.
- **The insured's own appraiser signed.** Foster executed the award. A court will ask why the family is suing to set aside an amount their own party-selected representative agreed to. The factual answer — that Foster was chosen by Pure Construction rather than by the family and had insurance-industry ties — is available but is itself an uphill showing of "not disinterested" within the meaning of § 65A.01, because Minnesota has not clearly defined what prior insurer relationships disqualify an appraiser under the statute.
- **The 90-day window for a standalone motion to vacate is already past.** The award issued 9/12/2025; the parallel 90-day window ran out about 12/11/2025. That means the family cannot file a freestanding vacatur motion; A3 must be packaged inside the breach-of-contract suit as an alternative request for relief. Courts may be more skeptical of vacatur requests that appear months after the award in the posture of a secondary count.
- **Foster signed, no umpire report issued, and no misconduct is yet on the record.** To meet *Mork*'s standard, the family needs more than the inferences they can draw today. The strongest misconduct candidates — panel offloaded scope to the umpire; umpire offloaded decisions to the party appraisers; undisclosed conflicts — have to be developed in discovery. At the pleading stage, alleging them in good faith is permissible, but the court will not vacate on those allegations alone.
- ***Noonan v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.***, 929 F.3d 633 (8th Cir. 2019), is adverse authority on one of the factual predicates (Semco-defunct matching) that would otherwise support the phantom-pricing modification/correction theory. That does not directly defeat A3's itemization/remand argument, but it limits how far the "the award doesn't cover the real cost" narrative can push the court.
- ***Quade* confines the panel's authority to "amount of loss," not coverage.** That works for the family on the causation finding (which is binding), but it also cuts against dragging coverage defenses into a § 572B.24-style modification attack. The clearer A3 theories stay inside the appraisal clause — itemization, disinterestedness, substantial compliance — and leave coverage to A1 and A2.

### 2.4 What "45/100" means practically

A3 is a supporting, alternative-pleaded count in the complaint. It is *not* the anchor (A1 is). A3 should be filed for three reasons:

1. To preserve the *Herll*/*Rymer* remand request, which is the single cleanest procedural path to moving the dollar number above the deductible.
2. To preserve the *Mork* vacatur attack in case discovery produces the misconduct facts the current record only hints at.
3. To lock in the § 65A.01, Subd. 3 "stating separately ... to each item" argument, which is a fresh statutory hook that Farmers has not briefed in any correspondence and that does not depend on matching or scientific hail-damage expertise.

A3 is not likely to "win" in the sense of a court wholesale vacating the award. It is likely to partially win in the sense of pulling the award back to the panel for clarification, which is exactly the mechanism *Herll* contemplates and exactly the procedural state the record today supports. The 45/100 reflects that mixed probability profile.

**Takeaway:** These facts support a clean statutory-noncompliance argument under § 65A.01, Subd. 3. The award does not state actual value and loss separately to each item; it is not itemized on its face; and Farmers' own conflicting descriptions confirm there is no single itemization of record.

### 3.2 Ground B — Ambiguity warranting remand under *Herll* and *Rymer*

If you need information as to why additional windows were not included in the award then you will need to contact your appraiser to obtain that information." ### 3.3 Ground C — "Mistake" or failure of substantial compliance with the appraisal clause

- **Only 2 windows were awarded; the family's documented damage spans nearly every window.** The 2020 Xactimate on Farmers' own records approved exactly 2 casement windows. *(EventDocuments/FinalDocument_2020.pdf line items 1–4; EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png)*

The HailShapes research assembled by the family (24 peer-reviewed, government, and professional sources) supports this framing. *(HailShapes/DocumentIndex.md; HailShapes/KeyDecisionDocument.md; HailShapes/RebuttalToFarmersDenial.md)*

- **The Semco pricing question is a further "mistake" predicate, if Semco was in fact used.** The 9/18/2025 Farmers email describes the awarded windows as manufactured by Semco with stamps from 1993, 1997, and 2008; the family's account is that Semco Windows and Doors ceased operations in 2019 (this fact needs independent verification before filing). If the panel's $12,800 RCV rests on phantom Semco pricing rather than available replacement pricing, the valuation is a "mistake of fact" producing an unintended award. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/18/2025 entry); family's contemporaneous account — Semco-closure fact is NOT independently verified in the project folder and should be confirmed before filing)*
- **No itemized umpire methodology exists to show that all windows were evaluated.** Because Farmers confirmed on 9/24/2025 that the umpire produced no report or estimate, there is no record evidence that the panel performed an "amount of loss" determination "to each item" as § 65A.01, Subd. 3 requires. *(EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md (9/24/2025 entry))*

**Takeaway:** These facts support a "mistake" / failure-of-substantial-compliance theory. The argument is not that the panel was negligent but that the panel did not complete the work the statute and the policy required, and that the work it did complete was built on a narrow damage standard and potentially a defunct manufacturer's pricing.

### 3.5 Ground E — Disinterestedness failure as to Foster (§ 65A.01 Subd. 3; *McQuaid*)

- **Foster was picked by Pure Construction, which is the contractor whose work would follow any award** — not by the insured. *(EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf p. 2)*

**Takeaway:** The "disinterested" attack is a fact-dependent showing that Minnesota has not clearly quantified. It should be preserved but not led with. If discovery surfaces an undisclosed relationship between Foster and Farmers, Pure Construction, or another insurer-side party that was not disclosed to the family on 8/3/2025, the strength of this ground would materially increase.

## 4. Interaction with A1 and the Broader Case

A3 is a subsidiary, alternative-pleaded count, not a standalone lawsuit. The realistic filing architecture echoes `appraisal_vacatur_caselaw.md` §4 and `A1.md` §4:

- **Lead with A1 (breach of contract).** The anchor count. Frames the case around coverage for hail-damaged windows the panel did not evaluate (*Quade* coverage track) and matching (*Cedar Bluff* coverage track). Does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting the award.
- **Plead A2 (declaratory judgment) to lock in coverage rulings.** Whether J6944 applies, whether matching is required, and whether Farmers' original exclusion defenses survive the *Quade* causation finding.
- **Plead A3 as alternative relief in three tiers.**
  1. **Tier 1 — Remand to the appraisal panel for clarification under *Herll* and *Rymer*.** Easiest lift, most likely to be granted, most impactful if granted. Does not require proving misconduct.
  2. **Tier 2 — Modification or correction of the award to comply with § 65A.01, Subd. 3's "stating separately ... to each item" requirement.** Use the itemization defect directly. This is effectively the statutory-noncompliance predicate for Tier 1 and can be pled as its own alternative.
  3. **Tier 3 — Vacatur under *Mork*.** Reserve for the facts discovery actually produces. Do not lead with it.

So A3 does not try to win by itself. It preserves the procedural tools necessary to break the $17,720 deductible by either (a) pulling the award back to the panel and letting clarification expand the scope, or (b) setting the defective award aside partially or wholly so the A1 contract claim can proceed on the full record.
