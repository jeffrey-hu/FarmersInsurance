## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 Insurance policies are contracts

A Minnesota insurance policy is treated as any other written contract and is subject to ordinary contract-law remedies when the insurer fails to perform. *See* Minn. Stat. § 60A.08 (insurance policies are contracts); *Bob Useldinger & Sons, Inc. v. Hangsleben*, 505 N.W.2d 323, 327 (Minn. 1993) ("Insurance policies are contracts."). The insurer's promise to pay for covered losses, and the insured's corresponding performance (premium payment, notice, cooperation, proof of loss), are the reciprocal obligations that form the contract.

### 1.2 Elements of a breach-of-contract claim in Minnesota

To prevail on a breach of contract, a plaintiff must show:

1. **Formation** — a valid contract existed.
2. **Performance (or excuse) by the plaintiff** — the insured performed any conditions precedent required by the policy.
3. **Breach by the defendant** — the insurer failed to perform when performance was due.
4. **Damages** — the plaintiff suffered loss caused by the breach.

*Park Nicollet Clinic v. Hamann*, 808 N.W.2d 828, 833 (Minn. 2011); *Lyon Fin. Servs., Inc. v. Ill. Paper & Copier Co.*, 848 N.W.2d 539, 543 (Minn. 2014). Failure of an insurer to pay a covered loss — regardless of motive — is a breach of the insurance contract. *See, e.g., Short v. Dairyland Ins. Co.*, 334 N.W.2d 384, 387–88 (Minn. 1983).

### 1.3 Specific duties under this policy

Reviewing `HomePolicy/2014_newBusiness_policy.pdf` (the full policy form) and `HomePolicy/2023_Renewal.pdf` (the declarations in force on the loss date), Farmers' contractual promises included:

- Coverage A (Dwelling) of **$886,000** for direct physical loss to the residence, including loss by **windstorm and hail**, subject to exclusions and a **$17,720 windstorm/hail deductible**.
- Payment for damaged property on the policy's replacement-cost basis, up to applicable limits, net of any depreciation held back pending actual repair.
- Good-faith performance of the appraisal clause incorporated from Minn. Stat. § 65A.01 when the parties failed to agree on "amount of loss."
- Compliance with the policy's "Duties After Loss" and investigation obligations.

### 1.4 Statute-of-limitations framework

The policy contains a 2-year contractual suit limitation running from the date of loss. Loss date is 7/13/2024, so the filing deadline is **7/13/2026**. The contractual period controls unless a Minnesota statute (e.g., Minn. Stat. § 65A.01's Standard Fire Policy language) supplies a different period; that question is open and should be confirmed before filing, but for planning purposes the 7/13/2026 deadline is binding.

### 1.5 What this claim looks like on the face of a complaint

The A1 count in a complaint would plead, in substance:

> "Defendant Fire Insurance Exchange issued and renewed Policy 98055-49-65 covering the dwelling at 17756 George Moran Dr., Eden Prairie, MN. On July 13, 2024, hail damaged insured windows at the property. Plaintiffs performed all conditions precedent under the policy, including timely notice, cooperation with three inspections, sworn proof of loss, and participation in the contractual appraisal process. Despite an appraisal award confirming hail as the cause of loss, Defendant has failed and refused to pay for the full scope of covered hail damage. Defendant's failure constitutes a breach of the insurance contract. As a direct and proximate result, Plaintiffs have suffered damages in excess of $50,000, including the unpaid replacement cost of the hail-damaged windows and consequential water-intrusion damage."

Damages sought would be the unpaid covered loss, prejudgment interest under Minn. Stat. § 549.09, and costs.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **65 / 100**

That number reflects (a) strength of the argument as the **anchor count** in the initial complaint — i.e., its ability to survive a Rule 12 motion, support discovery, and reach a damages determination — not a pure prediction of dollar recovery.

- **Strength of the base elements**: 85/100. Formation, performance, and damages are well-documented and largely undisputed.
- **Strength of "breach" given the appraisal award**: 50/100. The appraisal award ($12,800 RCV / $0 net after the $17,720 deductible) is the single biggest drag. Whether Farmers "breached" depends on succeeding in one of several sub-battles that do not have uniform strength.
- **Procedural viability as the lead count**: 90/100. Breach of contract is the standard, statutorily appropriate vehicle for first-party property disputes in Minnesota; it is also the predicate count required before bad faith can be added under Minn. Stat. § 604.18, Subd. 4.

Weighted blend, with emphasis on the breach battleground: **~65/100**.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down from the base

The award is presumptively valid under *Mork v. Eureka-Security Fire & Marine Ins. Co.*, 230 Minn. 382, 42 N.W.2d 33 (1950), and the family faces a high burden — "clear evidence" of "fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing," not "mere inadequacy." Courts resist second-guessing panel valuations. *See also* `LegalReferences/appraisal_vacatur_caselaw.md` §1.1–1.2. The 90-day window for a standalone motion to vacate under arbitration-style procedure has already passed (12/11/2025), so any attack has to be embedded in the breach complaint.

The insured's own appraiser (Foster) signed the award; the family did not interview or select him. A court will ask why the family is suing over an amount their own representative agreed to. That must be addressed head-on in the pleading and discovery plan.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up

- **Causation is locked in by *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012).** The panel's finding that hail caused the window damage is binding. Farmers cannot, on the portion the panel evaluated, revive wear/tear, marring, corrosion, inherent vice, neglect, or faulty-construction defenses from the 11/4/2024 denial. Those defenses are foreclosed by the award they obtained.
- **The award is facially ambiguous.** It lists only "F1.7" and "B1.2" against a lump $12,800 — no itemization, no umpire report, no list of windows evaluated versus excluded. *Herll v. Auto-Owners Ins. Co.*, 879 F.3d 293 (8th Cir. 2018) (applying Minnesota law), and *Cincinnati Ins. Co. v. Rymer Cos.*, No. 24-3356 (8th Cir. Mar. 27, 2026), both hold that ambiguous appraisal awards are remanded to the panel for clarification. A remand request is a lower lift than vacatur.
- **Coverage questions remain with the court under *Quade*.** Whether J6944 (matching) applies and whether the policy's "comparable material and quality" obligation requires broader replacement when Semco Windows is defunct are coverage questions the panel did not decide. Those questions live in a breach-of-contract suit regardless of the award.
- **The record supports "performance" (element 2) overwhelmingly.** Timely notice, three inspections, sworn proof of loss, appraisal participation — the family did everything the policy asked. That clean performance record narrows Farmers' defenses to scope and amount.
- **Conduct evidence is bad for Farmers.** Shifting rationales (no opening → wear/tear → below deductible), premature 9/3/2024 denial before any engineer was engaged, missing Brau inspection notes, the "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule, and ignored NWS evidence all color the court's reading of whether performance has actually occurred. This conduct does not convert breach into bad faith on the face of the initial complaint (§ 604.18 must be added by motion), but it supports the narrative that Farmers has not fully paid a covered loss.

### 2.4 What "65/100" means practically

A breach-of-contract count on these facts is clearly filable, clearly survives Rule 12, and is the correct anchor for the lawsuit. It is not a sure winner on damages because the deductible math and the award together cap the easy path. The family's actual dollar recovery is contingent on prevailing on one or more of: (a) Herll/Rymer remand showing unevaluated windows; (b) *Quade* coverage-question relief for windows outside the appraisal's scope; (c) *Cedar Bluff* matching for full window replacement against J6944 (with *Noonan* as adverse 8th-Circuit authority); or (d) limited *Mork* vacatur/modification of a specifically flawed portion of the award. Each of those is a separate subsidiary argument (A2, A3, etc. in the arguments list). Breach of contract is the frame that holds them.

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal?

### 4.1 Short answer

**Partially.** The breach-of-contract claim does not require vacatur as a pleading matter. It can be filed today as a standalone count on the policy, and significant relief is available without disturbing the appraisal award. But whether the family ultimately recovers beyond $0 depends, in several scenarios, on either modifying or clarifying the award. The pleading strategy and the recovery strategy are different questions.

### 4.2 The claim rests on three tracks, which have different dependency profiles

**Track 1 — Unevaluated-windows coverage (STANDALONE).**
For every hail-damaged window that the appraisal panel did not evaluate, Farmers' failure to pay is a breach of the policy that the court can adjudicate without touching the award. This rests on *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012) (appraisers decide "amount of loss"; coverage questions stay with the court) and the *Herll*/*Rymer* doctrine that ambiguous awards are remanded to the panel for clarification. The award's codes "F1.7" and "B1.2" are facially narrow; the umpire produced no report; Tomes cannot describe the scope consistently. On this track the court is not being asked to "vacate" — it is being asked to enforce the policy for windows the panel never resolved, and alternatively to remand for clarification of scope. This is the strongest and cleanest path.

**Track 2 — Matching as a coverage question (STANDALONE on the law; J6944-limited on the facts).**
Whether the policy's "comparable material and quality" obligation requires broader window replacement because Semco is defunct is a coverage question under *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 857 N.W.2d 290 (Minn. 2014). Coverage questions stay with the court under *Quade*. The appraisal panel did not decide this question, and its silence does not bar the court from deciding it. J6944 (Limited Matching of Undamaged Property) is the core defensive hurdle — *Noonan v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 929 F.3d 633 (8th Cir. 2019) enforced a similar endorsement — but nothing about either case requires vacating the existing award. The court can leave the $12,800 finding undisturbed for the two awarded windows and still grant (or deny) matching relief.

**Track 3 — Modifying or correcting the award for windows the panel did evaluate (DEPENDENT).**
If the family wants more money for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows themselves — for example, by arguing the $12,800 RCV reflects phantom Semco pricing — that is a direct attack on the award's valuation and requires either (a) a request to modify/correct the award under the common-law *Mork* standard (fraud, misfeasance, malfeasance, or wrongdoing, proved by clear evidence), or (b) a *Herll*/*Rymer* remand to the panel for clarification of methodology, which can lead to a corrected amount. This track is dependent on appraisal relief in a meaningful way.

### 4.3 Putting the three tracks together in one pleading

The realistic filing plan, echoing `appraisal_vacatur_caselaw.md` §4:

1. **Plead breach of contract (A1) as the lead count**, framed around Tracks 1 and 2 — coverage for unevaluated windows and matching as a coverage question. This count does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting the award.
2. **Plead declaratory judgment (A2)** asking the court to declare the scope of coverage, the non-applicability or limits of J6944, and the bindingness of *Quade* causation against Farmers' original exclusion defenses. Again, no vacatur required.
3. **Plead an alternative request for remand to the appraisal panel for clarification of scope** under *Herll* and *Rymer*. This is a clarification, not a vacatur, and it strengthens Track 1.
4. **Plead in the alternative (A3) for modification, correction, or vacatur under Mork** only for the portions of the award the family wants changed — specifically, the $12,800 valuation to the extent it rests on Semco pricing or an artificially narrow damage definition. This is the only sub-claim that is formally dependent on disturbing the award.

So the **A1 count itself is not dependent on vacating, modifying, or correcting the appraisal**. Adjacent alternative counts (A3) are, but A1 stands on its own and should be the pleading anchor.

### 4.4 Where the dependency actually bites

The practical — not pleading — dependency is this: **to recover more than $0, damages must exceed $17,720 after whatever adjustments the court orders.** The appraisal found $12,800 RCV for two windows. The family needs additional compensable loss of $4,920 or more (at minimum) to break the deductible.

- Under Track 1, additional damages come from windows the panel never evaluated. The court can order coverage for those on the policy without touching the award. That route does not require vacating, modifying, or correcting anything.
- Under Track 2, additional damages come from matching relief. Same answer: the award is untouched; the court independently interprets the policy on matching.
- Under Track 3, additional damages come from a higher RCV for the F1.7 and B1.2 windows. That route does require modification or correction of the amount.

The family should not structure the case around Track 3. The cleanest way to break the deductible is adding unevaluated windows (Track 1) and/or matching relief (Track 2), neither of which requires vacatur. Track 3 is a fallback if discovery produces concrete evidence that the panel used Semco phantom pricing or excluded scientifically-legitimate damage patterns — the kinds of facts that can satisfy *Mork* — and should be preserved in the alternative.

### 4.5 The appraisal bar to § 604.18 is a separate question

Bad faith under Minn. Stat. § 604.18 is not A1 and is not pleaded in the initial complaint. The appraisal bar in § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) applies to claims "resolved or confirmed by arbitration or appraisal." Whether that bar applies on these facts turns on whether the court reads the 9/12/2025 award as "resolving" the claim. That is a § 604.18 issue, not a breach-of-contract issue, and does not affect A1's standalone viability.

### 4.6 Bottom line on the key question

- The breach-of-contract claim **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to be filed, to survive Rule 12, or to reach the merits on a significant portion of the damages.
- For the subset of damages tied to the two specific windows the panel did evaluate, recovery is dependent on award modification or correction — but that is a subsidiary sub-claim (A3), not A1.
- The recommended lead count is therefore A1 framed around (i) unevaluated windows as a *Quade* coverage question, (ii) matching as a *Cedar Bluff* coverage question, with (iii) alternative *Herll*/*Rymer* remand and (iv) alternative A3 modification/vacatur preserved in the pleading.
