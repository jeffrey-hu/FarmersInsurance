## 1. Legal Basis

### 1.1 The statute

Minn. Stat. § 72A.20 is titled "Methods, Acts, and Practices Which Are Defined as Unfair or Deceptive." It is part of Chapter 72A, Minnesota's Insurance Industry Trade Practices chapter, and supplies the substantive definitions of "unfair methods of competition" and "unfair or deceptive acts or practices" that the Commissioner of Commerce is empowered to enforce under §§ 72A.17–72A.32.

The section runs across many subdivisions targeting different categories of insurer conduct (advertising, agent licensing, discrimination, rebates, twisting, etc.). The subdivision that maps directly onto first-party claim handling is **Subdivision 12 — "Unfair Settlement Practices."** Subdivision 12 prohibits an insurer from "[c]ommitting or performing with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice any" of an enumerated list of practices. The list includes (paraphrasing the most salient items for this case):

- (1) misrepresenting pertinent facts or insurance policy provisions relating to coverages at issue;
- (2) failing to acknowledge and act reasonably promptly upon communications with respect to claims;
- (3) failing to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation of claims;
- (4) refusing to pay claims without conducting a reasonable investigation based upon all available information;
- (5) failing to affirm or deny coverage of claims within a reasonable time after proof of loss is submitted;
- (6) not attempting in good faith to effectuate prompt, fair, and equitable settlements of claims in which liability has become reasonably clear;
- (7) compelling insureds to institute litigation to recover amounts due under an insurance policy by offering substantially less than the amounts ultimately recovered in actions brought by such insureds;
- (8) attempting to settle a claim for less than the amount to which a reasonable person would believe the insured was entitled by reference to written or printed advertising material accompanying or made part of an application;
- (9) attempting to settle claims on the basis of an application that was altered without notice to or knowledge or consent of the insured;
- (10) making claims payments without an accompanying statement setting forth the coverage under which payments are being made;
- (11) making known to insureds or claimants a policy of appealing from arbitration awards in favor of insureds for the purpose of compelling them to accept settlements or compromises less than the amount awarded in arbitration;
- (12) delaying the investigation or payment of claims by requiring an insured, claimant, or the physician of either, to submit a preliminary claim report and then requiring the subsequent submission of formal proof of loss forms, both of which submissions contain substantially the same information;
- (13) failing to promptly settle claims, where liability has become reasonably clear, under one portion of the insurance policy coverage in order to influence settlements under other portions of the insurance policy coverage; and
- (14) failing to promptly provide a reasonable explanation of the basis in the insurance policy in relation to the facts or applicable law for denial of a claim or for the offer of a compromise settlement.

The exact paragraph numbering and phrasing should be confirmed against the current published version of the statute before filing — the section has been amended several times and the Revisor's published text controls. The substance of Subdivision 12 has been stable for decades, and the practices most directly relevant on this record (paragraphs (1), (3), (4), (5), (7), (13), and (14) above) appear in every version of the statute reviewed for this memo.

### 1.2 § 72A.20 vs. § 72A.201 — adjacent but distinct provisions

Minnesota separates "trade practices that are unfair or deceptive" (§ 72A.20) from "regulation of claims practices" (§ 72A.201). The two sections cover overlapping ground. The distinction matters in the following way for argument C2 versus argument C1:

- **§ 72A.20 (C2)** defines categories of "unfair or deceptive" trade practices, generally requiring that the conduct be performed "with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice." It is the substantive statute the Commissioner of Commerce uses to take enforcement action under §§ 72A.21–72A.27.
- **§ 72A.201 (C1)** is a more granular claim-handling rulebook — specifying timeframes (e.g., 30 business days for an investigation), required disclosures (e.g., the right to file a complaint with the Department of Commerce), and discrete prohibitions on individual claim conduct (e.g., not denying without referring to a specific policy provision). It is regulation of single-claim conduct, not trade-practice characterization.

Because § 72A.20 carries the "general business practice" gloss, it is the harder section to anchor on a single insured's claim experience. A C2 argument has to rely on conduct that either (a) is itself a discrete violation of one of the enumerated practices in Subd. 12, or (b) is supported by inference that the conduct toward this claim reflects a broader institutional pattern. The available record for the Lu/Hu claim supports the first framing for several paragraphs of Subd. 12 and supplies inferential, but not direct, support for the second.

### 1.3 The Morris bar — no private right of action under Chapter 72A

The single most important legal fact about C2 is *Morris v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 386 N.W.2d 233 (Minn. 1986). In *Morris*, an insured tried to amend her complaint to add a count alleging violations of Chapter 72A's Unfair Claims Practices Act (the chapter that contains § 72A.20). The Minnesota Supreme Court affirmed the denial of leave to amend and held that **Chapter 72A does not create a private cause of action**. The Court reasoned that:

1. The savings clause in § 72A.29 Subd. 1 ("nothing in this chapter shall be construed as creating or implying a private cause of action") does not itself create a new cause of action and there is no language elsewhere in the chapter giving a party the right to sue.
2. The chapter is structured for administrative enforcement by the Commissioner of Commerce, including investigation, charges, hearings, cease-and-desist orders, civil penalties, and license suspension/revocation.
3. The statutory architecture is "ill designed" for private enforcement because most of the enumerated practices (including the Subd. 12 unfair-settlement-practices list) require proof of a "general business practice," which a private plaintiff has no realistic mechanism to develop in single-claim discovery.
4. The "great majority of state versions" of the underlying NAIC Model Unfair Trade Practices Act have likewise been read by other jurisdictions as not creating private rights of action.

The result is that an insured cannot, in 2026, simply plead a count under § 72A.20 in a Minnesota state-court complaint and seek damages. *Morris* forecloses that path. Subsequent Minnesota authority has reaffirmed the no-private-action rule; no Minnesota appellate decision identified in research for this memo has carved out a private right of action under § 72A.20 for first-party insureds.

### 1.4 The § 8.31 Subd. 3a Private Attorney General route — structurally available, practically narrow

Minn. Stat. § 8.31 Subd. 3a (the "Private AG" statute) gives "any person injured by a violation of any of the laws referred to in subdivision 1" a private right of action with attorney-fee recovery. The laws "referred to in subdivision 1" are the consumer-protection statutes the Attorney General is charged with enforcing.

The threshold question is whether Chapter 72A counts among those "laws referred to in subdivision 1." That question is open and unfavorable on the available authority:

- Until 1983, § 8.31 contained a Subd. 4 that *expressly excluded* persons engaged in the insurance business from § 8.31's coverage. The 1983 repeal of Subd. 4 was intended to let the Attorney General investigate and take action on insurer unfair claims practices — not to open a private cause of action. Legislative history cited in *Morris* makes that expressly clear.
- *Morris* itself acknowledged that "it is unclear whether Chapter 72A was ever contemplated by the legislature as subject to the private civil action provision of section 8.31, subd. 3a." The Court did not resolve the question because it disposed of the case on the simpler ground that Chapter 72A on its own does not create a private action.
- More recent Minnesota Court of Appeals decisions on the scope of the Private AG statute have been narrow about *which* statutes Subd. 3a actually reaches; an insurance-industry trade-practices argument under Subd. 3a is not a settled or comfortable position.

In addition, the Private AG route requires a showing that the action benefits "the public," not just the named plaintiff. *Ly v. Nystrom*, 615 N.W.2d 302 (Minn. 2000), and its progeny require a "public benefit" element that single-claim insurance disputes typically struggle to establish without class-style allegations.

The bottom line: a § 72A.20 / § 8.31 Subd. 3a private action is theoretically pleadable but is unsupported by Minnesota appellate authority, requires multiple unsettled doctrinal moves, and carries a meaningful risk of dismissal at the Rule 12 stage with a fee-shifting award against the family. It is not a recommended posture.

### 1.5 What § 72A.20 still does for the case

Even with no private right of action, § 72A.20 retains real value in the litigation in three roles:

1. **Standard-of-conduct benchmark for § 604.18 (B1).** Minnesota courts evaluate the § 604.18 "objective absence of a reasonable basis to deny" prong against the regulatory baseline § 72A.20 establishes for insurer claim handling. Each documented Subd. 12 violation feeds Prong 1 of B1 as evidence that no reasonable insurer would have handled the claim this way.
2. **Predicate for the Department of Commerce complaint (already filed).** The family's 11/19/2024 complaint to the Minnesota Department of Commerce (Complaint ID 93547) was, in substance, a § 72A.20 / § 72A.201 complaint. Although Commerce ultimately could not compel reversal, the complaint preserves the regulatory record and supports the family's narrative of administrative exhaustion.
3. **Evidentiary backdrop for the breach-of-contract narrative (A1) and the estoppel/waiver narrative (B4).** Subdivision 12's prohibitions on misrepresenting policy provisions (paragraph (1)) and on failing to provide reasonable explanations for denials (paragraph (14)) directly characterize the kinds of conduct documented on this record. Even where § 72A.20 cannot itself supply a remedy, courts and juries reading the conduct evidence in a breach case naturally use the regulatory rules as a yardstick.

### 1.6 What this argument does NOT look like in the complaint

Given *Morris*, the C2 argument should **not** appear as a freestanding "Count — Violation of Minn. Stat. § 72A.20" in the initial complaint. The realistic uses are:

- A reference to § 72A.20 / § 72A.201 in the factual recitals of the complaint, framing the conduct as departing from Minnesota's regulatory baseline (without claiming § 72A.20 itself supplies the cause of action).
- An exhibit-and-citation backbone for the affidavits supporting the eventual § 604.18 motion under Subd. 4(a), where the regulatory rule is *the* benchmark for "reasonable basis."
- A standard-of-conduct citation in summary-judgment briefing or jury instruction discussions for A1 (breach) or any equitable theories that survive Rule 12.

If a § 72A.20 count is nonetheless pleaded — for example, as a Private AG count under § 8.31 Subd. 3a — it should be in the alternative, narrowly framed, and accompanied by a candid acknowledgment in the brief that *Morris* is the controlling authority. Filing it without that framing risks a Rule 11 / § 549.211 fee-shifting response from Farmers.

## 2. Overall Strength Assessment for an Initial Claim

### 2.1 Rating: **10 / 100** as a standalone count; **45 / 100** as a § 604.18 standard-of-conduct input; **25 / 100** as a Private AG count under § 8.31 Subd. 3a

The split rating reflects that C2's value is overwhelmingly indirect. As a freestanding cause of action, *Morris* effectively kills it. As a regulatory-standard input into other counts, the underlying factual record is genuinely strong.

- **C2 as a standalone count under § 72A.20: ~10/100.** *Morris* forecloses a private cause of action. The expected Rule 12 outcome is dismissal. The marginal evidentiary value is zero beyond what the same facts already deliver inside A1, B1, and B4.
- **C2 as standard-of-conduct input into B1 (§ 604.18 bad faith): ~45/100.** The Subd. 12 enumerated practices map cleanly onto multiple documented Farmers acts; this is real fuel for the bad-faith Prong 1 showing once B1 is added by motion. Limited to ~45/100 because § 604.18 is itself procedurally constrained (B1's overall rating is ~40/100 at filing, ~60/100 conditional on the A1/A3 scope attack), and C2 only narrows that range marginally.
- **C2 as a Private AG count under § 8.31 Subd. 3a: ~25/100.** Theoretically pleadable; not supported by Minnesota appellate authority; requires multiple unsettled doctrinal moves (Chapter 72A within § 8.31 Subd. 1; "public benefit" element under *Ly v. Nystrom*); fee-shifting risk on dismissal. Modest upside if the court is willing to entertain it; meaningful downside if it isn't.

Weighted blend for the question the user asked — "strength as an initial legal claim to be drafted in MN state court" — interpreted strictly as "C2 as a standalone count": **~10/100.**

If the question is interpreted to allow C2 to function as a regulatory benchmark woven into A1 and B1, the practical weight rises to **~30–45/100**, but at that point C2 is not really a separate "claim" — it is a standard-of-conduct citation supporting other counts.

### 2.2 What drives the rating down

**Morris is on point and dispositive.** *Morris v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 386 N.W.2d 233 (Minn. 1986), squarely holds that Chapter 72A does not provide a private cause of action. Subsequent Minnesota authority has not overruled *Morris*; the Legislature has not statutorily created the private action *Morris* read out of the chapter. Until either of those things changes, a § 72A.20 count is a Rule 12 dismissal waiting to happen.

**The "general business practice" requirement is structurally hostile to single-claim plaintiffs.** Subdivision 12's gateway language — "with such frequency as to indicate a general business practice" — was the *Morris* Court's central reason for thinking the chapter was "ill designed" for private actions. A single insured cannot, in single-claim discovery, develop the multi-claim record needed to prove a general business practice. On the Lu/Hu record, the family can document Farmers' conduct as to *its own* claim with primary-source citations, but it has no current record of Farmers' handling of other Minnesota homeowners' hail claims (other than the broader Burgess and "Hail Focus Initiative" backdrop catalogued in `LegalReferences/CaseLawReference.md`, which involves a different insurer and is admissible only as a pattern argument, not as proof of *Farmers'* practices).

**Section 8.31 Subd. 3a's threshold question is unfavorable.** The repealed Subd. 4 of § 8.31 expressly excluded insurance from the chapter's reach. The repeal was for AG action, not private action. *Morris* expressly noted the open question. No subsequent Minnesota appellate decision has held that Chapter 72A is among "the laws referred to in subdivision 1" of § 8.31. A Private AG framing therefore requires the trial court to make new law in the family's favor on a question that has been pending for forty years.

**The "public benefit" requirement under *Ly v. Nystrom* and progeny.** Even if the threshold "laws referred to in subdivision 1" question were decided favorably, the Private AG remedy requires a showing that the action benefits the public, not just the named plaintiff. A single homeowner's hail-claim dispute typically fails that test absent class allegations or a documented pattern of insurer conduct affecting a defined community. The Lu/Hu record could support a "public benefit" argument framed around the City of Eden Prairie permit data and the 14-of-19 neighbor canvass — but that argument is novel and untested.

**The remedy under § 72A.20 is administrative.** Even where the Commissioner of Commerce finds violations, the statutory remedy is institutional: cease-and-desist orders, civil penalties up to $10,000 per offense (per § 72A.25), license suspension, and similar regulatory tools. None of those remedies puts dollars in the family's pocket. The family's filed Commerce complaint (11/19/2024) followed exactly that path and produced no compelled reversal.

**Substantive overlap with § 604.18 (B1) limits marginal value.** Every Subd. 12 violation that supports C2 also supports the B1 § 604.18 motion's Prong 1 showing. C2 adds no new evidentiary material that B1 doesn't already use. Pleading C2 separately would be cumulative.

### 2.3 What drives the rating up (in the standard-of-conduct role)

**The factual fit with Subd. 12 enumerated practices is unusually clean.** The Lu/Hu record documents discrete acts that map onto multiple Subd. 12 paragraphs with primary-source citations:

- **Paragraph (1) — Misrepresenting pertinent facts or insurance policy provisions.** The "engineer's report only" gatekeeping rule articulated by supervisor Bradlee Monson on 2/3/2025 and by adjuster Nic Brau in his 2/10/2025 voicemail represents Farmers to the family that the policy required engineer-sourced evidence — a misrepresentation of the policy's actual terms. *(Source: `EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG`; family's contemporaneous account of the 2/3/2025 call.)*
- **Paragraph (3) — Failing to adopt and implement reasonable standards for the prompt investigation of claims.** The 9/3/2024 denial issued five days after the first inspection and *before any engineer was engaged* is itself a failure of reasonable investigation standards. *(Source: `EventDocuments/FarmersDenyLetter_09_03_2024.pdf` pp. 1–2.)*
- **Paragraph (4) — Refusing to pay claims without conducting a reasonable investigation based upon all available information.** Farmers' refusal on 2/10/2025 (Brau voicemail) and on 2/3/2025 (Monson) to consider the family's neighbor canvass, City of Eden Prairie permit data, and stormersite weather records, combined with the silence after the 5/28/2025 NWS / Interactive Hail Maps submission, is the textbook example of refusing to pay without reasonable investigation. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/2025-02-10-TheyDon_tLookAtAnyNewFactsEvidence1.PNG`; `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md`.)*
- **Paragraph (5) — Failing to affirm or deny coverage of claims within a reasonable time after proof of loss is submitted.** The Sworn Proof of Loss filed 8/3/2025 was followed by the appraisal award on 9/12/2025 confirming hail causation; Farmers continued to position the claim as "settled at $0 below deductible" without affirming or denying coverage on the unevaluated windows. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/AppraisalDemand.pdf`; `EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Front.png`; `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (10/21/2025 entry).)*
- **Paragraph (7) — Compelling insureds to institute litigation to recover amounts due.** The result here is a binding $0 settlement that the family must now sue to disturb in order to obtain anything. The deductible-vs-RCV arithmetic and Farmers' refusal to revisit scope after the appraisal effectively force litigation. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/Appraisal Documents/Appraisal_Result_Back.png`; `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (10/21/2025 entries).)*
- **Paragraph (14) — Failing to promptly provide a reasonable explanation of the basis in the insurance policy in relation to the facts or applicable law for denial of a claim or for the offer of a compromise settlement.** Farmers' inability to describe the appraisal scope consistently across 9/18, 9/19, and 10/14/2025; the 9/24/2025 admission that the umpire produced no report or estimate; and the 10/21/2025 referral of the family back to their own appraiser for any explanation are all failures to provide a reasonable, fact-and-policy-grounded explanation of the outcome. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (9/18, 9/19, 9/24, 10/14, 10/21/2025 entries).)*

That is six different paragraphs of Subd. 12 with primary-source documentary support — a rare density of statutory hooks for a single first-party claim.

**The family already exhausted the Commerce route.** The 11/19/2024 complaint to the MN Department of Commerce (Complaint ID 93547) preserves the regulatory record and supports an "administrative exhaustion" framing. While Commerce could not order Farmers to reverse course, the existence of the complaint, the agency's internal escalation to the director level, and Commerce's eventual recommendation that the family pursue legal action all reinforce the family's narrative of regulatory engagement before litigation. *(Sources: `EventDocuments/11-19-complaint-to-dept-of-commerce.pdf`; `EventDocuments/Copy of ComunicationLogs.docx.md` (2/13/2025 and post-2/13 entries).)*

**The "general business practice" requirement is not fatal in the standard-of-conduct role.** Where C2 is used as a regulatory benchmark for B1 Prong 1 rather than as a standalone count, the "general business practice" gloss does not need to be proven. The court is asked only to consult § 72A.20 as the regulatory baseline for what reasonable insurer conduct looks like, not to find a violation of § 72A.20 itself. That is a meaningful distinction in litigation practice.

**Cross-reference Burgess and the State Farm "Hail Focus Initiative" pattern.** `LegalReferences/CaseLawReference.md` already catalogues litigation showing that systematic underpayment of hail claims is a documented industry pattern. While those cases involve other insurers, they support the inferential argument that a Subd. 12 "general business practice" theory is not far-fetched for hail claims in particular. This is contextual rather than dispositive but useful for a § 8.31 Subd. 3a "public benefit" framing if that route is attempted.

### 2.4 What "10/100" and "45/100" mean practically

- **Do not plead C2 as a dedicated freestanding count labeled "Count — Violation of Minn. Stat. § 72A.20."** The *Morris* Rule 12 risk is essentially certain.
- **Do cite § 72A.20 — particularly Subd. 12 — in the factual recitals of the breach-of-contract complaint** as the regulatory standard that Farmers' conduct departed from. Make clear in the brief that the citation is to a regulatory standard of conduct, not to a private cause of action.
- **Do build the C2 factual narrative into the affidavits supporting the eventual § 604.18 motion (B1).** Subd. 12 is the regulatory yardstick that gives Prong 1 of § 604.18 its content. Each Subd. 12 paragraph the record supports is a separate documented departure from the regulatory baseline.
- **Do not plead a § 8.31 Subd. 3a Private AG count without first obtaining current Westlaw/Lexis confirmation that no Minnesota appellate decision has resolved the threshold "Chapter 72A within Subd. 1" question favorably.** If pleaded, plead in the alternative, accompanied by a candid *Morris* acknowledgment, and prepare for a Rule 12 motion and possible fee-shifting exposure.
- **Do not let C2 substitute for or duplicate B1.** The two arguments draw on the same factual record; pleading both as separate counts adds no value and complicates the procedural posture (because B1 is added by motion under § 604.18 Subd. 4(a) and C2 cannot be).

## 4. Does This Claim Depend on Vacating, Modifying, or Correcting the Appraisal Award?

### 4.1 Short answer

**No, in its only viable role.** As a regulatory standard-of-conduct input into B1 (§ 604.18) and as a citation supporting A1's breach narrative, C2 stands on Farmers' pre-appraisal and inter-appraisal conduct, which is independent of the 9/12/2025 award. The Subd. 12 violations documented on this record predate the appraisal in their critical respects (premature denial, misrepresenting policy terms, refusing to consider new evidence, missing investigation records).

**For the post-appraisal Subd. 12 paragraphs (12(7) compelling-litigation and 12(14) explanation-of-basis), the analysis runs adjacent to the appraisal but does not require disturbing it.** The 12(7) "compelled to litigate" framing is reinforced by the appraisal outcome but does not require vacating the award. The 12(14) explanation-of-basis framing is actually *stronger* if the award stands undisturbed — because Farmers cannot describe its own binding award consistently and the umpire produced no report. Neither paragraph requires the court to set aside the award.

The only way C2's analysis becomes appraisal-dependent is if the Private AG route under § 8.31 Subd. 3a is attempted *and* the Subd. 12(7) "ultimately recovered in actions brought by such insureds" element becomes a live issue at trial. That element measures the gap between the insurer's offer and the insured's actual recovery, which only resolves when the larger lawsuit resolves. But that is not appraisal-dependence in the same sense as A1 Track 3 (modifying the $12,800 RCV) or B1's Subd. 4(b) appraisal bar — it is just trial sequencing.

### 4.2 The C2 / appraisal interaction by piece

**Pre-appraisal Subd. 12 conduct — independent of the award.** Subd. 12(1) misrepresentation (the "engineer's report only" rule articulated 2/3 and 2/10/2025), Subd. 12(3) reasonable investigation standards (the 9/3/2024 premature denial; the missing Brau 9/6/2024 notes; the missing Hancock workproduct), and Subd. 12(4) refusal to investigate based on all available information (the 1/31, 2/10, and 5/28/2025 ignored submissions) all happened *before* the 9/12/2025 award. The award does not affect them.

**Inter-appraisal Subd. 12(5) — independent of the award.** The Sworn Proof of Loss was submitted 8/3/2025 with the appraisal demand. Farmers' continued refusal to affirm or deny coverage on the unevaluated windows is independent of the panel's award on F1.7 and B1.2 — the panel did not evaluate the unevaluated windows.

**Post-appraisal Subd. 12(7) — adjacent to but not dependent on the award.** The "compelling litigation" framing is informed by the $0 net outcome but is not contingent on the award being changed. The family is being compelled to litigate either way: to recover anything for the unevaluated windows, the family must sue under A1; to recover more than $6,400 ACV for the F1.7 / B1.2 windows, the family must sue under A3. The Subd. 12(7) "compelling" element is satisfied by Farmers' refusal to engage post-appraisal, not by the award's correctness.

**Post-appraisal Subd. 12(14) — strengthened by the award standing.** The explanation-of-basis paragraph is at its sharpest where Farmers cannot consistently describe its own binding award (9/18, 9/19, 10/14/2025 inconsistencies), where the umpire produced no supporting report (Farmers' 9/24/2025 admission), and where Farmers refers the family to their own appraiser for any explanation (10/21/2025 reply). All of those facts depend on the award existing — but none depends on the award being disturbed.

### 4.3 Where any C2 / appraisal dependency lies

If a § 8.31 Subd. 3a Private AG count under § 72A.20 Subd. 12(7) is attempted (not recommended), the "amounts ultimately recovered in actions brought by such insureds" element matures only as the underlying lawsuit progresses. That is sequencing, not appraisal dependence. The same trial that would resolve A1 / A3 would also resolve the Subd. 12(7) gap calculation, regardless of whether the appraisal award is left intact, modified, or vacated.

If C2 is invoked instead as a regulatory benchmark for B1 Prong 1, the appraisal-bar question in § 604.18 Subd. 4(b) becomes load-bearing — but that is B1's problem, not C2's. C2's content does not change based on whether the Subd. 4(b) bar applies; only B1's recovery does.

### 4.4 Bottom line on the key question

- C2 **does not require** vacating, modifying, or correcting the 9/12/2025 appraisal award to function in its only viable role (regulatory standard-of-conduct input into B1; factual-recital support in A1).
- C2 **is not weakened** by leaving the award in place. Several of the strongest Subd. 12 paragraphs on this record (5, 7, 14) are about post-award conduct and are sharper while the award stands.
- C2 **is foreclosed as a standalone private cause of action** by *Morris v. American Family Mut. Ins. Co.*, 386 N.W.2d 233 (Minn. 1986). That foreclosure has nothing to do with the appraisal — it is the doctrinal background condition for any § 72A.20 argument in Minnesota.
- C2 **does not depend on the appraisal** for its only realistic uses: feeding § 604.18, supporting the breach-of-contract narrative, and reinforcing the administrative-exhaustion record from the 11/19/2024 Commerce complaint.
