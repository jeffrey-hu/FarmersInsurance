## A. Affirmative Causes of Action (Contract-Based)

### A1. Breach of Contract
Insurance policy is a contract. Elements: (1) contract in force, (2) conditions precedent satisfied by insured, (3) breach by insurer. Hook: appraisal confirmed hail causation; Farmers paid $0 on a $310,000 sworn proof of loss, and the family contends the award scope omitted the bulk of damaged windows.

### A2. Declaratory Judgment — Minn. Stat. Ch. 555
Available for any unresolved coverage dispute. Candidate questions: (a) scope of appraisal award relative to claimed damage; (b) whether J6944 matching endorsement applies; (c) validity of each exclusion cited in the 9/3/2024 and 11/4/2024 denials; (d) whether the award is binding on Farmers where its own appraiser refused to sign.

### A3. Action to Vacate, Modify, or Correct the Appraisal Award
Minnesota treats appraisal awards like arbitration awards (analogize to Minn. Stat. § 572B.23 and common-law grounds: partiality, fraud, misconduct, evident miscalculation, appraisers exceeding powers). Factual hooks on this record: (i) insured's appraiser Foster selected by contractor Pure Construction with prior insurance-industry background — arguable failure of § 65A.01 Subd. 3 "disinterested" requirement; (ii) award lists only "F1.7" and "B1.2" against a lump sum, arguably violating § 65A.01 Subd. 3's requirement to state "separately ... loss to each item"; (iii) no umpire report or estimate produced; (iv) Farmers' appraiser Tomes did not sign. Research `appraisal_vacatur_caselaw.md` in this folder as a starting point.

### A4. Unjust Enrichment / Restitution (Alternative Pleading)
Standard alternative to contract when the contract theory might fail. Likely not necessary if the policy is indisputably in force, but commonly pled in the alternative.

## B. Tort / Statutory Bad Faith Claims

### B1. Bad Faith Denial — Minn. Stat. § 604.18
First-party bad faith statute. Two-prong test: (1) objective absence of a reasonable basis to deny; (2) insurer's knowledge of or reckless disregard for that absence. Remedies include up to one-half of proceeds in excess of any offer (capped at $250,000), attorney fees, and interest. Procedural note: § 604.18 claims cannot be included in the initial complaint — must be added by motion under § 604.18 Subd. 4 supported by affidavits. Hooks: first denial before any engineer engaged; Prieve's "no hail after 2020" assertion contradicted by NWS/permits/neighbor canvass and by Farmers' own appraisal; shifting rationales across 9/3, 11/4, and post-appraisal positions; refusal to review new evidence. Key precedent: *Selective Ins. Co. of S.C. v. Sela*, No. 20-2029 (8th Cir. 2021).

### B2. Common-Law Fraud and/or Negligent Misrepresentation
Minnesota recognizes both as separate torts with separate damages. Hook: the Prieve report's affirmative statement that "most recent hail in Eden Prairie reported in 2020" is objectively disprovable and was adopted by Farmers in the 11/4/2024 denial letter — potentially a false representation of material fact on which the family was expected to rely.

### B3. Negligent Claims Handling (Independent Tort)
Minnesota caselaw generally rejects this as a standalone tort separate from § 604.18 (see *Short v. Dairyland Ins. Co.*, 334 N.W.2d 384 (Minn. 1983)). Worth listing so a future session can confirm the current state of the law before ruling it out.

### B4. Estoppel / Waiver of Denial Rationales
The 9/3/2024 denial affirmatively found "no other damages observed" and cited "no opening." Farmers' later theories (wear/tear, 2020-already-paid, under-deductible) rest on incompatible factual premises. Doctrine of estoppel/waiver can bar an insurer from asserting new grounds inconsistent with earlier denial positions. Research frame: Minnesota waiver/estoppel doctrine as applied to successive insurer denial rationales.

## C. Statutory Claims Practices Predicates

### C1. Unreasonable Investigation — Minn. Stat. § 72A.201
Requires 30-business-day investigation, reasonable procedures, and fair weighing of all evidence. Whether § 72A.201 supports a direct private cause of action is contested; it more reliably functions as a predicate for § 604.18 and as a standard-of-conduct benchmark. Hooks: 5-day phone denial before engineering review; refusal to consider neighbor canvass, City permit data, and NWS records; missing Brau inspection documentation.

### C2. Unfair Methods / Deceptive Acts — Minn. Stat. § 72A.20
Enumerated unfair practices include misrepresenting policy provisions, failing to acknowledge claims, failing to adopt reasonable investigation standards, and compelling insureds to litigate. Historically enforced by the Commissioner rather than by private action; a future research session should confirm whether any private right of action is available (directly or via § 8.31) on these facts.

### C3. Minnesota Consumer Fraud Act — Minn. Stat. § 325F.69, enforceable via § 8.31 Subd. 3a (Private Attorney General)
Prohibits "fraud, false pretense, false promise, misrepresentation, misleading statement, or deceptive practice" in connection with the sale of merchandise. Whether insurance qualifies as "merchandise" under § 325F.69 is contested — research is needed. § 8.31 Subd. 3a supplies a private right of action and fee-shifting if the underlying violation is actionable.

### C4. Claims-Practices Regulations under Minn. Stat. § 65A.26 and Related Chapters
General fair-claims-handling obligations for property insurers in Minnesota. Useful as a standard-of-conduct reference; private enforceability should be confirmed.

## D. Remedies and Evidentiary Doctrines

### D1. Punitive Damages — Minn. Stat. § 549.20
Separate from § 604.18 taxable-costs remedy. Requires clear-and-convincing evidence of "deliberate disregard for the rights or safety of others." Must be added by post-pleading motion under Minn. Stat. § 549.191.

### D2. Prejudgment Interest — Minn. Stat. § 549.09
Freestanding statutory entitlement on contract recovery, independent of any § 604.18 award.

### D3. Spoliation / Adverse Inference
Not an independent tort in Minnesota, but supports sanctions and adverse-inference jury instructions where a party knew or should have known evidence should be preserved. Hooks: Brau's 9/6/2024 inspection (personal observation of dents, measurements taken) with no surviving written record per Farmers' 10/10/2025 statement; Hancock inspection workproduct not produced beyond the photo set.

### D4. Discovery Sanctions — Minn. R. Civ. P. 37 (or Fed. R. Civ. P. 37 if removed)
Distinct from spoliation. If Farmers fails to produce inspection notes, claim file portions, or other responsive documents during litigation, Rule 37 remedies include issue preclusion, fee-shifting, and default — independent of the pre-litigation preservation theory.

## E. Doctrines of Construction and Defensive Sub-Issues

### E1. Appraisal Causation Is Binding — *Quade v. Secura Ins.*, 814 N.W.2d 703 (Minn. 2012)
"Amount of loss" under the appraisal clause includes causation. Used affirmatively: the 9/12/2025 award's hail-causation finding is binding on Farmers and defeats the wear/tear, marring, inherent vice, neglect, and faulty construction exclusions cited in the 11/4/2024 denial.

### E2. Matching Endorsement J6944 — Scope Limits
Defensive sub-issue. Key precedent: *Cedar Bluff Townhome Condo. Ass'n v. Am. Family Mut. Ins.* (Minn. Ct. App. 2014) established the baseline matching obligation; *Noonan v. Am. Family Mut. Ins.* (8th Cir. 2019) and *Pleasure Creek Townhomes v. Am. Family Mut. Ins.* (Minn. Ct. App. 2019) upheld matching-exclusion endorsements. Family's counter-frame: J6944 only reaches *undamaged* property; if all windows sustained hail damage, J6944 is not engaged.

### E3. Reasonable Expectations Doctrine — *Atwater Creamery Co. v. Western Nat'l Mut. Ins. Co.*, 366 N.W.2d 271 (Minn. 1985)
Interpretive principle: ambiguous or hidden policy provisions are construed to honor the reasonable expectations of the insured. Candidate targets include the wear/tear and matching-exclusion provisions.

### E4. *Contra Proferentem*
Ambiguities in insurance policies are construed against the drafter (insurer). Standard interpretive rule; supports E3.

## F. Arguments Deliberately Excluded / Marginal

Listed so future sessions do not redo this triage:

- **Independent tort of spoliation** — Minnesota does not recognize one; covered by D3 remedies instead.
- **Class action / RICO** — no factual predicate on this record.
- **Reformation of the policy** — no allegation of scrivener's error or mutual mistake.
- **Specific performance** — inapt for money damages.
- **Promissory estoppel** — arguably subsumed into B4.
