1️⃣ Why starting in state court is considered “safer”

The short answer: because filing in state court preserves your claims even if federal jurisdiction fails, while filing in federal court does not.

Let’s unpack that precisely.

A. What happens if you start in federal court and jurisdiction fails?

If you file originally in federal court and the court later determines it lacks subject-matter jurisdiction (e.g., diversity fails because the insurer is a reciprocal exchange with MN members):

The federal court must dismiss the case
It cannot transfer it to state court
The dismissal is typically without prejudice, but…
⚠️ The statute of limitations problem

A federal dismissal for lack of jurisdiction does not automatically toll Minnesota’s contractual limitation period.

That means:

If you filed in federal court before 2 years, but
The case is dismissed after 2 years,

you may face a limitations defense when you refile in state court.

Minnesota does not have a general “savings statute” that automatically rescues you from this scenario in contract cases.

👉 This is the single biggest risk of starting in federal court.

B. What happens if you start in state court and they remove?

This is the mirror image — and it’s much safer.

If you file in Minnesota state court:

Filing in state court stops the limitations clock
If the insurer removes the case to federal court:
The case is treated as continuing, not newly filed
The federal court inherits the case as of the original filing date

So even if:

X = state filing date < 2 years
Y = removal date > 2 years

✅ Your case is still timely, because timeliness is measured from X, not Y.

Removal does not reset or re-trigger limitations.

This is black-letter removal law.

2️⃣ Can you “just refile” in state if federal jurisdiction fails?
In theory: yes
In practice: risky and sometimes fatal

Here’s the critical distinction:

Scenario	Limitations Risk
File state → removed to federal	❌ No risk
File state → stay in state	❌ No risk
File federal → jurisdiction upheld	❌ No risk
File federal → dismissed for lack of jurisdiction	⚠️ High risk

Why?

Because:

Federal courts cannot remand cases that were originally filed in federal court
A dismissal for lack of jurisdiction does not validate the original filing as a state-court filing
You are now relying on:
Equitable tolling (hard)
Waiver/estoppel (fact-intensive)
Or insurer mistake (unlikely)

Insurers love this trap.
